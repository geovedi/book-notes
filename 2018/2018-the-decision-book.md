# The Decision Book: Fifty Models for Strategic Thinking
**Authors**: Mikael Krogerus, Roman Tschäppeler, Jenny Piening | **Year**: 2018 | **Category**: Philosophy & Critical Thinking

## Summary
The Decision Book is a practical toolkit containing 50 decision-making models that help structure thinking and improve strategic choices. Designed for anyone who faces daily decisions—both big and small—this collection of visual frameworks simplifies complex situations into manageable analyses. Each model serves as a thinking tool rather than an answer provider, helping users organize their thoughts and evaluate options more systematically.

Perfect for managers, professionals, students, and anyone seeking better decision-making habits, this book offers quick, actionable techniques for personal growth, professional development, and strategic planning. Readers will gain mental models to cut through analysis paralysis, prioritize effectively, and make choices with greater confidence and clarity.

## Core Decision-Making Frameworks

### The Eisenhower Matrix: Master Your Priorities
This classic time-management tool helps distinguish between urgent and important tasks. Divide your activities into four quadrants: urgent/important (do immediately), not urgent/important (schedule), urgent/not important (delegate), and neither urgent nor important (eliminate). The key insight is that we often neglect the crucial "important but not urgent" quadrant where strategic thinking happens.

> "The most urgent decisions are rarely the most important ones." — Dwight D. Eisenhower

**Quick Take**: Schedule regular time for important but non-urgent tasks before they become crises.

### SWOT Analysis: Strategic Situational Awareness
Assess any situation by listing Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, and Threats. This versatile framework works equally well for business strategy, career planning, or personal development. The real power comes not just from listing items but from analyzing how they interconnect—how can you leverage strengths to capture opportunities, or how can you use your strengths to mitigate threats?

> "If you're not failing, you're not trying hard enough." — Gretchen Rubin

**Quick Take**: Use SWOT quarterly to review progress and adjust your strategy across all life areas.

### The Pareto Principle: Focus on What Matters Most
Also known as the 80/20 rule, this principle reveals that roughly 80% of results come from 20% of efforts. Identify and focus on the vital few activities that generate most of your desired outcomes. Apply this ruthlessly to your time, energy, and resources—find your 20% and invest heavily there.

> "I am definitely going to take a course on time management… just as soon as I can work it into my schedule." — Louis E. Boone

**Quick Take**: Track your activities for one week, then eliminate the 80% that produces minimal results.

### The BCG Box: Strategic Portfolio Management
Originally developed for business investment analysis, this four-quadrant matrix helps evaluate any set of options based on two criteria. Classify items as "Cash Cows" (high return, low effort), "Stars" (high return, high effort), "Question Marks" (uncertain return, high effort), or "Dogs" (low return, high effort). Focus your resources on Cash Cows and Stars.

**Quick Apply**: List your current projects and categorize them using this matrix to guide resource allocation.

### The Feedback Analysis: Know Your Strengths
Systematically track decisions and their outcomes to understand your natural strengths and decision-making patterns. When you make a decision, write down your reasoning and expected results. Review after 9-12 months to identify patterns in your thinking and natural abilities. This creates a personal decision-making database.

**Quick Apply**: Start a decision journal today and review quarterly to uncover your hidden strengths.

### The Flow Model: Find Your Optimal State
Understanding flow states helps identify activities where you're most effective and fulfilled. Flow occurs when challenge level matches skill level—too easy creates boredom, too hard creates anxiety. Regularly assess your activities to find your flow zones and seek more opportunities in those areas.

**Quick Apply**: Map your weekly activities using the flow model to identify where you perform best and feel most engaged.

### The Cognitive Dissonance Model: Resolve Internal Conflicts
This model explains why we hold contradictory beliefs and how to resolve mental conflicts. When beliefs and actions misalign, we experience discomfort that drives either belief change or behavior change. Understanding this helps recognize when you're rationalizing poor decisions or need to align actions with values.

**Quick Apply**: When feeling conflicted about a decision, identify the mismatched belief-behavior pair and address it directly.

## Quick Reference Models

### For Personal Effectiveness
- **The John Whitmore Model**: Use SMART goals (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound) to pursue the right objectives
- **The Rubber Band Model**: For dilemmas, list pros/cons, then trust your initial intuition
- **The Stop Rule**: Set clear decision criteria beforehand to know when to stop gathering information

### For Understanding Others
- **The Maslow Pyramids**: People prioritize basic needs before higher-level aspirations
- **The Conflict Resolution Model**: Address issues through interests, not positions
- **The Swiss Cheese Model**: Understand that mistakes happen through multiple small failures, not single events

### For Strategic Thinking
- **The Rumsfeld Matrix**: Consider known knowns, known unknowns, unknown knowns, and unknown unknowns
- **The Black Swan Model**: Prepare for rare, high-impact events that defy normal expectations
- **The Consequences Model**: Make decisions promptly because delayed choices have their own costs

## Decision-Making Action Items
- **Start a Decision Journal**: Track important choices and their outcomes for 6 months
- **Create Weekly Priorities**: Use the Eisenhower Matrix every Sunday to plan your week
- **Conduct Monthly SWOT Reviews**: Assess your personal and professional situation monthly
- **Identify Your 20%**: Use Pareto analysis to find your most productive activities
- **Map Your Flow States**: Track when you're most engaged and effective
- **Practice Rapid Decision-Making**: Use the Yes/No rule for small choices to build decision confidence

## Questions to Consider
- Which decisions have been consuming your mental energy without producing results?
- What patterns emerge when you review your past decisions?
- Are you spending time on urgent but unimportant activities instead of strategic thinking?
- Where do your natural decision-making strengths lie?
- What would happen if you trusted your intuition more often?
- Which mental models do you already use successfully?
- How might your decisions change if you focused only on your 20% high-impact activities?

## Conclusion
This book is worth your time if you frequently feel overwhelmed by choices or struggle with decision-making clarity. Its greatest strength is providing visual frameworks that transform abstract thinking into structured analysis. While no single model solves every problem, having this toolkit allows you to match the right mental model to each decision type.

The biggest reason to read this book is that decision-making is a skill that compounds—better choices today create better options tomorrow. In a world of increasing complexity and information overload, having proven frameworks to cut through noise is invaluable. The practical, visual nature of these models makes them immediately applicable, and the variety ensures you'll find several that resonate with your thinking style.

Whether you're leading a team, planning your career, or simply trying to make better daily choices, this book provides the mental scaffolding to think more clearly and act more decisively.
