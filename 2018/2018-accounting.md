# Accounting: Step by Step Guide to Accounting Principles & Basic Accounting for Small Business
**Authors**: Mark Smith | **Year**: 2018 | **Category**: Business & Finance

## Summary
"Accounting" is a comprehensive beginner's guide that demystifies the world of accounting for small business owners and entrepreneurs. The book breaks down complex financial concepts into understandable, actionable components, starting with the fundamental distinction between bookkeeping and accounting and progressing through essential financial statements, tax considerations, and practical implementation tips.

This guide is perfect for new business owners, freelancers, and anyone who needs to understand the financial foundations of running a business. You'll gain the confidence to manage your company's finances, create required financial documents, and make informed decisions based on accurate financial data.

## Core Insights

### Understanding Bookkeeping vs. Accounting
The book clarifies a common confusion: bookkeeping is simply recording financial transactions, while accounting involves analyzing, summarizing, and reporting that data to inform business decisions. Bookkeepers track daily transactions, while accountants interpret the data, prepare financial statements, and provide strategic insights.

> "The accountant can then take this information and help the company by analyzing, summarizing, and reporting the results of what they found."

**Quick Take**: You need both bookkeepers to record what happens and accountants to understand what it means for your business.

### The Three Essential Financial Statements
Every business must master three core financial documents:

1. **Income Statement** - Shows revenue and expenses over time, revealing whether you're making a profit
2. **Balance Sheet** - A snapshot of what you own (assets) versus what you owe (liabilities)
3. **Cash Flow Statement** - Tracks actual cash movement in and out of your business

> "These financial statements are very important to a business. First off, they are required by the SEC for any company that is publicly traded so having an accountant go through your records and create them can be important."

**Quick Take**: These documents tell the complete story of your business's financial health.

### The Fundamental Accounting Equation
All accounting rests on one simple formula: **Assets = Liabilities + Shareholder's Equity**

This equation must always balance, serving as a built-in check for accuracy. Everything your business owns must be funded by either debt (liabilities) or owner investment (equity).

> "What all this means is that all the assets, or the money used to operate the company, need to be balanced out by the financial obligations of the company, along with any of the equity investment that comes back to that company."

**Quick Take**: If your balance sheet doesn't balance, something is wrong with your numbers.

### Tax Accounting vs. GAAP
The book explains two different accounting rule sets: GAAP (Generally Accepted Accounting Principles) for public reporting and IRS tax rules for tax filing. These can differ significantly - what's required for financial statements may not match what the IRS requires for taxes.

> "While accounting is going to have a little bit to do with all of the financial transactions, tax accounting is going to focus all its energy on transactions that will affect the tax burden of a company."

**Quick Take**: Follow IRS rules for taxes and GAAP for financial statements - they serve different purposes.

### Choosing Accounting Software
The book reviews four main software options:

- **QuickBooks** ($10-60/month): Comprehensive, good for growing businesses
- **FreshBooks** ($15-50/month): Best for freelancers and service businesses
- **Xero** ($9-70/month): Similar to QuickBooks but includes payroll
- **Wave** (Free): Great for budget-conscious sole proprietors

> "QuickBooks is a name that a lot of people know about, and it can work well for businesses, no matter their shape or size."

**Quick Take**: Match your software choice to your business size, complexity, and budget.

## Action Items

- **Start simple**: Begin as a sole proprietorship if possible to minimize complexity
- **Separate finances**: Keep personal and business expenses completely separate from day one
- **Track everything**: Document all business expenses for potential tax deductions
- **Hire help**: Consider hiring a professional accountant, even part-time, to ensure accuracy
- **Schedule time**: Set aside regular time each week for bookkeeping tasks
- **Plan ahead**: Set aside money for major expenses like taxes and equipment upgrades
- **Follow up**: Actively pursue unpaid invoices to maintain cash flow

## Questions to Consider

- Do you understand the difference between profit (income statement) and cash (cash flow statement)?
- Are your personal and business finances truly separate?
- Do you have a system for tracking every business expense?
- Are you prepared for tax deadlines and major expenses?
- Do you know which accounting software best fits your business needs?

## Key Accounting Terms to Know

- **Assets**: What your business owns
- **Liabilities**: What your business owes
- **Equity**: Owner's investment stake
- **Revenue**: Money coming in
- **Expenses**: Money going out
- **Cash Flow**: Actual cash movement
- **GAAP**: Standard accounting rules for financial reporting
