# Investing: Make Money By Investing In Stock Market, Real Estate Rental Property, Bonds, Options, Cryptocurrency And Build Passive Income Business Portfolio

**Authors**: Brandon Hagstrom, Robert Turner | **Year**: 2018

## Summary

This comprehensive guide serves as an entry point for beginners looking to transform their relationship with money and build wealth through strategic investing. The book demystifies complex investment concepts while challenging conventional wisdom about saving and working for money. Drawing insights from financial legends like Warren Buffett and Robert Kiyosaki, it presents investing not as gambling, but as a deliberate strategy for making your money work for you.

The book covers the full spectrum of investment vehicles—from stocks and bonds to real estate and alternative investments—while emphasizing the psychological aspects that separate successful investors from those who fail. It's designed for anyone who wants to escape the cycle of trading time for money and build lasting financial freedom.

## Core Insights

### The Paradigm Shift: Working Smart vs. Working Hard

The book challenges the traditional "work hard, save money" mentality that keeps most people financially trapped. Instead, it promotes Robert Kiyosaki's philosophy that the rich don't work for money—they make money work for them. The key insight is that saving alone is no longer sufficient due to inflation and low interest rates. In the 1970s, savings accounts earned 15% interest; today they earn less than 1%, meaning even a million dollars in savings won't generate enough to live on.

The book explains that the middle-class mindset is consumerism: "work hard, buy things, repeat." In contrast, the wealthy mindset focuses on investing profits into assets that generate returns. This isn't about how much money you make, but how much you keep and how hard your money works for you.

> "The amount of money that someone has doesn't determine whether they're rich or poor. It's their mentality that counts."

**Quick Take**: Your financial mindset matters more than your income. Someone earning $2,000 monthly who invests $500 has a richer mentality than someone earning $15,000 who spends it all.

### The Power of Compound Interest

Albert Einstein called compound interest "the greatest mathematical discovery of all time" and "the most powerful force of the universe." The book demonstrates how this principle can transform modest investments into substantial wealth over time. When you invest $1,000 at 6% interest, you earn $60 the first year. Leave it invested, and in year two you earn $63.60—not just on your original $1,000, but also on the $60 interest from year one.

The magic works best with three ingredients: reinvested earnings, time, and an early start. A $100 investment earning 20% annually compounds to $3,833.74 over 20 years—38 times the original investment. This explains why starting early is crucial; time is the most powerful factor in wealth building.

> "Compound interest transforms your invested capital into a very powerful income-generating mechanism."

**Quick Take**: Start investing now, even with small amounts. The combination of time and reinvested returns creates exponential growth that late starters can never catch up to.

### Mastering Market Psychology

Successful investing requires controlling emotions that lead to poor decisions. The book identifies three emotional traps: panic during downturns, regret over missed opportunities, and hoping for recovery instead of taking action.

When stocks drop, most people panic and sell, but savvy investors see opportunities to buy quality assets below market value. The authors advise avoiding stocks you've sold to prevent regret-driven decisions. Most importantly, never rely on hope—use reason and research instead.

Flexibility is essential as markets constantly change. Rigid thinking leads to missed opportunities, while adaptability allows you to capitalize on market movements and protect your capital.

> "A real investor always assesses the risks prior to making an investment. You've to do a thorough analysis and invest wisely, instead of just praying for Lady Luck to smile at you."

**Quick Take**: Market downturns create buying opportunities. When everyone else is panicking and selling, that's often the best time to buy quality investments at discount prices.

### Portfolio Management and Diversification

Building a resilient portfolio requires understanding different investment types and spreading risk across multiple assets. The book covers the full spectrum: stocks, bonds, real estate, options, and alternative investments. Each has different risk-reward profiles and roles in a balanced portfolio.

Diversification works by investing in various businesses and commodities so that if one falls, others can hedge your losses. During recessions, high-yield dividend stocks perform well because they provide cash flow and tend to be less volatile. Tax-free municipal bonds also offer stability during economic downturns.

The authors recommend allocating 15% of gross income to investments, ideally through automated 401(k) contributions. An extra $100 monthly in a 401(k) starting at age 25 could generate $330,000 by retirement at 65 (assuming 8% annual returns).

> "The more time someone has to reinvest his or her earnings, the more earnings they'll likely receive due to compounding."

**Quick Take**: Diversify across different asset classes and risk levels. Use automated investing to ensure consistency and take advantage of compound growth.

### Expert Wisdom and Common Pitfalls

The book compiles insights from investment legends and financial experts. Warren Buffett's famous advice: "Be afraid when others are greedy, and greedy when others are afraid." This encapsulates the contrarian approach that often yields the best returns.

Common mistakes include lack of diversification, following Wall Street hype blindly, emotional decision-making, and failing to understand your risk tolerance. The experts emphasize that no investment is right for everyone—personal factors like age, financial situation, and personality should guide your strategy.

> "The goal for your personal investments isn't to be normal, because normal, in this day and age, is broke."

**Quick Take**: Develop your own investment strategy based on research and personal factors. Don't follow the crowd or hot trends blindly.

## Action Items

- Start investing immediately, even with small amounts. Time is more valuable than timing.
- Set up automatic monthly investments through your 401(k) or IRA. Aim for at least 15% of your income.
- Build a 6-month emergency fund before aggressive investing, but don't wait until you're debt-free to start.
- Research investments thoroughly before committing money. Never invest based on hype or tips.
- Diversify across different asset classes (stocks, bonds, real estate) and within each class.
- Review your portfolio quarterly, but don't make emotional changes based on short-term market movements.
- Consider high-dividend stocks during recessions for cash flow and stability.
- Use dollar-cost averaging by investing fixed amounts regularly regardless of market conditions.

## Questions to Consider

- What's your current relationship with money—consumer or investor mindset?
- How much risk can you truly tolerate without losing sleep?
- Are you letting emotions drive your investment decisions?
- Do you understand the difference between investing and gambling?
- How long do you have before needing to access your investment money?
- What percentage of your income are you currently investing for the future?
- Are you following expert advice or taking tips from friends who aren't successful investors?
- Would you buy the entire company if you could afford it when considering a stock purchase?

## Conclusion

This book is worth your time if you're ready to challenge conventional wisdom about money and take control of your financial future. It provides a solid foundation for understanding how investing works while avoiding the complexity that often paralyzes beginners. The biggest reason to read it is to shift from a saving mindset to an investing mindset—making your money work for you instead of just working for money.

The book succeeds in demystifying investing while emphasizing the psychological aspects that truly determine success. It's not about finding secret formulas or hot tips, but about developing patience, discipline, and the right approach to risk. For anyone tired of trading time for money and ready to build lasting wealth, this guide provides the roadmap to get started on the right foot. The most valuable takeaway is that building wealth isn't about how much you earn—it's about how you think about and use money to create more money.
