# In Math We Trust
**Authors**: Simon Dingle | **Year**: 2018 | **Category**: Business & Finance

## Summary
"In Math We Trust" is a comprehensive exploration of Bitcoin and cryptocurrency's revolutionary impact on money, trust, and economic systems. Written in 2018, the book traces the evolution of money from physical shells and bones to digital currency, explaining how Bitcoin's emergence in 2009 fundamentally changed our understanding of trust and value. Dingle argues that our current financial system, built on centralized authority and what he calls the "bullshit standard" of fiat currency, is fundamentally flawed and being replaced by a new paradigm of mathematical trust.

This book is perfect for anyone curious about cryptocurrency beyond the hype, financial professionals looking to understand the technological disruption ahead, or individuals seeking to understand how they can take control of their own financial future. Readers will gain practical insights into becoming their own bank while understanding the broader economic and social implications of this technological revolution.

## Core Insights

### The Evolution of Trust and Money
Money has always been a story of trust—from the shells and bones used by early tribal societies to the gold coins of kingdoms and the digital currency of today. Each evolution required us to outsource our trust to increasingly centralized authorities. The current fiat currency system operates on what Dingle calls the "bullshit standard"—money has value because governments say so, not because of any intrinsic worth. This system has created unprecedented wealth inequality and economic instability, with central banks able to print money at will, leading to inflation and financial crises like the 2008 subprime lending collapse that Bitcoin was designed to address.

> "The history of MONEY is a story of trust. Money has come with us from the beginning of our cultural evolution and is a symbol of the faith we have in each other."

**Quick Take**: Understanding money's evolution helps us see why Bitcoin's mathematical approach to trust is so revolutionary and necessary.

### Bitcoin's Revolutionary Genesis
Bitcoin emerged on Halloween 2008 when the mysterious Satoshi Nakamoto posted a white paper to a cryptography mailing list with the simple declaration: "I've been working on a new electronic cash system that's fully peer-to-peer, with no trusted third party." This wasn't just another digital currency—it combined decades of cryptographic research to solve the double-spending problem that had plagued previous digital money attempts. The timing was crucial: Satoshi mined the first Bitcoin block (Genesis Block) on January 3, 2009, the same day the UK Chancellor announced a second bank bailout, embedding the message "The Times 03/Jan/2009 Chancellor on brink of second bailout for banks" in the blockchain—a permanent critique of the failing financial system.

> "I've been working on a new electronic cash system that's fully peer-to-peer, with no trusted third party."

**Quick Take**: Bitcoin wasn't just technological innovation; it was a direct response to financial system failures, created at the perfect moment to offer an alternative.

### Digital Scarcity: The Impossible Innovation
Before Bitcoin, digital abundance ruled the internet—you could copy anything infinitely at near-zero cost. Bitcoin introduced the concept of digital scarcity for the first time, making it possible to transfer unique digital items without creating copies. This breakthrough enabled a new digital economy where things could be genuinely rare and valuable online. Bitcoin's supply is capped at 21 million coins, with the last Bitcoin scheduled to be mined in 2140. Each Bitcoin is divisible into 100 million units called "satoshis," allowing for precise microtransactions while maintaining scarcity that increases value over time, unlike inflationary fiat currencies.

> "In 2140 THE LAST Bitcoin will be mined. It might sound crazy that something digitally produced could also be scarce, but this paradox is the reason Bitcoin is such a big deal."

**Quick Take**: Digital scarcity enables a new economic paradigm where value can appreciate rather than depreciate, fundamentally changing how we think about money and assets.

### Network Topologies: From Centralized to Distributed
The book explains that all information networks fall into three categories: centralized (star networks with single points of control), decentralized (some nodes have more power than others), and distributed (all nodes are equal). Our current financial system uses centralized networks where banks and governments control everything. Bitcoin operates on a decentralized network that works toward becoming fully distributed, with "full nodes" that enforce rules and miners that process transactions. This distributed nature makes Bitcoin incredibly resilient—there's no central point of failure or attack.

> "It's impossible to capture a distributed system because the network will interpret any attempt at control as damage and route around it."

**Quick Take**: Network architecture determines who has power—distributed networks return control to individuals rather than concentrating it in institutions.

### The Blockchain and Smart Contracts Revolution
While Bitcoin pioneered blockchain technology, platforms like Ethereum expanded its capabilities beyond simple transactions. Blockchains are essentially "mathematical proof that something happened"—immutable records that grow more secure over time. This technology enables smart contracts that execute automatically when conditions are met, eliminating the need for trusted intermediaries in everything from legal agreements to financial derivatives. The famous CryptoKitties example demonstrated how unique digital assets could be created, bred, and traded on blockchain, with some virtual cats selling for over $100,000, showing real economic value in purely digital scarcity.

> "Whereas most technologies tend to automate workers on the periphery doing menial tasks, blockchains automate away the center."

**Quick Take**: Blockchain technology eliminates middlemen across industries, creating direct peer-to-peer interactions that are more efficient and equitable.

### Being Your Own Bank
The core promise of cryptocurrency is financial sovereignty—being truly in control of your own money without relying on banks or governments. This doesn't mean living as a hermit with gold bars; it means using cryptographic tools to store and transact value directly. In the current system, when you deposit money in a bank, it legally becomes the bank's property, and you're just an unsecured creditor. With cryptocurrency, your private keys give you direct ownership that cannot be seized or frozen. This shift from permission-based to permissionless finance represents one of the biggest transfers of power in human history.

> "Being your own bank means not having to rely on companies telling you when and how you may use your money and, most importantly, recognition that your money is yours and not legally the property of a company."

**Quick Take**: Financial sovereignty is possible today through cryptocurrency, but it comes with the responsibility of managing your own security.

## Action Items

- **Start small with cryptocurrency**: Begin by buying a small amount of Bitcoin to understand how wallets, transactions, and private keys work before committing significant funds.

- **Set up secure storage**: Use hardware wallets (like Ledger or Trezor) for storing significant amounts, and always keep backup copies of your private keys or recovery phrases in multiple secure locations.

- **Diversify across different cryptocurrencies**: While Bitcoin remains the foundation, explore other cryptocurrencies like Ethereum for smart contracts and specialized use cases that match your interests.

- **Educate yourself continuously**: The cryptocurrency space evolves rapidly—follow reputable sources, join communities, and never stop learning about new developments and security practices.

- **Practice operational security**: Use two-factor authentication, be wary of phishing attempts, and never share your private keys with anyone or enter them on websites you don't completely trust.

- **Consider running a full node**: If you're technically inclined, running a Bitcoin full node contributes to network security and gives you voting rights on protocol upgrades.

- **Explore DeFi opportunities**: Investigate decentralized finance platforms for lending, borrowing, and earning interest on your cryptocurrency holdings without traditional banks.

## Conclusion

"In Math We Trust" is more than just a Bitcoin book—it's a comprehensive guide to understanding the biggest financial revolution since the invention of banking itself. Dingle does an excellent job of making complex cryptographic concepts accessible while maintaining technical accuracy and avoiding the hype and conspiracy theories that plague much cryptocurrency writing.

The book is worth your time if you want to understand not just what Bitcoin is, but why it matters and how it fits into the broader evolution of human trust systems. Dingle's accessible writing style and practical focus make this an ideal starting point for anyone looking to understand cryptocurrency beyond speculative trading.

The biggest reason to read this book is that it helps you see cryptocurrency not as an isolated technology, but as part of humanity's ongoing journey toward more efficient, fair, and trustworthy systems of cooperation. Whether you're a financial professional, technologist, or simply someone curious about the future, this book provides the mental models needed to navigate the changes coming to money, trust, and economic power in the 21st century.

The future Dingle envisions—one of seamless, automated transactions and financial sovereignty for all—may seem distant, but as he demonstrates, the technology already exists. What's missing is broader understanding and adoption. This book helps fill that gap, making it essential reading for anyone who wants to be part of, rather than victim to, the financial revolution underway.
