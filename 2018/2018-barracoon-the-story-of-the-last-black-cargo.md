# Barracoon: The Story of the Last "Black Cargo"
**Authors**: Zora Neale Hurston, Deborah G. Plant (ed.) | **Year**: 2018 | **Category**: History & Biography

## Summary
"Barracoon" is the extraordinary oral history of Cudjo Lewis (born Kossula), the last known survivor of the transatlantic slave trade. Captured in West Africa at age 19 and transported to America on the illegal slave ship Clotilda in 1859, Kossula spent five years in enslavement before emancipation. The book captures his firsthand account of African village life, the brutal raid that tore him from his home, the horrors of the barracoon and Middle Passage, and his subsequent life in America where he helped found Africatown, Alabama.

This groundbreaking work gives voice to someone long silenced—offering not just the perspective of the captured, but the rare, authentic voice of the "cargo" itself. Readers seeking to understand the human cost of slavery beyond statistics will find an intimate, haunting portrait of resilience, memory, and cultural survival.

## Core Insights

### The Only Voice from the Cargo Hold
This book represents something unprecedented in slave trade literature: the actual voice of someone transported as cargo, rather than the sellers, captains, or abolitionists. Kossula's narrative fills the silence that Zora Neale Hurston identified when she wrote, "All these words from the seller, but not one word from the sold." His testimony preserves African cultural practices, family structures, and spiritual beliefs that would otherwise have been lost to history.

> "Oh Lor', I no see Afficky soil no mo'!"
**Quick Take**: Personal testimony preserves what official records erase—human experience.

### Memory as Cultural Resistance
Kossula's determination to maintain his African identity in the face of systematic erasure demonstrates the power of memory as resistance. He insists on being called by his African name Kossula rather than the Americanized Cudjo Lewis. He preserves African cultural practices, from storytelling to spiritual beliefs, even while adapting to life in America. His detailed recollections of African village life serve as an act of cultural preservation against the forces that sought to strip him of his identity.

> "My name, is not Cudjo Lewis. It Kossula. When I gittee in Americky soil, Mr. Jim Meaher he try callee my name, but it too long, you unnerstand me, so I say, 'Well, I yo' property?' He say, 'Yeah.' Den I say, 'You callee me Cudjo. Dat do.' But in Afficky soil my mama she name me Kossula."
**Quick Take**: Naming yourself is an act of resistance when others try to define you.

### The Human Cost Behind Historical Records
The book reveals how the illegal slave trade continued long after abolition, driven by greed and opportunity. The Meaher brothers' 1859 voyage on the Clotilda wasn't an anomaly but part of a larger system of exploitation. Kossula's story transforms abstract historical statistics into lived experience—the raid on his village, the death of his father, the separation from everything he knew, and the ongoing trauma of displacement.

> "I so lonely. My wife she left me since de 1908. Cudjo all by hisself."
**Quick Take**: History happens to real people who feel loneliness, loss, and love.

### Creating Home in Exile
After emancipation, Kossula and his fellow captives established Africatown (now Plateau, Alabama), creating a new community rooted in African cultural traditions while adapting to American circumstances. This act of place-making represents both resistance to displacement and the creative adaptation necessary for survival. The community became a living repository of African culture in the American South.

> "Thankee Jesus! Somebody come ast about Cudjo! I want tellee somebody who I is, so maybe dey go in de Afficky soil some day and callee my name and somebody dere say, 'Yeah, I know Kossula.'"
**Quick Take**: Home isn't just where you're from—it's what you build and preserve.

### The Complexity of Faith and Identity
Kossula's navigation between African spiritual beliefs and Christianity reveals the complex ways enslaved people adapted religious frameworks while maintaining core cultural values. He explains that in Africa "we always know dere was a God; he name Alahua, but po' Affickans we cain readee de Bible, so we doan know God got a Son." This demonstrates cultural adaptation rather than wholesale rejection of previous beliefs.

> "Yeah, in Afficky we always know dere was a God; he name Alahua, but po' Affickans we cain readee de Bible, so we doan know God got a Son. We ain' ignant—we jes doan know."
**Quick Take**: Cultural adaptation often involves blending rather than replacing beliefs.

## Action Items
- **Listen to marginalized voices**: Seek out firsthand accounts from communities whose stories have been historically silenced
- **Preserve family histories**: Record and share elder stories before they're lost to time
- **Acknowledge complex truths**: Recognize that historical injustices often involved multiple parties, including African complicity in the slave trade
- **Visit historical sites**: If possible, visit places like Africatown to connect with physical manifestations of these histories
- **Support cultural preservation**: Learn about and support efforts to maintain endangered cultural practices and languages

## Questions to Consider
- How does hearing a firsthand account change your understanding of historical events compared to reading statistics or third-person narratives?
- What aspects of your cultural identity would you fight to preserve if everything else was taken away?
- How do communities maintain cultural continuity when displaced from their homelands?
- In what ways does personal storytelling serve as resistance against historical erasure?
- How might we apply lessons of cultural resilience to contemporary struggles for justice and recognition?

## Conclusion
"Barracoon" is essential reading because it transforms abstract historical tragedy into intimate human experience. Kossula's voice bridges centuries, allowing us to understand not just what happened during the slave trade, but how it felt to live through it. His story reveals both the devastating costs of displacement and the remarkable resilience of the human spirit.

The book's greatest strength lies in its preservation of African cultural memory and its unflinching look at how slavery destroyed not just lives but entire worlds. Yet within this loss, Kossula's testimony demonstrates how memory, storytelling, and community-building can serve as powerful acts of resistance and survival.

This is worth your time because it offers something rare: the authentic voice of someone who experienced one of history's greatest tragedies firsthand, speaking not as a symbol or statistic but as a complete human being with loves, losses, beliefs, and an unbreakable connection to home. In an era where historical narratives are increasingly contested, Kossula's story reminds us that truth often lives in the personal details rather than grand pronouncements.
