# How to Audit Your Account without Hiring an Auditor
**Authors**: Aaron Efuribe | **Year**: 2018

## Summary

This practical guide demystifies the auditing process for small and medium-sized business owners who want to take control of their financial oversight without the expense of external auditors. The author presents a comprehensive framework for implementing internal controls and conducting self-audits, arguing that business owners are uniquely positioned to examine their own records effectively because they understand their operations intimately.

Perfect for entrepreneurs, small business owners, and managers who want to reduce costs while maintaining financial integrity. Readers will gain the knowledge and confidence to implement robust internal controls, detect fraud, and ensure accurate financial reporting without hiring expensive external auditors.

## Core Audit Framework

### Understanding Auditing Fundamentals

Auditing is fundamentally about independent examination of financial information to ensure accuracy and fairness. While external audits are statutory requirements for public companies, small businesses can conduct effective internal audits to maintain financial discipline and prevent fraud. The key is understanding that auditing is not about finding every single error—it's about creating systems that make errors and fraud difficult to perpetrate.

> "Auditing is basically examining records, financial activities incurred during a period and ensuring that such record is error free, fraud free and that records are accurate, reliable and efficient."

**Quick Take**: You don't need accounting expertise to audit your own business effectively—your intimate knowledge of operations is actually an advantage.

### Building Your Internal Control System

The foundation of effective self-auditing is a comprehensive internal control system. This involves eight key components: proper organization structure, segregation of duties, arithmetic controls, personnel procedures, authorization processes, physical security, supervision, and management oversight. The most critical element is ensuring no single person controls entire transaction cycles.

> "The involvement of several people reduces the risk of intentional manipulation or accidental error and increases the element of checking of work."

**Quick Take**: Separate duties so that no one employee can initiate, approve, execute, and record a complete transaction without oversight.

### Understanding Financial Risks

Businesses face three main financial threats: errors (unintentional mistakes), irregularities (intentional distortions), and fraud (criminal deception). Employee fraud typically includes fictitious payments, wage fraud, cash misappropriation, and outright theft. Management fraud involves deceiving external parties like government agencies and creditors.

> "Audit fees are cost charged in the expense profile of a company and I want to let you know that this expense is avoidable because it's not statutorily required to have your account audited."

**Quick Take**: Most audit costs are avoidable expenses that erode profits—implementing strong internal controls is more cost-effective than hiring external auditors.

## Transaction Control Systems

### Receipt Management

Establish strict procedures for handling all incoming funds: use official receipt books with security markings, maintain receipt registers, require revenue collectors to account for collections at specified intervals, and ensure all collections are banked within 24 hours. Never make expenditures from daily collections—bank all receipts intact.

**Essential Controls**:
- Limit authority to receive money to as few people as possible
- Record all receipt books in a register with signature tracking
- Bank all collections within 24 hours without exception
- Never mix daily receipts with expenditure funds

### Payment Authorization

All payments must be covered by duly approved vouchers showing amount, payee, purpose, account to be debited, and authorization. Major payments should be made by crossed checks marked "account payee only" with minimal cash transactions. Maintain strict control over check books and ensure proper documentation for all expenditures.

**Essential Controls**:
- Use standard payment vouchers for all transactions
- Set clear authorization limits based on amount and payment type
- Make major payments by check only
- Maintain petty cash on strict imprest basis

### Payroll Protection

Prevent wage fraud through clear employment procedures, proper timekeeping systems, segregated payroll preparation duties, and identification verification during payment. Unclaimed wages should be returned to the bank and investigated immediately as they may indicate ghost workers.

**Essential Controls**:
- Separate payroll preparation, approval, and payment functions
- Verify worker identity during payment distribution
- Investigate all unclaimed wages promptly
- Compare actual payroll costs against budget regularly

### Purchase and Sales Controls

For purchases, implement proper requisition systems, written orders with clear specifications, physical inspection of goods, and invoice verification before payment. For sales, establish credit approval procedures, proper order recording, authorized discount policies, and regular debt collection monitoring.

**Essential Controls**:
- Use written purchase orders for all procurement
- Inspect goods for quantity and quality before acceptance
- Grant credit only after proper approval
- Monitor aged debtors and collection patterns

## Self-Audit Implementation

### Compliance Testing

Regularly verify that your internal control procedures are working as designed. Check that staff are following established protocols, that segregation of duties is maintained, and that all transactions are properly authorized and recorded. Your intimate knowledge of the business makes you uniquely qualified to assess whether controls are effective in practice.

### Verification Testing

Apply the CAVEBOP principle to verify key assets and liabilities:
- **Cost**: Verify asset values against purchase documentation
- **Authorization**: Confirm proper acquisition and disposal approvals
- **Valuation**: Check depreciation and asset impairment calculations
- **Existence**: Physically inspect assets to confirm they exist
- **Beneficial Ownership**: Ensure assets belong to the company
- **Presentation**: Verify proper financial statement disclosure

### Monthly Audit Routine

Conduct monthly examinations after receiving financial reports. Focus on high-risk areas like cash handling, payroll, procurement, and expense claims. Document your findings and implement corrective actions immediately. Consider annual independent audits for legal compliance, but rely on your monthly reviews for ongoing control.

- How quickly could you detect if cash was being misappropriated?
- Do you have documented procedures for all major financial transactions?

## Conclusion

This book is worth your time if you're a small business owner looking to reduce costs while maintaining financial integrity. The biggest reason to read it is that it demystifies auditing and provides practical, implementable systems that don't require accounting expertise. The author's most valuable insight is that business owners are actually better positioned than external auditors to detect irregularities because they understand normal business patterns and staff behavior.

While some control recommendations may seem excessive for very small businesses, the framework provides a solid foundation that can be scaled according to business size and complexity. The CAVEBOP verification principle alone could save many businesses from significant financial losses through simple, systematic asset verification.
