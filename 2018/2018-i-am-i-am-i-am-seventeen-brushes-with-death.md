# I Am, I Am, I Am: Seventeen Brushes with Death
**Author**: Maggie O'Farrell | **Year**: 2018

## Summary
Maggie O'Farrell's memoir is a haunting exploration of mortality structured around seventeen near-death experiences throughout her life, organized by the body parts involved. From a childhood encephalitis that left her with permanent neurological damage to a terrifying childbirth emergency and her daughter's life-threatening allergies, O'Farrell examines how close encounters with death reshape our relationship with life.

This is not a book about dying, but about living with an acute awareness of death's proximity. O'Farrell's luminous prose transforms potentially morbid subject matter into a celebration of resilience, the fragility of human existence, and the fierce determination to survive. Each brush with death becomes a lens through which she examines memory, fear, love, and the extraordinary miracle of ordinary existence.

Readers who have faced their own mortality, cared for seriously ill loved ones, or simply want to deepen their appreciation for life's precarious beauty will find this memoir both terrifying and profoundly affirming.

## Core Insights

### The Body as Storyteller
O'Farrell organizes her experiences not chronologically but by body parts—Neck (1990), Lungs (1988), Spine, Legs, Pelvis, Abdomen, Head (1977), and finally Daughter (present day). This brilliant structure reveals how our bodies carry the maps of our survival stories, each scar and vulnerability becoming a testament to our capacity to endure.

> "When you are a child, no one tells you that you're going to die. You have to work it out for yourself."

The memoir shows how our relationship with our bodies changes after trauma. The body becomes both betrayer and savior, both fragile and remarkably resilient. O'Farrell's permanent neurological damage from childhood encephalitis serves as constant reminder of how close she came to not surviving, yet also proof of her body's determination to heal.

**Quick Take**: Your body tells the story of what you've survived; listen to it with compassion rather than resentment.

### The Clarity that Comes from Crisis
In moments of life-threatening danger, O'Farrell discovers how thinking sharpens and priorities crystallize. Whether racing through the Italian countryside with her daughter suffering from anaphylactic shock or lying in a hospital bed as a child unable to move, she experiences a profound clarity about what truly matters.

> "In moments like these, your thinking shrinks, sharpens, narrows. The world shutters up and you are reduced to a crystalline pinpoint, to a single purpose: to keep your child alive, to ensnare her in the world of the living, to hang on to her and never let go."

This crisis clarity strips away trivial concerns and reveals the essential: love, connection, survival. The mundane preoccupations of daily life fall away, leaving only what truly matters.

**Quick Take**: Life-threatening experiences don't just show us what we're afraid of losing—they reveal what we most value living for.

### The Aftermath: Living with Death's Shadow
Perhaps most powerful is O'Farrell's exploration of what happens after the crisis passes. How do you live knowing how easily you could have died? How does knowledge of your own vulnerability or your child's fragility reshape your relationship with the world?

> "I have to clear my throat and take a deep breath. 'The thing is...' I begin, and then I relate to them, in brief, in essence, the contents of this letter."

The memoir reveals how near-death experiences create a permanent shift in perspective. Simple moments become extraordinary, ordinary days feel like gifts, and the awareness of mortality becomes not a burden but a lens that brings life into sharper focus.

**Quick Take**: Living with death's shadow doesn't diminish life—it illuminates what makes it precious.

### The Interconnectedness of Our Stories
O'Farrell masterfully weaves her personal experiences with scientific, historical, and medical context. From the ancient Egyptian pharaoh who died from anaphylaxis to the neurological explanations of cerebellar damage, she places her individual story within the broader tapestry of human vulnerability and resilience.

> "Our brains are a mass, a web, of interconnecting cells, which are lit up, like strings of fairy-lights, by communication. We are, at our very core, in our very essence, animated by circuitry, by the transmission of information."

This approach transforms personal suffering into universal understanding, showing how our individual brushes with death connect us to the long lineage of humans who have faced mortality and found ways to survive.

**Quick Take**: Your survival story is part of humanity's larger story of resilience—recognizing this connection makes both the struggle and the triumph feel less lonely.

## Takeaways
- **Practice awareness**: Notice the small miracles of your body functioning normally—breathing, moving, healing
- **Create emergency plans**: Having contingency plans for medical emergencies can provide crucial peace of mind
- **Trust your instincts**: When something feels wrong with your health or your child's, advocate persistently for medical attention
- **Find your support network**: Build relationships with medical professionals and others who understand your health challenges
- **Live with appreciation**: Let awareness of mortality enhance rather than diminish your enjoyment of life

## Questions to Consider
- What brushes with death have you or your loved ones experienced, and how have they shaped your perspective on life?
- How do you balance vigilance about health risks with living fully and without constant fear?
- What do your own body's scars and vulnerabilities tell you about your resilience story?
- How might awareness of mortality help you prioritize what truly matters in your daily life?
- Who are the people—medical professionals, family, friends—who have supported you through health crises?

## Conclusion
Maggie O'Farrell's memoir is both terrifying and ultimately life-affirming. She doesn't romanticize suffering or offer easy answers about mortality. Instead, she presents an honest, lyrical exploration of how close encounters with death can illuminate what makes life worth living.

This book is essential reading for anyone who has faced serious illness, cared for someone with health challenges, or simply wants to deepen their appreciation for the fragile miracle of existence. O'Farrell's prose is so luminous and her insights so profound that the potentially grim subject matter becomes instead a celebration of human resilience and the extraordinary courage required to keep living when death feels so close.

The biggest reason to read this book is not morbid curiosity but O'Farrell's remarkable ability to transform vulnerability into wisdom, showing how the awareness of death can deepen our capacity for living fully. It's a powerful reminder that every day we survive is worth celebrating, every breath we take is a small miracle, and every moment with those we love is infinitely precious.
