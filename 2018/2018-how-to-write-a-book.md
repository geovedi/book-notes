# How to Write a Book
**Authors**: David Kadavy | **Year**: 2018
**Category**: Business & Finance

## Summary
"How to Write a Book" presents a practical 11-step process that transforms book writing from an overwhelming mystery into an achievable project. Kadavy reveals that writing a book is both harder than you can imagine (requiring dedication few apply to anything) and easier than you can imagine (it's just putting words on a page consistently). The book demolishes common myths that paralyze aspiring authors - that writing must always be hard, that books are written straight through, and that you shouldn't give your writing away.

This guide is perfect for aspiring authors who feel paralyzed by perfectionism, procrastination, or the sheer scale of writing a book. You'll gain a step-by-step system for building writing habits, developing your voice through publishing, and creating a book that readers actually want to buy. By the end, you'll have the confidence that writing a book is a learnable skill, not an innate talent reserved for the chosen few.

Build a daily writing habit so small it's nearly impossible to fail. Kadavy recommends aiming for just 100 words or 10 minutes per day. The key is making the habit ridiculously small - you should feel it's easier to do than skip.

> "The Grand Canyon was not formed overnight. The Colorado River carved a little bit of it each day."

**Quick Take**: Stack your writing habit after an existing routine (like brushing teeth) and quit when you hit your minimum target, even if you want to continue. This builds positive associations with writing.

Before writing your book, study successful books as commercial products. Download Kindle samples and analyze what makes readers buy. Read the first sentence and notice your reaction. Study titles, subtitles, and openings to understand how they promise value.

> "A book is much more than just a collection of words. The words are arranged in a certain way so that the reader will enjoy it and recommend it to a friend."

**Quick Take**: Regularly read Kindle samples with a "product analyst" mindset. Why would someone buy this book? What problem does it solve?

Publish your writing regularly to develop courage and get feedback. Kadavy recommends publishing on Medium daily. This builds your "publishing muscle" and helps you overcome fear of imaginary critics.

> "Publishing your work is in itself a skill. The more you do it, the better you get at it."

**Quick Take**: Use Medium's highlight feature to see exactly what resonates with readers. People highlight what they want to remember and share - this is market research gold.

Start collecting email addresses early. Having even a few hundred readers who eagerly await your work makes the book writing process worthwhile and motivating. Use simple sign-up links in your published content.

**Quick Take**: Your email list becomes your accountability partners and first readers when you're ready to write your book.

Your title needs to pass the "cocktail party test" - people should feel cool saying they're reading your book. Consider keywords, spoken clarity, rhythm, and emotional resonance. Your title makes people want to buy before they read a word.

**Quick Take**: Test titles by imagining saying them aloud. Would you be proud or embarrassed to tell someone you're reading a book with this title?

You can't effectively outline until you've developed a point of view through consistent writing and publishing. After months of daily writing, topics will naturally connect in your mind, making outlining much easier.

**Quick Take**: Don't force early outlining. Let your ideas percolate through regular writing until a coherent perspective emerges.

### Step 7: Write the First Draft Fast ðŸš€
Once you can see each chapter in your head, write the first draft quickly using "motivational judo" - tell yourself you only need to write 250-500 words per chapter, even if you end up writing more. Hold yourself accountable by promising readers early access.

> "It's like you have a bunch of pieces of fabric and now you're going to stitch them together into a quilt."

**Quick Take**: Create accountability by sharing your chapter outline and promising daily delivery to your email list. This builds audience and motivation simultaneously.

### Step 8: Read Your Draft Objectively ðŸ‘€
Print your first draft and read it away from computers. First read through to "feel" the book, then read again to make structural notes. Let it incubate for weeks before revisiting.

**Quick Take**: The physical act of holding your printed book creates powerful motivation and helps you see your work as a reader would.

After letting your draft incubate, write a completely new outline from memory. You'll likely discover a clearer structure and may need to split your book or remove entire sections.

**Quick Take**: First-time authors typically cram too many ideas into one book. Be ruthless about what stays and what gets cut.

### Step 10: Write the Second Draft âœ¨
Focus on structure, opening hooks, transitions, and storytelling. You're no longer just a writer - you're a tour guide leading readers through the jungle of your mind.

**Quick Take**: Pay special attention to your opening. Study your favorite Kindle samples and make sure your first pages compel readers to buy the full book.

Get editing help, whether professional or from your email list ("crowd-editing"). Remember that perfect books don't exist - your goal is "good enough" to publish. You can always update digital editions later.

> "Every book you write is going to be imperfect. How imperfect can you handle it being?"

**Quick Take**: Consider offering early access to your email list in exchange for feedback. This creates invested advocates who will promote your book.

## Essential Writing Mantras

- **"I am a bricklayer"** - Writing is work, not magic inspiration

## Conclusion
Kadavy's genius lies in transforming book writing from an act of heroic inspiration into a systematic process of habit-building. The 11-step framework removes the mystery and replaces it with actionable daily practices. This book is worth your time if you've ever felt paralyzed by the scale of writing a book or believed that authors are somehow different from regular people.

The biggest reason to read this guide is that it gives you permission to be imperfect while providing a clear path to improvement. You'll finish knowing that writing a book isn't about finding your muse or waiting for inspiration - it's about consistent daily practice and understanding books as products that serve readers' needs. By the end, you'll have the confidence that you can write a book, and more importantly, the practical tools to actually do it.
