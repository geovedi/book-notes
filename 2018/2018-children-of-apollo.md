# Children of Apollo
**Authors**: Adam Alexander Haviaras | **Year**: 2018

## Summary
Children of Apollo is a gripping historical fiction novel set in 3rd century Roman North Africa, following Lucius Metellus Anguis, a young Roman tribune serving in Emperor Septimius Severus's legions. The story weaves together military campaigns, political intrigue, and elements of historical fantasy as Lucius navigates the treacherous world of Roman frontier warfare while grappling with divine destiny and mortal threats.

This epic tale transports readers to the harsh deserts of North Africa and the bustling streets of Rome, where Lucius must prove himself as a leader while uncovering traitors within his ranks, surviving assassination attempts, and discovering his connection to the god Apollo. Perfect for fans of Roman military fiction, historical fantasy, and epic adventures that blend meticulously researched history with mythological elements.

## The Story Arc

### **The Desert Tribune**
Lucius Metellus Anguis begins as a young, idealistic tribune stationed in the African province, commanding men in brutal desert campaigns against nomadic raiders. Armed with his father's dragon-embossed armor and a deep love for Roman literature, Lucius struggles with the harsh realities of military leadership while maintaining his sense of honor and compassion.

> "He was like a weathered sack of bones, possessed bones. The seeress rocked back and forth, inhaling the pungent smoke of the fire."

**Quick Take**: Lucius represents the collision of Roman ideals with brutal reality - a scholar forced into warrior's shoes.

### **Divine Intervention and Betrayal**
The story takes a dramatic turn when Lucius saves a mysterious stranger named Ashur in the desert, who later reveals himself as connected to the gods. This encounter triggers a series of supernatural events and warnings about traitors within Lucius's own ranks. The revelation that his men have been colluding with nomadic enemies forces Lucius to make devastating moral choices.

> "You are blessed, Metellus. I had a dream...in it...I saw this. This is the symbol of your line to come."

**Quick Take**: Divine purpose collides with human treachery, testing Lucius's faith in both gods and men.

### **The Price of Command**
As Lucius uncovers a conspiracy reaching deep within his cohort, he must order the brutal punishment of decimation - executing one in ten of his own men. This traumatic experience hardens him as a leader but leaves emotional scars. The aftermath includes threats from surviving traitors and growing recognition of his capabilities from senior commanders.

> "The life of each auxiliary cavalry trooper in that courtyard was in his hands. He stood tall and serious in full armour as his crimson cloak bristled in the dusty breeze."

**Quick Take**: Leadership requires impossible choices that define both character and destiny.

### **Return to Rome**
The second half of the novel shifts to Rome itself, where Lucius is summoned to the capital. Here he navigates the complex web of Roman political society, reunites with family including his sister Alene, and faces new threats in the urban jungle. The contrast between frontier warfare and Roman political maneuvering highlights Lucius's growth and adaptability.

**Quick Take**: Survival skills honed in the desert prove equally valuable in Rome's treacherous political landscape.

### **Divine Destiny Unveiled**
Throughout his journey, Lucius gradually discovers his unique connection to Apollo and the significance of the dragon symbol that has marked his family line. This mythological element elevates the story from pure historical fiction to historical fantasy, suggesting that Lucius's struggles and triumphs are part of a larger divine plan orchestrated by the gods themselves.

**Quick Take**: Mortal battles reflect divine wars, with humanity caught between godly ambitions.

## Key Characters

- **Lucius Metellus Anguis**: The protagonist - young tribune torn between scholarly ideals and military reality
- **Argus**: Lucius's loyal childhood friend and fellow soldier, protective and pragmatic
- **Ashur**: Mysterious stranger with divine connections who guides and warns Lucius
- **Alene**: Lucius's sister, representing family ties and Roman domestic life
- **Antanelis**: Young centurion serving under Lucius, representing the next generation of Roman soldiers
- **Brutus**: Traitorous centurion who becomes Lucius's nemesis

## What Readers Gain

- **Deep immersion in 3rd century Roman military life** - from desert campaigns to urban political maneuvering
- **Understanding of Roman moral complexities** - where honor, duty, and survival often conflict
- **Insight into leadership challenges** - showing how difficult choices forge character
- **Appreciation for historical fantasy blending** - where mythological elements enhance rather than overwhelm historical authenticity
- **Knowledge of Roman frontier warfare** - the constant struggle to maintain imperial borders against determined enemies

## Key Themes

- **Honor vs. Survival** - The constant Roman struggle to maintain moral principles in brutal circumstances
- **Divine vs. Human Agency** - How much of our fate is predetermined by gods versus shaped by our choices
- **Leadership Burdens** - The heavy cost of command and impossible decisions
- **Cultural Conflict** - Roman civilization clashing with desert nomadic cultures
- **Family and Duty** - Balancing personal relationships with military and political obligations

## Questions to Consider

1. How does Lucius's scholarly nature make him both better and worse suited for military command?
2. What role does divine intervention play in the story, and how does it affect human responsibility?
3. How does the brutal punishment of decimation reflect Roman values and their approach to military discipline?
4. In what ways does the harsh desert environment shape both the characters and the plot?
5. How does Lucius's relationship with his family contrast with his military life?

## Conclusion
Children of Apollo is absolutely worth your time if you enjoy meticulously researched historical fiction enhanced with mythological elements. Haviaras creates an immersive world that feels authentic to 3rd century Rome while delivering compelling character development and military action. The biggest reason to read this book is its masterful blend of historical detail and human drama - Lucius's journey from idealistic young officer to hardened commander is both believable and emotionally resonant. The novel succeeds as both military fiction and historical fantasy, offering readers action, political intrigue, and thoughtful exploration of Roman values and leadership challenges.

What sets this book apart is its attention to the psychological toll of Roman military service and the way it seamlessly weaves divine mythology into realistic historical settings. Perfect for fans of Simon Scarrow, Ben Kane, or Lindsey Davis, but with added mythological depth that elevates it beyond standard Roman military fiction.
