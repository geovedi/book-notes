# 7 Habits of Business English Success
**Author**: Winn Trivette, MA | **Year**: 2018 | **Category**: Business & Finance

## Summary
"7 Habits of Business English Success" is a practical guide designed for both native and non-native English speakers who want to excel in the global business arena. The book focuses on seven essential language skills that determine success in international business: reading smartly, writing well, listening intently, speaking confidently, using vocabulary expertly, giving presentations persuasively, and negotiating toughly.

Professionals who master these habits will gain a competitive edge in today's English-dominated global marketplace. Whether you're seeking career advancement, aiming to close international deals, or wanting to communicate more effectively with colleagues and clients worldwide, this book provides the foundational tools needed to achieve Business English fluency and professional success.

## Core Insights

### Read Smartly
Efficient business reading requires mastering four key techniques: reading voraciously, skimming, scanning, and drawing inferences. Successful professionals like Bill Gates and Warren Buffett attribute much of their success to reading extensively—Gates consumes about 50 books annually, while Buffett reads 600-1000 pages daily.

> "88 percent of wealthy individuals spent at least 30 minutes each day reading in order to expand their knowledge."

**Quick Take**: Choose business-related nonfiction over romance novels, use skimming to get the gist quickly (read title, first paragraph, first sentence of other paragraphs, headings, and last paragraph), and scanning to find specific information.

### Write Well
Business writing demands clarity, conciseness, and purpose. Five essential habits will transform your writing: use strong verbs instead of weak ones with adverbs, employ active voice for directness, use signposting to guide readers, follow the 7 C's (Clear, Concise, Courteous, Compelling, Coherent, Cohesive, Correct), and eliminate unnecessary words.

> "The governor waxed about several of the merits of education reform in her speech." (14 words)
> "The governor touted education reform in her speech." (8 words)

**Quick Take**: Replace weak verb-adverb combinations with strong verbs, use active voice to express more information with fewer words, and use signposting phrases like "Importantly," "In contrast," and "Therefore" to guide readers through your logic.

### Listen Intently
Active listening is purposeful listening that requires both effort and thinking. While the average person spends 63% of their day listening, they only retain about 25% of what they hear. Business professionals can't afford this low retention rate.

> "Active listening is listening intently—with purpose. When you actively listen, you focus intentionally on who you are listening to."

**Quick Take**: Establish eye contact with speakers, focus on key ideas rather than just facts, delay judgment until you've heard everything, take notes even in your native language, and expand your vocabulary daily.

### Speak Confidently
Mastering English stress patterns is crucial for clear communication. Unlike languages with even stress (Spanish, French, Italian), English is a stress-timed language where only certain words receive emphasis. Content words (main verbs, nouns, adjectives, adverbs, negative auxiliaries) get stressed, while function words (pronouns, prepositions, articles, conjunctions, auxiliary verbs) are unstressed.

> "Marta **can** **write** **three** **novels** **quickly** in a **week**."

**Quick Take**: Practice sentence stress with native speakers, remember that stress conveys meaning, and understand that getting sentence stress right may be more important than individual word stress for being understood.

### Use Vocabulary Expertly
Building a strong business vocabulary requires systematic effort. Start by maintaining a vocabulary notebook where you analyze new words: pronunciation, word type, different forms, synonyms, antonyms, and example sentences. Business English includes specialized collocations (words that naturally occur together), idioms (expressions with meanings not deducible from individual words), and phrasal verbs.

> "Vocabulary acquisition is often a neglected part of English courses."

**Quick Take**: Record and analyze every new word you encounter, learn business collocations like "close a deal" and "brand awareness," understand idioms like "throw money at something," and master phrasal verbs through context rather than memorization.

### Give Presentations Persuasively
A compelling business presentation requires developing a strong business voice with four key elements: enthusiasm, self-awareness of your voice, strategic use of volume, and varied intonation. Your voice is your strongest asset for delivering effective presentations.

> "Your self-assuredness comes from rehearsing your pitch in front of the mirror. An enthused voice also creates instant credibility for your message!"

**Quick Take**: Record yourself to understand how others hear you, vary your volume for emphasis, avoid monotone delivery, and use professional phrases like "I'm going to divide this talk into four parts" and "Let's turn now to..." to structure your presentation.

### Negotiate Toughly
Business negotiations in English are challenging for non-native speakers due to potential language nuances and cultural differences. The key to successful negotiation is seeking clarification through power questions that help you understand the other party's true interests rather than just their positions.

> "When in doubt, ask for clarification!"

**Quick Take**: Use clarification questions like "Could you clarify your point regarding...?" and "What precisely do you mean by...?", focus on interests rather than positions, and understand that language barriers can cause misunderstandings that appropriate questioning can prevent.
