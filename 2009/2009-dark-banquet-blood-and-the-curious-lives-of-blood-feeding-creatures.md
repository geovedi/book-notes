# Dark Banquet: Blood and the Curious Lives of Blood-Feeding Creatures
**Authors**: Bill Schutt, Illustrated by Patricia J. Wynne | **Year**: 2009
**Category**: Science & Nature

## Summary
"Dark Banquet" is a fascinating exploration into the world of sanguivores—creatures that feed on blood. Written by zoologist Bill Schutt, this book combines personal field research with scientific insights to reveal the remarkable adaptations and behaviors of blood-feeding animals ranging from vampire bats to leeches, bed bugs, and even some unexpected blood-feeders like vampire finches. Schutt's firsthand experiences studying vampire bats in Trinidad provide compelling narratives, while the book also delves into the historical relationship between humans and bloodletting practices.

Perfect for anyone curious about nature's most specialized feeders, this book offers equal parts adventure story, biological insight, and medical history. Readers will gain a newfound appreciation for the evolutionary ingenuity of blood-feeding creatures while learning about their ecological importance and medical significance.

## Core Insights

### The Remarkable World of Vampire Bats
Vampire bats represent the pinnacle of blood-feeding specialization, with only three species out of 1,100 bat species having adopted this lifestyle. The white-winged vampire bat (Diaemus youngi) demonstrates incredible behavioral adaptability—Schutt documents how these bats have learned to mimic baby chicks to approach domestic chickens undetected. They nuzzle against the hen's brood patch (a feather-free area with dense blood vessels used for egg warming), causing the bird to relax and even close its eyes, allowing the bat to feed peacefully.

What makes this particularly fascinating is that this behavior isn't innate—it has been learned since the arrival of Europeans and their domestic fowl, suggesting cultural transmission from mother bats to their offspring. The common vampire bat (Desmodus rotundus) exhibits spider-like agility on the ground and can launch itself into flight from a standing position through a powerful push-up motion, adaptations that set it apart from all other bats.

> "My God, I thought, these bats have learned to mimic chicks!"

**Quick Take**: Evolution isn't always about gradual genetic change—sometimes rapid behavioral adaptation can be just as powerful for survival.

### The Hidden Dangers and Misunderstandings
While vampire bats consume only about a tablespoon of blood nightly (roughly half their body weight), the real danger comes from anticoagulants in their saliva that keep wounds bleeding long after feeding. This creates open wounds susceptible to infections that can be fatal to their prey. More seriously, vampire bats can transmit rabies, which led to a devastating outbreak in Trinidad from 1925-1935, killing 89 people and thousands of cattle.

Schutt documents how superstitions about blood-feeding creatures have caused additional problems. In Trinidad, locals feared soucouyants—human-sized blood feeders believed to be old crones who could shed their skin and transform into fiery balls. People would sprinkle rice outside their doors, believing soucouyants couldn't enter until they counted every grain.

**Quick Take**: The biological reality of blood-feeding creatures is often less dramatic than human imagination, but the real medical risks demand serious attention.

### The Surprising Medical History of Bloodletting
The book contains a gripping account of George Washington's death in 1799, which was accelerated by physicians removing 80 ounces of his blood (about 40% of his total blood volume) over a 13-hour period. This wasn't malpractice—it was standard medical treatment based on 2,000 years of humoral theory dating back to Hippocrates and Galen, who believed that health required balancing four bodily fluids: black bile, yellow bile, phlegm, and blood.

Bloodletting remained popular for millennia because it sometimes appeared to work—reducing fever and inflammation, though we now understand this was often harmful. The practice was performed by barber-surgeons (the origin of the red and white barber pole symbol) and was used to treat everything from asthma and bone fractures to mental illness and even drowning victims.

> "What the hell were those guys thinking?" I asked myself, initially. But as easy as it is to scoff at Washington's physicians and their chosen course of treatment, I know now that I was dead wrong in doing so.

**Quick Take**: Even widely accepted medical practices can be fundamentally wrong—progress requires questioning assumptions, not just accumulating more data.

### The Complex Biology of Blood Itself
Schutt provides accessible explanations of blood's remarkable properties. Blood is actually a connective tissue with liquid plasma (55% of volume) carrying red blood cells, white blood cells, and platelets (45%). Hemoglobin in red blood cells is so efficient at carrying oxygen that without it, humans would need 75 gallons of fluid instead of 5 liters to transport the required oxygen.

The book explains how blood transfusion knowledge evolved slowly, with disastrous early experiments like Jean-Baptiste Denis's 1667 attempt to calm a mentally disturbed man by transfusing him with calf blood (believing blood carried personality traits). The patient suffered a severe immune reaction, though Denis interpreted the vomiting and dark urine as successful removal of madness. It wasn't until 1901 that Karl Landsteiner discovered blood types, making safe transfusions possible.

**Quick Take**: Blood's complexity reminds us that even substances we take for granted are the result of millions of years of evolutionary refinement.

### Nature's Ingenious Blood-Feeding Adaptations
Beyond vampire bats, Schutt explores diverse sanguivores including leeches (still used in modern medicine for reconstructive surgery), bed bugs (making a major comeback in cities worldwide), and even unexpected blood-feeders like vampire finches in the Galápagos and vampire moths in Southeast Asia.

Each creature has evolved specialized adaptations: leeches use suction and anticoagulants, bed bugs have developed resistance to common pesticides, and the infamous candiru catfish of the Amazon reportedly swims up human urethras (though Schutt questions whether this behavior occurs naturally or is primarily associated with humans urinating in water).

Perhaps most fascinating is how these creatures solve the common challenges of blood feeding: locating hosts, accessing blood vessels without causing immediate defensive reactions, preventing blood from clotting during feeding, and avoiding detection by their hosts.

**Quick Take**: Evolution finds remarkably different solutions to similar problems, showcasing nature's endless creativity.

## Takeaways
- Vampire bats demonstrate that behavioral adaptation can occur rapidly when evolutionary pressure is strong
- Many blood-feeding creatures pose serious health risks through disease transmission rather than blood loss
- Medical progress requires questioning established practices, not just accumulating more evidence
- Blood's complexity and efficiency are products of millions of years of evolutionary refinement
- Different creatures have evolved remarkably diverse solutions to the common challenges of blood feeding

## Questions to Consider
- How do cultural beliefs about blood-feeding creatures compare to their biological reality?
- Why has evolution produced blood-feeding adaptations in so many different animal groups?
- What makes blood such an attractive food source despite the challenges of accessing it?
- How might our understanding of blood-feeding creatures influence public health approaches to disease control?
- What can the study of specialized feeders teach us about evolutionary processes?

## Conclusion
"Dark Banquet" is worth your time if you're fascinated by nature's extremes and enjoy learning about biological adaptations that push the boundaries of what seems possible. Schutt's blend of personal adventure, scientific insight, and historical context creates a compelling narrative that transforms potentially repulsive subjects into objects of fascination. The biggest reason to read this book is its ability to make you appreciate evolutionary ingenuity while providing a deeper understanding of creatures that most people instinctively fear or dismiss. It's a reminder that nature's solutions to survival challenges are often far more creative and sophisticated than human engineering.
