# The Wordy Shipmates
**Author**: Sarah Vowell | **Year**: 2009
**Category**: History & Biography

## Summary
Sarah Vowell's "The Wordy Shipmates" is a witty and insightful exploration of the Massachusetts Bay Puritans who sailed to America in 1630, focusing on Governor John Winthrop and his fleet of eleven ships, including the flagship Arbella. Through her signature blend of humor and historical analysis, Vowell challenges modern stereotypes of Puritans as boring, judgmental killjoys, revealing them instead as intellectual, literary, and surprisingly complex figures who profoundly shaped American identity and values. The book traces how these "wordy shipmates" — obsessed with books, learning, and biblical interpretation — laid the groundwork for American exceptionalism, founded Harvard just six years after arriving, and created a legacy that continues to influence U.S. foreign policy and national self-understanding.

This book is perfect for history enthusiasts who appreciate humor with their scholarship, Americans curious about the roots of national identity, and readers who enjoy seeing historical figures humanized through contemporary perspective. Readers will gain a more nuanced understanding of Puritanism beyond caricature, insight into how 17th-century religious convictions still echo in modern American politics, and an appreciation for how literacy and education became foundational to American democracy.

## Core Insights

### The Intellectual Puritans: Beyond the Stereotypes
Contrary to modern portrayals of Puritans as anti-intellectual fanatics, Vowell reveals them as deeply literary and scholarly people obsessed with books and learning. They founded Harvard in 1636 to ensure their clergy could read Hebrew and Greek, and their single-minded focus on the Bible made words — not land, money, or power — the center of their lives. This intellectual legacy, passed down through descendants like Ralph Waldo Emerson and John Adams, helped establish education as a cornerstone of American democracy.

> "Puritanism was not an anti-intellectual fundamentalism; it was a learned, scholarly movement that required on the part of the leaders, and as much as possible from the followers, not only knowledge but a respect for the cultural heritage."

**Quick Take**: The Puritans' love of learning and literacy created a foundation for American democratic values that persists today.

### "City Upon a Hill": The Birth of American Exceptionalism
John Winthrop's famous "city upon a hill" sermon, delivered aboard the Arbella, established the concept of American exceptionalism that continues to influence U.S. foreign policy. Vowell traces how this idea — that America was chosen by God to be a model for the world — evolved from Winthrop's original communitarian vision about mutual obligation and community into justification for American interventionism worldwide.

> "In private, however, Winthrop will soon tell his fellow colonists the very opposite. 'We shall be as a city upon a hill,' he says."

**Quick Take**: Understanding the origins of American exceptionalism helps explain America's ongoing role in global affairs and national self-image.

### The Nonseparating Puritan Dilemma
Unlike the Mayflower Pilgrims who were Separatists, the Massachusetts Bay colonists were Nonseparating Puritans who wanted to reform the Church of England from within while simultaneously fleeing it. This created a fascinating tension between their public declarations of loyalty to England and their private revolutionary actions. Their "Humble Request" to King Charles I portrayed themselves as harmless "hobos in the woods," while they were actually establishing a self-governing colony based on democratic principles.

> "I admire the Mayflower Pilgrims' uncompromising resolve to make a clean break, and their fortitude, so fundamental to the American national character that Sinclair Lewis called one of our core ideals 'Plymouth Rock in a sleet-storm.' Still, I find the Arbella passengers' qualms messier and more endearing."

**Quick Take**: The Puritans' ability to hold contradictory beliefs — loyalty and rebellion, humility and exceptionalism — reflects enduring tensions in the American character.

### We're Here to Help: The Interventionist Impulse
Vowell identifies what she calls the Massachusetts Puritans' "most enduring bequest to the future United States" — the conviction that they had a divine mission to help others, whether those others wanted help or not. This worldview, derived from Scripture, evolved into America's interventionist foreign policy that continues to justify military and humanitarian involvement in other nations' affairs.

> "The worldview behind that motto — we're here to help, whether you want our help or not — is the Massachusetts Puritans' most enduring bequest to the future United States."

**Quick Take**: America's tendency to intervene in global conflicts has deep religious and cultural roots in the Puritan missionary impulse.

### Literature as Leadership
One of the book's most surprising insights is how Winthrop's "Model of Christian Charity" was not written by a minister but by a governor — demonstrating that the Puritans saw literature and writing as essential tools of leadership. Their obsession with documentation and written record means that history is literally written by the writers, and the "quill-crazy New Englanders" left behind extensive archives that shape our understanding of early America.

> "History is written by the writers. The quill-crazy New Englanders left behind libraries full of statements of purpose in the form of letters, sermons, court transcripts, and diaries."

**Quick Take**: The Puritans' literary legacy reminds us that written words have power to shape history and national identity.

## Best Quotes
- "The only thing more dangerous than an idea is a belief. And by dangerous I don't mean thought-provoking. I mean: might get people killed."
- "I'm always disappointed when I see the word 'Puritan' tossed around as shorthand for a bunch of generic, boring, stupid, judgmental killjoys. Because to me, they are very specific, fascinating, sometimes brilliant, judgmental killjoys who rarely agreed on anything except that Catholics are going to hell."
- "The United States is often called a Puritan nation. Well, here is one way in which it emphatically is not: Puritan lives were overwhelmingly, fanatically literary. Their single-minded obsession with one book, the Bible, made words the center of their lives — not land, not money, not power, not fun."
- "Because of the 'city upon a hill' sound bite, 'A Model of Christian Charity' is one of the formative documents outlining the idea of America. But dig deep into its communitarian ethos and it reads more like an America that might have been, an America fervently devoted to the quaint goals of working together and getting along. Of course, this America does exist. It's called Canada."

## Conclusion
"The Wordy Shipmates" is absolutely worth your time, especially if you think you dislike Puritans or find early American history boring. Vowell's witty, accessible writing transforms what could be dry historical material into a compelling narrative that reveals how much of modern America has its roots in 17th-century religious convictions. The biggest reason to read this book is its masterful ability to connect past to present, showing how ideas born aboard a ship in 1630 continue to influence American foreign policy, national identity, and cultural values today. Vowell doesn't just recount history — she makes it relevant, funny, and surprisingly moving, especially in her moments of personal connection to the Puritan dilemma of when to stay and reform versus when to leave and start something new. This book will change how you understand both the Puritans and yourself as an American.
