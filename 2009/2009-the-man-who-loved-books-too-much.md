# The Man Who Loved Books Too Much
**Author**: Allison Hoover Bartlett | **Year**: 2009
**Category**: Fiction & Literature

## Summary
This captivating true crime narrative follows John Gilkey, a prolific book thief who stole hundreds of thousands of dollars worth of rare books across America, and Ken Sanders, the determined rare book dealer who made it his mission to catch him. What begins as a simple cat-and-mouse story evolves into a fascinating exploration of obsession, the psychology of collecting, and the blurred lines between love and possession in the rare book world.

The story reveals how Gilkey, working menial jobs but dreaming of a sophisticated life, used stolen credit card numbers to acquire rare books he couldn't afford—never to sell, but solely to possess. Meanwhile, Sanders, appointed as security chair for the Antiquarian Booksellers' Association, becomes a self-taught detective tracking the mysterious thief plaguing his fellow dealers. Their eventual confrontation exposes two very different relationships with books and the intense passions they inspire.

This book is perfect for true crime enthusiasts, bibliophiles, and anyone fascinated by the psychology of obsession. Readers will gain insight into the secretive world of rare book collecting while pondering fundamental questions about why we desire objects and how far we'll go to possess them.

## Core Insights

### The Thief Who Stole for Love
Unlike typical criminals motivated by profit, John Gilkey represents that rarest of criminals: someone who steals purely for the joy of possession. Working low-paying jobs but dreaming of sophistication, Gilkey saw rare books as tickets to the cultured life he craved. His thefts weren't investments—they were trophies. What makes Gilkey particularly fascinating is his complete lack of remorse and his elaborate justifications for his crimes.

> "I have known men to hazard their fortunes, go long journeys halfway about the world, forget friendships, even lie, cheat, and steal, all for the gain of a book." —A. S. W. Rosenbach

**Quick Take**: Gilkey's case reveals how collecting can cross from passionate hobby into criminal obsession when desire outweighs ethics.

### The Reluctant Detective
Ken Sanders, a salt-of-the-earth rare book dealer from Salt Lake City, stumbled into his role as "bibliodick" through circumstance rather than choice. Appointed security chair of the Antiquarian Booksellers' Association, Sanders created an email alert system that revolutionized how dealers shared theft information. His dogged pursuit of Gilkey transformed him from bookseller into investigator, showing how ordinary people can rise to extraordinary challenges when their community is threatened.

**Quick Take**: Sanders' story demonstrates how passion for one's work can evolve into unexpected forms of public service and leadership.

### The Psychology of Collecting
The book explores why collectors become so intensely attached to objects, revealing that collecting is less about the items themselves and more about constructing identity. From prestigious institutional collections like Henry Huntington's to personal libraries built piece by piece, collections reflect their owners' dreams, values, and place in the world. Gilkey's criminal collecting and Sanders' protective stewardship represent two sides of the same intense passion for books.

**Quick Take**: Understanding the psychology behind collecting helps explain why people will risk everything—including their freedom—for the objects they desire.

### A Secret World Exposed
Bartlett pulls back the curtain on the rare book trade, revealing a world built on trust, relationships, and shared passion. Unlike typical commercial transactions, rare book dealing often operates on handshakes and personal relationships, making it uniquely vulnerable to deception. The book shows how this tight-knit community balances openness with necessary suspicion, and how they've adapted to modern threats while preserving traditional values.

**Quick Take**: The rare book world's blend of sophistication and vulnerability creates unique challenges that require both old-fashioned integrity and modern security measures.

### The Blurred Lines of Justice
One of the most unsettling aspects of the story is how difficult it is to achieve justice in book theft cases. Gilkey receives minimal punishment for his crimes, and many stolen books remain missing. The legal system often treats book theft as a minor offense, undervaluing both the cultural significance of rare books and the devastating impact on dealers and collectors. This raises questions about how society values cultural heritage versus other forms of property.

**Quick Take**: The story reveals gaps in how our legal system handles crimes against cultural property, suggesting the need for better protection of literary heritage.

## Key Takeaways

- **Trust is both the rare book world's greatest strength and its greatest vulnerability**—the same personal relationships that make the trade special also create opportunities for deception

- **Obsession exists on a spectrum**—the same passion that drives legitimate collectors can, without ethical boundaries, become criminal compulsion

- **Cultural property deserves stronger legal protection**—the current system often fails to adequately punish book theft or recover stolen literary treasures

- **Community matters**—Ken Sanders' success came from organizing fellow dealers and creating systems for sharing information, showing how collective action can address individual vulnerabilities

- **Books are more than their content**—the physical objects carry history, meaning, and emotional weight that transcends the words on their pages

## Questions to Consider

1. Where is the line between healthy passion and dangerous obsession in collecting?

2. How does society determine the value of cultural property versus other forms of wealth?

3. What role does trust play in specialized communities, and how can they balance openness with necessary safeguards?

4. Why are some people willing to risk everything for objects they desire, while others show no such attachment?

5. How do the objects we collect reflect—and shape—our identities and aspirations?

## Conclusion

This is a fascinating true crime story that offers much more than the typical cat-and-mouse narrative. Bartlett uses Gilkey's crimes as a lens to examine deeper questions about human desire, the psychology of collecting, and the value we place on cultural objects. The book works because it avoids simplistic villainy—Gilkey is neither glorified nor entirely condemned, but presented as a complex figure whose actions, while criminal, stem from recognizably human desires.

The story's greatest strength is its exploration of the fine line between love and possession, and how the same passion that drives legitimate collectors can, without ethical boundaries, become destructive. Sanders' parallel story provides a perfect counterpoint—showing how passion for books can also lead to protecting and preserving rather than taking.

This book is absolutely worth your time, especially if you love books, true crime, or stories that explore human psychology. It will make you think differently about why we desire objects and what those desires say about who we are and who we want to become. The biggest reason to read it is that it reveals a world most of us never see, while using that world to ask universal questions about human nature.
