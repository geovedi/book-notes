# I Want to Die but I Still Want to Eat Tteokbokki
**Original Title**: 죽고 싶지만 떡볶이는 먹고 싶어 | **Author**: Baek Sehee | **Year**: 2019
**Category**: Psychology & Mental Health 

## Summary
This raw, intimate memoir chronicles Baek Sehee's journey through psychotherapy as she confronts depression, self-harm, and suicidal thoughts. The book presents actual therapy transcripts alongside Sehee's personal reflections, offering an unflinching look at mental health treatment. Through dialogues with her psychiatrist over several years, Sehee learns to challenge negative self-perceptions, confront childhood wounds around body image and rejection, and gradually build self-acceptance. The title captures the central paradox of living with depression—simultaneously experiencing despair while still finding small joys and reasons to continue living.

This book offers profound comfort to anyone struggling with depression, anxiety, or self-worth issues, as well as those curious about the therapeutic process. It's especially valuable for readers who feel alone in their mental health struggles, demonstrating that healing is possible through patience, professional help, and radical self-acceptance.

## Core Insights

### The Power of Speaking Your Pain
Sehee discovers that verbalizing her deepest traumas to both her therapist and partner dramatically reduces their emotional power. When she finally shares childhood experiences of being bullied for her skin condition and weight, the shame that once felt overwhelming becomes manageable. The therapy transcripts reveal how putting pain into words creates distance, allowing rational observation rather than emotional immersion.

> "Once you put the situation outside of yourself by using words, you can judge the situation from an observer's perspective. Rationally."

**Quick Take**: Trauma loses its grip when spoken aloud rather than held in silent shame.

### Self-Pity as Comfortable Prison
Sehee honestly examines how self-pity became a familiar "comfortable room" where she would retreat during depressive episodes. While easy to dismiss as negative, she acknowledges the strange comfort of familiar suffering. The real work isn't eliminating self-pity entirely, but recognizing when it becomes a default escape and choosing different responses.

> "Familiarity felt like safety to me. Which is why, whenever depression or emptiness came calling, I was all too eager to open the door of self-pity and go right inside."

**Quick Take**: Healing requires recognizing the deceptive comfort in familiar suffering patterns.

### Past Wounds Don't Vanish—They Transform
Contrary to popular self-help advice to "leave the past behind," Sehee learns that suppressed wounds continue affecting the present until properly acknowledged. Rather than erasing her past, she integrates it, understanding that her childhood experiences with rejection and body shame shaped but don't define her current self.

> "I didn't like that trauma and wounds could connect to the present. I think I was afraid I would never be able to leave the past behind."

**Quick Take**: True healing comes from accepting, not erasing, the parts of your story that still hurt.

### Depression as Chronic Condition, Not Temporary Illness
Sehee reframes her understanding of depression from a temporary "mind flu" to a chronic condition requiring ongoing management. This perspective eliminates pressure for complete "cure" and instead focuses on developing sustainable coping strategies and self-awareness for long-term navigation of mental health challenges.

> "I no longer consider depression as 'the flu of the mind.' For someone who has lived with depression as long as I have... the disease is more like an incurable chronic illness than a brief cold."

**Quick Take**: Managing depression long-term is more realistic than seeking permanent elimination.

### Self-Acceptance as Revolutionary Act
The culmination of Sehee's journey is reaching a place where she no longer hates herself—a simple yet profound transformation. After years of self-criticism, body shame, and feeling unworthy of love, she gradually accepts her "little bits and fragments" without judgment.

> "The biggest gain from my treatment and publishing my book has been the fact that I no longer hate myself."

**Quick Take**: Learning to stop saying horrible things to yourself is revolutionary healing.

## Action Items
- Start therapy or counseling if you're struggling—professional help provides tools for understanding your patterns
- Practice verbalizing your pain to trusted people or in journaling to reduce its emotional power
- Notice when self-pity becomes comfortable and consciously choose alternative responses
- Accept that depression may be a chronic condition requiring ongoing management rather than complete cure
- Challenge negative self-talk and practice self-acceptance, even in small increments
- Share your story authentically—vulnerability creates connection and reduces shame

## Questions to Consider
- What familiar "comfortable rooms" of suffering do you retreat to during difficult times?
- Which past wounds continue influencing your present behavior and self-perception?
- How could speaking your pain aloud (to someone trustworthy) transform its power over you?
- What would it look like to manage your mental health as a chronic condition rather than seeking permanent cure?
- In what ways do you still hate yourself, and what would self-acceptance feel like?

## Conclusion
**This is absolutely worth your time** if you've ever struggled with depression, self-worth, or feeling alone in your mental health journey. Sehee's radical honesty offers both validation and hope, showing that healing doesn't mean erasing your pain but learning to live with it differently. The therapy transcript format provides invaluable insight into the therapeutic process, demystifying what actually happens in treatment.

The biggest reason to read this book is its fearless portrayal of mental health recovery as messy, non-linear, and deeply human. Sehee doesn't offer easy answers or miracle cures—she offers something far more valuable: company in the darkness and proof that gradual healing is possible. Her journey from self-hatred to self-acceptance demonstrates that the goal isn't never feeling depressed again, but learning to treat yourself with compassion even when you do.
