# Backbone
**Authors**: Julia Dye, Ph.D. | **Year**: 2019
**Category**: Business & Finance

## Summary
"Backbone" explores the essential leadership traits that make Marine Corps Non-Commissioned Officers (NCOs) the backbone of the United States Marine Corps. Through compelling historical accounts and contemporary interviews, the book reveals how 14 core leadership traits—Initiative, Bearing, Unselfishness, Dependability, Endurance, Knowledge, Judgment, Enthusiasm, Tact, Decisiveness, Integrity, Justice, Loyalty, and Courage—are forged through Marine training and demonstrated in combat and business environments.

The book demonstrates that Marine Corps NCOs aren't rigid rule-followers but character-driven leaders who bring their unique personalities to leadership roles. Unlike other military branches that promote primarily officer heroes, the Marine Corps reveres its NCO leaders like John Basilone, Dan Daly, and Carlos Hathcock as the epitome of leadership excellence.

## Core Insights

### Initiative: Taking Action Without Orders
Marine NCOs are trained to take initiative in chaotic situations where waiting for orders isn't an option. The Corps pushes decision-making authority down through the ranks, trusting those closest to the action to make critical decisions based on ground-level intelligence.

> "NCOs can help develop initiative if the guys know you have their backs and that finding a solution won't put them in jeopardy. And it's necessary to do so in the Marine Corps as there's never enough of the 'correct' stuff. Innovate, adapt, overcome."

**Quick Take**: True initiative isn't just taking action—it's taking the RIGHT action for the mission while supporting your team's growth and confidence.

### Bearing: Leadership Presence Under Pressure
Bearing encompasses more than military appearance—it's about maintaining composure, situational awareness, and forward momentum under extreme stress. Marines develop bearing through obstacle courses that build confidence while under pressure, teaching them to think clearly when chaos erupts.

The book contrasts how Marines and Army units approached obstacles on Saipan Beach: Marines focused on the objective while working around barriers, while the Army treated obstacle removal as the primary goal, losing sight of the bigger mission.

**Quick Take**: Leadership bearing means understanding your ultimate destination so well that obstacles don't distract you from the mission.

### Unselfishness: The Foundation of Trust
Marine training instills unselfishness as a survival trait. From ensuring subordinates eat first to sharing scarce resources, unselfishness builds the trust necessary for units to function under extreme conditions. The book highlights how POWs in Japanese camps survived through collective unselfishness, with Marines maintaining superior discipline and group spirit compared to other prisoners.

**Quick Take**: In high-stakes environments, unselfishness isn't just nice—it's essential for mission survival and team cohesion.

### Dependability: The Currency of Leadership
The Marine Corps views dependability as sacred. When leaders make promises, they must deliver—or morale collapses and trust evaporates. The book contrasts WWI hero Sergeant William Campbell (who inspired Sergeant York) with modern business executives, showing how dependability transcends contexts.

A broken promise to a private about payroll issues cascades through entire units, damaging leadership credibility far beyond the individual slight.

**Quick Take**: In leadership, dependability is the foundation currency—without it, you can't buy trust, respect, or mission success.

### The Marine Corps Leadership Ethos
What distinguishes Marine leadership development is its emphasis on ethics—loyalty, integrity, courage, and honor—as manifested through small unit leadership. Every Marine, officer and enlisted alike, goes through the same training experience, creating cohesiveness unmatched by other services.

> "Because of its reliance on small unit leadership and because of its expertise in unconventional warfare, no branch of the U.S. Armed Forces has placed a greater emphasis on manifestations of ethics—loyalty, integrity, courage, and honor—than the Marine Corps."

**Quick Take**: True leadership development comes from shared hardship and universal standards, not privileged training tracks.

## Action Items
- **Develop Initiative**: Practice identifying problems and taking constructive action without waiting for permission
- **Cultivate Bearing**: Work on maintaining composure under stress while keeping focus on ultimate objectives
- **Practice Unselfishness**: Put your team's needs before your own in critical situations
- **Build Dependability**: Make fewer promises but keep every single one, no matter the cost
- **Lead by Example**: Demonstrate the values you expect from others through your daily actions

## Questions to Consider
- How can you develop initiative in your team members without creating chaos?
- What obstacles in your environment are distracting you from your ultimate mission?
- Where do you need to put others' needs ahead of your own to build trust?
- What promises have you made that you need to keep to maintain credibility?
- How can you develop the same level of situational awareness that Marines cultivate?

## Conclusion
"Backbone" is essential reading for anyone who leads or aspires to lead. The Marine Corps has spent centuries perfecting leadership development under the most demanding conditions imaginable—life and death situations where failure means casualties. Their 14 leadership traits aren't theoretical concepts but battle-tested principles that work equally well in boardrooms as they do on battlefields.

The book's greatest strength is its focus on NCO leaders rather than officers, emphasizing that true leadership comes from character and competence, not rank or position. The stories of Marines like Randy Burgess, Donald MacGregor, and William Campbell demonstrate that extraordinary leadership is available to anyone willing to develop these traits through consistent practice under pressure.

If you want to understand what makes the Marine Corps the world's most respected fighting organization—and how to apply those same leadership principles in your own context—"Backbone" delivers the blueprint in compelling, actionable detail.
