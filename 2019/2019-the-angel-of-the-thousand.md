# The Angel of the Thousand
**Original Title**: L'Angelo dei Mille
**Author**: Marco Ferrari | **Year**: 2019

## Summary
This compelling biography unveils the forgotten story of Rosalia Montmasson, the only woman among Garibaldi's legendary "Thousand" who fought for Italian unification. Born in 1823 in a small Savoyard village, Rosalia transformed from a humble washerwoman to a revolutionary courier, battlefield nurse, and eventually the abandoned wife of Italian Prime Minister Francesco Crispi. Her journey takes us from the secret revolutionary circles of mid-19th century Europe to the bloody battlefields of Sicily, from the corridors of political power to an isolated old age in Rome.

This book is essential reading for anyone interested in untold histories, women's contributions to nation-building, and the human cost of political idealism. Readers will gain insight into how revolutionary movements operated behind the scenes and the price paid by those who helped create modern Italy but were written out of its official history.

## Core Insights

### The Revolutionary's Invisible Advantage
Rosalia's humble background as a washerwoman and ironer became her greatest asset in the revolutionary cause. Unlike prominent revolutionaries known to police across Europe, she could move freely across borders, delivering messages and money between Mazzinian republicans, Carbonari conspirators, and Garibaldian volunteers. Her ability to pass unnoticed through customs posts and police checkpoints made her an invaluable courier for the Italian unification movement.

> "She, unlike many revolutionaries known to the police of half the continent, could move with greater ease, crossing frontiers with a simple smile"
> _Original: "Lei, a differenza di tanti rivoluzionari noti alle polizie di mezzo continente, poteva muoversi con maggiore facilità, attraversando frontiere con un semplice sorriso"_

**Quick Take**: Sometimes seeming unimportant is the perfect cover for changing the world.

### The Angel of Calatafimi
At the Battle of Calatafimi, Rosalia earned her legendary nickname by treating wounded soldiers under fire. She worked alongside Dr. Pietro Ripari, assisting in amputations and comforting dying men. Her courage was so remarkable that even decades later, veterans recognized her in the streets of Rome and shouted "Santa Rosalia! The Angel of the Thousand!"

> "In the bloodied field of Calatafimi she was sister, she was mother, she was everything to the wounded"
> _Original: "Nel campo insanguinato di Calatafimi fu sorella, fu madre, fu tutto ai feriti"_

**Quick Take**: True heroism isn't about glory—it's about serving others in their darkest moments.

### The Political Handover
After helping create unified Italy, Rosalia found herself discarded like a tool no longer needed. Her husband Francesco Crispi, once a revolutionary, transformed into a mainstream politician who repudiated his first wife to marry the younger Lina Barbagallo. Accused of bigamy, Crispi won the court case that effectively annulled his marriage to Rosalia, leaving her without legal status or recognition.

> "She was the female voice that had made Italy, but no one was supposed to know it"
> _Original: "Lei era la voce femminile che aveva fatto l'Italia, ma nessuno doveva saperlo"_

**Quick Take**: Political revolutions often consume their own architects, especially when they're women.

### The Burden of Memory
In her final years, Rosalia lived in poverty in Rome, surrounded by cats and war medals, her heroic past known only to a dwindling number of veterans. When a former soldier recognized her in 1903, the brief moment of recognition reawakened decades of suppressed memories and the painful contrast between her contributions and her obscurity.

> "She had learned to live with the silence of lost things"
> _Original: "Non avrebbe avuto senso raccontare a uno come Elia, che ancora alimentava il proprio mito di garibaldino, il singhiozzo di tenersi ogni rimpianto dentro"_

**Quick Take**: Being forgotten can be harder than facing danger.

## Action Items

- **Preserve Forgotten Histories**: Actively seek out and share stories of women and marginalized groups who contributed to major historical events
- **Question Official Narratives**: Look beyond textbook heroes to understand who else helped shape historical outcomes
- **Visit Historical Sites**: When visiting places like Palermo or Rome, seek out the lesser-known memorials and stories
- **Support Women Veterans**: Honor and support women who serve in military and humanitarian roles today

## Questions to Consider

1. How many other crucial historical contributors have been forgotten due to their gender or social status?
2. What price do political and social movements exact from their most dedicated participants?
3. How does writing women out of history affect our understanding of how change actually happens?
4. What would our understanding of Italian unification (or any major historical event) look like if we centered the stories of women like Rosalia?

## Conclusion

This book is absolutely worth your time. It's not just the biography of one remarkable woman, but a window into how history actually gets made—and then remade to exclude inconvenient contributors. Ferrari's meticulous research, which includes tracking down Rosalia's descendants and even rediscovering her lost marble bust, demonstrates how dedication can restore what time and prejudice have tried to erase.

The biggest reason to read this book is its powerful reminder that behind every great historical moment stand countless unsung heroes. In an era when we're finally questioning who gets written into our histories, Rosalia's story serves as both inspiration and warning: inspiration for what one determined person can achieve, and warning about how easily contributions can be forgotten when they don't fit the official narrative.

You'll close this book looking at historical monuments and textbooks differently, wondering who else is missing from the story—and perhaps resolve to help find them.
