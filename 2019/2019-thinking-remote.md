# Thinking Remote
**Authors**: Pilar Orti, Maya Middlemiss | **Year**: 2019
**Category**: Business & Finance

## Summary
"Thinking Remote" is a comprehensive guide for leaders navigating the transition to distributed teams and remote work. Based on the authors' experience at Virtual Not Distant and compiled from their blog posts and podcast reflections, this book provides practical insights for building effective remote team cultures. The book addresses both the technological and human aspects of remote collaboration, emphasizing that successful remote work requires intentional design of digital workspaces, fostering psychological safety, and maintaining visibility across distributed teams.

This guide is essential for team leaders, managers, and organizations transitioning to remote or hybrid work models. Readers will gain actionable frameworks for designing digital environments that encourage collaboration, establishing trust-based accountability systems, and creating inclusive cultures that support both productivity and wellbeing regardless of physical location.

## Core Insights

### Digital Workspace Design: Mirror Physical Space Intentions
The authors argue that organizations invest heavily in physical office design but rarely apply the same thoughtful approach to digital workspaces. Just as physical office layouts influence behavior and interaction patterns, digital environments must be consciously designed to support desired culture and collaboration. Digital spaces should include both areas for group interaction and private individual work, with "attractors" that encourage serendipitous encounters—like shared documents where multiple people can work simultaneously and see each other's presence.

> "What is the culture like in your organisation? Is it collaborative or competitive? If it's competitive, designing an open space for discussion, where everyone can hear everyone else and see each other's designs or scribbles, might not be the best option."

**Quick Take**: Map your desired team culture onto your digital tools before choosing them, ensuring your technology reinforces rather than undermines your workplace values.

### Technology Should Serve Needs, Not Drive Them
The book cautions against "the tail wagging the dog" where workflows are dictated by available tools rather than strategic needs. Organizations should regularly assess whether their current technology stack serves their actual collaboration requirements, avoiding both tool-lock-in and constant tool-hopping. The focus should be on choosing the right tool for specific work types rather than adopting trends.

**Quick Take**: Start with "what do we need to accomplish" rather than "what tool should we use," and regularly review whether your technology still serves your strategic objectives.

### Psychological Safety Is Critical in Virtual Meetings
Drawing from Google's Project Aristotle research, the authors emphasize that psychological safety is the single most important factor in high-performing teams—virtual or colocated. In online meetings, this requires leaders to explicitly invite participation, share their own emotions, avoid interrupting, and create space for vulnerability. The technology itself can create barriers if not managed properly.

> "Psychological safety refers to: 'an individual's perception of the consequences of taking an interpersonal risk or a belief that a team is safe for risk taking in the face of being seen as ignorant, incompetent, negative, or disruptive.'"

**Quick Take**: Train leaders in facilitation skills for virtual environments and establish group norms that encourage risk-taking and honest communication.

### Visibility Should Enable Collaboration, Not Performance Theater
The book addresses the common concern that remote workers won't be "seen" working, warning against replicating presenteeism in digital form. Instead of constant status updates and performative productivity displays, teams should focus on making work visible in ways that actually help collaboration. The right level of transparency varies by culture and work type, but should always serve the work itself rather than just demonstrating busyness.

**Quick Take**: Establish clear accountability based on outcomes rather than activity monitoring, and ensure "working out loud" practices serve collaborative needs rather than visibility anxiety.

### Trust and Autonomy Are Remote Work Foundations
Remote work success fundamentally requires trust between managers and team members. The authors guide leaders to examine their own insecurities about managing remote productivity before projecting concerns onto their teams. Clear expectations about communication, core hours, and deliverables create frameworks where autonomy can thrive without abandoning accountability.

**Quick Take**: Define results-based success metrics and provide team members flexibility in how and when they achieve those outcomes, rather than monitoring their activity throughout the day.

## Action Items

- **Audit your digital workspace**: Map your current digital tools and spaces against your desired team culture. Identify gaps and alignment opportunities.
- **Establish psychological safety practices**: Train meeting facilitators in virtual psychological safety techniques and establish clear group norms for online interactions.
- **Redefine accountability**: Shift from activity-based monitoring to results-based evaluation frameworks with clear deliverables and success metrics.
- **Create intentional serendipity**: Design digital "attractors" and shared spaces where team members can naturally encounter each other during work.
- **Review technology regularly**: Schedule periodic assessments of whether your current tools still serve strategic needs rather than dictating workflows.

## Questions to Consider

- How does our current digital environment reflect or undermine our desired team culture?
- Are our "working out loud" practices enabling collaboration or creating performance anxiety?
- What unconscious biases might we have about visibility and productivity in remote team members?
- How can we create psychological safety in virtual meetings when we can't read body language?
- Are we choosing tools based on actual needs or trends and what everyone else is using?

## Conclusion
"Thinking Remote" provides essential guidance for leaders navigating the shift to distributed work. The book's strength lies in its practical, experience-based approach that addresses both the technical infrastructure and human dynamics of remote collaboration. Rather than offering one-size-fits-all solutions, the authors provide frameworks for thinking through remote work challenges tailored to specific organizational contexts and cultures.

The book is particularly valuable for its emphasis on intentionality—designing digital spaces with the same care as physical ones, establishing trust-based relationships, and creating inclusive virtual environments. For any leader managing remote teams or considering the transition to distributed work, this guide offers both the strategic mindset and tactical approaches needed to build effective, humane remote organizations.

The future of work is indeed flexible, and this book provides the roadmap for making that flexibility work for both people and productivity.
