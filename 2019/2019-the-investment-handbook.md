# The Investment Handbook
**Authors**: David Bateman, Jonathan Reuvid | **Year**: 2019
**Category**: Business & Finance

## Summary
"The Investment Handbook" is a comprehensive guide for entrepreneurs seeking funding to start or grow their businesses. This essential resource demystifies the complex world of investment by providing practical frameworks for approaching investors, creating compelling business plans, and navigating the due diligence process. The book combines strategic insights with an extensive directory of venture capital firms and angel networks across the UK, US, and global markets.

This handbook serves as both a strategic advisor and practical contact database, making it invaluable for early-stage entrepreneurs, startup founders, and business owners looking to scale. Readers will gain insider knowledge of what investors really want, common pitfalls to avoid, and alternative funding options when traditional investment isn't available.

## Core Insights

### The 14-Page Business Plan Framework
Investors receive up to 100 business plans weekly, so your plan must deliver its core message within five minutes. The most effective approach is a concise 14-page structure following the KISS principle ("Keep It Simple, Stupid"). Each page should contain no more than six bullet points covering: Executive Summary, Opportunity, Background, USP, Progress, Market, Route to Market, Competition, Management, Business Model, Financials, Investment Request, Exit Strategy, and Conclusion.

> "To gain attention your Plan needs to be clear and concise, delivering its message and your core proposition within the first five minutes of reading which is probably all the time that an investor will give to their first scan."

**Quick Take**: Your business plan is your first pitch – make it impossible to ignore by focusing on clarity over completeness.

### Understanding Investor Psychology
All equity investors share common expectations regardless of their category. They typically assume no more than 30% of investments will succeed (venture capitalists often expect only 10% winners), which means they look for high returns from successful ventures. Most aim to exit within 3-4 years, so your planning should align with this timeline.

Investors fall into three main categories: Business Angels (£10,000-£100,000 investments, often through networks like OION), Venture Capitalists (£250,000+ packages, more demanding), and Private Equity funds (multi-million pound funding). Each has different expectations for involvement, risk tolerance, and returns.

> "Angel investors are usually businesspeople who have often started successful businesses which are the basis of their wealth and who favour early stage businesses."

**Quick Take**: Match your funding stage to the right investor type and understand their risk/reward expectations before approaching them.

### The Six Essential Investor Requirements
Before any serious discussion can begin, investors evaluate six critical areas: Presentation quality, Pitch effectiveness, Business growth potential, Management capability, Business plan thoroughness, and Risk/reward balance. Only satisfaction across all six leads to term sheet discussions.

The management team is particularly crucial – investors look for "been there, done that" experience alongside innovative brilliance. Young teams may need to add older members (40+) in supporting roles, especially for finance or as non-executive chairs with established networks.

> "From an investor's viewpoint, 'capability' includes financial commitment. The core management team will be expected to invest significant personal funds for their shareholdings."

**Quick Take**: Your management team's depth and personal financial commitment matter as much as your business idea.

### Due Diligence Preparation
Due diligence falls into four critical categories: Financial (historical, current, and future performance), Legal (contracts, incorporation, IP protection), Technical (product fitness and uniqueness), and Commercial (market analysis and sales forecast validation). Preparation is paramount – missing documents or inadequate preparation can stop investment proceedings instantly.

For early-stage companies without significant financial history, cashflow forecasts and assumptions become especially important. Salaries and overheads will be scrutinized, with investors expecting reasonable compensation levels that show commitment to reinvesting in the business.

> "Having produced an enticing Business Plan and succeeded in having a Term Sheet agreed with investors, it would be a great pity to then trip up at the due diligence stage so preparation and being as comprehensive as possible are key."

**Quick Take**: Due diligence is where good deals die – prepare documentation and answers before investors ask for them.

### Valuation Reality Check
Valuing early-stage companies is more art than science. Common mistakes include unrealistic valuations that alienate investors and failing to account for future funding rounds. For pre-revenue, high-risk technology companies, angel investors typically expect valuations around £500,000-£1,000,000, meaning £100,000 investment should secure 10-20% equity.

Negotiation strength comes from commercial positioning – having cash in the bank, attracting experienced advisors, demonstrating market validation, and showing relevant comparable examples. Remember that valuation reflects the investor's value-add beyond capital – networks, expertise, and credibility can justify lower valuations.

> "If an investor is interested and has in mind that they would be willing to invest to get 20% of the company, with a valuation that will mean they will get only 1% of the company, both parties' views are so far apart that negotiation seems pointless and the investor will simply walk away."

**Quick Take**: Be realistic about valuation and understand that the right investor adds value beyond just money.

## Action Items

- **Create your 14-page business plan** following the recommended structure, keeping each page to 6 bullet points maximum
- **Research investors thoroughly** before contact – check their investment levels, sectors, portfolio companies, and current activity
- **Prepare due diligence documentation** in advance: financial records, legal contracts, IP documentation, and market research
- **Build a balanced management team** with both innovative energy and experienced members, including possible non-executive directors
- **Develop realistic valuations** based on your stage, market, and comparable companies
- **Plan your funding timeline** – assume at least 6 months to raise investment and start the process when you don't desperately need the money
- **Prepare backup plans** including grants, alternative lenders, crowdfunding, and bootstrapping options

## Questions to Consider

- Does your business have a clear Unique Selling Proposition that differentiates it from competitors?
- Is your management team balanced with both innovative thinking and practical experience?
- Have you validated your market through actual customer research or initial sales?
- Are your financial projections realistic and backed by solid assumptions?
- What value-add beyond capital would the ideal investor bring to your business?
- How will you use the investment to reach specific milestones that increase company value?
- What is your realistic timeline and plan for investor exit?

## Conclusion
"The Investment Handbook" is an essential resource that transforms the intimidating process of raising investment into a manageable, strategic activity. The book's greatest strength is its combination of insider insights with practical, actionable frameworks that entrepreneurs can implement immediately.

The comprehensive directory of investors alone makes this worth having, but the real value lies in the strategic understanding of investor psychology, the detailed preparation frameworks, and the realistic perspective on the funding process. This handbook could save entrepreneurs months of wasted effort and dramatically increase their chances of securing the right investment from the right partners.

While some content focuses on UK-specific programs and investors, the core principles and frameworks apply universally across markets. For any entrepreneur serious about raising capital, this book provides the roadmap for turning innovative ideas into investable businesses and avoiding the common pitfalls that derail promising ventures.
