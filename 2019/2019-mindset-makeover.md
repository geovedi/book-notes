# Mindset Makeover
**Original Title**: Mindset Makeover: Tame Your Fears, Change Your Self-Sabotaging Thoughts, And Learn From Your Mistakes - Make Assertive And Mindful Choices
**Author**: Steven Schuster | **Year**: 2019

## Summary

"Mindset Makeover" is a practical guide to understanding and transforming the cognitive biases and emotional patterns that sabotage our success and happiness. Schuster reveals how our brains deceive us, why emotions often override rational thinking, and how we can rewire our mental habits for better outcomes. The book combines neuroscience, psychology, and personal development to help readers recognize their self-sabotaging patterns and replace them with healthier, more effective mindsets.

This book is perfect for anyone who feels stuck in negative thought patterns, struggles with emotional reactivity, or wants to understand the psychological mechanisms holding them back. Readers will gain practical tools for emotional regulation, habit formation, and mental clarity.

## Core Insights

### The Deceptive Brain: Your Thinking vs. Observing Mind

Your brain operates with two distinct minds: the thinking mind (that constant chatter in your head) and the observing mind (your "right mind" or better judgment). Most people let their thinking mind run unchecked, leading to emotional hijacking where feelings override rational thought. The key insight is that emotions are millions of years older than rational thinking—your emotional brain evolved first and reacts much faster than your thinking brain.

The emotional brain creates "certainty" before your thinking mind can even process what's happening. When you ask yourself "Why did I say that?" after an emotional outburst, the honest answer is "I don't know"—you felt it first, thought later. The solution isn't to suppress emotions but to observe them without becoming them. Instead of "I am angry," say "I feel anger."

> "Your emotions are not you. Your thoughts are not you. You're not a failure. You're not angry. You failed. You feel anger."

**Quick Take**: Separate yourself from your emotions by observing them rather than identifying with them. This creates space for rational choice even in emotional moments.

### Goal vs. Habit Mindset: The Real Key to Lasting Change

Most people approach transformation with a goal-oriented mindset, which is why they fail. Goals are exciting to visualize but require constant discipline and energy. When life throws curveballs (like getting fired), goal-focused people revert to old habits because their willpower is depleted.

Habit-focused people succeed by building automatic behaviors that persist regardless of circumstances. The difference is profound: "People who stay fat want to lose weight to change their lives. People who get fit want to change their lives to lose weight." Goals deliver linear growth at best, while habits create exponential growth because they compound over time.

**Quick Take**: Focus on adopting 30-day habits rather than chasing distant goals. Start small and choose habits with multiple benefits (like cooking, which improves health, saves money, and builds relationships).

### It's Not About You: Breaking the Self-Centered Bias

Humans naturally believe they're the center of their universe—and by extension, the actual universe. Every experience includes us as the protagonist, so we assume everything that happens is about us. This leads to taking things personally, whether negative (criticism) or positive (praise).

The reality is that almost nothing is about you. When someone criticizes you, it's about them—their fears, their issues, their perspective. When good things happen, it's rarely because you're special. Accepting that you're not the center of the universe is liberating because it removes the burden of taking everything personally.

> "Accepting your own place on Earth, which is not central but just as important as anyone else's, is one of the best mindset makeovers you can do for yourself."

**Quick Take**: Practice detachment from both good and bad events. When you stop taking everything personally, you become calmer, more balanced, and less emotionally volatile.

### Stop Being Faithful to Your Ideas: The Flexibility Advantage

We're taught to "stand by our beliefs" and "stay true to our convictions," but this rigidity holds us back. When beliefs are challenged, most people double down rather than reconsider. This happens because we identify with our ideas—challenging them feels like challenging ourselves.

The most successful people remain flexible in their thinking. They're willing to question long-held beliefs and change their minds when presented with better information. This isn't weakness—it's strength. Your grandparents' stubbornness about outdated beliefs seems foolish, yet most of us do the same thing with our own cherished ideas.

**Quick Take**: Regularly question your beliefs and be willing to change your mind. The quality of your thinking is more important than the consistency of your opinions.

### Find Meaning Beyond Goals: The Purpose Mindset

Goals have endings, and when you achieve them, you often feel empty unless you have deeper purpose. The most fulfilled people chase something that doesn't have a definitive ending point—greater causes, ongoing improvement, service to others.

You don't need a grandiose purpose to experience meaning. Sometimes it's as simple as making dinner for your family or being kind to a stranger. Meaning comes from making small positive impacts in your immediate world, not from changing the entire planet.

> "Sometimes there is magic in a simple act. Sometimes it is enough to make your family smile and find happiness in it. Sometimes the present meaning of life is a chicken stew with fresh vegetables."

**Quick Take**: Find purpose in small, immediate actions rather than distant, overwhelming goals. Ask yourself: "If I wanted to make the world better right now, what would I do?"

## Action Items

- Practice emotional observation: Instead of saying "I am angry," say "I feel anger." Create distance between yourself and your emotions.

- Start a 30-day habit challenge: Choose one small habit (like cooking meals at home or walking daily) and commit to it for just one month.

- Practice the "not about you" mindset: When you feel criticized or offended, ask yourself how this might not be about you at all.

- Question one long-held belief: Pick a belief you've held for years and genuinely research opposing viewpoints with an open mind.

- Find immediate meaning: Each morning, ask "How can I make someone's day better today?" and follow through with one small action.

## Questions to Consider

- What emotions do you identify with rather than just experience? How could observing them change your reactions?

- Which of your current goals could you reframe as habits instead?

- In what areas of your life do you take things too personally? How would you act differently if it wasn't about you?

- What beliefs do you hold that might be outdated or based on faulty information?

- Where can you find meaning in small, everyday actions rather than distant goals?

## Conclusion

"Mindset Makeover" is absolutely worth your time if you're tired of self-sabotage and want practical tools for mental transformation. The biggest reason to read it is the combination of scientific insight and actionable advice—Schuster doesn't just explain why your brain works against you, he gives you concrete strategies to work with your brain's natural tendencies instead of fighting them.

What makes this book valuable is its emphasis on small, sustainable changes rather than dramatic overhauls. The habit-based approach, the emotional observation techniques, and the "not about you" mindset are simple but profound shifts that can dramatically improve your life with minimal resistance. This isn't another "think positive" book—it's a practical guide to working with your brain's actual wiring for better results.
