# The Science of Being Human
**Authors**: Marty Jopson | **Year**: 2019
**Category**: Science & Nature

## Summary
"The Science of Being Human" explores the fascinating and often counter-intuitive scientific aspects of what makes us human. Marty Jopson takes readers on an eclectic journey through multiple scientific disciplines to understand human evolution, biology, behavior, and our relationship with technology. The book reveals how human beings are "gloriously, unnecessarily and inexplicably complicated and counter-intuitive" - biological systems that defy simple categorization and often break the expected rules of physics and mathematics.

This book is perfect for curious readers who enjoy science writing that challenges common assumptions and reveals the hidden complexities of everyday human experiences. You'll gain a deeper appreciation for the remarkable ways our bodies and minds work, often in ways we rarely consider.

## Core Insights

### Human Evolution: Messier Than You Think
The traditional "March of Progress" image showing linear human evolution is fundamentally wrong. Human evolution was not a straightforward progression but a complex web of interbreeding species. Modern humans contain 1-4% Neanderthal DNA and up to 6% Denisovan DNA, showing that Homo sapiens interbred with other human species. We also discovered remarkable Neanderthal sophistication - they created cave paintings 64,000 years ago, proving they were not the brutish cavemen of popular imagination. The reality is that multiple human species coexisted, interbred, and shared cultural achievements.

> "Biology is messy. Biological systems and, by extension, human beings, are gloriously, unnecessarily and inexplicably complicated and counter-intuitive."

**Quick Take**: Human evolution wasn't a ladder of progress but a tangled bush of interconnected species sharing genes, culture, and territory.

### The Uncanny Valley: Why Almost-Human Creep Us Out
When something looks almost human but not quite perfect, our brains experience cognitive dissonance that creates revulsion. This "uncanny valley" effect explains why computer-generated characters in films like "The Polar Express" can be unsettling, and why realistic robots and virtual humans often feel creepy rather than comforting. Our brains struggle to categorize these near-human entities, triggering an evolutionary defense mechanism that may be designed to detect disease or abnormality. This phenomenon has serious implications for medical applications of virtual reality and artificial intelligence.

> "The worry is that if animations fall into the uncanny valley they may hinder rather than help the patients."

**Quick Take**: Our brains are hardwired to detect imperfections in human faces, making us feel uneasy around almost-but-not-quite human representations.

### Your Microbiome: More Bacterial Than Human
You contain about 40 trillion bacterial cells compared to 37 trillion human cells - technically making you more bacteria than human. These microorganisms, weighing about 2 kilograms, function as a hidden organ that influences everything from mood to athletic performance. The gut microbiome affects mental health, stress response, and even physical abilities. Studies show that specific bacteria like Bifidobacterium can reduce anxiety, while elite cyclists share unique gut bacteria that may enhance athletic performance. This discovery challenges our understanding of where "self" ends and "other" begins.

> "The human body is made up of some 37 trillion cells... there are at least the same number of bacteria living in you."

**Quick Take**: Your body is a complex ecosystem where bacterial cells outnumber human cells, fundamentally influencing your health, mood, and capabilities.

### Proprioception: Your Hidden Sixth Sense
You possess an extraordinary internal sense called proprioception that tells you where your body parts are relative to each other without looking. This sense allows you to touch your nose with your eyes closed and navigate complex physical movements. Proprioception works through specialized nerve endings in muscles, joints, and tendons that continuously feed position data to your brain. This sense can be disrupted by alcohol (hence field sobriety tests), during teenage growth spurts (causing clumsiness), and can even be extended to include tools and instruments through practice. Athletes, musicians, and artists develop this sense to extraordinary levels.

> "Proprioception is the most important of the senses that fall outside of the usual five we were all taught in school."

**Quick Take**: Your body contains a sophisticated internal positioning system that you can train and extend to incorporate tools, making them feel like parts of yourself.

### Digital Humanity: How Technology Changes Our Behavior
The internet reveals surprising aspects of human psychology through the "online disinhibition effect" - we tend to behave worse online because evolutionary social brakes are removed without face-to-face contact. Research shows that making quick decisions leads to cooperation, while deliberation often leads to selfishness. The internet's anonymity creates toxic behavior, but also connects niche communities that wouldn't otherwise exist. Perhaps most fascinating is how artificial intelligence systems have created their own languages and ways of communicating when not constrained by human programming.

> "What makes human beings what we are and what makes us so different to the rest of the animal kingdom of organisms? On some basic biological levels, the answer to this is nothing."

**Quick Take**: Technology strips away our evolved social constraints while revealing fundamental truths about human cooperation and communication.

### Crowd Physics: Humans Break the Rules of Fluids
When humans gather in crowds, we don't behave like liquids or gases as scientists initially expected. In fluid dynamics, flow is fastest in the center of a channel and slowest near walls due to friction. But in crowds, people move at the same rate regardless of their position because walls don't create friction - other people do. This leads to counter-intuitive behaviors like the "egg-timer enigma" where people at edges of exits move faster than those in front. The London Millennium Bridge disaster revealed how humans unconsciously synchronize their steps when walking on moving surfaces, creating dangerous oscillations.

> "People are in the habit of breaking the laws of fluid dynamics."

**Quick Take**: Human crowds follow different physics than fluids, with edges often moving faster than centers and synchronization creating unexpected collective behaviors.

### The Surprising Mathematics of Queues
When you're in multiple queues, the other lines genuinely do seem to move faster - and it's not paranoia, just probability. In three equal queues, you only have a 33% chance of being in the fastest line, making it twice as likely that another queue will beat yours. This mathematical reality helps explain why queue-watching is so frustrating. Queuing theory, developed since 1909, shows that random elements in service times create exponential increases in waiting times - doubling service time quadruples waiting time.

> "What are the chances that one of the other two queues will be faster than your queue? The answer is a two thirds or 66 per cent chance."

**Quick Take**: Your queue really does move slower than others most of the time due to simple probability, not bad luck or conspiracy.

## Action Items

- **Pay attention to your proprioception**: Try closing your eyes and touching different parts of your body to appreciate this hidden sense
- **Support your microbiome**: Eat a diverse diet rich in vegetables and fruits to maintain healthy gut bacteria
- **Practice face-to-face interactions**: Make conscious efforts to have in-person conversations to maintain evolved social behaviors
- **When in crowds, move to the edges**: Position yourself near walls or barriers when navigating through dense crowds to move faster
- **Choose edge queues in supermarkets**: Select checkout lines at the edges for statistically faster service
- **Practice good oral hygiene**: Brush and floss regularly - it may help prevent not just gum disease but potentially Alzheimer's
- **Be mindful of online behavior**: Remember that disinhibition online doesn't reflect your real self

## Conclusion
"The Science of Being Human" is absolutely worth your time for its remarkable ability to reveal the hidden complexity beneath seemingly simple human experiences. Marty Jopson excels at making sophisticated scientific concepts accessible while preserving their wonder and counter-intuitive nature. The biggest reason to read this book is how it transforms your understanding of yourself - you'll never think about touching your nose, standing in line, or interacting online the same way again.

What makes this book particularly valuable is its focus on the gaps between our assumptions about human nature and scientific reality. By showing how evolution, biology, and physics create unexpected behaviors and capabilities, Jopson helps readers appreciate both the limitations of common sense and the extraordinary sophistication of human systems. The book serves as a powerful reminder that what makes us human is far more complex, interconnected, and fascinating than our everyday understanding suggests.
