# The Psychology of Salesmanship
**Authors**: William Walker Atkinson | **Year**: 2019 (Originally written in early 20th century)
**Category**: Business & Finance

## Summary
"The Psychology of Salesmanship" reveals that selling is fundamentally a psychological process—an interaction between minds governed by established principles of human nature. Atkinson demonstrates that successful salesmanship depends not on manipulation or tricks, but on understanding and applying the science of mind to both the seller's and buyer's psychology. The book presents a complete framework from mental preparation to closing the sale, emphasizing that every step in selling is a mental process that can be mastered through understanding psychological laws.

Business professionals, salespeople, entrepreneurs, and anyone interested in persuasive communication will benefit from this systematic approach to understanding the mental dynamics of buying and selling. Readers will gain timeless insights into human nature, practical techniques for developing sales skills, and a scientific framework for influencing others ethically and effectively.

## Core Insights

### The Two Minds in Every Sale: Salesman and Buyer
Successful salesmanship requires mastery of both your own mind and understanding of the customer's mind. Atkinson identifies that a sale is "the action and reaction of mind upon mind" according to psychological principles. The salesman must develop specific mental qualities including Self-Respect (not egotism), Poise (mental balance), Cheerfulness (a magnet for success), Politeness (true courtesy from within), and Enthusiasm (inspired earnestness that approaches "soul-power"). Simultaneously, the salesman must understand that the buyer's mind operates on two levels: the "Easyboy" (emotional, impulsive, suggestible) and "Hardfellow" (logical, analytical, cautious), with successful appeals targeting both aspects appropriately.

> "A sale is the action and reaction of mind upon mind, according to well established psychological principles and rules. Salesmanship is essentially a psychological science."

**Quick Take**: Develop your own mental qualities first, then learn to recognize and work with both the emotional and rational aspects of your customer's mind.

### The Pre-Approach: Victory Before the Battle
The Pre-Approach is foundational—90% of sales success is determined before the customer contact. This involves three critical elements: thorough knowledge of your goods (so complete you could demonstrate them to experts or novices), comprehensive knowledge of your customer (their characteristics, habits, business history), and most importantly, developing the proper Mental Attitude through auto-suggestion. Atkinson emphasizes using "catechisms"—positive affirmations about your product, company, and ability—to build unshakable confidence. He shares a powerful example of a salesman who would daily affirm: "Am I going to sell every man I call on to-day? YOU BET I AM!"

> "Before a salesman starts to sell other men, he should sell himself. He should make this sale to himself at the beginning of every day's work."

**Quick Take**: Master your product, research your customer, and program your mind for success through daily auto-suggestion and belief-building exercises.

### The Ten Stages of Every Purchase
Every customer follows the same psychological sequence in making a purchase decision: (1) Involuntary Attention, (2) First Impression, (3) Curiosity, (4) Associated Interest, (5) Consideration, (6) Imagination, (7) Inclination, (8) Deliberation, (9) Decision, and (10) Action. The salesman's job is to guide the customer through these stages systematically. Each stage requires specific techniques—curiosity is aroused through "something new," consideration through logical benefits, imagination through vivid descriptions of ownership, and inclination through emotional appeals to desires and fears.

> "The several mental states manifested by every buyer in an original purchase are given below in the order of sequence in which they are usually manifested."

**Quick Take**: Map your sales presentation to these ten psychological stages, ensuring you don't rush the customer through this natural mental progression.

### The Power of First Impressions and Association
The First Impression occurs in the first five minutes and can make or break the sale. Atkinson reveals that this impression operates through the psychological principle of Association—customers categorize salespeople based on appearance, manner, and carriage. The most effective approach is to embody the "Gentleman" persona—combining strength with courtesy, the "iron hand in the velvet glove." Physical carriage directly affects mental attitude: carrying yourself with head held high (ear lobes over shoulders) creates poise and confidence in both yourself and your customer.

> "Remember, the first five minutes in front of a prospect is worth more than all the remainder in the matter of getting the check."

**Quick Take**: Master your physical presence and manner, as they trigger powerful associations in your customer's mind that influence the entire sales interaction.

### Understanding Customer Temperaments and Types
People can be categorized into three main temperaments: Vital (physical, pleasure-seeking, emotional), Motive (action-oriented, determined, persistent), and Mental (analytical, imaginative, sensitive). Each temperament responds to different appeals—Vital types through feelings and pleasure, Motive types through results and efficiency, Mental types through logic and innovation. Additionally, Atkinson identifies specific buyer types like the "Argumentive Buyer" (let them win minor points), the "Stone Wall Buyer" (go around their defenses), the "Conceited Buyer" (acknowledge their importance), and the "Cautious Buyer" (blend new ideas with familiar ones).

> "An understanding of a man's temperament will often give one the key to his general character and disposition, which will be of the greatest advantage to the Salesman."

**Quick Take**: Quickly identify your customer's dominant temperament and adapt your approach to match their psychological makeup and decision-making style.

### The Psychology of Closing: Decision and Action
The Closing is where many salesmen fail due to fear rather than customer resistance. Atkinson teaches that the key is recognizing the "psychological moment" when the customer is ready to decide. This requires developing a "sixth sense" for timing—knowing when to stop presenting and start closing. The transition from Deliberation to Decision should be swift and confident, using techniques like pointed questions that demand positive answers, the "Suggestion of Imitation" (showing orders from others), or the dramatic revelation that creates a mental "click" of decision. Never ask questions easily answered with "No"—always frame your closing questions to lead toward "Yes."

> "The main cause of the failure to bring the prospect to a favorable Decision is this: He hasn't been convinced!"

**Quick Take**: Develop timing intuition, use positive-framed questions, and create a clear moment of decision that transforms consideration into commitment.

- "The best 'front' is the real one—the one which is the reflection of the right Mental Attitude and Character—the 'front' of the Gentleman."

- "Faint Heart never won Fair Lady. Fortune favors the brave. When you feel the psychological urge of the moment—step in!"

## Action Items
- Create and use daily auto-suggestion "catechisms" to build confidence and belief in your product
- Practice proper physical carriage (head held high, shoulders back) to influence both your mental state and customer perception
- Study and categorize your customers by temperament to adapt your approach effectively
- Map your sales presentations to the ten psychological stages of purchasing
- Develop your intuition for recognizing the "psychological moment" to close
- Avoid asking questions that can be answered with "No"—frame all questions toward positive responses
- Leave immediately after closing the sale to avoid "unselling" the customer

## Questions to Consider
- How can you better develop the key mental qualities Atkinson identifies (Self-Respect, Poise, Cheerfulness, Enthusiasm)?
- In what ways do you currently appeal to both the "Easyboy" and "Hardfellow" aspects of your customers' minds?
- How well do you understand your product—could you demonstrate it effectively to both experts and complete novices?
- What auto-suggestion practices could strengthen your confidence and mental attitude before customer interactions?
- How can you improve your ability to recognize customer temperaments and adapt your approach accordingly?

## Conclusion
"The Psychology of Salesmanship" is absolutely worth your time as a sales professional or business leader. Its greatest strength is providing a complete scientific framework for understanding and applying psychological principles to sales, rather than offering superficial techniques or manipulative tricks. Atkinson's systematic approach to both the seller's mental development and the buyer's psychology remains remarkably relevant despite being written over a century ago. The book's biggest value lies in elevating salesmanship from a haphazard art to a scientific discipline that can be studied, mastered, and consistently applied. Whether you're new to sales or a seasoned professional, this classic work provides timeless insights into human nature and persuasive communication that will enhance your effectiveness and ethical approach to influencing others.
