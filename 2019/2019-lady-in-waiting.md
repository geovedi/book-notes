# Lady in Waiting: My Extraordinary Life in the Shadow of the Crown
**Author**: Anne Glenconner | **Year**: 2019

## Summary
Anne Glenconner's memoir offers a captivating glimpse into nine decades of extraordinary life as a member of British aristocracy and as Princess Margaret's Lady-in-Waiting for thirty years. Born Lady Anne Coke in 1932, eldest daughter of the 5th Earl of Leicester, she grew up in the magnificent Holkham Hall in Norfolk, forming a childhood friendship with Princess Margaret that would shape her entire life. The book chronicles her journey through privilege and tragedy, from serving as Maid of Honour at the Queen's Coronation to her turbulent marriage to the charismatic but difficult Colin Tennant, Lord Glenconner, who transformed the Caribbean island of Mustique into a playground for the rich and famous. Her story is one of remarkable resilience - enduring her husband's infidelities and temper, the devastating losses of three of her five sons (one to AIDS, another to hepatitis C from drug addiction, and a third who survived a catastrophic accident), and Princess Margaret's decline and death. Yet through it all, Glenconner maintains an spirit of adventure, humor, and unwavering loyalty that makes her memoir both heartbreaking and inspiring.

This memoir will appeal to readers interested in British royalty, aristocratic life, and stories of resilience in the face of overwhelming personal tragedy. Those fascinated by the behind-the-scenes reality of royal life and the human stories behind public personas will find this particularly compelling, as will anyone who appreciates memoirs that balance privilege with profound personal suffering and remarkable strength.

## Core Insights

### A Childhood Fit for a Princess
Growing up at Holkham Hall, the fifth-largest estate in England, Anne's early life was one of incredible privilege yet emotional restraint. As the eldest daughter when a male heir was desperately wanted, she was "the greatest disappointment" to her father, who hoped she'd be born a boy to inherit the earldom. Despite this, her childhood was filled with magical moments - sandcastles with Princesses Elizabeth and Margaret on Holkham beach, jumping out at nursery footmen with young Margaret, and forming a friendship that would last a lifetime. Her aristocratic upbringing taught emotional reserve but also gave her the strength and dignity that would carry her through future tragedies.

> "I had tried awfully hard to be a boy, even weighing eleven pounds at birth, but I was a girl and there was nothing to be done about it."

**Quick Take**: Great privilege doesn't guarantee emotional fulfillment - the constraints of aristocratic life shaped both her opportunities and limitations.

### The Turbulent Marriage That Defined Her
Anne's marriage to Colin Tennant was a rollercoaster of passion, creativity, and pain. Colin was charismatic, visionary, and utterly captivating - he bought Mustique and transformed it into a paradise for Princess Margaret and celebrities like Mick Jagger. Yet he was also mercurial, unfaithful, and prone to explosive rages. Their marriage endured numerous affairs (his and eventually hers), financial extravagance, and Colin's emotional distance from their children. Yet somehow, despite everything, they maintained a friendship and loyalty that lasted fifty-four years. Their story shows how complex marriages can endure through friendship and shared history, even when romantic love falters.

> "We were brought up not to throw in the towel but to bite bullets and fold towels neatly."

**Quick Take**: Long-term relationships require accepting imperfection and finding alternative foundations for commitment when romantic ideals fail.

### Princess Margaret: Royal Friendship and Loyalty
Anne's relationship with Princess Margaret evolved from childhood playmates to lifelong friends and professional colleagues. For thirty years as Lady-in-Waiting, she witnessed Margaret's quick wit, vulnerability, and complexity firsthand. She paints a picture of a woman often misunderstood by the public - not difficult as commonly portrayed, but often bored or frustrated by circumstances. Margaret was fiercely loyal during Anne's darkest moments, visiting Henry when he had AIDS and supporting her through family tragedies. Their friendship transcended royal protocol, involving shell-collecting on Mustique, evenings at Basil's Bar watching for the "green flash," and moments of genuine human connection that money and status couldn't buy.

> "People complained about Princess Margaret being difficult, but I think quite often it was because she was bored or fed up."

**Quick Take**: True friendship transcends status and position - even princesses need genuine human connection and understanding.

### Creating Paradise and Paying the Price
The story of Mustique reads like a fairy tale turned cautionary tale. Colin bought the island for a song, turned it into a cotton plantation, then brilliantly pivoted to creating exclusive villas for the wealthy when cotton failed. His legendary parties - the Golden Ball with gold-painted everything, the Peacock Ball with Princess Margaret crowned "Queen of Mustique" - attracted royalty and rock stars. But this paradise came at enormous cost: financial extravagance that drained their fortune, and Colin's eventual disinheritance of the family in favor of his assistant Kent. The island represents both Colin's visionary genius and his self-destructive tendencies - a paradise built and lost through the same personality traits.

> "Colin had turned his attention to building and selling houses because the cotton wasn't making any money."

**Quick Take**: Visionary creativity can create extraordinary beauty, but without balance, the same passion can lead to destruction.

### The Unimaginable Loss of Three Sons
The heart of Anne's story is her endurance of unimaginable tragedy. Henry died of AIDS at twenty-nine, one of the first aristocrats to speak publicly about the disease. Charlie struggled with drug addiction and mental health issues before dying of hepatitis C at forty-six. Christopher survived a devastating motorcycle accident that left him in a four-month coma, requiring Anne's extraordinary efforts to bring him back to consciousness. Through these losses, Anne discovered reserves of strength she never knew she possessed, creating "coma kits" to help other families and finding healing through spirituality in India after Henry's death. Her ability to continue functioning and find joy despite such overwhelming pain is nothing short of miraculous.

> "The pain of losing your children never goes away. But I try not to dwell on the sad things in my past, instead concentrating on the present, trying to make the most of my life."

**Quick Take**: The human capacity for resilience is far greater than we imagine until tested by circumstances we can't anticipate.

## Key Takeaways

- **Privilege doesn't protect against pain**: Despite enormous wealth and status, Anne faced tragedies that no amount of money could prevent or soften
- **Friendship transcends position**: Her relationship with Princess Margaret shows that even royals need genuine human connection beyond protocol
- **Resilience can be learned**: Anne discovered strength she never knew she had when faced with overwhelming circumstances
- **Acceptance over perfection**: Her long marriage endured through accepting imperfection rather than demanding ideal love
- **Adventure at any age**: Even in her 80s, Anne continues traveling the world and embracing new experiences with humor and enthusiasm

## Questions to Consider

- How would you respond if your spouse left everything to an employee after decades of marriage?
- What enables some people to endure unimaginable tragedy while others are crushed by lesser hardships?
- Does privilege make life easier or harder when facing deep personal suffering?
- How do royal protocols and public expectations affect genuine human relationships?
- What role does acceptance play in enduring difficult circumstances beyond our control?

## Conclusion

This memoir is absolutely worth your time for its candid glimpse into both royal life and extraordinary human resilience. Anne Glenconner writes with refreshing honesty about privilege without boasting, and about suffering without self-pity. Her story is compelling not just because of the famous names and exotic locations, but because it demonstrates how one woman navigated extraordinary privilege and devastating loss with grace, humor, and indomitable spirit. The biggest reason to read this book is to understand how resilience is forged - not through avoiding hardship, but through facing it with courage, friendship, and the willingness to keep living fully even after heartbreaking loss. At 87, Glenconner's perspective on a life lived to the fullest despite everything is both inspiring and deeply moving.
