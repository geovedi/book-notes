# Rats
**Author**: Robert Sullivan | **Year**: 2004 | **Category**: Science & Nature

## Summary
Robert Sullivan's "Rats" is a fascinating natural history exploration of New York City's most unwanted inhabitants. Sullivan spends a year observing rats in a downtown alley, using these creatures as a lens to examine urban ecology, social history, and humanity's relationship with nature. The book reveals how rats are our "mirror species"—thriving in our shadow world, surviving on our waste, and adapting alongside us in cities across the globe.

This book appeals to urban dwellers, natural history enthusiasts, and anyone curious about the hidden ecosystems beneath our feet. Readers will gain a new perspective on cities, waste management, and the remarkable resilience of nature's most adaptable survivors.

## Core Insights

### Rats as Our Mirror Species
Sullivan presents rats not as vermin but as humanity's reflected counterpart—creatures that have followed us to every continent we've colonized. Rats live in "man's parallel universe, surviving on the effluvia of human society; they eat our garbage." Like humans, rats conquer new territories, multiply until resources stretch thin, then decline in cycles. Sullivan argues that while grizzly bears indicate wilderness, rats indicate human presence—making them perfect barometers of civilization.

> "I think of rats as our mirror species, reversed but similar, thriving or suffering in the very cities where we do the same."

**Quick Take**: Rats succeed by exploiting what humans discard, turning our waste into their survival strategy—a lesson in resourcefulness and adaptation.

### The Urban Ecosystem Hidden in Plain Sight
Through nightly observations in Edens Alley, Sullivan reveals the complex ecosystem thriving in urban spaces most people avoid. He discovers that rats live lives of "quiet desperation, hiding beneath the table of man, under stress, skittering in fear, under siege by larger rats." The alley becomes a living laboratory where Sullivan witnesses rat territorial disputes, feeding patterns, and survival strategies—all unfolding just blocks from Wall Street while humans sleep unaware.

> "All I did was stand in an alley—a filth-slicked little alley that is about as old as the city and secret the way alleys are secret and yet just a block or two from Wall Street."

**Quick Take**: Urban spaces contain entire ecosystems operating on different schedules than human activity—nature persists even in the most artificial environments.

### Rats as Vehicles of History and Social Change
The book traces how rats have shaped human history, from carrying plague that killed one-third of Europe's population during the Black Death to becoming symbols of social injustice in modern times. Sullivan chronicles Jesse Gray's 1960s rent strikes in Harlem, where tenants used rats as powerful symbols of urban neglect and landlord exploitation. "The tenants are like rats now," Gray declared. "Rats feel their power, and they come out in broad daylight."

> "Rats will always be the problem. Rats command a perverse celebrity status—nature's mobsters, flora and fauna's serial killers."

**Quick Take**: Animals become powerful symbols in human social movements, often reflecting society's hidden problems and inequalities.

### The Myth of the "Natural" World
Sullivan challenges our definitions of "natural" versus "unnatural," pointing out that humans arbitrarily decide which species belong in our urban ecosystems while others are labeled "invaders." He notes that even plants we consider "native" may have arrived via ancient human migrations. The ostracism of rats from "the pantheon of natural wonders" reveals more about human prejudice than about the animals themselves.

> "It is the very ostracism of the rat, its exclusion from the pantheon of natural wonders, that makes it appealing to me, because it begs the question: who are we to decide what is natural and what is not?"

**Quick Take**: Our categories of "natural" vs. "unnatural" often reflect human preferences rather than ecological realities—nature adapts wherever conditions allow survival.

### Nature's Remarkable Adaptability
The book showcases rats' extraordinary ability to survive in any environment where humans live. Sullivan describes how rats can squeeze through quarter-inch openings, fall from heights without injury, and reproduce at astonishing rates. Their success stems from behavioral flexibility—they eat anything, live anywhere, and constantly adapt to human attempts at eradication. This adaptability makes them perfect subjects for studying evolution in action.

> "If the presence of a grizzly bear is the indicator of the wildness of an area, the range of unsettled habitat, then a rat is an indicator of the presence of man."

**Quick Take**: Survival in changing environments depends on flexibility and the ability to exploit available resources—traits that make rats nature's ultimate urban survivors.

## Action Items
- **Observe urban wildlife**: Take time to notice the animals and plants thriving in overlooked urban spaces—alleys, vacant lots, and other "unloved" areas
- **Question "natural" categories**: Examine your own assumptions about which species belong where and why certain animals are valued while others are reviled
- **Follow the waste stream**: Consider where your garbage goes and what creatures depend on human waste for survival
- **Look for hidden ecosystems**: Recognize that nature operates everywhere, not just in designated parks and wilderness areas
- **Appreciate resilience**: Study how rats and other urban species adapt to human environments—their strategies might offer insights for human adaptation

## Questions to Consider
- What does our treatment of "unwanted" animals reveal about our society's values and fears?
- How would urban planning change if we designed cities to accommodate all species, not just humans?
- In what ways do humans resemble rats in our adaptability and dependence on waste?
- What makes certain animals "charismatic" while others are considered pests?
- How does studying urban wildlife change our understanding of what "nature" really means?

## Conclusion
"Rats" is worth your time if you're interested in urban ecology, natural history, or seeing cities from a completely different perspective. Sullivan transforms one of nature's most reviled creatures into a fascinating lens for examining human civilization, waste, and the hidden ecosystems thriving beneath our feet. The book's biggest strength is its ability to make readers question their assumptions about nature, cities, and the creatures we share our world with.

Sullivan's patient observations and historical research reveal that rats are not just pests but survivors, teachers, and mirrors reflecting our own society's successes and failures. After reading this book, you'll never see an alley—or a rat—the same way again. The book succeeds precisely because it forces us to look where we normally don't want to look, finding wonder and wisdom in the places we usually avoid.
