# Candyfreak
**Author**: Steve Almond | **Year**: 2004

## Summary
"Candyfreak" is part memoir, part culinary exploration, and part elegy for the vanishing world of independent American candy makers. Steve Almond, a self-proclaimed "candyfreak" who has eaten candy every day of his life, takes readers on a cross-country journey to discover the small, family-owned chocolate companies struggling to survive against industry giants like Hershey's and Mars.

The book serves as both a love letter to forgotten candy bars and a commentary on American consumer culture. Almond mourns discontinued favorites like the Caravelle and explores why some truly excellent candy bars disappear while mediocre ones thrive. His quest becomes a meditation on memory, nostalgia, and the pursuit of happiness in a world dominated by mass production.

Anyone who has ever felt passionate about a discontinued product, wondered about the stories behind their favorite treats, or simply loves candy will find something to cherish in this sweet and surprisingly profound journey through America's chocolate underbelly.

## Core Insights

### The Candy Bar as Emotional Time Machine
Candy isn't just sugar and chocolate—it's a portal to childhood memories and emotions. Almond reveals how candy served as "one of the few permissible forms of self-love" in his troubled household, where he and his father both used candy as "a kind of antidepressant."

**Quick Take**: The candy bars we love are rarely just about taste—they're emotional anchors that connect us to specific moments, people, and feelings from our past.

### The Brutal Economics of the Candy Industry
The candy business mirrors broader capitalist dynamics where big companies crush small innovators. Almond discovers that many beloved candy bars disappeared not because they weren't good, but because they couldn't compete with massive marketing budgets and distribution networks of industry giants.

**Quick Take**: When you see the same few candy bars dominating store shelves, you're witnessing market consolidation, not meritocracy. The best products don't always win.

### The Passion of Small Chocolate Makers
The book introduces readers to the dedicated, often eccentric individuals running small candy companies across America. These "chocolate gods" are fueled by passion rather than profit, maintaining traditions and creating products with love that mass producers can't replicate.

**Quick Take**: Behind every great product are people who care deeply about their craft, and supporting small producers preserves innovation and quality in any industry.

### The Pursuit of Happiness as Redemptive Impulse
Ultimately, Almond's candy obsession becomes a metaphor for how humans seek small moments of joy and comfort in an often difficult world. His journey suggests that these "frivolous" pleasures are actually essential to our wellbeing.

**Quick Take**: Don't underestimate the power of small joys—sometimes the pursuit of happiness comes in the form of a perfectly crafted candy bar, not grand achievements.

## Best Quotes

> "The bottom line here is that candy was, for my father and then for me, one of the few permissible forms of self-love in a household that specialized in self-loathing."

> "I think about the candy bars of my youth that no longer exist, the Skrunch Bar, the Starbar, Summit, Milk Shake, Powerhouse, and more recent bars which have been wrongly pulled from the shelves."

> "There is something incredibly liberating about a holiday that encourages children to take candy from strangers."

> "The laws of the candy world are the laws of the broader world: the strong survived, the weak struggled, people sought pleasure to endure their pain."

## Conclusion
"Candyfreak" is far more than a book about candy—it's a profound meditation on memory, family, passion, and the struggle to maintain authenticity in a mass-produced world. Almond's journey from personal obsession to cultural commentary reveals how the seemingly trivial pursuit of the perfect candy bar connects to deeper questions about American capitalism, family dynamics, and the universal human need for small moments of joy.

The book is absolutely worth your time if you've ever felt nostalgic for a discontinued product, wondered about the stories behind your favorite foods, or simply believe that passion and craftsmanship still matter in an age of mass production. Almond's writing is funny, vulnerable, and surprisingly wise, turning what could have been a quirky food memoir into a universal story about love, loss, and the sweet consolations that help us navigate life's bitter moments.

The biggest reason to read "Candyfreak" is that it will make you see the world differently—every candy bar, every family business, every small pleasure becomes infused with new meaning and appreciation.
