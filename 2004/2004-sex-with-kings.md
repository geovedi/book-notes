# Sex with Kings
**Authors**: Eleanor Herman | **Year**: 2004 | **Category**: History & Biography

## Summary
"Sex with Kings" reveals how royal mistresses shaped European history for 500 years, moving from the shadows of medieval courts to become powerful political players who influenced nations. From Diane de Poitiers to Madame de Pompadour, these women used more than just beauty—they wielded intelligence, charm, and political savvy to become indispensable advisors, power brokers, and even rulers in their own right. Herman exposes the fascinating double standards of royal courts, where kings could be promiscuous with impunity while mistresses faced constant threats of dismissal, disgrace, or death.

This compelling narrative will captivate history enthusiasts, political junkies, and anyone fascinated by how intimate relationships can shape world events. Readers will gain insights into power dynamics, gender politics, and the surprisingly sophisticated ways women found to exercise influence in male-dominated societies.

## Core Insights

### The Political Power of the Bedroom
Royal mistresses were far more than sexual partners—they became unofficial cabinet members, treaty negotiators, and political strategists. Diane de Poitiers sat on the French council and signed laws with Henri II using a joint signature. Madame de Pompadour practically ruled France for nineteen years, running the army during the Seven Years' War and managing diplomatic relations. Gabrielle d'Estrées helped end France's religious civil wars through her influence over Henri IV.

> "When the destiny of a nation is in a woman's bedroom, the best place for the historian is in the antechamber." — Charles-Augustin Sainte-Beuve

**Quick Take**: Power dynamics haven't changed as much as we think—personal relationships still drive political decisions.

### The Art of Irreplaceability
The most successful mistresses didn't rely on beauty or sexual prowess alone. They made themselves indispensable by mastering multiple roles: therapist, entertainment director, political advisor, surrogate mother, and social coordinator. Madame de Pompadour created entire worlds for Louis XV, decorating rooms to his taste, organizing theatrical performances, reading intercepted court correspondence for entertainment, and never showing fatigue or boredom, no matter how exhausted she felt.

**Quick Take**: True influence comes from solving multiple problems for powerful people, not just excelling at one thing.

### The Mathematics of Royal Marriage
Royal marriages were political disasters that created the space for mistresses to thrive. Princesses were selected as "walking uteruses" to produce heirs, with beauty, compatibility, or personal happiness as irrelevant afterthoughts. Henry VIII's horror at discovering Anne of Cleves looked nothing like her portrait—calling her his "Flanders mare"—was typical of royal mismatches that drove kings into more satisfying relationships with mistresses.

> "I want to marry a womb." — Napoleon, on choosing a royal bride

**Quick Take**: Institutional arrangements often fail because they ignore basic human needs for connection and compatibility.

### The Double Standard That Defined Everything
Kings could maintain multiple mistresses simultaneously while expecting absolute fidelity from their principal mistress. Charles II had several official mistresses at court who vied for attention like "a squawking hen-house." When Barbara Palmer, Lady Castlemaine, became pregnant by another lover, she threatened to dash the baby's brains out in front of the king unless he claimed paternity—which he did, kneeling in submission.

**Quick Take**: Power doesn't eliminate hypocrisy—it often protects and amplifies it.

### The Geography of Adultery
Different cultures developed distinct approaches to royal mistresses. French courts embraced the maîtresse-en-titre as an official position with titles, income, and duties. English courts maintained a more hypocritical stance—tolerating mistresses while publicly condemning them. Spanish courts sent dismissed mistresses to convents, considering them too sanctified by royal contact for any mortal man to touch. These cultural differences revealed deeper attitudes about power, religion, and gender.

**Quick Take**: Cultural context shapes how societies handle the same basic human behaviors.

### The Economics of Seduction
Royal mistresses operated as sophisticated financial enterprises, converting sexual access into wealth, titles, and political influence. Alice Perrers, mistress of Edward III, became one of England's largest landowners by exploiting her royal connection. The most successful mistresses built financial empires through pensions, gifts, and strategic investments that often outlasted their royal relationships.

**Quick Take**: Intimate relationships with the powerful have always been economic transactions as much as emotional ones.

## Conclusion

"Sex with Kings" is surprisingly relevant reading for anyone interested in how power actually works, beyond organizational charts and official titles. Herman shows that intimate relationships have always been crucial channels of influence, policy-making, and social control. The book's greatest value lies in revealing how women found sophisticated ways to exercise power in societies that formally excluded them—from becoming political advisors to managing court culture and even directing military strategy.

While focusing on entertaining historical anecdotes, the book delivers profound insights into human nature, power dynamics, and the eternal tension between public duty and private desire. It's worth your time if you enjoy history told through compelling personal stories, or if you want to understand how influence really operates in hierarchical systems. The biggest reason to read it? It will permanently change how you see the connection between private relationships and public power.
