# Under the Banner of Heaven
**Author**: Jon Krakauer | **Year**: 2004 | **Category**: Spirituality & Religion

## Summary
"Under the Banner of Heaven" explores the brutal 1984 murders of Brenda Lafferty and her infant daughter Erica, committed by her brothers-in-law Ron and Dan Lafferty in the name of God. Krakauer uses this horrifying true crime story as a lens to examine the history of Mormon fundamentalism and the dangerous intersection of absolute faith and violence. The book weaves together the present-day murder investigation with a comprehensive history of the Mormon religion, from its founding by Joseph Smith to modern fundamentalist groups practicing polygamy and awaiting divine revelations.

This compelling narrative is perfect for true crime enthusiasts, readers interested in religious history, and anyone seeking to understand how ordinary people can commit extraordinary acts of violence in the name of faith. Readers will gain insight into the psychology of religious extremism, the origins of fundamentalist beliefs, and the fine line between divine revelation and dangerous delusion.

## Core Insights

### The "Removal Revelation" That Led to Murder
At the heart of this story is Ron Lafferty's claimed divine revelation commanding him to "remove" several people, starting with Brenda and her baby. This wasn't a momentary madness but a calculated plan shared with their religious group before the killings. The revelation specifically ordered: "It is My will and commandment that ye remove the following individuals in order that My work might go forward. First thy brother's wife Brenda and her baby, then Chloe Low, then Richard Stowe."

What makes this chilling is how Ron and Dan genuinely believed they were doing God's work. After the murders, Dan explained calmly: "I didn't feel anything... I'm pretty sure she didn't suffer." The brothers felt no remorse because they believed they were instruments of divine will, not murderers.

> "Nothing makes you feel better than doing what the prophet commands you to do... You can refuse to pay the guy, or even kill somebody, or whatever, and feel completely good about it."
**Quick Take**: When people believe they're acting on God's direct commands, they relinquish personal moral responsibility.

### The Mountain Meadows Massacre Legacy
Krakauer reveals that violence in the name of Mormon faith didn't begin with the Lafferty murders. The book extensively covers the 1857 Mountain Meadows Massacre, where Mormon militia killed 120 innocent men, women, and children traveling through Utah. This historical context shows that the Lafferty brothers weren't an anomaly but part of a longer tradition of religiously-motivated violence within Mormon fundamentalism.

The massacre was carried out under the doctrine of "blood atonement" - the belief that some sins can only be redeemed through the shedding of the sinner's blood. This theology, which suggests that killing can sometimes be a righteous act, creates a dangerous framework for justifying murder.

**Quick Take**: Religious ideologies that can theoretically justify violence create fertile ground for extremist interpretations.

### The Psychology of Religious Certainty
The book masterfully explores how absolute religious certainty eliminates doubt and moral questioning. Both Ron and Dan Lafferty were so convinced of their divine mission that they approached murder with the same calm deliberation they would any other task. Dan Lafferty's matter-of-fact description of slitting a 15-month-old baby's throat demonstrates how religious certainty can override basic human empathy.

Krakauer contrasts this with former fundamentalist DeLoy Bateman's observation that religious certainty provides comfort: "it's not having to make those critical decisions that many of us have to make, and be responsible for your decisions." This psychological comfort comes at the cost of individual moral judgment.

**Quick Take**: Absolute religious certainty can be psychologically comforting but morally dangerous.

### Modern Fundamentalist Communities
The book provides a fascinating look into contemporary fundamentalist groups like the FLDS in Colorado City, Arizona/Hildale, Utah. These communities practice polygamy, welfare fraud ("bleeding the beast"), complete social isolation, and authoritarian control. Women and girls often have no choice in marriage arrangements, with some girls as young as 14-15 forced into marriages with much older men.

What's most revealing is how these communities create alternate realities where outside laws and moral norms don't apply. As one resident explains, "I think people within the religion—are probably happier, on the whole, than people on the outside. But some things in life are more important than being happy. Like being free to think for yourself."

**Quick Take**: Closed religious communities can provide happiness at the cost of personal freedom and critical thinking.

### The Problem of Authenticating Revelation
A central question Krakauer explores is: how do we distinguish genuine divine revelation from delusion? He draws parallels between Joseph Smith's revelations that founded Mormonism and Ron Lafferty's revelation that led to murder. Both claimed divine communication, both were absolutely certain, yet society accepts one as legitimate while condemning the other as madness.

The book suggests there's no objective way to verify personal revelations - we must rely on the character of the person claiming them and whether the revelation produces good or evil fruit. This creates a dangerous vulnerability for religious communities that accept personal revelations without critical scrutiny.

**Quick Take**: When religious communities accept personal revelations without verification, they become vulnerable to dangerous manipulation.

## Action Items

- **Question authority**: Develop critical thinking skills to evaluate claims of divine revelation, even from trusted religious leaders
- **Understand the psychology**: Recognize how absolute certainty can override moral judgment in yourself and others
- **Support at-risk individuals**: Learn to identify signs of religious extremism and manipulation in communities
- **Balance faith and reason**: Practice integrating spiritual beliefs with rational moral evaluation
- **Educate yourself**: Study religious history to understand patterns of extremism and how they develop

## Conclusion

"Under the Banner of Heaven" is a masterful work of investigative journalism that reads like a thriller while asking profound questions about faith, violence, and human nature. Krakauer treats his subjects - both victims and perpetrators - with complexity and compassion, refusing to settle for easy answers about why good people do terrible things in God's name.

This isn't an anti-religious book but rather a thoughtful exploration of how faith can be twisted to justify violence. The greatest strength is Krakauer's ability to connect a specific crime to broader patterns in religious history and human psychology. By showing how the Lafferty murders fit into a longer tradition of Mormon violence and fundamentalist thinking, he reveals the systemic dangers of absolute religious certainty.

The book is essential reading for anyone seeking to understand religious extremism, true crime fans who appreciate depth beyond sensationalism, and readers willing to confront uncomfortable questions about the relationship between faith and violence. It's a disturbing but ultimately enlightening journey into the dark side of religious belief that will change how you think about the relationship between God, revelation, and human action.
