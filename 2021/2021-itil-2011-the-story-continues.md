# ITIL 2011: The Story Continues
**Authors**: Dr. Pratul Sharma | **Year**: 2021
**Category**: Technology & Computing

## Summary
"ITIL 2011: The Story Continues" transforms the complex world of IT Service Management into an accessible, story-driven learning experience. Dr. Sharma uses practical examples from a fictional telecom company "GoodTel" to explain how ITIL frameworks help businesses deliver value through effective IT service management. The book bridges the gap between technical IT professionals and business leaders by demystifying ITIL concepts through relatable scenarios.

This comprehensive guide covers both ITIL v2.0 and v3.0 frameworks, showing their evolution and practical application in modern business environments. It's designed for professionals who want to understand IT service management without getting lost in technical jargon.

## Core Insights

### The Service-First Mindset
ITIL fundamentally shifts IT from being a cost center to becoming a value-creating business partner. The core principle is that business should always take precedence over technology, with IT serving as an enabler rather than the driver. This mindset helps organizations align their IT services with actual business needs and customer expectations.

> "We must understand that the business has prime importance over and above any of its enabling component."

**Quick Take**: Start every IT initiative by asking "What business problem does this solve?" rather than "What technology can we implement?"

### Service Delivery: The Foundation (ITIL v2.0)
Service Delivery consists of six crucial processes that prepare services before they go live:

**Financial Management**: Ensures money is available for services and tracks budgets rigorously. A powerful example shows how analyzing taxi bills across business units revealed policy abuse, leading to operational controls that reduced costs significantly.

**Service Level Management**: Acts as the bridge between customer expectations and IT capabilities. It negotiates SLAs with customers and ensures they're met through supporting OLAs (internal agreements) and UCs (supplier contracts).

**Availability Management**: Focuses on ensuring services are accessible when needed, using the FARMS approach (Fault Tolerance, Availability, Reliability, Maintainability, Serviceability).

**Capacity Management**: Balances resources and costs by ensuring you have "just the right amount" at the right time. The BlackBerry server example shows how capacity planning prevents overinvestment while meeting growing demand.

**Security Management**: Protects information through comprehensive security policies that comply with CIA principles (Confidentiality, Integrity, Availability).

**IT Service Continuity Management**: Ensures critical services continue during disasters through proper planning and recovery arrangements.

### Service Support: Keeping Services Running
Service Support processes manage live services without disrupting operations:

**Incident Management**: The primary goal is "Restore Service As Soon As Possible" (RSAP). The Service Desk function serves as the Single Point of Contact (SPOC), reducing negative business impact by keeping customers calm.

**Problem Management**: Goes beyond fixing incidents to find root causes. When multiple users report network issues, Problem Management identifies the underlying cause (loose router pin) rather than just treating individual incidents.

**Change Management**: Provides structured approach to implementing changes through proper impact analysis, authorization, and testing. The router replacement example shows how changes require coordination across multiple processes.

**Configuration Management**: Tracks all Configuration Items (CIs) and their relationships through the CMDB, essential for effective change and incident management.

**Release Management**: Builds, tests, and deploys changes while minimizing risk to live services.

### The Service Lifecycle Revolution (ITIL v3.0)
ITIL v3.0 introduced the Service Lifecycle concept with five phases that create continuous improvement:

**Service Strategy**: Focuses on Service Portfolio Management (maximizing business value), Financial Management (budgeting and charging), and Demand Management (understanding user profiles and behavior patterns). Demand Management moved from operations to strategy because it fundamentally shapes how services are delivered.

**Service Design**: Creates comprehensive Service Design Packages covering PSTPM aspects (Portfolio, Service Solution, Technology, Processes, Measurement). This phase includes eight processes from Design Coordination to Capacity Management.

**Service Transition**: Ensures new services don't jeopardize live operations through disciplined transition approaches, enabling high volumes of successful changes.

**Service Operation**: Focuses on day-to-day service delivery and support, balancing stability and responsiveness.

**Continual Service Improvement**: Uses structured measurement and the Seven Step Improvement Process to enhance services continuously.

### The Power of RACI and PDCA
**RACI Model** (Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed) provides clarity on roles and responsibilities. Each activity must have exactly one Accountable person but can have multiple Responsible parties.

**PDCA Cycle** (Plan, Do, Check, Act) drives continuous improvement, ensuring processes evolve based on experience and results.

### Service vs. Process Ownership
The distinction between Service Owners and Process Owners is crucial: Service Owners (like VP of BlackBerry Services) are accountable for end-to-end service availability, while Process Owners ensure specific processes (like Incident Management) meet their targets.

## Action Items

- **Implement RACI matrices** for all critical processes to clarify roles and responsibilities
- **Develop Service Packages** with clear Core Services, Supporting Services, and Service Level Packages
- **Create comprehensive Service Catalogues** that accurately reflect available services
- **Establish Problem Management** to move from reactive incident fixing to proactive root cause analysis
- **Apply the PDCA cycle** to continuously improve processes and services
- **Use Service Level Management** to align customer expectations with IT capabilities
- **Develop financial controls** through proper budgeting, accounting, and charging mechanisms

## Questions to Consider

1. How can your organization shift from viewing IT as a cost center to recognizing it as a value creator?
2. What services in your organization lack proper Service Level Agreements, and how does this impact customer satisfaction?
3. Are you treating symptoms (incidents) rather than curing diseases (problems) in your service delivery?
4. How can Demand Management help optimize resource utilization in your organization?
5. What's the balance between stability and innovation in your current service delivery model?

## Conclusion
"ITIL 2011: The Story Continues" succeeds where many technical books fail – it makes complex IT service management concepts accessible through practical examples and relatable stories. The GoodTel telecom case study provides a consistent thread that helps readers understand how different ITIL processes work together in real business scenarios.

The book's greatest strength is its business-first approach, showing that ITIL isn't about technology for technology's sake, but about delivering business value through well-managed services. The distinction between Service Delivery (preparation) and Service Support (execution) provides a clear mental model for understanding ITIL's structure.

Whether you're an IT professional looking to understand the bigger picture or a business leader wanting to better leverage IT services, this book provides the foundational knowledge needed to implement effective IT Service Management. The real-world examples and clear explanations make it worth your time if you're involved in any aspect of service delivery or IT operations.
