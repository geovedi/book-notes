# The Story of Two Sisters Who Were Jealous of Their Younger Sister
**Original Title**: أَلْفُ لَيْلَةٍ وَلَيْلَةٌ‎ (Alf Layla wa-Layla) | **Authors**: Andrew Lang (Translator) | **Year**: 2021
**Category**: Fiction & Literature

## Summary
This enchanting fairy tale from One Thousand and One Nights tells the story of Sultan Kosrouschah, who overhears three sisters discussing their ideal husbands. The eldest wishes to marry the Sultan's baker, the second his chief cook, while the youngest—and most beautiful—dreams of marrying the Sultan himself. The Sultan, amused and intrigued, grants all three wishes, leading to his marriage to the youngest sister.

As time passes, the elder sisters' jealousy festers, driving them to commit a terrible crime against their sister: kidnapping and abandoning her three newborn children in a canal. The babies are rescued by the royal intendant of gardens, who raises them as his own. Years later, the siblings discover their true heritage through a magical quest for three legendary treasures: the Talking Bird, the Singing Tree, and the Golden Water. Through courage, wit, and perseverance, they not only secure these magical items but also reunite their family and restore justice.

This timeless tale explores themes of jealousy's destructive power, the resilience of family bonds, and the triumph of virtue over evil. Readers who enjoy classic fairy tales with strong moral foundations, magical elements, and satisfying resolutions will find this story particularly rewarding. The narrative serves as both entertainment and a meditation on human nature's complexities.

## Core Insights

### The Corrosive Power of Jealousy
The story vividly illustrates how jealousy, left unchecked, can transform sisters into villains. The two elder sisters cannot contain their envy of their youngest sister's good fortune, despite having their own wishes fulfilled. This bitterness consumes them completely, driving them to commit increasingly wicked acts: first kidnapping their sister's children, then lying about their births, and ultimately condemning their sister to public humiliation.

> "I would a thousand times rather lose the Talking Bird, and the Singing Tree and the Golden Water, than that you should run into danger."

The story demonstrates that jealousy often blinds people to their own blessings and leads to self-destruction. The sisters' plot ultimately fails, and they face execution for their crimes, while the innocent Sultana regains her honor and family.

### The Resilience of Family Bonds
Despite being separated at birth, the three royal siblings maintain an unbreakable connection. Prince Bahman, Prince Perviz, and Princess Parizade exhibit remarkable loyalty and love for each other throughout their upbringing. This bond becomes their greatest strength when they embark on their perilous quest.

> "My dear sister," replied Prince Bahman, "that you should care about the matter is quite enough, even if we took no interest in it ourselves. But we both feel with you, and I claim, as the elder, the right to make the first attempt."

The siblings' unity ultimately allows them to succeed where others have failed, proving that familial love can overcome even magical obstacles and restore what was unjustly taken.

### Courage and Wisdom Overcome Magical Challenges
The quest for the three magical treasures requires more than brute strength—it demands courage, wisdom, and self-control. Many have attempted the journey and failed because they turned to respond to taunting voices, becoming stone statues. Princess Parizade succeeds where her brothers fail by using her intelligence to stop her ears with cotton, demonstrating that wisdom often trumps force.

> "Good dervish," answered the princess, "I feel in my heart that I shall succeed, and it only remains for me to ask you the way I am to go."

This insight shows that different challenges require different approaches. While her brothers relied on traditional courage, Parizade's innovative thinking—literally blocking out distractions—allows her to succeed where others have fallen.

### Truth Eventually Reveals Itself
The story emphasizes that falsehood cannot stand forever. The Sultan's unjust punishment of his innocent wife persists for years, but the truth ultimately emerges through magical intervention. The Talking Bird serves as the catalyst for revelation, speaking uncomfortable truths that restore justice.

> "The women, sire," said the bird, "were the sisters of the Sultana, who were devoured with jealousy at the honour you had done her, and in order to revenge themselves invented this story. Have them examined, and they will confess their crime. These are your children, who were saved from death by the intendant of your gardens, and brought up by him as if they were his own."

This insight reminds readers that while injustice may prevail temporarily, truth has a way of emerging, often through unexpected means.

### Hospitality and Kindness Bring Unexpected Rewards
The royal intendant's decision to rescue and raise the foundling children, despite knowing they likely came from the palace, demonstrates how kindness can lead to unforeseen blessings. His compassionate act preserves the royal lineage and ultimately brings him honor.

> "My wife," he exclaimed as he entered the room, "heaven has denied us any children, but here is one that has been sent in their place. Send for a nurse, and I will do what is needful publicly to recognise it as my son."

This theme runs throughout the story, showing that treating others with compassion and providing aid when possible creates positive ripples that extend far beyond the initial act.

## Action Items / Takeaways
- Guard against jealousy by focusing on your own blessings rather than others' good fortune
- Demonstrate courage through wisdom and creative problem-solving, not just brute force
- Practice hospitality and kindness without expecting immediate rewards
- Maintain family bonds through loyalty and mutual support during difficult times
- Trust that truth will eventually emerge, even when injustice prevails temporarily
- Face challenges with determination, using your unique strengths rather than following others' methods
- Act with integrity even when doing so is difficult or unpopular

## Questions to Consider
- How might the story have differed if the eldest sisters had chosen gratitude over jealousy?
- What does the contrast between the brothers' failed attempts and the sister's success reveal about different approaches to problem-solving?
- In what ways does the story reflect real-world dynamics within families, especially regarding favoritism and resentment?
- How does the eventual restoration of justice serve as commentary on leadership and the responsibility to correct mistakes?
- What role does magical intervention play in resolving what seem like unsolvable human problems?

## Conclusion
This classic Arabian Nights tale remains relevant centuries after its creation, offering readers a compelling blend of magic, moral complexity, and ultimately, justice. The story's strength lies in its exploration of timeless human emotions—particularly jealousy's destructive power and family bonds' resilience—set against a backdrop of magical quests and royal intrigue.

The tale is worth reading for several reasons: it provides a satisfying narrative arc where good triumphs over evil, demonstrates the importance of wisdom alongside courage, and offers a nuanced exploration of family dynamics. The magical elements—the Talking Bird, Singing Tree, and Golden Water—serve not merely as fantasy devices but as catalysts for revealing deeper truths about human nature and justice.

For readers who appreciate fairy tales that balance entertainment with moral depth, this story delivers both. It reminds us that while jealousy may seem powerful in the moment, truth, courage, and family loyalty ultimately prevail. The story's conclusion, where the reunited family rides triumphantly back to the palace "with the songs of the Talking Bird," provides a satisfying emotional payoff that rewards readers for journeying through the trials and tribulations alongside its characters.
