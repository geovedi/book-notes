# Babka, Boulou & Blintzes
**Authors**: Michael Leventhal | **Year**: 2021

## Summary

"Babka, Boulou & Blintzes" is a unique cookbook that uncovers the remarkable 500-year history of Jewish involvement in the global chocolate trade while presenting a diverse collection of Jewish chocolate recipes from around the world. The book reveals how Jewish traders, merchants, and chocolatiers played pivotal roles in introducing chocolate to numerous countries, often facing persecution and discrimination yet ultimately leaving an indelible mark on chocolate culture worldwide. Compiled by Michael Leventhal as a fundraiser for Chai Cancer Care, this volume combines historical narrative with practical recipes, offering readers both knowledge and culinary inspiration.

This book will appeal to food historians, Jewish cultural enthusiasts, home bakers, and anyone interested in the fascinating intersection of Jewish history and chocolate. Readers will gain not only a collection of international recipes but also a deeper appreciation for how Jewish resilience, entrepreneurship, and culinary tradition shaped the global chocolate landscape we know today.

## Core Insights

### The Hidden Jewish History of Chocolate

For over five centuries, Jewish traders have been instrumental in developing the global chocolate industry, yet their contributions remain largely unrecognized. The journey begins with Christopher Columbus's potential Jewish ancestry and converso crew members who first encountered cocoa beans in 1502. Spanish Jews, barred from many professions due to anti-Semitic prejudice, embraced chocolate trading as an accessible business opportunity. When the Spanish Inquisition forced Jewish families to flee, they carried their chocolate-making expertise across Europe and the Americas, establishing chocolate industries in France, Belgium, England, the Caribbean, and eventually North America. This diaspora of chocolate knowledge demonstrates how Jewish communities repeatedly adapted their skills to new environments while facing persecution and discrimination.

### Chocolate as Cultural Survival

The story of Jewish chocolate-making reveals deeper patterns of Jewish resilience and adaptation. In Bayonne, France—now celebrated as the "chocolate capital"—Jewish converso families established thriving chocolate businesses in the early 1600s, despite being forced to leave the city each evening before sunset and carry heavy equipment between homes and workshops. When their success provoked resentment and restrictive laws, Jewish chocolatiers simply moved to new territories. This pattern repeated itself in Martinique, where Jewish cocoa planters built a lucrative chocolate export industry until the Code Noir expelled them, leading them to establish successful chocolate operations in Curaçao instead. Chocolate became more than commerce—it was a vehicle for cultural preservation and economic survival across generations of displacement.

### The Definition of "Jewish Recipes"

The book tackles the complex question of what makes a recipe "Jewish," presenting three distinct categories: traditional Jewish recipes like babka and rugelach deeply connected to Jewish cultural practices; recipes using distinctly Middle Eastern ingredients like mahlab and tahini that carry Jewish flavor profiles; and recipes contributed by Jewish cooks from around the world, representing the global diaspora's culinary adaptations. This approach celebrates the diversity of Jewish culinary traditions while acknowledging that Jewish food culture is not monolithic but varies significantly across geographic regions and cultural contexts.

### Chocolate in Jewish Ritual and Innovation

While chocolate plays no formal role in Jewish religious rituals, the book reveals fascinating connections between chocolate and Jewish cultural practices. Chanukah chocolate gelt (coins), established in 1920s America, represents how commercial chocolate products can acquire Jewish cultural significance. More unexpectedly, chocolate even played a role in WWII espionage, when German explosives disguised as chocolate bars were intended to assassinate Winston Churchill—a plot partly foiled by Jewish scientist Victor Rothschild. This demonstrates how chocolate permeates Jewish experience beyond the kitchen, touching everything from celebration traditions to wartime survival.

### From Historical Narrative to Modern Kitchen

The recipes themselves bridge historical traditions with contemporary innovation, ranging from classic babka and boulou to modern creations like sourdough rye brownies and chocolate beetroot ice cream. Contributors include professional chefs, home cooks, and food writers from multiple countries, each bringing their unique cultural perspective to chocolate-based recipes. This collection transforms historical appreciation into practical application, allowing readers to taste the legacy of Jewish chocolate history while supporting cancer charity work through their culinary exploration.

## Key Recipes to Try

- **Traditional Babka**: The quintessential Jewish braided yeast cake with chocolate and halva filling, representing Eastern European Jewish baking traditions
- **Boulou**: A Jewish Tunisian sweet crossing cake and biscuit, traditionally served at Yom Kippur with black coffee
- **Curaçao Hot Chocolate and Panlevi**: A Caribbean Jewish recipe served at brit milah ceremonies, showing the global reach of Jewish chocolate traditions
- **Sourdough Rye Brownies**: A modern innovation demonstrating how contemporary Jewish bakers continue to evolve chocolate traditions
- **Chocolate Gelt**: While not providing a recipe, the book discusses the history of Chanukah chocolate coins and their cultural significance

- What role has entrepreneurship played in Jewish survival and adaptation throughout history?
- How can we better recognize and celebrate the contributions of marginalized groups to industries and cultural practices we take for granted?
- In what ways does food transcend nourishment to become identity, memory, and resistance?
- How does the global diaspora experience continue to evolve and enrich Jewish culinary traditions today?

## Conclusion

"Babka, Boulou & Blintzes" is far more than a cookbook—it's a revelation of hidden history and a celebration of resilience. While the recipes alone would make this a valuable addition to any baker's library, the historical narrative gives each dish profound cultural context and significance. The book succeeds in transforming our understanding of chocolate from a simple pleasure to a testament to Jewish entrepreneurship, adaptability, and cultural continuity across five centuries of global migration.

The biggest reason to read this book is not just to expand your baking repertoire, but to gain a new appreciation for how cultural contributions can shape industries while remaining largely unrecognized. It reminds us that behind every food we enjoy lies a complex history of human endeavor, migration, and innovation. Whether you're drawn to the historical revelations, the diverse recipes, or the charitable mission supporting cancer care, this book offers a compelling reason to explore the intersection of Jewish culture and chocolate—a combination that, as the book proves, represents both cultural preservation and sweet innovation.
