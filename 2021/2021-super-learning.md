# Super Learning
**Authors**: Peter Hollins | **Year**: 2021
**Category**: Self-Help & Personal Development

## Summary
"Super Learning" is a practical guide that transforms how we approach learning by working with our brain's natural mechanisms rather than against them. Hollins, drawing from decades of research, presents a systematic approach to accelerated learning that focuses on understanding processes, optimizing conditions, and using scientifically-backed techniques. The book challenges common misconceptions about learning—like the effectiveness of highlighting or rereading—and replaces them with evidence-based strategies that actually work.

This guide is perfect for students, professionals, or anyone who wants to learn faster, retain information longer, and develop expertise systematically. Readers will gain practical techniques they can apply immediately, understand the science behind effective learning, and develop the ability to learn anything more efficiently.

## Core Insights

### Optimize Your Learning Environment
The foundation of effective learning starts with creating the right conditions. Research shows our attention span averages only 15 minutes, with optimal learning blocks between 30-50 minutes. Beyond this, cognitive fatigue sets in and retention drops dramatically. The book recommends working with your brain's natural ultradian rhythm (90-minute cycles) and breaking learning into focused bursts with regular breaks.

> "It is long enough to be serious and short enough to hold people's attention. By forcing speakers who are used to going on for 45 minutes to bring it down to 18, you get them to really think about what they want to say."

**Quick Take**: Schedule 30-50 minute learning blocks with 5-10 minute breaks. Plan ahead and designate specific times for learning to work with, not against, your brain's natural attention cycles.

### Learn Concepts Before Facts
Surface learning (memorizing facts) is far less effective than deep learning (understanding concepts and relationships). When you understand the underlying concepts, facts naturally fall into place and become easier to recall. This approach mirrors how our brains evolved—retaining only meaningful, relevant information rather than random data.

> "Emphasizing the larger concepts surrounding the Miranda rule—defendants' rights, police procedure, or landmark Supreme Court cases—help to funnel the facts as they come up. A bigger narrative helps contextualize these facts and makes them mean something."

**Quick Take**: Start with the big picture and underlying principles. Facts will become easier to remember when they're anchored to meaningful concepts rather than memorized in isolation.

### Embrace Productive Failure
Counterintuitively, failure is a crucial component of effective learning. "Productive failure"—struggling with problems without immediate help—leads to deeper understanding than receiving guided instruction. When you work through challenges independently, you engage more deeply with concepts and develop lasting neural pathways.

> "The second group may not have solved the problem itself, but it learned more about the aspects of the problem and the ideas behind it."

**Quick Take**: Choose problems that challenge but don't frustrate you. Document your process, learn from mistakes, and use failures as stepping stones to deeper understanding.

### Master Memory Through Active Retrieval
Memory works through three steps: encoding (processing information), storage, and retrieval. Most learning techniques focus too heavily on encoding and storage, but retrieval practice—actively recalling information—is what truly cements knowledge. The forgetting curve shows we naturally lose information over time, but each retrieval refreshes the memory and flattens the curve.

> "Retrieval practice is the art of practicing what most cements memories—retrieving them. It is an active process and instills memory firmly."

**Quick Take**: Use spaced repetition and practice testing. Regularly quiz yourself rather than rereading material. Each successful recall strengthens the memory trace.

### Use Active Learning Techniques
Passive study methods like highlighting, summarizing, and rereading are surprisingly ineffective. Active techniques—practice testing, distributed practice, elaborative interrogation, self-explanation, and interleaved practice—produce much better results. The Feynman Technique, where you explain concepts in simple terms, is particularly powerful for revealing gaps in understanding.

> "More effective techniques are more active and applied: practice testing, distributed practice, elaborative interrogation, self-explanation and interleaved practice."

**Quick Take**: Replace passive review with active recall. Mix different topics in study sessions, ask "why" questions to deepen understanding, and try teaching concepts to others (or yourself).

## Best Quotes

- "Accelerating learning means working with the pre-existing, inbuilt mechanisms we all already possess. When we work with rather than against the brain, we can get the most from our learning experiences, and enjoy learning more."
- "Concepts before facts, understanding before memory. When you have a deep rather than surface appreciation of information, you anchor new ideas in context and make it easier to remember and apply them."
- "One surefire way to enhance your learning is to act in such a way that it doesn't feel like you're learning at all. When you make learning secondary, i.e. you make your gains in skill and understanding a side effect of some other task you can't help but get carried away with, you learn more quickly and more easily."
- "Tackling the learning question from the teacher's point of view enriches your own understanding, and gives you a firmer grasp on the things you yourself want to learn better."

## Action Items
- Structure learning in 30-50 minute blocks with 5-10 minute breaks
- Always start with the big picture and underlying concepts before memorizing details
- Practice active recall through self-testing rather than passive rereading
- Use spaced repetition to combat the forgetting curve
- Apply the Feynman Technique: explain concepts in simple language to identify knowledge gaps
- Mix different subjects in study sessions (interleaved practice)
- Document your learning process and learn from failures
- Try teaching concepts to others to deepen your own understanding
- Use the PQ4R method for reading: Preview, Question, Read, Reflect, Recite, Review
- Take Cornell Notes with separate columns for notes, cues, and summaries

## Conclusion
"Super Learning" is worth your time if you've ever felt frustrated by ineffective study methods or wanted to accelerate your learning capabilities. The book's greatest strength is its focus on evidence-based techniques rather than learning fads. While some concepts may feel counterintuitive (like embracing failure or mixing subjects), the scientific backing makes a compelling case.

The biggest reason to read this book is that it teaches you how to learn—a meta-skill that pays dividends across all areas of life. Whether you're a student, professional, or lifelong learner, these strategies will transform how you approach new knowledge and skills. The practical, actionable techniques mean you can start applying the principles immediately, making this one of those rare books that delivers value from the first page to the last exam.
