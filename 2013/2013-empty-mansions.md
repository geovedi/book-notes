# Empty Mansions
**Authors**: Bill Dedman, Paul Clark Newell Jr. | **Year**: 2013 | **Category**: History & Biography

## Summary
Empty Mansions tells the extraordinary true story of Huguette Clark, one of America's wealthiest and most reclusive heiresses, who inherited a vast copper fortune but chose to live in near-total isolation for decades. Born in 1906 to Senator William Andrews Clark—a controversial Gilded Age copper king who was once considered wealthier than Rockefeller—Huguette was raised in unimaginable luxury but gradually withdrew from society, eventually spending the last 20 years of her life living in a simple hospital room despite owning magnificent properties worth millions.

The book weaves together investigative journalism and intimate family history, beginning when reporter Bill Dedman stumbled upon mysterious empty mansions owned by an unseen heiress and teamed up with Huguette's cousin Paul Newell to uncover the truth. Their investigation reveals a complex woman who was simultaneously a generous patron of the arts, a devoted friend, and a deeply private individual who chose isolation over public life.

Perfect for readers fascinated by Gilded Age excess, family mysteries, and the complex psychology of wealth, this book offers a compelling exploration of how money can both enable and imprison those who possess it.

## The Mystery and Resolution

### The Empty Mansions Discovery
The story begins with an incredible mystery: three magnificent properties—a Connecticut castle, a California oceanfront estate, and a sprawling Fifth Avenue apartment—maintained in pristine condition for decades but never occupied by their owner. Huguette Clark owned properties totaling nearly the size of the White House, yet her staff had never met her. When a reporter investigating a $24 million Connecticut estate learned the owner hadn't visited since 1951, he uncovered one of the most intriguing puzzles of American wealth.

> "Let me ask you a question," he said. "Do you suppose she's been dead all these years?"

**Quick Take**: The mystery of Huguette's empty mansions reveals how extreme wealth can create absurd situations—maintaining multiple palace-like homes for no apparent reason while their owner lives in seclusion.

### The Copper King's Legacy
Huguette's father, W.A. Clark, embodied the rags-to-riches American dream, rising from a Pennsylvania log cabin to become one of the wealthiest men in America through copper mining. But his wealth came at tremendous cost—including bribery scandals that forced him to resign from the U.S. Senate, only to be reelected later. When he died in 1925, he left an estate worth up to $3.4 billion in today's money, with one-fifth going to eighteen-year-old Huguette.

**Quick Take**: Clark's story illustrates the dark side of the Gilded Age—how immense fortunes were built through ruthless ambition, political corruption, and exploitation of natural resources and workers.

### The Transformation into a Recluse
Huguette's withdrawal from society wasn't instantaneous but gradual. After a brief, unsuccessful marriage in her twenties and the death of her mother in 1963, she increasingly retreated from the world. By 1991, when doctors discovered her suffering from untreated cancers in her luxurious Fifth Avenue apartment, she weighed just 75 pounds and chose to move to a hospital room—never to return home.

**Quick Take**: Huguette's reclusiveness reveals how wealth and isolation can create a self-perpetuating cycle—each year of withdrawal making it harder to re-enter society, even when resources are unlimited.

### The Final Years and Legal Battle
In her later years, Huguette became extraordinarily generous to her caretakers, giving millions to her nurse, Hadassah Peri, who received over $30 million in gifts, and to her attorney and accountant. This generosity raised questions about potential exploitation, especially since her accountant was a convicted sex offender. After her death at age 104 in 2011, nineteen distant relatives challenged her will, alleging fraud and undue influence.

**Quick Take**: Huguette's final years demonstrate how extreme isolation and wealth can create vulnerable situations, even for brilliant, sophisticated individuals, raising uncomfortable questions about consent, capacity, and exploitation.

## Key Characters

### Huguette Clark (1906-2011)
The central figure—a brilliant, shy, artistic woman who preferred dolls and painting to people, spoke multiple languages, and despite her reclusiveness, maintained meaningful relationships through correspondence and phone calls.

### William Andrews Clark (1839-1925)
Huguette's father—the controversial copper king whose wealth enabled his daughter's isolation but whose ambition and political corruption left a complicated legacy that may have influenced her desire to avoid public life.

### Etienne de Villermont (1907-1982)
Huguette's lifelong love interest and correspondent, a French marquis with whom she maintained a romantic, though never consummated, relationship spanning half a century through hundreds of letters and visits.

### Hadassah Peri
Huguette's private nurse who received over $30 million in gifts from the heiress, becoming the central figure in the legal battle over Huguette's estate and motivations.

## Questions to Consider
- What does Huguette's choice to live in isolation despite unlimited resources reveal about the relationship between wealth and happiness?
- How did her father's controversial legacy and public scandals potentially influence her desire for privacy?
- Was Huguette's generosity to her caretakers genuine appreciation or manipulation by enablers?
- What responsibility do society and institutions have to protect extremely wealthy, isolated individuals from potential exploitation?
- Can extreme wealth itself become a form of imprisonment, limiting life choices and authentic human connection?

## The Bottom Line
Empty Mansions is a mesmerizing blend of Gilded Age history, family mystery, and contemporary true crime that reads like a novel but carries the weight of careful investigative journalism. The book succeeds not just in solving the puzzle of Huguette's empty mansions, but in painting a nuanced portrait of a woman who defied easy categorization—simultaneously victim of her wealth, beneficiary of her privilege, and master of her own mysterious destiny.

The biggest reason to read this book is its exploration of how extreme wealth affects human psychology and relationships. Huguette's story asks profound questions about the nature of happiness, the price of privacy, and whether money ultimately liberates or imprisons those who possess it. Her life serves as both a cautionary tale and a fascinating case study in the complex interplay between personality, privilege, and choice.

The book is worth your time for its masterful storytelling alone—it reads like a mystery that unfolds gradually, maintaining suspense even as it answers fundamental questions. But its lasting value lies in how it forces readers to reconsider assumptions about wealth, happiness, and what makes a life well-lived. Huguette Clark may have lived in the shadows, but her story illuminates universal truths about human nature that resonate far beyond her extraordinary circumstances.
