# The Rivals of Sherlock Holmes
**Authors**: Nick Rennison (Editor) | **Year**: 2013

## Summary
This anthology showcases fifteen detective stories from the golden age of crime fiction (1890-1914), featuring the forgotten competitors who challenged Sherlock Holmes' dominance. While Holmes reigned supreme as the undisputed king of fictional detectives, he was far from alone in solving mysteries in the gaslit streets of Victorian and Edwardian England. The stories reveal how writers creatively differentiated their detectives through unique characteristics, specialized knowledge, or distinctly different approaches to crime-solving.

This collection is perfect for mystery enthusiasts, history buffs, and anyone interested in the evolution of detective fiction. Readers will discover a treasure trove of innovative sleuths who used everything from supernatural investigations to scientific methods, from gypsy intuition to railway expertise to crack their cases.

## Core Insights

### The Golden Age of Detective Fiction
The years between 1890 and 1914 witnessed an explosion of crime fiction in English and American magazines. Sherlock Holmes' unprecedented success in The Strand magazine created an insatiable demand for detective stories, inspiring countless writers to create their own crime-solving heroes. This period wasn't just about imitating Holmes—it was about innovating beyond him.

> "The years between 1890 and 1914 were a golden age for English magazines and most of them published crime and detective fiction."
**Quick Take**: The magazine format created the perfect environment for serialized detective fiction to flourish, allowing writers to develop recurring characters and build loyal followings.

### Innovation Through Differentiation
Faced with Holmes' overwhelming popularity, writers developed creative strategies to make their detectives stand out. Some created "Holmes clones" with even greater intellectual abilities, while others took the opposite approach, emphasizing their detectives' ordinariness. The most successful strategy was giving each detective a Unique Selling Point.

> "By far the most common technique writers used in competing with Holmes, however, and one that is still employed today, was to give their characters a Unique Selling Point which was emphasised in every story."
**Quick Take**: The most memorable detectives weren't necessarily the smartest—they were the most distinctive.

### The Rise of Female Detectives
The late 1880s and 1890s saw the emergence of the "New Woman" proto-feminist movement, which directly influenced detective fiction. A surprising number of female detectives appeared in magazine stories, reflecting women's growing presence in previously masculine domains. These characters challenged gender norms while solving crimes with intelligence and determination.

> "A large number of female detectives can be found in the pages of the magazines... This may seem surprising at first but the reason is not hard to find. The late 1880s and the 1890s were the years of the 'New Woman'."
**Quick Take**: Social change directly influenced literary innovation, with detective fiction reflecting evolving gender roles.

### Specialized Expertise as Detective Tool
Many rivals of Holmes relied on specialized knowledge rather than pure deduction. From railway detectives who understood train systems to pawnshop owners who learned secrets from objects people brought in, from Canadian woodsmen who could track anyone to scientific detectives who used new technology—each turned their unique expertise into crime-solving advantages.

> "The period offers (amongst others), a blind detective (Max Carrados), a detective who is a Canadian woodsman and hunter (November Joe), a detective who solves crimes from a corner seat in a London teashop, a gypsy who own a pawnshop (Fergus Hume's Hagar)."
**Quick Take**: Specialized knowledge can be more valuable than general intelligence in solving specific types of crimes.

### The Ordinary Detective Strategy
Some writers deliberately created detectives who were the opposite of Holmes—colorless, ordinary, and relatable. Characters like Arthur Morrison's Martin Hewitt solved crimes through common sense and determination rather than brilliant deduction. This approach made readers feel they too could solve mysteries with careful observation.

> "Unlike the eccentric genius of Baker Street, Arthur Morrison's Martin Hewitt... is a deliberately colourless character. Hewitt is no deductive superman but someone not too different from the reader who solves his cases by the determined application of common sense."
**Quick Take**: Sometimes the most effective detectives are those readers can identify with rather than admire.

### The Global Reach of Detective Fiction
The rivalry to Holmes wasn't limited to Britain. American writers created their own detective heroes, from Jacques Futrelle's "Thinking Machine" to Arthur B. Reeve's scientific detective Craig Kennedy. This transatlantic competition enriched the genre with different cultural perspectives and approaches to crime-solving.

> "It was not just in Britain that writers created detective heroes for the magazines. Some American authors published their work in British magazines."
**Quick Take**: Detective fiction became a truly international phenomenon, with different cultures contributing their own unique crime-solving traditions.

### The Supernatural vs. Rational Investigation
Some detectives, like William Hope Hodgson's Carnacki the Ghost Finder, specialized in supernatural investigations. These stories often blurred the line between rational explanation and paranormal phenomena, reflecting the Edwardian fascination with spiritualism and the occult while maintaining the detective story's emphasis on logical resolution.

> "Carnacki stories, about a Sherlockian investigator of supernatural events... Most of the stories are formulaic and depend for their effects on the reader's willingness to accept the existence of entities of unspeakable evil."
**Quick Take**: The best supernatural detective stories maintain the tension between rational explanation and paranormal possibilities.

### The Magazine Format's Influence
The monthly magazine format shaped detective fiction in crucial ways. Stories needed to be self-contained yet engaging enough to bring readers back for the next installment. This created the perfect environment for developing memorable recurring characters while ensuring each story delivered complete satisfaction.

> "The truth is that, if Sherlock Holmes had only been the leading character in the first two novels about him Doyle published, it is extremely unlikely that he would be remembered today by anyone but a specialist in late Victorian literature."
**Quick Take**: Format matters more than quality when it comes to literary longevity.

- "Holmes was not an entirely original creation (Doyle openly borrowed elements from earlier detectives like Poe's Dupin and Gaboriau's Lecoq) but he rapidly became the most famous of all fictional detectives, a position he has held ever since."

- "He did so because he appeared in short stories published in a monthly magazine."

- "I wanted very much for the anthology to dig more deeply into the mountain of crime fiction that is to be found in the magazines of the era."

- "None of these characters attained even a tenth of the fame of Holmes but all did their bit to increase the popularity of crime fiction."

- "The quarter of a century from the beginning of the 1890s to the outbreak of the First World War was a golden age for detective fiction. Sherlock Holmes reigned over it as undisputed king but, as this anthology endeavours to demonstrate, there were plenty of rivals to his crown."

## Essential Takeaways

- **Creative differentiation beats imitation**: The most successful rivals of Holmes didn't copy him—they created distinctly different types of detectives with unique approaches to crime-solving.

- **Specialized knowledge trumps general brilliance**: Many successful detectives relied on specific expertise rather than pure intellectual power, making their solutions more believable and satisfying.

- **Format shapes content**: The magazine format created the perfect environment for serialized detective fiction, allowing characters to develop while keeping stories accessible to new readers.

- **Cultural context matters**: The emergence of female detectives reflected real social changes, showing how literature responds to evolving social norms and expectations.

- **International competition enriches genres**: The transatlantic rivalry between British and American detective fiction brought different perspectives and approaches to crime-solving.

- What role did specialized knowledge play in making detectives more believable and their solutions more satisfying?

- How did the global nature of detective fiction competition influence the development of crime-solving methods and character types?

## Conclusion

"The Rivals of Sherlock Holmes" is more than just a collection of forgotten detective stories—it's a fascinating glimpse into a revolutionary period in literary history when an entire genre was being invented and reinvented through competition. While Sherlock Holmes deservedly remains the king of detectives, this anthology reveals that the kingdom was filled with clever, innovative, and sometimes brilliantly eccentric rivals who each contributed something unique to the evolution of detective fiction.

The book is absolutely worth your time if you love mystery stories, are interested in literary history, or simply want to discover the forgotten ancestors of modern detective fiction. The biggest reason to read it isn't just to enjoy good mysteries—it's to understand how creative competition can drive innovation in any field, and how one phenomenally successful creation can inspire countless others to try something different.

The stories vary in quality, as the editor honestly admits, but even the lesser tales provide valuable insights into the cultural and literary forces shaping the early 20th century. In discovering these rivals, we not only appreciate Holmes' genius more deeply—we understand the rich ecosystem of creativity that made his success possible.
