# Make That Grade: Organisational Behaviour
**Authors**: Michèle Kehoe | **Year**: 2013 | **Category**: Business & Finance

## Summary
Organisational Behaviour (OB) is the systematic study of human behavior in organizational settings, focusing on how individuals, groups, and organizational structures interact to influence workplace effectiveness. This comprehensive textbook breaks down the complex dynamics of modern workplaces into 12 essential areas, from individual personality and motivation to leadership, communication, and change management.

This bite-sized guide is perfect for business students, managers, HR professionals, and anyone seeking to understand the psychological and social forces that drive organizational success. Readers will gain practical frameworks for improving team performance, managing conflict, motivating employees, and leading organizational change effectively.

## Core Insights

### Understanding Organisational Behaviour
Organisational behaviour is "the study of the structure, functioning and performance of organizations and the behaviour of groups and individuals within them" (Buchanan and Huczynski, 2010). The field operates at three levels: individual processes (personality, perception, attitudes), interpersonal processes (leadership, communication, teamwork), and organizational processes (change, culture, design). Understanding OB is crucial for managers because employees are an organization's greatest asset—no technology can match human effectiveness and efficiency.

The study of OB helps explain why people behave the way they do at work, predicts behavioral consequences, and provides frameworks for managing behavior to enhance organizational effectiveness. In today's rapidly changing workplaces, understanding these human dynamics is essential for organizational survival and success.

> "Employees are an organisation's greatest asset and no matter how efficient its technology and equipment may be, this is no match for the effectiveness and efficiency of its staff."

**Quick Take**: OB provides the scientific foundation for people management—transforming workplace intuition into systematic understanding.

### Personality and Individual Differences
Personality refers to the relatively stable patterns of behavior, thoughts, and emotions that distinguish people from one another. The Big Five personality framework (Openness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, Neuroticism) provides a scientifically validated model for understanding individual differences in the workplace. Research shows that conscientiousness is the strongest personality predictor of job performance across most occupations.

Understanding personality differences helps managers match people to suitable roles, build balanced teams, and develop targeted training programs. Personality testing, when used professionally, can provide individuals with greater self-awareness and identify development areas, while helping organizations create teams with complementary strengths and working styles.

**Quick Take**: Personality isn't about judging people—it's about understanding differences to optimize team composition and individual development.

### Learning and Development in Organizations
Learning is "the process of acquiring knowledge through experience which leads to a lasting change in behavior" (Buchanan and Huczynski, 2010). Three major approaches explain how people learn: behaviorist (stimulus-response), cognitive (mental processing), and social learning (observation and modeling). Modern workplace learning combines all three approaches through training programs, mentorship, and experiential learning.

Key learning principles include reinforcement schedules, the power of observation, and the importance of feedback. Effective organizations create learning cultures where continuous development is encouraged, mistakes are viewed as learning opportunities, and knowledge sharing is rewarded. Understanding how people learn helps managers design more effective training and development programs.

> "Learning is a complex process and people do not just respond to stimuli, they organise and make sense of the information received."

**Quick Take**: Learning organizations adapt and thrive because they treat employee development as a strategic advantage, not just an HR function.

### Motivation: The Driving Force of Performance
Motivation is "the set of forces that leads people to behave in particular ways" (Moorhead and Griffin, 2012). Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs suggests people progress through five levels: physiological, safety, social, esteem, and self-actualization needs. Herzberg's Two-Factor Theory distinguishes between hygiene factors (which prevent dissatisfaction) and motivators (which create satisfaction and drive performance).

Performance depends on three factors: Performance = f(Motivation, Ability + Environment). Effective managers understand that different people are motivated by different factors and that motivation strategies must be tailored to individual needs and circumstances. The key is creating environments where employees can satisfy their needs while contributing to organizational goals.

**Quick Take**: Motivation isn't one-size-fits-all—effective managers understand individual needs and create environments where personal and organizational goals align.

### Leadership: From Theory to Practice
Leadership is "the process of influencing the activities of an organized group in its efforts toward goal setting and goal achievement" (Buchanan and Huczynski, 2010). Three major approaches to understanding leadership have evolved: trait approaches (focusing on leader characteristics), behavioral approaches (examining leader actions), and contingency approaches (emphasizing situational adaptation).

Research shows that effective leaders adapt their style to fit the situation, balancing task-oriented and relationship-oriented behaviors based on team maturity, organizational culture, and environmental factors. The Path-Goal theory emphasizes that leaders must clarify paths to goals and remove obstacles while providing support tailored to follower needs.

**Quick Take**: Great leadership isn't about having one perfect style—it's about adapting your approach to fit the people and situation you're facing.

### Communication: The Organization's Lifeblood
Communication is the process of transmitting information, ideas, and emotions between people. Effective communication involves both sending and receiving messages accurately, with non-verbal communication constituting two-thirds of all communication. Communication problems in organizations often stem from information overload, semantic barriers, cultural differences, and psychological filters.

Organizations must manage both formal communication channels (official policies, procedures, and hierarchies) and informal networks (grapevines and social connections). Effective communication practices include active listening, clear message construction, appropriate channel selection, and feedback mechanisms. Modern organizations also need to manage digital communication platforms while maintaining human connection.

> "Non-verbal communication constitutes two-thirds of all communication. It is more important in understanding human behaviour than words alone."

**Quick Take**: In the age of information overload, effective communication isn't just about sending messages—it's about ensuring understanding and connection.

### Teams and Group Dynamics
Groups form through stages of development: forming, storming, norming, performing, and adjourning. Effective teams combine complementary skills, clear goals, strong leadership, and mutual accountability. Research shows that team-based working provides numerous benefits: efficient processes, flexible response to change, improved effectiveness, reduced costs, increased innovation, and enhanced employee commitment.

Group performance is influenced by factors including team size, composition, norms, cohesion, and task structure. High-performing teams balance task-oriented behaviors with relationship maintenance, creating environments where both psychological safety and constructive conflict are encouraged. Modern organizations increasingly rely on cross-functional teams and collaborative structures to solve complex problems.

**Quick Take**: Great teams aren't just collections of talented individuals—they're integrated units with shared purpose, complementary skills, and collective accountability.

### Conflict Management: Turning Challenges into Opportunities
Conflict is inevitable in organizations but can be either functional (constructive) or dysfunctional (destructive). Functional conflict stimulates creativity, improves decision quality, and leads to better solutions when managed effectively. The key is distinguishing between task-focused conflict (healthy) and relationship-focused conflict (often destructive).

Five conflict-handling styles include competing, collaborating, compromising, avoiding, and accommodating. Effective conflict management involves appropriate style selection, open communication, interest-based negotiation, and third-party facilitation when needed. Organizations should create cultures where healthy disagreement is encouraged while destructive personal conflicts are minimized.

**Quick Take**: Conflict isn't inherently bad—the challenge is channeling disagreements toward constructive outcomes rather than letting them become destructive.

### Managing Change in Dynamic Environments
Organizational change is triggered by internal factors (new technologies, restructuring, performance gaps) and external forces (market changes, regulatory requirements, competitive pressures). Resistance to change is natural and comes from both individual sources (fear, habit, security concerns) and organizational factors (structural inertia, limited resources, embedded cultures).

Successful change management involves clear communication, employee participation, leadership support, and adequate resources. Change models emphasize the importance of creating urgency, building coalitions, developing vision, communicating broadly, empowering action, generating short-term wins, and anchoring new approaches in organizational culture.

**Quick Take**: Change resistance isn't opposition—it's an natural human response that requires understanding, empathy, and structured support to overcome.

## Action Items
- **Assess personality fit**: Use personality frameworks to match employees with suitable roles and build complementary teams
- **Create learning opportunities**: Design training programs that combine behaviorist, cognitive, and social learning approaches
- **Motivate through needs satisfaction**: Understand individual employee needs and create environments where they can be met while achieving organizational goals
- **Adapt leadership styles**: Develop flexibility in leadership approaches to match different situations and team needs
- **Improve communication clarity**: Focus on both message transmission and understanding, with special attention to non-verbal cues
- **Build team cohesion**: Structure teams for optimal size and composition, with clear goals and accountability systems
- **Channel conflict constructively**: Create processes for healthy disagreement and conflict resolution
- **Manage change systematically**: Use structured approaches to overcome resistance and ensure successful change implementation

## Conclusion
"Make That Grade: Organisational Behaviour" provides a comprehensive foundation for understanding human behavior in workplace settings. Its greatest strength is making complex psychological and sociological concepts accessible and applicable for students and practitioners. The book successfully bridges theory and practice, offering frameworks that can be immediately applied to real organizational challenges.

This is worth your time if you're serious about developing evidence-based people management skills. The biggest reason to read it is that it transforms workplace intuition into systematic understanding—helping you move beyond common sense to scientific approaches for building effective teams and organizations. While the academic foundations are solid, the practical examples and applications make it valuable for anyone who manages people or wants to understand organizational dynamics more deeply.
