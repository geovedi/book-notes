# The Power of MAP to Entrepreneurial Success
**Original Title**: A Practical Guide to Entrepreneurship
**Authors**: Alison Price, David Price | **Year**: 2013

## Summary
"A Practical Guide to Entrepreneurship" redefines entrepreneurship as a balanced journey rather than a high-risk gamble. The authors advocate for a lower-risk approach to starting businesses and social enterprises, drawing from real case studies ranging from bakeries to cricket bat factories. The book presents entrepreneurship not just about financial achievement, but about finding meaningful work that generates positive emotions while creating value.

This guide is perfect for aspiring entrepreneurs who feel trapped in unfulfilling careers and those seeking a more balanced approach to business success. Readers will discover how to build ventures that align with their personal values while maintaining emotional well-being throughout their entrepreneurial journey.

## Core Insights

### The "MAP to Success" Framework
The book introduces a revolutionary framework that redefines entrepreneurial success beyond mere financial achievement:

**M - Meaning**: True success requires work that matters. This can come from building deep relationships, being part of something bigger than yourself, leaving a legacy, putting your talents toward something important, or gaining meaningful life experiences. Entrepreneurs can find meaning either through their work itself or by ensuring their business compatibility with other meaningful life activities.

**A - Achievement**: While traditional entrepreneurship focuses heavily on achievement, the authors argue it's only one-third of the success equation. Achievement should be measured not just in financial terms, but in the impact and milestones reached toward meaningful goals.

**P - Positive Emotions**: Success requires feeling good about your journey. The authors emphasize that entrepreneurs should aim for net positive emotional experiences, recognizing that while challenges are inevitable, overall fulfillment comes from enjoying the process, not just reaching destinations.

> "If you're happy, that's probably the most important thing. Everyone probably has their own definition of success, for me it's happiness. Do I enjoy what I'm doing? Do I enjoy the people I'm with? Do I enjoy my life?"
> _Michael Dell, founder of Dell computers_
**Quick Take**: True entrepreneurial success balances meaningful work with genuine happiness, not just financial metrics.

### The Push and Pull Factors of Entrepreneurship
The authors identify the common drivers that lead people to entrepreneurship:

**Push factors** (escaping current reality):
- Feeling undervalued and underpaid
- Lack of progression opportunities
- Frustration with poor management and office politics
- Being caught in the rat race

**Pull factors** (creating better future):
- Desire for control in uncertain times
- Building work around personal passions
- Living in harmony with personal values
- Gaining flexibility in work arrangements
- Creating meaningful impact with precious time
- Better utilization of technical skills for personal benefit

> "Everybody else at university talked about getting a job, but I wanted the freedom, challenge, excitement and adventure of having my own business."
> _Steve Roe, Hoopla_
**Quick Take**: Understanding your personal motivations is crucial for sustainable entrepreneurial success.

### Lower-Risk Entrepreneurship Approach
The book advocates for a modern approach to entrepreneurship that minimizes risk while maximizing potential:

**Key principles**:
- Start ventures while maintaining existing income streams when possible
- Build incrementally rather than taking massive initial risks
- Focus on proven business models and existing market needs
- Leverage existing skills and experiences rather than starting from scratch
- Create safety nets through careful planning and gradual transition

This approach helps entrepreneurs avoid the emotional and financial devastation that comes from traditional "all-or-nothing" startup methodologies.

### Case Study: SportInspired
Peter Thomond and Richard Raynes created SportInspired by starting with a single community sports festival in Hackney. Their approach demonstrates the book's principles:

**Meaning**: Sport had built their confidence as children, and they wanted to pass this gift to thousands of young people, focusing on the toughest 10% of UK communities.

**Achievement**: Within five years, they worked with over 35,000 people and received recognition from the Prince of Wales's charity, Business in the Community.

**Positive Emotions**: As Peter describes, "You know you're alive when you're running your own enterprise. It's scary, things can go wrong, but you are at the locus of control and that is an exciting place to be."

Their success came from starting small, proving the concept, and then scaling through corporate partnerships rather than seeking massive initial investment.

## Action Items

- **Assess Your MAP**: Before starting any venture, evaluate what gives you Meaning, what Achievement looks like for you, and what generates Positive Emotions in your work life.

- **Start Small**: Begin your entrepreneurial journey as a side project while maintaining your current income, allowing you to test ideas without financial pressure.

- **Leverage Existing Skills**: Focus on ventures that utilize your current expertise and experience rather than starting from scratch in unfamiliar territory.

- **Build Support Networks**: Connect with other entrepreneurs to understand the emotional highs and lows and prepare mentally for the journey ahead.

- **Create Safety Nets**: Develop formal plans and contracts with partners, set realistic milestones, and maintain backup options before fully committing to your venture.

## Questions to Consider

- What would make you look back at the end of your life and say, "I did something really valuable with my time"?
- Are you being pushed away from your current situation or pulled toward a better future?
- How can you structure your venture to achieve meaningful goals while maintaining emotional well-being?
- What existing skills and experiences can you leverage to reduce entrepreneurial risk?
- Are you prepared for the emotional rollercoaster of entrepreneurship, and what support systems do you have in place?

## Conclusion

"A Practical Guide to Entrepreneurship" is worth your time if you're seeking a balanced, sustainable approach to business ownership rather than the glorified high-stakes gambling often portrayed in startup culture. The book's biggest strength is its "MAP to Success" framework, which provides a holistic view of entrepreneurial achievement that values personal fulfillment alongside business results.

The authors' emphasis on lower-risk strategies and real-world case studies makes this guide particularly valuable for first-time entrepreneurs and those transitioning from traditional employment. While it may not satisfy those seeking rapid growth at all costs, it offers invaluable wisdom for building businesses that enhance rather than consume your life.

If you're tired of the traditional entrepreneurship narrative that celebrates burnout and extreme risk-taking, this book provides a refreshing alternative that shows how to build successful ventures without sacrificing your well-being or values.
