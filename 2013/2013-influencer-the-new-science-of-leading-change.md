# Influencer: The New Science of Leading Change
**Authors**: Kerry Patterson, Joseph Grenny, David Maxfield, Ron McMillan, Al Switzler | **Year**: 2013

## Summary
"Influencer" reveals a systematic approach to creating rapid, profound, and sustainable behavior change. The authors argue that leadership isn't about authority or charisma—it's about your ability to influence human behavior. Through extensive research across business, healthcare, education, and social change, they've identified a learnable framework that anyone can master to become a powerful agent of change.

This book is essential for leaders, managers, parents, healthcare professionals, and anyone who needs to motivate others to change. Whether you're trying to improve team performance, transform organizational culture, or change personal habits, you'll learn a science-based approach that increases your odds of success tenfold.

## Core Insights

### The Three Keys to Influence

**Key 1: Focus and Measure**
Great influencers start with crystal clarity. They define exactly what they want to achieve and how they'll measure progress—frequently and obsessively. Instead of vague goals like "improve customer service," they measure specific behaviors daily. The key is ensuring your measures drive the right actions, not just track results.

> "Where most of us celebrate a victory once a year, influencers measure hundreds of times a day."

**Quick Take**: Don't wait for annual results. Measure the behaviors that lead to success, and do it often enough to guide daily decisions.

**Key 2: Find Vital Behaviors**
Influencers don't try to change everything at once. They identify the 2-3 high-leverage behaviors that, if changed, will trigger cascading positive effects throughout the system. These "vital behaviors" are typically exercised during crucial moments—those split-second decisions that determine success or failure.

To find them, study people who already succeed where others fail (positive deviants), identify moments when things typically go wrong, and look for behaviors that challenge unhealthy cultural norms.

> "A handful of high-leverage behaviors drives most of the improvement."

**Quick Take**: Don't boil the ocean. Find the few behaviors that matter most and focus your energy there.

**Key 3: Engage All Six Sources of Influence**
This is where influencers separate themselves from everyone else. Instead of relying on one or two favorite techniques, they systematically apply six sources of influence simultaneously. By combining personal, social, and structural strategies, they "overdetermine" success—making it almost inevitable.

> "Where most of us apply a favorite influence tool or two, influencers identify all varied forces and get them working for rather than against them."

**Quick Take**: One source of influence rarely works. Combine multiple sources until success becomes inevitable.

### The Six Sources of Influence Framework

#### Personal Sources

**1. Personal Motivation - Make It Matter**
Help people connect emotionally to the value of the behavior. Let them experience consequences directly or through powerful stories. Create choice rather than compulsion. People resist being controlled but thrive when they understand the personal significance.

**Example**: Thailand's AIDS campaign reduced new infections by 80% by connecting condom use to saving families, not abstract health statistics.

**Quick Take**: Make the behavior intrinsically rewarding, not just instrumentally valuable.

**2. Personal Ability - Build Mastery**
New behaviors require new skills. Overinvest in training, using deliberate practice with immediate feedback. Break complex actions into manageable steps. Focus on creating early wins that build confidence and momentum.

**Example**: KIPP schools achieve 4x college graduation rates by ensuring students experience success from day one, not after years of struggle.

**Quick Take**: Don't assume people can do it. Invest heavily in skill development until mastery feels natural.

#### Social Sources

**3. Social Motivation - Harness Peer Power**
Engage respected opinion leaders and create cultures of mutual accountability. Use social praise and appropriate peer pressure. When people see those they admire modeling the behavior, they're more likely to adopt it themselves.

**Example**: At Google, engineers who champion innovative projects gain social status, encouraging others to take creative risks.

**Quick Take**: Make the desired behavior socially normal and rewarding.

**4. Social Ability - Enable Through Others**
Provide coaching, mentoring, and resources. Create "each one teach one" cultures where people help each other succeed. Sometimes just knowing help is available makes the difference between trying and giving up.

**Example**: Delancey Street's 90% rehabilitation success rate comes from residents teaching and supporting each other, not professional counselors alone.

**Quick Take**: Build support networks that make success possible for everyone.

#### Structural Sources

**5. Structural Motivation - Reward Intelligently**
Use modest, smart rewards for early successes. Ensure incentives support rather than undermine vital behaviors. Apply rewards after addressing motivation and ability—not as the first solution.

**Example**: Instead of massive bonuses, one company gave public recognition and extra vacation days to teams that adopted new processes quickly.

**Quick Take**: Use rewards to reinforce, not replace, intrinsic motivation.

**6. Structural Ability - Change the Environment**
Modify physical surroundings to make good behaviors easier and bad ones harder. Remove barriers, create visual cues, and use "propinquity" (make desired behaviors more convenient than undesired ones).

**Example**: Hospitals increased handwashing compliance by placing sanitizer dispensers at eye level and every 10 feet—the exact spots where doctors needed them.

**Quick Take**: Design the environment so the right choice is the easy choice.

## Action Items
- **Define Success**: Before trying to change anything, clarify exactly what you want to achieve beyond surface goals
- **Measure Obsessively**: Track progress daily, not annually. Focus on behaviors, not just outcomes
- **Find the Few**: Identify 2-3 vital behaviors that will drive most of your results
- **Diagnose First**: Analyze which of the six sources work against your change efforts before implementing solutions
- **Combine Forces**: Apply multiple influence sources simultaneously until success becomes inevitable
- **Start Small**: Test your approach with a small group, learn from failures, then scale
- **Make It Visible**: Create visual cues and reminders that keep vital behaviors top of mind

## Questions to Consider
- What behavior change would have the biggest impact on your results if you could make it happen?
- Who are the positive deviants in your organization who already succeed where others struggle?
- What crucial moments determine success or failure in your key processes?
- Which of the six sources of influence are currently working against your goals?
- How can you make the desired behavior easier and the undesired behavior harder?
- What social support systems need to change to reinforce new behaviors?
- What early wins could you create to build momentum and confidence?

## Conclusion
"Influencer" offers perhaps the most practical, research-backed framework for behavior change available today. The authors' three keys to influence and six sources model provide a complete roadmap for transforming anything from personal habits to organizational culture to global health initiatives.

What makes this book invaluable is its systematic approach. Instead of random tips or one-size-fits-all solutions, you get a diagnostic framework that helps you identify exactly what's preventing change and how to address each barrier systematically. The case studies—from saving 100,000 hospital lives to eradicating Guinea Worm disease—prove these principles work at scale.

Is it worth your time? Absolutely if you need to influence anyone's behavior (including your own). The biggest reason to read it is that you'll stop wasting energy on approaches that rarely work and start applying a comprehensive strategy that has been proven effective across countless contexts.

The book's genius lies in making complex change feel manageable. By focusing on vital behaviors and combining multiple sources of influence, you can achieve results that seem impossible to others. That's the promise—and the science—of becoming an influencer.
