# Perlefter: The Story of a Bourgeois
**Authors**: Joseph Roth | **Year**: 2013 (English translation), written 1928-1930 | **Category**: Fiction & Literature

## Summary
Perlefter: The Story of a Bourgeois is an unfinished but brilliant satirical novel discovered among Joseph Roth's papers decades after his death. The story follows Naphtali Kroj, an orphaned young man from a small Eastern European town who comes to live with his wealthy relative Alexander Perlefter in Vienna. Through Kroj's observant eyes, we witness the absurdities of bourgeois life in early 20th-century Austria, as Perlefter desperately tries to advance his social standing, marry off his four difficult daughters, and maintain his comfortable existence while avoiding any genuine emotion or decisive action.

The novel is a masterful character study of Alexander Perlefter, a timid businessman who has achieved material success through caution and mediocrity. Unlike the great conqueror he's named after, this Alexander prefers to linger on bridges rather than cross them, avoids commitment, and measures art by its size rather than its quality. The household becomes a microcosm of Austro-Hungarian society on the eve of its collapse, filled with memorable characters including Perlefter's four daughters (the intellectual Karoline, the fragile Julie, the flighty Margarete, and the foolish Fredy), his long-suffering wife, and the maid Henriette with whom Kroj falls in love.

This book offers readers a sharp, ironic portrait of bourgeois values and social climbing, told with Roth's characteristic psychological insight and literary elegance. Though unfinished, it captures the anxieties and pretensions of a class about to be swept away by history.

## Core Insights

### The Illusion of Social Success
Perlefter represents the ultimate bourgeois success story - a man who has achieved wealth and status through timidity rather than courage. His membership in the Moderate Party Club epitomizes his approach to life: avoiding decisive action, seeking consensus rather than principle, and measuring worth by material comfort rather than moral conviction. His cautious nature has served him well in business but left him spiritually empty, unable to love genuinely or act decisively.

> "He liked to linger on the bridges that link one to both here and there because they allowed the person upon them to choose neither. Alexander Perlefter always crossed bridges."

**Quick Take**: True success requires courage, not just caution - Perlefter's material wealth masks emotional poverty.

### The Tragedy of Assimilation
The Perlefter household represents Jewish assimilation in Austria-Hungary - successful on the surface but rootless underneath. Perlefter has abandoned his Orthodox upbringing for bourgeois respectability, yet remains an outsider in Vienna society. His daughters' various rebellions and failed engagements reflect deeper cultural confusion, as they struggle between tradition and modernity, Jewish identity and Austrian culture.

> "There were twenty-four cabs in our city, one for each hour of the day. My father had cab number 17. To this day I love that number."

**Quick Take**: Cultural assimilation often creates internal conflict that material success cannot resolve.

### The Irony of Family Obligation
Perlefter's extended family network reveals the absurdity of bourgeois family values. He takes in his orphaned relative Kroj not out of genuine affection but from social expectation, yet Kroj becomes the most observant critic of the family's pretensions. The various matchmaking attempts for the daughters, conducted by the well-meaning but comical Tante Kempen, show how family relationships become transactions rather than emotional bonds.

> "I should insert here a general reflection about the complex nature of humans. People can have a very anxious nature and still derive pleasure out of their own anxiety."

**Quick Take**: Family duty without genuine love becomes performance rather than connection.

### Art as Status Symbol
Perlefter's relationship with art perfectly captures bourgeois philistinism. He buys paintings not for aesthetic pleasure but as status symbols, measuring quality by size rather than beauty. His highest praise for a work of art is "As large as the wall!" - revealing that culture has become another commodity to be consumed rather than experienced.

> "He appreciated only the dimensions of a painting. Perlefter liked to say, 'Colossal! Such a painting!' He was actually only describing its size."

**Quick Take**: When culture becomes status, we lose the ability to experience genuine beauty.

### Love as Social Strategy
The romantic relationships in the novel reveal love's subordination to social advancement. Henriette's marriage to a farmer is driven by ambition rather than affection, while the daughters' various suitors are evaluated primarily by their financial prospects. Even Kroj's love for Henriette is complicated by class differences and social expectations.

> "I was determined to marry her, but she cheated on me with a chimney-sweep, yet loved me still. The older I got the more she loved me."

**Quick Take**: When love serves social ambition, genuine connection becomes impossible.

## Key Takeaways
- Be wary of success achieved through caution rather than courage
- Material wealth often masks emotional and spiritual poverty
- Family obligations without genuine love become empty performances
- Culture consumed as status symbol loses its transformative power
- Social climbing ultimately creates more anxiety than security

## Questions to Consider
- How does Perlefter's cautious nature serve him in business but fail him in relationships?
- What does the novel reveal about the limitations of assimilation as a survival strategy?
- In what ways does Roth use humor to critique bourgeois values without moralizing?
- How does the narrator Kroj's outsider perspective enable sharper social observation?
- What makes this unfinished novel still valuable as social commentary?

## Conclusion
Perlefter is worth your time as a masterful character study and social satire that captures the anxieties of bourgeois life on the eve of historical cataclysm. Roth's psychological insight and ironic wit create a portrait that is both specific to its time and eerily relevant today. The biggest reason to read it is to experience Roth at the peak of his literary powers, combining sharp social observation with deep human understanding. Though unfinished, it stands as a fine addition to the Roth canon and offers valuable insights into the human cost of social climbing and cultural assimilation.
