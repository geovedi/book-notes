# The Reason I Jump
**Authors**: Naoki Higashida, KA Yoshida, David Mitchell | **Year**: 2013 | **Category**: Psychology & Mental Health

## Summary
"The Reason I Jump" is a remarkable memoir written by Naoki Higashida, a thirteen-year-old boy with severe autism who communicates primarily through an alphabet grid. The book offers unprecedented insight into the autistic mind through 58 questions that reveal why people with autism behave the way they do. Naoki's explanations provide a bridge between the autistic and neurotypical worlds, demonstrating that behind seemingly inexplicable behaviors lies a rich inner life of curiosity, emotion, and intelligence.

Parents, educators, and anyone seeking to understand autism will gain profound insights into the sensory, cognitive, and emotional experiences of autistic individuals. The book transforms how readers perceive autistic behavior, replacing confusion with empathy and understanding.

## Core Insights

### The Communication Barrier
Naoki reveals that the inability to speak doesn't reflect a lack of thoughts or feelings, but rather a disconnect between thinking and speaking. He explains that for people with autism, words often vanish before they can be spoken, making conversation feel like "speaking in an unknown foreign language every minute of every day." This insight challenges the assumption that non-verbal means non-thinking.

> "When I'm talking to myself my voice is really loud, even though I still can't say what I need to... This is one of those things I can't control. It really gets me down."

**Quick Take**: Assume intelligence and understanding first. Communication difficulties don't indicate cognitive limitations.

### Sensory Overload and Different Perception
The autistic world involves unfiltered sensory input. Imagine "twenty radios, all tuned to different stations, are blaring out voices and music" with no off-switches. This overwhelming sensory environment explains many autistic behaviors like covering ears, avoiding eye contact, or seeking repetitive movements that provide comfort.

> "When I'm jumping, it's as if my feelings are going upwards to the sky... When I jump, I can feel my body parts really well, too – my bounding legs and my clapping hands – and that makes me feel so, so good."

**Quick Take**: What appears "abnormal" behavior often serves a crucial regulatory function for the autistic person.

### Memory and Time Perception
Autistic memory works differently – not as a continuous line but as "a pool of dots" that must be actively accessed. This explains repeated questioning and difficulty with sequential tasks. Similarly, the sense of time can be distorted, making it hard to distinguish between a minute and an hour.

> "Inside my head there really isn't such a big difference between what I was told just now, and what I heard a long, long time ago."

**Quick Take**: Patience and repetition aren't just helpful – they're essential for effective communication.

### Emotional Richness Behind the Exterior
Contrary to myths about autism lacking empathy, Naoki shows that autistic people feel emotions deeply but express them differently. He describes the most painful aspect of autism as "the knowledge that I make other people stressed out and depressed," demonstrating profound emotional awareness.

> "True compassion is about not bruising the other person's self-respect."

**Quick Take**: Look beyond unusual expressions to recognize the emotional depth beneath.

### The Need for Structure and Predictability
Repetitive behaviors and special interests provide comfort in a chaotic sensory world. Numbers, patterns, and routines offer reliable anchors when everything else feels overwhelming. This explains why autistic people often memorize train timetables or line up toys obsessively.

> "Numbers are fixed, unchanging things. The number 1, for example, is only ever, ever the number 1. That simplicity, that clearness, it's so comforting to us."

**Quick Take**: Structure and predictability aren't preferences – they're necessities for navigating an overwhelming world.

- "To live my life as a human being, nothing is more important than being able to express myself."
- "We may look like we're not with anyone, but we're always in the company of friends."

## Action Items
- **Practice radical patience**: Allow extra time for responses and repeat information without frustration
- **Presume competence**: Assume understanding and intelligence regardless of communication challenges
- **Create predictable environments**: Establish routines and provide advance notice of changes
- **Look beyond behavior**: Ask "what need is this meeting?" rather than "how do I stop this?"
- **Honor sensory needs**: Recognize that stimming, jumping, or other movements serve important functions
- **Treat by age, not ability**: Address autistic individuals according to their chronological age, not developmental level

## Questions to Consider
- How might our world change if we viewed autistic differences as variations rather than deficits?
- What behaviors that seem "normal" might appear equally strange from an autistic perspective?
- How can we better support communication beyond traditional speech?
- What assumptions do we make about intelligence based on communication style?
- How might we create environments that work for diverse sensory needs?

## Conclusion
"The Reason I Jump" is essential reading for anyone seeking to understand autism beyond stereotypes and surface behaviors. Naoki's voice transforms how we see autistic individuals, revealing rich inner worlds behind challenging exteriors. The book's greatest gift is demonstrating that communication and intelligence come in many forms, and that empathy begins with understanding different ways of experiencing the world.

This slim but powerful book delivers life-changing insights in under 200 pages. It's not just a book about autism – it's a testament to the human spirit's capacity to connect across differences and a reminder that behind every behavior we don't understand lies a person worth knowing. For parents, educators, or anyone who interacts with autistic individuals, these insights will permanently transform your understanding and relationships.
