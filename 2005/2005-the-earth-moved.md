# The Earth Moved
**Authors**: Amy Stewart | **Year**: 2005 | **Category**: Science & Nature

## Summary
"The Earth Moved" reveals the astonishing hidden world beneath our feet, where blind, deaf earthworms perform geological-scale transformations that shape our planet's surface. Stewart weaves together Darwin's pioneering research, cutting-edge soil science, and her own hands-on experiences as a gardener and worm composter to expose these humble creatures as unsung heroes of the ecosystem. Through engaging storytelling and scientific rigor, she demonstrates how earthworms are not merely garden dwellers but powerful agents of change that have influenced agriculture, ecology, and even human history.

This book will transform how you see soil, gardening, and the intricate relationships that sustain life on Earth. Anyone interested in sustainable agriculture, organic gardening, environmental science, or simply understanding the hidden workings of nature will gain a newfound appreciation for these remarkable creatures and their vital role in planetary health.

## Core Insights

### Earthworms as Geological Engineers
Earthworms are nature's ultimate ploughs, capable of moving staggering amounts of soil and creating the fertile topsoil that sustains agriculture. Darwin's research revealed that a healthy earthworm population can process twenty tons of soil per acre annually, passing the entire top few inches of soil through their digestive systems every year. In the Nile valley, earthworms deposit up to a thousand tons of castings per acre, explaining Egypt's legendary agricultural fertility. These blind creatures accomplish this through collective action—individual worms may seem insignificant, but together they reshape landscapes over decades and centuries.

> "It may be doubted whether there are many other animals which have played so important a part in the history of the world, as have these lowly organised creatures." — Charles Darwin

**Quick Take**: Earthworms are the most important livestock you'll ever raise—work with them, not against them, to build living soil that feeds your plants.

### The Three Earthworm Niches
Scientists classify earthworms by their functional roles in soil ecosystems. Epigeic worms (like red wigglers) live in surface litter and excel at composting; anecic worms (like nightcrawlers) build deep vertical burrows and till soil from below; endogeic worms inhabit horizontal root zones and process soil internally. Each type plays a crucial role—epigeic worms rapidly break down organic matter, anecic worms create deep channels for air and water penetration, while endogeic worms improve soil structure around plant roots. Understanding these differences helps gardeners and farmers support diverse worm populations for maximum soil health.

> "The problem with the way we farm now is that we treat our soil like dirt."

**Quick Take**: Different worms for different jobs—support all three types through diverse soil management practices.

### The Soil Food Web Connection
Earthworms are crucial links in the soil food web, transforming decaying organic matter into nutrients that plants can actually use. As worms digest organic material, they excrete castings rich in available nitrogen, phosphorus, and beneficial microorganisms. Research shows that plants grown in soil amended with 20% worm castings grow faster, look better, and resist disease more effectively than those grown in conventional media. Remarkably, studies suggest that plants grown in vermicompost may even deter pests like aphids and whiteflies, possibly due to beneficial compounds in the plants' sap.

> "Chemical fertilizers may feed the plant, but organic fertilizers feed the soil."

**Quick Take**: Feed your worms, and they'll feed your plants—the healthiest crops grow in soil teeming with microbial life.

### Earthworm Intelligence and Behavior
Contrary to assumptions about worms as simple creatures, Darwin's meticulous experiments revealed that earthworms make genuine decisions. When pulling materials into their burrows, they consistently choose the most efficient method—pulling leaves by tips rather than stems, selecting optimal shapes and sizes. Darwin tested this with paper triangles and found that 62% were drawn in by their apexes, the most efficient method. This suggests worms possess rudimentary intelligence rather than operating purely on instinct, allowing them to adapt to local conditions and solve problems like burrow construction and food selection.

> "We may therefore infer—improbable as is the inference—that worms are able by some means to judge which is the best end by which to draw triangles of paper into their burrows."

**Quick Take**: Never underestimate the wisdom of worms—these simple creatures solve complex problems daily beneath our feet.

### The Dark Side of Earthworm Invasion
Not all earthworms are beneficial everywhere. In northern forests that evolved without earthworms since the last ice age, invasive European worms are devastating ecosystems. By rapidly consuming the duff layer of fallen leaves, worms eliminate habitat for ground-nesting birds like ovenbirds, shift soil chemistry from fungal to bacterial dominance, and prevent native wildflowers and tree seedlings from germinating. Scientists have documented forests where earthworm invasion reduces plant diversity by 80% and eliminates entire food webs that depend on the forest floor. This ecological crisis reveals how even beneficial creatures can become destructive outside their native habitats.

> "The change in soil texture could lead to erosion, especially in the summer when water runs across hard, bare ground in sheets."

**Quick Take**: Be mindful of local ecology—never release bait worms or add compost containing live worms to sensitive forest areas.

## Action Items
- Start a worm composting bin with red wigglers to convert kitchen scraps into nutrient-rich castings for your garden
- Practice no-till gardening methods to preserve earthworm burrows and soil structure
- Add organic mulches like leaves and compost to feed earthworm populations and improve soil health
- Avoid chemical fertilizers and pesticides that harm beneficial soil organisms including earthworms
- Plant cover crops like clover to provide year-round food and habitat for earthworms
- Never release bait worms or transport worms between different ecosystems
