# Why Do Men Have Nipples?
**Authors**: Mark Leyner, Billy Goldberg, M.D. | **Year**: 2005

## Summary
This humorous medical guide tackles hundreds of bizarre, embarrassing, and downright strange questions that people only feel comfortable asking doctors after their third martini. Written by an emergency room physician and a satirical novelist, the book demystifies odd bodily functions, debunks medical myths, and provides scientific answers to questions you've always been afraid to ask. From "Why do men have nipples?" to "Can you drink your own urine?", the authors blend genuine medical expertise with comedic timing to create an entertaining educational experience.

Perfect for curious readers, hypochondriacs, or anyone who's ever wondered about the weird things their body does, this book delivers medical knowledge without the stuffy clinical jargon.

## Core Insights

### The Cocktail Party Medicine Phenomenon
The book reveals a fascinating social phenomenon: people only ask their most embarrassing medical questions when inhibitions are lowered by alcohol. Dr. Goldberg shares countless real questions posed during cocktail parties, suggesting that traditional medical education fails to address what the public genuinely wants to know about their bodies. This gap between medical training and public curiosity creates the perfect opportunity for this type of accessible medical education.

> "This is not a self-help book or a medical manual. It's a glimpse at some of the strange things that people want to know from their doctors, but are too embarrassed to bring up in the ER or during an office visit."

**Quick Take**: Medical education should address real human curiosity, not just textbook conditions.

### Biology is Often Weird and Purposeless
Many of the book's answers reveal that human biology contains numerous evolutionary leftovers and seemingly pointless features. Male nipples serve as the perfect example—they exist because all embryos start as female, and nipples develop before sexual differentiation occurs. The book is filled with similar explanations that show how evolution often works like "a tinkerer rather than an engineer," leaving behind remnants and oddities.

> "Why do men have nipples? Because all embryos begin as female, and nipples develop before sex differentiation occurs."

**Quick Take**: Your body contains evolutionary leftovers that might not serve obvious purposes.

### Medical Myths are Surprisingly Persistent
The authors systematically debunk countless old wives' tales and misconceptions that have persisted for generations. From "cracking your knuckles causes arthritis" to "you need to wait 30 minutes after eating before swimming," the book demonstrates how much "common knowledge" about health is simply wrong. These myths often have grainy kernels of truth but have been distorted over time through repetition and misunderstanding.

**Quick Take**: Question health advice that sounds too simple or has been passed down for generations.

### The Body's Gross Functions are Actually Fascinating
What seems disgusting—snot, gas, sweat, and other bodily byproducts—serves important biological purposes. The book transforms repulsion into fascination by explaining the science behind these functions. Morning breath, for instance, results from reduced saliva production during sleep, allowing bacteria to multiply. Understanding the "why" makes the "what" less gross and more interesting.

**Quick Take**: Understanding the science behind bodily functions can transform disgust into fascination.

### Practical Medical Knowledge is Empowering
Beyond the humor, the book provides genuinely useful medical information that readers can apply in real situations. Knowing how to treat jellyfish stings, recognizing the limitations of home remedies, and understanding when to seek professional medical attention gives readers practical health literacy that could prove valuable in emergencies.

**Quick Take**: Basic medical knowledge isn't just interesting—it can be genuinely useful and potentially lifesaving.

## Key Questions Answered
- **Why do men have nipples?** All embryos start as female, and nipples develop before sexual differentiation
- **Does sugar make kids hyperactive?** No—studies show no causal link between sugar consumption and hyperactivity
- **Can you break your penis?** Yes—though there's no actual bone, penile fractures can occur from trauma
- **Why does asparagus make urine smell?** Specific compounds break down into sulfur-containing chemicals during digestion
- **Is cracking knuckles bad for you?** No—it doesn't cause arthritis, though it may reduce grip strength over time
- **Do oysters make you horny?** They're high in zinc (important for testosterone) but the aphrodisiac effect is mostly psychological
- **Why does coffee make you poop?** Coffee stimulates colon muscles and increases rectal pressure
- **Can you drink your own urine?** Technically yes, but it's sterile only when leaving the body and contains waste products

## Conclusion
This book is absolutely worth your time if you've ever been curious about the weird things your body does or if you enjoy learning through humor rather than dry medical texts. Leyner and Goldberg have created something rare: a genuinely educational book that's laugh-out-loud funny while providing legitimate medical insights. The biggest reason to read it is that it answers questions you've always wondered about but never felt comfortable asking—plus plenty you haven't even thought of yet. Perfect for bathroom reading or as a conversation starter, this book delivers both entertainment and enlightenment in equal measure.

**Read it if**: You're curious about human biology, enjoy humor with your education, or need good cocktail party conversation starters.

**Skip it if**: You prefer straightforward medical texts without humor, or are easily offended by bodily function discussions.

**Bottom line**: A hilarious, educational, and surprisingly useful guide to the weird wonders of human anatomy.
