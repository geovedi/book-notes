# Assassination Vacation
**Author**: Sarah Vowell | **Year**: 2005

## Summary
Sarah Vowell's "Assassination Vacation" is a witty, unconventional travelogue that follows the author's pilgrimage to historical sites related to the first three U.S. presidential assassinations: Lincoln, Garfield, and McKinley. What begins as macabre tourism evolves into a meditation on American history, political obsession, and the strange ways we connect with our national past. Vowell blends humor with historical insight as she visits museums, monuments, and obscure relics—from Lincoln's skull fragments to the ceramic tiles where Garfield was shot—while contemplating her own complicated relationship with patriotism during the George W. Bush administration.

This book appeals to history enthusiasts, travel writing fans, and anyone who appreciates dark humor blended with intelligent commentary. Readers will gain a fresh perspective on American history and how we memorialize our nation's most violent moments.

## Core Insights

### The Pilgrimage of Presidential Death
Vowell frames her travels as a modern pilgrimage, drawing parallels between medieval relic worship and historical tourism. Just as religious believers traveled to see saints' body parts for spiritual connection, she seeks out presidential assassination artifacts to make abstract history tangible and real. She compares herself to a Spanish king who kept Saint Francis's rotting corpse in his bed for healing powers, acknowledging the similar psychological impulse to touch something real from the past.

> "I crave my relics for the same reason Señor Bewitched bunked with the late saint. We're religious. I used to share the king's faith. And while I gave up God a long time ago, I never shook the habit of wanting to believe in something bigger and better than myself."

**Quick Take**: History tourism satisfies our deep human need for tangible connection to abstract beliefs, whether religious or patriotic.

### The Egomaniac Brothers: Presidents and Assassins
Vowell makes a striking observation about the psychological similarities between those who seek to become presidents and those who seek to kill them. Both require extraordinary ego and self-importance to believe they can fundamentally change the country through their actions. She calls them "brothers of sorts" and "one-dimensional company towns built up out of the desert by the sheer will of true believers."

> "The assassins and the presidents invite the same basic question: Just who do you think you are?"

**Quick Take**: The line between presidential ambition and violent narcissism is thinner than we'd like to admit.

### History's Magnetic Pull on the Present
The book is haunted by Robert Todd Lincoln, Abraham's son, who was present at all three presidential assassinations. This "jinxed Zelig of doom" becomes a metaphor for how past violence continues to affect future generations. Vowell uses this historical coincidence to explore how we're all connected to and shaped by events that came before us, whether we choose to be or not.

**Quick Take**: We cannot escape history's gravitational pull, even when we try to run from it.

### The Inescapable Politics of Memory
Vowell candidly admits that her assassination obsession intensified during the Iraq War, when her frustration with the current president "cranked up into contempt." She examines how historical interpretation is always colored by present-day politics, and how we use the past to process current events. The book becomes both escape from and commentary on contemporary American political life.

> "Like director Tim Douglas, my simmering rage against the current president scares me. I am a more or less peaceful happy person whose lone act of violence as an adult was shoving a guy who spilled beer on me at a Sleater-Kinney concert."

**Quick Take**: Our relationship with history is always filtered through our present-day political frustrations and hopes.

### The Sacred Space of American Memory
The Lincoln Memorial serves as Vowell's secular church, representing where all American historical roads lead for her. She explores how this monument functions as both religious site and political statement, reflecting and shaping American identity. Her observation about the reflecting pool's psychological power extends to all of our historical sites—they don't just display history, they actively create meaning.

**Quick Take**: Historical monuments are active participants in shaping national identity, not passive containers of the past.

## Conclusion
"Assassination Vacation" is worth your time if you appreciate intelligent humor blended with genuine historical insight. Vowell's unique approach to American history manages to be both deeply personal and universally relevant, showing how our relationship with the past shapes our understanding of the present. The biggest reason to read this book is Vowell's voice—witty, self-aware, and surprisingly moving as she transforms morbid curiosity into meaningful reflection on what it means to be American.

This isn't just a book about dead presidents; it's about how the living grapple with history, how we find meaning in the past, and how we process our complicated relationship with our country. Vowell proves that you can learn more about America from looking at its wounds than from celebrating its victories, and that sometimes the darkest parts of our history illuminate our national character most clearly.
