# We Thought You Would Be Prettier
**Author**: Laurie Notaro | **Year**: 2005

## Summary
"We Thought You Would Be Prettier" is a collection of hilarious, self-deprecating essays chronicling the misadventures of Laurie Notaro, an unapologetically imperfect woman navigating life's most embarrassing moments. From disastrous book tours to gym mishaps and identity theft woes, Notaro transforms everyday humiliations into comedic gold. The book follows her journey as she embraces her role as the "dorkiest girl alive," finding humor and solidarity in life's most awkward situations.

Perfect for readers who enjoy relatable humor, embarrassing stories, and authors who aren't afraid to laugh at themselves. This book offers comfort in knowing you're not alone in your awkward moments and celebrates the beauty of imperfection.

## Core Insights

### 1. The Power of Self-Deprecating Humor
Notaro masterfully demonstrates how embracing our flaws and laughing at ourselves can be both liberating and deeply relatable. Rather than hiding her embarrassing moments, she amplifies them for comedic effect, turning potential sources of shame into sources of connection and laughter.

> "Are you an idiot?" the man behind the counter at the garage yelled at me. "Just how stupid are you? How could you be so stupid?"

**Quick Take**: Your most embarrassing stories often become your best connections with others.

### 2. The Reality Behind the "Author" Image
The book pulls back the curtain on the less-than-glamorous reality of being a published author, complete with orange alerts, lost wallets, security humiliations, and encounters with readers who expect someone... well, prettier. It's a refreshing antidote to author worship.

**Quick Take**: Success doesn't automatically eliminate awkward moments—sometimes it just adds more material.

### 3. Finding Community in Imperfection
Notaro's "Idiot Girls' Action-Adventure Club" represents a tribe of women who embrace their dorkiness and support each other through life's absurdities. The book shows how our imperfections can create stronger bonds than our polished achievements ever could.

**Quick Take**: Real community forms around shared vulnerability, not manufactured perfection.

### 4. The Comedy of Everyday Disasters
From Japanese basketball teams at airports to inappropriate encounters with children at gyms, Notaro finds humor in life's most frustrating moments. Her essays demonstrate how the best comedy often comes from our worst days.

**Quick Take**: If you don't laugh at your disasters, you'll cry—laughing is more fun.

## Takeaways
- **Embrace your awkward**: Your most embarrassing moments are often your most humanizing
- **Laugh at yourself first**: If you find humor in your own mishaps, others will too
- **Community matters**: Finding others who celebrate imperfection is worth more than approval
- **Reality beats perfection**: Authentic, messy experiences are more valuable than curated perfection

## Questions to Consider
- What's your most embarrassing story, and could you find humor in it?
- How might embracing your imperfections actually make you more relatable?
- Where do you find community that celebrates your authentic, imperfect self?
- What disasters in your life might become comedy gold with perspective?

## Conclusion
"We Thought You Would Be Prettier" is absolutely worth your time if you enjoy laugh-out-loud humor that celebrates human imperfection. Notaro's writing feels like sharing drinks with that friend who tells the most embarrassing stories at dinner parties—the ones that make you shoot your drink out your nose while nodding vigorously because you've been there.

The biggest reason to read this book is its therapeutic quality. By laughing at Notaro's mishaps, you're given permission to laugh at your own. In a world of social media perfection, her unapologetic dorkiness is not just refreshing—it's revolutionary. This book reminds us that our imperfections don't just make us human; they make us interesting, relatable, and genuinely funny.
