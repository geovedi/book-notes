# Hunger: An Unnatural History
**Author**: Sharman Apt Russell | **Year**: 2005

## Summary
"Hunger: An Unnatural History" is a profound exploration of one of humanity's most fundamental experiences—hunger—examined from biological, cultural, historical, and personal perspectives. Russell takes readers on a journey through the physiology of starvation, the anthropology of food scarcity, the psychology of eating disorders, and the devastating social impacts of famine throughout history.

This book reveals hunger as far more than a simple physical sensation—it's a force that has shaped human evolution, influenced cultures, driven historical events, and continues to affect billions of lives today. Russell's approach is both scientific and deeply personal, weaving together cutting-edge research with intimate observations and historical accounts that will forever change how you think about food and survival.

Perfect for anyone interested in human biology, social justice, psychology, or simply wanting to understand their relationship with food, this book offers essential insights into why we eat, what happens when we don't, and how hunger continues to shape our world.

## Core Insights

### The Biology of Hunger: Your Body's Secret Intelligence

Hunger is managed by what scientists call the "second brain"—the complex nervous system in your gut that contains more nerve cells than your spinal cord. This system operates independently, sending and coordinating messages through hormones like ghrelin (the "feed me" hormone) and leptin (the "I'm full" hormone). Your digestive system is so sophisticated it can process information and initiate reflexes without any input from your brain.

When you don't eat, your body performs what scientists call "metabolic gymnastics." First, it burns through stored carbohydrates (glycogen), then begins breaking down fats, and finally resorts to consuming muscle tissue. Your brain is particularly protected—it receives glucose first, and during prolonged starvation, switches to using ketones, a rare ability shared only with lactating ungulates among mammals.

> "We are built to be hungry and we are built to withstand hunger. You know exactly what to do."

**Quick Take**: Your body has evolved sophisticated systems for both hunger and survival. Understanding this biological intelligence can help you make better decisions about eating habits and appreciate the remarkable adaptability of human physiology.

### The Psychology of Hunger Artists

Throughout history, humans have been fascinated by those who voluntarily refuse food. From medieval fasting saints to Kafka's hunger artists to modern performers like David Blaine, we've both admired and been repelled by those who can resist the most basic human drive. This fascination reveals our complex relationship with hunger—it's both something we fear and something that can be transformed into spiritual practice, political statement, or entertainment.

The psychological effects of hunger are profound and sometimes surprising. Beyond the physical discomfort, hunger can induce euphoria, heightened sensory awareness, and mystical experiences. Many fasters report feelings of clarity and even "drunkenness" from starvation. However, hunger can also trigger depression, rage, and cognitive impairment. The Minnesota Experiment showed that semi-starvation caused participants to become obsessed with food, irritable, and socially withdrawn—symptoms that mirrored those of anorexia nervosa.

> "I always wanted you to admire my fasting... I couldn't find the food I liked."

**Quick Take**: Voluntary hunger reveals how much our emotional and psychological states are tied to our physical needs. The line between spiritual practice and self-destruction is often determined by context and motivation.

### The Cultural Impact of Food Scarcity

Anthropological studies show that chronic hunger doesn't just affect bodies—it shapes entire cultures. Among the Siriono Indians of Bolivia, constant food shortage created a society with little sharing, secretive eating habits, and food-focused conflicts. The Gurage people of Ethiopia developed elaborate food rituals and preferred to let food rot rather than be seen as gluttonous.

Perhaps most disturbing is Colin Turnbull's study of the Ik people of Uganda, who, forced into marginal lands, abandoned social bonds and family structures during prolonged famine. Children were expelled from homes at age three, the elderly were left to die, and cooperation became malaptive to survival. These cases suggest that many cultural practices around food, hoarding, and sharing may be adaptations to historical periods of scarcity.

> "Most of us are unlikely to admit readily that we can sink as low as the Ik, but many of us do, and with far less cause."

**Quick Take**: Cultural practices around food and sharing often reflect historical adaptations to scarcity. Understanding this helps explain why different societies have such different relationships with hunger and generosity.

### The Science of Starvation

World War II produced unprecedented scientific research on hunger, both through horrific natural experiments (the Warsaw Ghetto) and controlled studies (the Minnesota Experiment). In the Warsaw Ghetto, Jewish doctors conducted secret research on "hunger disease," documenting how starvation affects every system in the body. They found that death from starvation was "like a candle burning out slowly" as the body systematically shut down to conserve energy.

The Minnesota Experiment, where conscientious objectors underwent six months of semi-starvation, revealed the psychological toll of food deprivation. Participants became obsessed with food, lost interest in sex, experienced depression and irritability, and showed many symptoms identical to anorexia nervosa. Crucially, the experiment demonstrated that refeeding must be done carefully—the body adapts to starvation so thoroughly that rapid refeeding can cause cardiac arrest.

> "Death from long-lasting hunger is like a candle burning out slowly."

**Quick Take**: Starvation affects not just the body but the mind and spirit. The psychological symptoms of semi-starvation are nearly identical to eating disorders, suggesting a biological basis for many symptoms we attribute to psychological problems.

### The Paradox of Modern Hunger

Today, we face a strange paradox: for the first time in history, more people suffer from obesity than from starvation, yet hunger remains a persistent problem. In America, 30 million people live in "food-insecure" households, often struggling with the dual burden of food scarcity and obesity. This occurs because cheap, high-calorie foods are more accessible than nutritious options, and the body's adaptation to periodic deprivation can lead to overeating when food is available.

The book also explores voluntary hunger in modern contexts—from calorie restriction enthusiasts who believe hunger will extend their lifespan to eating disorders where hunger becomes a form of control. Russell suggests that our confused relationship with hunger reflects broader cultural anxieties about abundance, control, and body image.

> "A hungry body exists as a potent critique of the society in which it exists. A sick body implicates no one."

**Quick Take**: Modern hunger is complex—often existing alongside abundance. Understanding this paradox helps explain why both obesity and eating disorders continue to rise even in wealthy societies.

## Action Items

- **Practice mindful eating**: Pay attention to your body's hunger and satiety signals rather than eating by habit or emotion
- **Support local food banks**: Even in wealthy nations, food insecurity affects millions, especially children
- **Learn about nutrition**: Understanding how your body processes food can help you make better choices
- **Challenge diet culture**: Recognize that many beauty standards around thinness are culturally constructed and potentially harmful
- **Advocate for food justice**: Support policies that ensure access to nutritious food for all communities
- **Practice intermittent fasting carefully**: While some benefits are documented, extreme restriction can be dangerous
- **Educate others**: Share knowledge about the global and local aspects of hunger and food security

## Questions to Consider

1. How has your personal experience with hunger shaped your relationship with food and your body?
2. What cultural attitudes about hunger, eating, and body size did you inherit, and which do you want to keep or reject?
3. How does the knowledge that starvation can fundamentally change personality and behavior affect how you view people living with food insecurity?
4. In what ways might modern diet culture be a form of voluntary hunger, and what are the psychological and social costs?
5. How can societies ensure food security while respecting cultural diversity in food practices?
6. What responsibility do individuals have to address hunger in their communities and globally?
7. How might understanding the biological basis of hunger help address both eating disorders and obesity?

## Conclusion

"Hunger: An Unnatural History" is essential reading because it reveals how this fundamental human experience shapes everything from our individual psychology to global politics. Russell masterfully weaves together science, history, and personal narrative to show that hunger is not merely a physical sensation but a force that has shaped human evolution, culture, and consciousness.

The book's greatest strength is its ability to connect the intimate experience of an empty stomach with vast historical forces and complex biological systems. By understanding hunger in all its dimensions—from the cellular level to global food systems—we gain profound insights into what it means to be human.

This is absolutely worth your time if you want to understand the deeper forces driving our relationship with food, our bodies, and each other. The biggest reason to read it is that it will forever change how you think about that most basic daily decision: what, when, and why we eat. In a world struggling with both obesity and hunger, never has such understanding been more crucial.
