# Your Call Is Important to Us: The Truth About Bullshit
**Author**: Laura Penny | **Year**: 2005 | **Category**: Philosophy & Critical Thinking

## Summary
Laura Penny's blistering social critique exposes how corporate doublespeak, marketing manipulation, and institutional bullshit have become the dominant language of modern life. The title phrase "Your call is important to us" perfectly encapsulates the insincere, profit-driven communication that pervades customer service, politics, and media. Penny argues that we're drowning in a sea of deliberately misleading language designed to obscure truth and protect corporate interests.

This book is essential for anyone exhausted by empty corporate jargon, misleading advertising, and the daily avalanche of bullshit that passes for communication. Readers will gain a framework for identifying and calling out institutional deception while understanding why cynicism has become a reasonable response to our bullshit-saturated culture.

## Core Insights

### Bullshit as Corporate Strategy
Bullshit isn't just occasional lying—it's a systematic approach to communication that prioritizes corporate interests over human connection. The phrase "Your call is important to us" exemplifies how companies use pleasant language to disguise uncomfortable truths about resource allocation (your time versus their money). Modern bullshit works best when delivered quickly, with plenty of visuals, in mass quantities, and without allowing questions.

> "Your call is important to us" has been chosen from a very deep reservoir of bullshit phrases for the title of this book because it best exemplifies the properties native to bullshit. It tries to slather some nice on the result of a simple ratio: your time versus some company's dough.

**Quick Take**: When you encounter overly pleasant corporate language, ask what uncomfortable truth it's trying to hide.

### The Economics of Deception
Advertising represents a double insult to consumers—it drives up product prices while reducing tax revenue through deductible business expenses. A pair of Nikes might cost only a few dollars to manufacture, but the price includes millions for celebrity endorsements designed to invest the product with "holy aura." The economics of bullshit mean consumers pay extra to be manipulated.

> By the way, if anyone here is in marketing or advertising... kill yourself.

**Quick Take**: Remember that advertising costs are built into product prices—you're paying to be sold to.

### Money as the Ultimate Value
One of modern life's great paradoxes is that money has become our primary good, indifferent to whether it serves honest dealers or flimflam artists. Money is fundamentally "me-minded" and self-replicating, creating a system where profit motive often overrides ethical considerations. This explains why corporations can simultaneously pursue destructive practices while maintaining polished, respectable public images.

> One of the great paradoxes of modern life is that money is our major good, since money is equally glad to pal around with the honest dealer and the flimflam artist.

**Quick Take**: Follow the money to understand why apparently irrational corporate decisions make perfect financial sense.

### Cynicism as Self-Preservation
Cynicism and apathy aren't character flaws—they're reasonable responses to living in a "refulgent tide of bullshit." Young people's disengagement from civic duties like voting stems from exhaustion with hollow rhetoric and broken promises. When optimistic slogans like "It's morning in America" consistently give way to scandals like Iran-Contra, disengagement becomes a protective mechanism.

> Cynicism and apathy are, in fact, reasonable responses to the refulgent tide of bullshit in which we have bobbed all our lives.

**Quick Take**: Your cynicism about institutions isn't paranoia—it's pattern recognition.

### The Information Overload Paradox
More information doesn't solve the bullshit problem when most content is commercial, ephemeral, or stripped of context. This "semi-knowledge" occupies valuable mental space without providing genuine understanding. We can't avoid knowing things not worth knowing, from celebrity gossip to corporate PR, crowding out more substantive engagement with the world.

> Too much information is no antidote to too little of it, since so much of this information is strictly commercial, ephemeral, or shorn of context.

**Quick Take**: Quality of information matters more than quantity—filter out the commercial noise.

## Best Quotes
- "Like most bullshit, the more times you hear it, the bullshittier it gets. This is why bullshit is best served quickly, with many visuals, in mass quantities, with no questions from the floor."
- "My objection to advertising is not merely aesthetic. Each and every shill is a tax-deductible cost of doing business, and thus a double insult."
- "There's nothing money loves more than hanging around with other money. Money can't get enough of money. It's like a Zen koan: It takes it to make it."
- "Those couple of daily white lies, about bad haircuts and spousal girth and the like, are entirely harmless and preferable to the useless, hurtful truth."

## Action Items
- **Question corporate pleasantries**: When companies use overly friendly language, identify the underlying business reality they're masking
- **Track the money trail**: Follow financial incentives to understand why organizations and individuals communicate the way they do
- **Practice media literacy**: Distinguish between genuine information and commercial content designed to sell products or ideas
- **Value authentic communication**: Support businesses and media that prioritize truth over polished presentation
- **Protect your attention**: Recognize when you're consuming "semi-knowledge" that occupies mental space without providing real value

## Questions to Consider
- How has your daily exposure to corporate bullshit shaped your ability to trust institutions?
- What would authentic communication look like in customer service, politics, and media?
- How can we distinguish between necessary social niceties and manipulative corporate doublespeak?
- Is it possible to opt out of the bullshit economy while still participating in modern society?
- What role does individual responsibility play in either perpetuating or resisting institutional bullshit?

## Conclusion
Laura Penny's "Your Call Is Important to Us" is both a cathartic rant and a sharp analysis of how deception became the default mode of modern communication. While her prescription for fighting bullshit remains somewhat elusive—beyond naming it when we see it—the book provides invaluable vocabulary for understanding the corporate doublespeak that dominates our lives.

The biggest reason to read this book is its validation of your skepticism. If you've ever felt that you're drowning in insincere corporate language, misleading advertising, and empty political rhetoric, Penny confirms you're not paranoid—you're paying attention. This book won't solve the bullshit problem, but it will equip you with the analytical tools to navigate it with your sanity intact.
