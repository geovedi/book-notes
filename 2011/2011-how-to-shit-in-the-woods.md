# How to Shit in the Woods
**Author**: Kathleen Meyer | **Year**: 2011

## Summary
"How to Shit in the Woods" is the definitive guide to environmentally responsible outdoor toileting, blending humor, practical wisdom, and environmental advocacy. Meyer transforms a taboo subject into essential knowledge for anyone who ventures into nature, from casual day hikers to serious expeditioners. The book addresses a critical gap in outdoor education: proper waste disposal in natural environments.

This guide is perfect for outdoor enthusiasts, environmental advocates, and anyone who wants to minimize their impact while enjoying nature. Readers will gain practical skills, environmental awareness, and the confidence to handle one of outdoor life's most universal challenges with dignity and ecological responsibility.

## Core Insights

### The Environmental Imperative: Why Your Poop Matters
The explosion of outdoor recreation has created an invisible crisis in our wilderness areas. Before the 1970s, you could drink directly from mountain streams, but today Giardia and Cryptosporidium—spread through fecal contamination—make this dangerous. Human waste doesn't just disappear; it can persist for years, contaminating water sources and spreading disease. A single act of improper disposal can affect countless others.

> "Anyone who has come upon a favorite, once-lovely beach or riverbank trashed with litter knows the horror. But greater than the visual impact of rapidly increasing human waste in the woods are the veiled environmental consequences."

**Quick Take**: Your personal bathroom habits in the wilderness have collective environmental consequences that extend far beyond your immediate campsite.

### The Art of the Perfect Cat Hole
Master the fundamental outdoor skill: digging a proper 6-8 inch deep hole for waste disposal. The ideal location is well above the high-water line (200 feet from water sources), in good soil with abundant bacteria for decomposition. Choose a spot with partial shade and abundant organic matter. Use a sturdy trowel—Meyer recommends the U-DIG-IT stainless steel model. After your business, stir the contents with a stick to mix soil bacteria with fecal matter, then cover completely.

> "It's something to think about: a small hole preventing the complete destruction of an ego. A proper hole is of great importance, not only in averting disasters such as Henry's, but in preventing the spread of disease and facilitating rapid decomposition."

**Quick Take**: A well-dug cat hole is your single most important tool for Leave No Trace outdoor ethics.

### Water Treatment: Assume Everything is Contaminated
Treat all surface water—streams, lakes, even springs—as potentially contaminated. Modern filtration systems, chemical treatments, or boiling (rolling boil for 1 minute, 3 minutes at high elevation) are essential. Giardia cysts can survive for months in cold water and are present in surface waters worldwide. Don't trust appearances—crystal clear mountain streams can harbor dangerous pathogens.

> "According to the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention in Atlanta, no surface water in the world is guaranteed free of the microscopic cysts responsible for a parasitic disease called giardiasis."

**Quick Take**: Water treatment isn't optional—it's as essential as your tent or sleeping bag for safe outdoor travel.

### Women's Wilderness Wisdom: Mastering Outdoor Relief
Women face unique challenges in outdoor toileting, but solutions abound. The two-rock technique: sit on one rock, prop your feet on another to avoid splashing and stay clean. For stand-up urination, female urinary devices (FUDs) like the Freshette or GoGirl enable women to pee standing up with confidence. Menstruation management requires packing out all supplies and waste in discreet containers.

> "With a little practice we, too, can cultivate the ultimate in blasé, while being proud of a challenge faced and won, a job well done."

**Quick Take**: Women can achieve the same outdoor freedom and dignity as men with the right techniques and equipment.

### When You Can't Dig: Pack It Out
In fragile environments, high-use areas, or frozen ground, packing out waste becomes necessary. Modern portable toilet systems (Wag Bags, RESTOP, Biffy Bags) use chemical treatments to neutralize waste and odors. For ocean travel, never dispose of waste within 3 miles of shore. In sensitive alpine or desert environments, consider packing everything out, including urine.

> "The easier we make it for ourselves, the harder it will be on Mother Earth."

**Quick Take**: Sometimes the most environmentally responsible choice is carrying your waste with you.

### Preventing Trekker's Trots: Health and Hygiene
Hand washing is your primary defense against gastrointestinal illness in the backcountry. Use biodegradable soap and water, or the "refried bean wash" system (a can with holes for water flow). Treat all water, watch what you eat, and pack rehydration supplies. If struck by illness, maintain hydration and practice meticulous hygiene to prevent spreading to others.

> "Because enteric pathogens (the intestinal bad guys) are transmitted by forms of fecal-oral contact, logically, then, the first step toward prevention is to ritualize hand washing."

**Quick Take**: Good hygiene isn't just about comfort—it's about preventing serious illness in remote environments.

## Essential Outdoor Toileting Kit

### Core Equipment
- **Sturdy trowel** (U-DIG-IT recommended) - $18-20
- **Toilet paper** (use sparingly, pack out all used paper)
- **Hand sanitizer/biodegradable soap**
- **Wet wipes or bidet bottle**
- **Small bags for waste disposal** (double-bagged for security)

### For Women
- **Female urinary device (FUD)** - Freshette, GoGirl, or Shewee
- **Menstrual supplies** with discreet storage container
- **Pee rag** (bandana for drying, attach to pack)
- **Appropriate clothing** with easy access

### For Group Leaders
- **Portable toilet system** for sensitive areas
- **Hand washing station** setup
- **Educational materials** about Leave No Trace principles
- **Waste disposal containers**

## Action Items

### Before Your Trip
1. **Research your destination's regulations** - Some areas require packing out all waste
2. **Practice at home** - Test your equipment and techniques before heading out
3. **Pack a complete toileting kit** - Include backups for essential items
4. **Share plans with your group** - Ensure everyone understands proper procedures

### During Your Trip
1. **Always dig 6-8 inch cat holes** when possible, 200 feet from water
2. **Pack out all toilet paper and hygiene products** - never bury or burn them
3. **Treat all water sources** - no exceptions, no matter how pristine they appear
4. **Practice meticulous hand washing** - Use soap and water whenever possible

### Special Situations
1. **Winter/frozen ground** - Pack out waste or use specialized containers
2. **High-altitude environments** - Decomposition is extremely slow above treeline
3. **Ocean travel** - Use designated waste disposal systems, never dump within 3 miles of shore
4. **Group expeditions** - Consider establishing group latrines or portable toilet systems

## Conclusion
"How to Shit in the Woods" is far more than a bathroom manual—it's a passionate environmental manifesto disguised as a practical guide. Meyer transforms a taboo subject into essential environmental education, using humor and frankness to deliver crucial lessons about personal responsibility and ecological impact.

This book is absolutely worth your time if you spend any time in nature, from local parks to remote wilderness. The biggest reason to read it is that it will change how you think about your environmental footprint. Meyer's core message is powerful: how we handle our most basic biological needs reveals our relationship to the natural world. By learning to shit properly in the woods, we become better environmental citizens everywhere.

In an era of increasing outdoor recreation and decreasing wilderness areas, this knowledge isn't just useful—it's essential for preserving the wild places we love. Meyer gives us the tools to be part of the solution rather than part of the problem, one properly dug cat hole at a time.
