# Money and Power: How Goldman Sachs Came to Rule the World
**Author**: William D. Cohan | **Year**: 2011 | **Category**: Business & Finance

## Summary
William D. Cohan's "Money and Power" is a definitive investigation into how Goldman Sachs evolved from a modest commercial paper business founded in 1869 to become the most powerful and influential investment bank on Wall Street. Through meticulous research and insider interviews, Cohan reveals the firm's 140-year journey marked by strategic brilliance, controversial decisions, and an unyielding pursuit of profit and influence.

The book chronicles the firm's transformation through the leadership of powerful figures like Sidney Weinberg, the "political banker" who cultivated unprecedented government connections; Gus Levy, the trading genius who revolutionized arbitrage; and Jon Corzine, who pushed the firm into public markets. Cohan exposes the internal power struggles, the firm's controversial role in major financial crises, and the culture that enabled Goldman to consistently outperform rivals while walking ethical gray lines.

**Perfect for**: Business leaders, finance professionals, and anyone interested in understanding how power and money truly work on Wall Street. Readers will gain insights into organizational culture, strategic thinking, and the complex relationship between finance and government that shapes modern capitalism.

## Core Insights

### From Humble Beginnings to Wall Street Dominance
Marcus Goldman started his business in 1869 by buying and selling promissory notes from New York merchants, essentially creating the market for commercial paper in America. His son-in-law Samuel Sachs joined the firm in 1882, and together they built Goldman Sachs into one of New York's leading investment banking houses. The early success was built on trust, relationships, and understanding merchants' financing needs better than anyone else.

The firm's culture was always different—focused on collaboration rather than cutthroat competition, with an emphasis on long-term client relationships over short-term profits. This approach, combined with a willingness to innovate and take calculated risks, set the foundation for future growth and enabled Goldman to weather financial panics that destroyed lesser firms.

> "Their MO is that we made as little money as possible, anything that makes it look like they didn't make money or they lost money is good for them, right? Because they don't want to be seen as benefiting during the crisis."

**Quick Take**: Goldman's early success demonstrates how building authentic relationships and delivering consistent value can create sustainable competitive advantages, even in the most competitive markets.

### The Weinberg Era: Political Banking Masterclass
Sidney Weinberg transformed Goldman Sachs through his unprecedented access to political power. Starting as an assistant janitor, Weinberg rose to become senior partner and created a new model of investment banking based on government connections rather than just corporate relationships. He advised multiple presidents, organized the Business Advisory Council to influence policy, and positioned Goldman as the go-to bank for major corporate deals during the post-war boom.

Weinberg's genius was understanding that in modern capitalism, political access could be as valuable as financial acumen. He cultivated relationships with regulators, politicians, and business leaders, creating a network that gave Goldman insider knowledge and preferential treatment in major transactions. This political banking model became the template for how modern Wall Street operates.

> "He yet presents a unity of race continuity which no other people has achieved. Living under legal disabilities in almost every land, he has become the power behind many a throne."

**Quick Take**: Building strategic relationships and understanding the political dimensions of business can provide insights and advantages that purely financial analysis cannot match.

### The Trading Revolution: Gus Levy and Arbitrage
Gus Levy revolutionized Goldman Sachs by transforming it from a relationship-based investment bank into a trading powerhouse. His mastery of risk arbitrage—betting on merger announcements and corporate restructurings—generated enormous profits and changed the firm's culture from staid banking to aggressive trading. Levy's philosophy was simple: "If he guesses right he can make a great deal of money. If he guesses wrong he doesn't stay in the arbitrage business very long."

Levy built a team of brilliant traders who developed sophisticated mathematical models for pricing risk and finding market inefficiencies. This trading culture brought Goldman huge profits but also created tensions with the traditional investment banking side of the business. The internal power struggles between traders and bankers mirrored the broader transformation of Wall Street from relationship-based capitalism to quantitative, data-driven finance.

> "Levy became a Goldman partner on January 1, 1946. Al Feld, who started working at Goldman as an office boy in July 1933, recalled how skillful Levy was at making money from trading railroad and utility bonds. 'Gus was very smart, and an innovator,' he told Ellis."

**Quick Take**: Understanding and exploiting market inefficiencies through superior analytics and risk management can generate outsized returns, but requires balancing short-term trading gains with long-term relationship building.

### Going Public: The Corzine Revolution
Jon Corzine's leadership marked the dramatic transformation of Goldman Sachs from a private partnership to a public company. This controversial decision in 1999 changed everything—making partners overnight millionaires but also exposing the firm to quarterly earnings pressure and public scrutiny. Corzine pushed the firm aggressively into new businesses including proprietary trading, merchant banking, and global expansion.

The IPO created immense wealth but also destroyed the partnership culture that had made Goldman unique. Partners were no longer owners but employees, and the long-term perspective that had characterized the firm gave way to quarterly earnings thinking. Many partners left immediately after the IPO, taking their knowledge and relationships with them. The transformation reflected the broader changes in American finance from relationship-based capitalism to market-driven capitalism.

> "The decision to keep him would be a fateful one for Goldman Sachs."

**Quick Take**: Going public can generate enormous wealth but may fundamentally change organizational culture and strategic decision-making in ways that undermine long-term competitive advantages.

### The 2008 Crisis: Controversial Survival Strategy
Goldman Sachs' navigation of the 2008 financial crisis represents both its greatest triumph and most controversial moment. While other banks collapsed or required bailouts, Goldman survived by betting against the same mortgage securities it was selling to clients. The firm's traders, led by Dan Sparks, Josh Birnbaum, and Michael Swenson, made $4 billion in 2007 by shorting the housing market while the firm continued to underwrite and sell mortgage-backed securities to investors.

This "big short" strategy generated enormous profits but also led to accusations that Goldman had bet against its own clients. The firm's defense was that they were simply managing risk and responding to market conditions, but the optics were terrible. The crisis revealed the fundamental conflict of interest in Goldman's business model and led to intense regulatory scrutiny and public criticism.

> "In July 2007, David Viniar, Goldman's longtime chief financial officer, referred to this proprietary bet as 'the big short' in an e-mail he wrote to Blankfein and others."

**Quick Take**: Managing risk intelligently can preserve capital during market crises, but strategies that profit from clients' losses create ethical and reputational risks that may outweigh financial gains.
