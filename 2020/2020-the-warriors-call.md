# The Warrior's Call
**Authors**: Richard Fierce | **Year**: 2020

## Summary
In the third installment of the Dragon Riders of Osnen series, dragon rider Eldwin Baines and his dragon Sion find themselves caught in a desperate war against the mysterious False King who threatens the realm. When a deadly creature begins hunting dragons at night, decimating their ranks, Eldwin and fellow rider Maren must journey to the Conclave to seek helpâ€”only to discover the magical organization has been brutally attacked. Their quest leads them through dangerous territories, griffon attacks, and ultimately to a final battle where alliances are forged and sacrifices are made.

This epic fantasy adventure combines dragon bonding magic, political intrigue, and the personal journey of a young rider coming to terms with his abilities and destiny. Perfect for fans of dragon-rider fantasy who want action-packed storytelling with emotional depth and well-developed dragon-human relationships.

## Core Insights

### The Power of the Dragon-Rider Bond
The telepathic connection between dragons and their riders serves as both a strength and vulnerability throughout the story. Eldwin and Sion's bond deepens as they face increasingly dangerous challenges together, demonstrating how trust and communication can overcome seemingly impossible odds. The bond provides tactical advantages in battle while also creating emotional resonance that drives character development.

> "I am with you, Sion said. If you encounter danger inside, I will know."
**Quick Take**: True partnership requires mutual trust and the ability to rely on each other's strengths.

### Sacrifice and Leadership
The novel explores the weight of leadership through characters like Anesko and Hrodin, who must make difficult decisions that cost lives to save the greater good. Maren's near-death experience after unleashing massive magical power highlights how true leadership often requires personal sacrifice.

> "When someone goes too far with magic, it can cause problems. Those problems can be temporary, or they can be permanent. In rare cases, it can mean death."
**Quick Take**: Leadership demands courage to make hard choices, even when they come at great personal cost.

### Cultural Understanding and Alliance
The journey to the Terranese people emphasizes how overcoming prejudice and building bridges between cultures can create powerful alliances. Katori and her warriors initially seem threatening but prove to be crucial allies in the fight against darkness.

> "I'm Eldwin Baines," I said as I climbed down off Sion's back. "And this is Sion."
**Quick Take**: True strength often comes from embracing diversity and finding common ground with unlikely allies.

### The Nature of Power and Responsibility
Eldwin's struggle with his perceived deficiencies and his growing magical abilities explores the theme that true power comes not from strength alone, but from wisdom in how that power is used. The contrast between the False King's tyrannical rule and Eldwin's reluctant heroism highlights different approaches to power.

> "As the master of the school, I have pardoned your previous expulsion and runaway," Anesko had told me. "You are more than welcome to stay here and continue to study, or you can go elsewhere if you wish."
**Quick Take**: With great power comes the responsibility to choose your own path and define your own worth.

## Key Characters
- **Eldwin Baines**: The protagonist, a young dragon rider developing his bond with Sion while grappling with his destiny and a physical disability
- **Sion**: Eldwin's dragon, whose telepathic bond and growing power provide both companionship and formidable combat abilities
- **Maren Toft**: A skilled dragon rider and princess of Osnen, whose magical abilities and complex relationship with Eldwin drive much of the emotional arc
- **Katori**: Leader of the Terranese warriors, initially an adversary who becomes a crucial ally in the final battle

## Conclusion
The Warrior's Call delivers a satisfying blend of epic fantasy action, emotional character development, and intricate world-building. While it stands as the third book in a series, new readers can still appreciate the character dynamics and battle sequences, though starting with earlier books would provide richer context. The dragon-rider bond remains compelling, and the themes of leadership, sacrifice, and cultural understanding resonate well beyond the fantasy genre. The book sets up intriguing possibilities for future installments while providing a complete narrative arc with genuine emotional stakes. This is definitely worth your time if you enjoy dragon-focused fantasy with strong character relationships and well-choreographed battle scenes.
