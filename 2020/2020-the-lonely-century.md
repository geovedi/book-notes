# The Lonely Century
**Authors**: Noreena Hertz | **Year**: 2020
**Category**: Psychology & Mental Health

## Summary
In "The Lonely Century," economist Noreena Hertz reveals how we've become increasingly isolated in an age of hyper-connectivity, creating a global loneliness crisis that predates COVID-19 but has been dramatically accelerated by it. Hertz argues that loneliness goes beyond merely lacking friends—it encompasses political disconnection, economic exclusion, and community alienation.

The book demonstrates that loneliness is not just a personal failing but a structural crisis driven by neoliberal economics, technological disruption, urbanization, and the erosion of community infrastructure. Hertz presents a compelling case that loneliness makes us physically ill (equivalent to smoking 15 cigarettes daily), economically burdened (costing billions in healthcare), and politically vulnerable to extremism and populism.

This essential read is perfect for anyone seeking to understand the underlying forces behind our fragmented society, from policymakers and business leaders to individuals feeling the strain of modern disconnection. Readers will gain a comprehensive framework for understanding how we arrived at this crisis and practical guidance for rebuilding authentic human connections.

## Core Insights

### Loneliness is a Health Crisis Worse Than Smoking
Loneliness triggers the same physiological stress response as feeling under attack, flooding our bodies with harmful hormones. Research shows loneliness is statistically equivalent to smoking fifteen cigarettes a day—more harmful than obesity, lack of exercise, or alcoholism. This isn't just mental health; it's physical health deterioration with measurable consequences for healthcare systems and workplace productivity.

> "Statistically, loneliness is equivalent to smoking fifteen cigarettes a day."
**Quick Take**: Your social connections are as vital to your health as diet and exercise—neglect them at your physical peril.

### Neoliberal Economics Engineered Our Isolation
The ideology of hyper-individualism, championed since the 1980s, fundamentally reshaped how we see each other and our obligations to one another. By prioritizing self-interest over community and competition over collaboration, neoliberalism made us view ourselves as consumers rather than citizens, competitors rather than collaborators. This economic system created vast inequality while dismantling the social safety nets and community bonds that traditionally protected against isolation.

> "Under neoliberalism we were reduced to homo economicus, rational humans consumed only by our own self-interest."
**Quick Take**: When society tells you to look out only for yourself, everyone ends up alone.

### Technology Connected Us Digitally but Divided Us Physically
Social media and smartphones haven't solved loneliness—they've transformed it. These platforms steal attention from people physically present, fuel tribal anger, reward performative rather than authentic connection, and erode our ability to communicate effectively. Even during COVID lockdowns, while some beneficial connections emerged, hate speech and conspiracy theories proliferated, and relationship counselors reported increased loneliness as partners became more consumed by their devices.

**Quick Take**: Digital connection is no substitute for physical presence—put down your phone when you're with people.

### Community Infrastructure Has Been Systematically Destroyed
The physical spaces where people traditionally gathered—libraries, community centers, public parks, youth clubs, third places like cafes and barbershops—have been neglected or actively destroyed, particularly after the 2008 financial crisis when austerity measures took a "sledgehammer" to these essential social binding spaces. Without these places to practice democracy, civility, and peaceful coexistence with diverse others, we inevitably pull further apart.

**Quick Take**: Support and use local community spaces—they're essential training grounds for a connected society.

### Loneliness Fuels Political Extremism and Populism
Feeling disconnected, unheard, and abandoned by mainstream institutions makes people vulnerable to populist leaders who offer belonging and recognition. Trump and similar figures succeeded by making supporters feel they "mattered" in ways traditional politicians didn't, satisfying our evolutionary need to be part of something greater than ourselves. The loneliness crisis is therefore not just personal but political, with consequences for democratic stability.

> "Loneliness and right-wing populism are, as we will see, close bedfellows."
**Quick Take**: Political division is often loneliness seeking community—we must offer inclusive belonging before exclusive ideologies do.

## Action Items
- **Assess your connection deficit**: Use the expanded definition of loneliness—consider not just friendships but your connection to community, work, politics, and society
- **Support community spaces**: Patronize and advocate for libraries, community centers, parks, and local gathering places
- **Practice presence**: Put away your phone during meals and conversations, prioritize face-to-face interactions
- **Build inclusive communities**: Reach across differences, practice civil disagreement, create spaces where diverse people feel they belong
- **Demand better policies**: Support politicians and policies that address structural causes of loneliness rather than blaming individuals

## Questions to Consider
- How has neoliberal individualism shaped your expectations about relationships and community?
- What community spaces in your area have been lost or neglected, and how might they be revived?
- In what ways does your technology use enhance or diminish your real-world connections?
- How can we balance individual freedom with collective responsibility and care?
- What small changes could you make to combat loneliness in your immediate circles and community?

## Conclusion
"The Lonely Century" is essential reading for understanding one of the defining crises of our time. Hertz masterfully connects personal loneliness to political and economic structures, showing how our isolation is not merely individual failure but the predictable result of specific policy choices and economic systems.

The book's greatest strength is its expansive definition of loneliness as not just lacking friends but feeling disconnected from work, politics, and community. This broader framework reveals how loneliness manifests in health crises, economic costs, and political vulnerability to extremism.

While the picture Hertz paints is alarming, the book offers hope through practical solutions and reminds us that we "do society too"—we have agency to rebuild connections. This is not just a diagnosis but a call to action for anyone concerned about the fragmentation of modern life.

Read this book if you want to understand the hidden forces behind our divided society and discover how we might build a more connected, compassionate future. The biggest reason to read it? It provides the language and framework to recognize and combat the loneliness that affects so many, yet is rarely discussed so comprehensively.
