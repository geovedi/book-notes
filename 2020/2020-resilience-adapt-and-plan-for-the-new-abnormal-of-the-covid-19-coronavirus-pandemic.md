# Resilience: Adapt and Plan for the New Abnormal of the COVID-19 Coronavirus Pandemic
**Author**: Gleb Tsipursky | **Year**: 2020
**Category**: Self-Help & Personal Development

## Summary
This book provides a research-based guide to navigating the COVID-19 pandemic by understanding why we make poor decisions in crises and implementing strategies to adapt effectively. Tsipursky, a cognitive neuroscientist and behavioral economist, explains how cognitive biases led to disastrous pandemic responses and provides frameworks for individuals and organizations to survive and thrive in the "new abnormal" of prolonged disruption.

The book is essential reading for leaders, professionals, and individuals seeking practical, science-backed strategies for decision-making during prolonged crises. Readers will gain actionable frameworks for adapting their personal lives, careers, and organizations to succeed in an environment of ongoing uncertainty and disruption.

## Core Insights

### Why We Fail: The Cognitive Biases That Sabotage Pandemic Response

Our brains are wired for immediate threats, not slow-moving disasters like pandemics. The "normalcy bias" makes us underestimate dangers and assume the future will resemble the past. This explains why leaders initially dismissed COVID-19 as "just the flu" and failed to prepare for long-term disruption.

> "The normalcy bias refers to the fact that our autopilot system drives us to feel that the future, at least in the short and medium term of the next couple of years, will function in roughly the same way as the past: normally."

**Quick Take**: Stop assuming things will return to "normal" quickly. The pandemic is a slow-moving train wreck that requires fundamental adaptation, not short-term fixes.

Other dangerous biases include:
- **Attentional Bias**: We focus on emotionally engaging information while ignoring critical but boring details (like early warnings from Wuhan)
- **Anchoring**: We get stuck on initial information and fail to update beliefs based on new evidence
- **Hyperbolic Discounting**: We sacrifice long-term benefits for short-term comfort
- **Planning Fallacy**: We're overly optimistic about our plans working out

### Personal Adaptation Framework: The Six Human Needs

To thrive in the pandemic environment, focus on meeting six fundamental needs through new methods that rely less on in-person interaction and more on virtual/home-based solutions:

**Safety**: Stock two months of essential supplies (not two weeks as CDC suggested) and establish mental health routines including indoor exercise and limited news exposure. Prepare for the possibility that household members may need to quarantine for extended periods.

**Connection**: Transform how you maintain relationships. For household members, establish healthy boundaries for 24/7 proximity. For others, develop meaningful virtual interactions. The Chinese community in Prato, Italy demonstrated this by voluntarily lockdown in late January—three weeks before Italy's first recorded case.

> "Remember: don't kill Grandma. And don't let other members of your household kill Grandma."

**Quick Take**: Treat every household member as a potential carrier and protect vulnerable family members with serious precautions.

**Self-Esteem**: Maintain confidence by adapting your career to remote work or transitioning to pandemic-resilient industries. Develop new skills and engage in productive home activities that create a sense of mastery over your environment.

**Exploration**: Pursue learning and growth through virtual means—take online courses, explore different cultures through internet connections, or engage in virtual volunteering that challenges you.

**Love**: Make positive impacts on others' lives through virtual channels. Organize virtual social events, send care packages, or provide companionship to isolated elderly adults through phone calls and video chats.

**Purpose**: Identify how you can contribute to something bigger than yourself during the crisis. Whether raising children, improving your community, or continuing your life's work through virtual means, maintain focus on meaningful service.

### Organizational Transformation: The Virtual-First Business Model

Companies must fundamentally transform their business models, not just implement temporary emergency measures. The traditional office-based approach is obsolete for the next several years.

**Internal Transformation Priority Areas**:

1. **Make Virtual Work Default**: Sell or end office leases when possible. Even during looser restrictions, avoid in-person meetings since asymptomatic carriers can infect entire teams.

2. **Address Motivation Challenges**: Unlike gradual remote work transitions, the abrupt pandemic shift forced many employees who are not self-motivated or who are extroverted into home environments. Provide resources, training, and support to help them set up productive workspaces.

3. **Combat Communication Breakdown**: Text-based communication loses crucial nonverbal cues. Train employees in virtual communication techniques and increase use of video/voice calls to reduce misunderstandings.

4. **Build Virtual Trust**: Replace spontaneous "watercooler" conversations with structured virtual social interactions. One successful approach is a daily "Morning Updates" channel where team members share personal and professional updates.

**Quick Take**: Virtual teams that never met in person perform worse than teams that transitioned from in-office to virtual. Invest heavily in relationship-building activities.

**External Business Model Adaptation**:

- Transform service delivery to virtual platforms
- Develop virtual relationship cultivation strategies
- Plan for supply chain disruptions and stakeholder challenges
- Anticipate fundamental shifts in customer behavior and expectations
- Prepare for "unknown unknowns" and black swan events

### Strategic Planning: The "Defend Your Future" Technique

Traditional planning fails in crisis conditions. Use this structured approach:

**Step 1: Scenario Planning**
- **Best Case**: Early 2022 vaccine availability (optimistic)
- **Moderate Case**: 2024-2025 for widespread vaccine access (realistic)
- **Worst Case**: No effective vaccine ever developed (possible)

**Step 2: Problem-Solution-Resource Analysis**
For each scenario, identify potential problems and opportunities, then determine resources needed to address them. Focus on threats you can control rather than those you cannot.

**Step 3: Cognitive Bias Check**
Audit your plan for common biases:
- Normalcy bias: Have you truly accepted long-term disruption?
- Anchoring: Are you stuck on pre-pandemic assumptions?
- Hyperbolic discounting: Are you sacrificing long-term stability for short-term comfort?
- Planning fallacy: Are you being overconfident about everything working according to plan?
- Confirmation bias: Are you seeking information that supports your existing beliefs?

**Step 4: Prepare for Unknown Unknowns**
- Identify potential "gray swans" (known but unlikely threats)
- Double resources allocated for these threats
- Prepare for true "black swans" (completely unpredictable events)

### Decision-Making Framework: 8 Steps for Critical Choices

When facing major decisions during the pandemic, use this systematic approach:

1. **Recognize Decision Need**: Don't let gut feelings or normalcy bias prevent necessary decisions
2. **Gather Diverse Perspectives**: Especially seek opinions that disagree with your initial assessment
3. **Define Clear Goals**: Address underlying issues, not just symptoms
4. **Establish Decision Criteria**: Create evaluation standards before considering options
5. **Generate Multiple Options**: Minimum five viable choices (brainstorm without judgment)
6. **Evaluate Objectively**: Separate options from who proposed them; avoid favoring initial preferences
7. **Plan Implementation**: Imagine both complete failure and success, then prepare for each scenario
8. **Monitor and Adapt**: Set clear success metrics and revise as needed

## Action Items

### Immediate Actions (Next 30 Days)
- Assess your household's two-month emergency supplies (food, medications, cleaning supplies)
- Identify vulnerable family members and establish protection protocols
- Audit your career for pandemic resilience and plan transitions if needed
- Begin virtual relationship cultivation with key personal and professional contacts

### Medium-term Adaptations (3-6 Months)
- Transform at least one aspect of your work to virtual-first approach
- Develop new skills suitable for remote work environments
- Create structured virtual social routines to replace in-person interactions
- Establish mental health practices for prolonged stress and isolation

### Strategic Planning (6+ Months)
- Use the "Defend Your Future" framework for personal and professional planning
- Prepare for multiple years of disruption waves (restrictions, easing, restrictions again)
- Develop contingency plans for various vaccine timeline scenarios
- Build flexibility into all major life and business decisions

## Questions to Consider

- What aspects of "normal" pre-pandemic life were actually weaknesses that the crisis has exposed?
- How might your personal and professional priorities change during several years of disruption?
- What skills and relationships will become most valuable in a virtual-first world?
- If the pandemic permanently changed certain aspects of society, what opportunities would this create?
- How can you use this disruption period as a catalyst for positive personal transformation?

## Conclusion

This book is worth your time if you're struggling with decision-making during the pandemic or seeking a systematic approach to adapting your personal life or organization to prolonged disruption. Tsipursky provides a rare combination of cognitive science expertise and practical crisis management experience, offering frameworks that are immediately applicable.

The biggest reason to read this book is its fundamental reorientation from short-term emergency thinking to long-term adaptation strategy. Unlike pandemic advice that focuses on immediate survival, Tsipursky prepares readers for years of disruption, providing tools that will remain valuable beyond COVID-19 for any slow-moving crisis.

While some specific recommendations may become dated as pandemic conditions evolve, the underlying frameworks for understanding cognitive biases and making decisions under uncertainty have enduring value. The book serves as both a practical guide for current challenges and a masterclass in evidence-based decision-making during turbulence.
