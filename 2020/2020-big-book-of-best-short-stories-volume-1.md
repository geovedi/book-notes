# 2020 Big Book of Best Short Stories Volume 1
**Authors**: Washington Irving, Oscar Wilde, Bram Stoker, H.G. Wells, Arthur Conan Doyle, Rudyard Kipling, H.P. Lovecraft, and others | **Year**: 2020
**Category**: Fiction & Literature

## Summary
This comprehensive anthology brings together 273 of the most influential short stories spanning from early American Gothic literature through the Victorian era and into early modern science fiction and horror. The collection is organized chronologically by author, showcasing the evolution of the short story form across different literary movements and cultural contexts. Readers will journey through Washington Irving's early American tales, Oscar Wilde's social commentary and fairy tales, Bram Stoker's gothic horror, H.G. Wells' scientific imaginings, Arthur Conan Doyle's detective mysteries, and the psychological horror of H.P. Lovecraft.

This anthology offers an unparalleled survey of short fiction's golden age, making it ideal for literature students, writers seeking inspiration, or readers who want to understand how the short story evolved from a simple entertainment form into a sophisticated literary vehicle capable of exploring complex themes and experimental narrative techniques.

## Core Insights

### The Birth of American Literature: Washington Irving's Legacy
Washington Irving pioneered American fiction with tales that blended European Gothic traditions with distinctly American settings and sensibilities. "The Legend of Sleepy Hollow" and "Rip Van Winkle" established archetypes that would define American storytelling for generations - the superstitious schoolmaster, the haunted landscape, the ordinary man caught in extraordinary circumstances.

> "However wide awake they may have been before they entered that sleepy region, they are sure, in a little time, to inhale the witching influence of the air, and begin to grow imaginative, to dream dreams, and see apparitions."

**Quick Take**: Irving created the first truly American literary voice by transforming European Gothic elements into stories about the emerging American frontier experience.

### Victorian Social Commentary Through Fantasy: Oscar Wilde's Dual Vision
Oscar Wilde mastered the art of using fantasy and fairy tales to critique Victorian society's hypocrisies and moral contradictions. Stories like "The Happy Prince" and "The Model Millionaire" blend beautiful prose with sharp social criticism, revealing Wilde's belief that art should both entertain and enlighten.

> "Why can't you be like the Happy Prince?" asked a sensible mother of her little boy who was crying for the moon. "The Happy Prince never dreams of crying for anything."

**Quick Take**: Wilde demonstrated that fantasy could be a powerful vehicle for social critique, using imagined worlds to comment on real-world injustice and materialism.

### The Scientific Imagination: H.G. Wells and the Birth of Science Fiction
Wells transformed contemporary scientific discoveries into compelling narratives that explored humanity's future possibilities and perils. Stories like "The Time Machine" established science fiction as a serious literary genre capable of examining social issues through speculative scenarios.

> He had the time machine in his hands, and he felt the thrill of discovery, the sense that he was standing on the threshold of a new world.

**Quick Take**: Wells pioneered the idea that science fiction could serve as literature's most effective tool for examining social evolution and humanity's ultimate destiny.

### Detective Fiction's Foundation: Sherlock Holmes and Rational Deduction
Arthur Conan Doyle created the first truly systematic detective, establishing archetypes and narrative techniques that would dominate mystery fiction for a century. The Holmes stories championed rationality and scientific method at a time when spiritualism and superstition were widespread.

> "When you have eliminated the impossible, whatever remains, however improbable, must be the truth."

**Quick Take**: Doyle established the detective story as a battle between rationality and chaos, making scientific thinking heroic and accessible to popular audiences.

## Action Items

- **Start with Irving**: Begin with Washington Irving's stories to understand the foundations of American literature and how European traditions were adapted to New World settings
- **Compare Gothic traditions**: Read Irving's "Sleepy Hollow" alongside Stoker's horror stories to see how American and British Gothic evolved differently
- **Track genre evolution**: Read the stories chronologically to witness how genres like science fiction and detective fiction emerged and developed
- **Study social commentary**: Pay attention to how Wilde and other Victorian writers used fantasy and satire to criticize contemporary society
- **Analyze narrative techniques**: Notice how each author developed distinctive approaches to creating suspense, character development, and thematic depth

## Conclusion

This anthology is worth your time because it offers a comprehensive education in the development of modern literature in a single volume. The biggest reason to read it is understanding how many of our contemporary storytelling conventions—whether in horror movies, science fiction novels, or detective dramas—can be traced directly back to these pioneering authors.

The collection's strength lies in its breadth and organization. By reading chronologically, you witness the birth of literary genres and see how authors influenced each other across time and geography. You'll recognize the DNA of modern entertainment in these classic tales, from the supernatural horror that fuels today's zombie apocalypse stories to the deductive reasoning that powers every police procedural.

While 273 stories might seem overwhelming, the anthology rewards both selective reading and comprehensive study. Literature lovers will appreciate discovering lesser-known works by famous authors, while casual readers can enjoy revisiting beloved classics. The book serves equally well as reference material, inspiration for writers, or simply an entertaining collection of masterfully crafted tales that continue to resonate with modern readers.

For anyone interested in understanding how short fiction became one of literature's most versatile and powerful forms, or for readers who simply want to experience some of the greatest stories ever told, this collection provides an essential and endlessly rewarding journey through literary history.
