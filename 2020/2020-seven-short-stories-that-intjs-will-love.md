# Seven Short Stories That INTJs Will Love
**Authors**: Nathaniel Hawthorne, O. Henry, Marcus Aurelius, Arthur Conan Doyle, Honoré de Balzac, Herman Melville, Oscar Wilde | **Year**: 2020

## Summary
This curated collection presents seven classic short stories specifically chosen to resonate with the INTJ personality type—strategic, independent thinkers who value intellectual depth, psychological insight, and philosophical exploration. The anthology spans different eras and genres, from Gothic romance and Stoic philosophy to detective fiction and social satire, each offering complex characters, moral ambiguity, and intellectual puzzles that appeal to the INTJ's analytical mind and love for profound ideas.

INTJs and other intellectually curious readers will discover carefully selected narratives that celebrate rational thinking, individualism, and the complexity of human nature. Each story provides a unique lens on themes of identity, logic versus emotion, and the consequences of living according to one's principles.

## Fast-Track Story Guide

### The Haunted Mind by Nathaniel Hawthorne
A haunting psychological exploration of consciousness during the liminal state between sleep and wakefulness. The narrator experiences vivid visions of past sorrows, disappointments, and regrets that manifest as ghostly figures surrounding his bed. This introspective journey through the "tomb and dungeon" of the human psyche ultimately leads to a peaceful resolution as the mind transitions from dark contemplation to serene acceptance, finding comfort in imagined companionship before final surrender to sleep.

> "In the depths of every heart there is a tomb and a dungeon, though the lights, the music and revelry, above may cause us to forget their existence and the buried ones or prisoners whom they hide."

**Key Theme**: The psychological landscape of human consciousness and our confrontation with inner darkness

### The Cactus by O. Henry
Trysdale, proud and conceited, attends his former beloved's wedding reception, consumed by regret and confusion over why she rejected him. Through flashbacks, we learn he allowed her to believe he spoke Spanish fluently to appear more impressive. The devastating irony reveals itself when his friend identifies the cactus she sent him as "Ventomarme"—Spanish for "Come and take me"—her misunderstood invitation to love that he never recognized due to his linguistic pretense.

> "How glad, how shy, how tremulous she was! How she fluttered like a snared bird when he laid his mightiness at her feet!"

**Key Theme**: The destructive nature of pride and ego in relationships, with a classic O. Henry ironic twist

### Meditations: Book Seven by Marcus Aurelius
A collection of Stoic philosophical reflections offering timeless wisdom on maintaining inner tranquility amid external chaos. Marcus explores the nature of badness, the importance of rational thinking, and the interconnectedness of all things. He advocates for emotional resilience, focusing on what we can control (our responses), and understanding our role within the larger cosmic order. These writings provide practical guidance for living virtuously and maintaining mental clarity regardless of circumstances.

> "The ruling faculty does not disturb itself; I mean, does not frighten itself or cause itself pain."

**Key Theme**: Rational self-mastery and the Stoic approach to life's challenges through reason and virtue

### A Scandal in Bohemia by Arthur Conan Doyle
Sherlock Holmes faces his most brilliant adversary—Irene Adler, the only woman who ever outwitted him. When the King of Bohemia hires Holmes to retrieve a compromising photograph from Adler, Holmes employs his legendary deductive skills to track her down. However, Adler anticipates his every move and ultimately escapes, keeping the photograph as insurance while leaving Holmes with lasting admiration for her intelligence and resourcefulness.

> "To a great mind, nothing is little."

**Key Theme**: Intellectual competition and the respect between equals of extraordinary capability

### The Atheist's Mass by Honoré de Balzac
Desplein, a famous atheist surgeon, is secretly observed attending Mass regularly, puzzling his student Bianchon. The mystery unfolds when Desplein reveals his connection to a bread-seller who once saved him from starvation by sharing her meager resources. Despite his atheism, Desplein honors her faith by performing Masses for her soul after her death—a complex moral gesture that transcends religious doctrine while honoring human compassion.

> "A man must have loved his mother very much to say a mass for her!"

**Key Theme**: The complexity of human motivations and the power of gratitude across philosophical divides

### Bartleby, the Scrivener by Herman Melville
A Wall Street lawyer hires Bartleby as a copyist, only to have the passive, enigmatic employee gradually refuse all work with the phrase "I would prefer not to." The lawyer's attempts to reason with, accommodate, and eventually remove Bartleby prove futile, leading to tragic consequences. This haunting exploration of passive resistance and individual conscience critiques the dehumanizing nature of commerce and society's inability to accommodate those who refuse to conform.

> "I would prefer not to."

**Key Theme**: Individual conscience versus societal expectations and the limits of rational accommodation

### The Canterville Ghost by Oscar Wilde
When pragmatic American Minister Hiram Otis buys haunted Canterville Chase, he refuses to believe in the resident ghost, Sir Simon. The Otis family treats supernatural occurrences with practical solutions—offering the ghost oil for his rusty chains and medicine for his mysterious ailments. Eventually, young Virginia Otis befriends the ghost, helps him find peace through prayer and compassion, and witnesses his soul's ascent to heaven, bridging American pragmatism with British Gothic tradition.

> "You Americans are always so practical. I have come to ask for your help."

**Key Theme**: The clash between modern pragmatism and tradition, resolved through empathy and understanding
