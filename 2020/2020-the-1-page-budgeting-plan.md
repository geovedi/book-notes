# The 1-Page Budgeting Plan
**Authors**: Zoe McKey | **Year**: 2020

## Summary
"The 1-Page Budgeting Plan" presents a straightforward approach to financial freedom through simplified budgeting, goal setting, and smart money management. The book transforms complex personal finance concepts into an accessible, single-page system that anyone can implement. McKey argues that financial success comes not from making more money, but from learning to manage what you already have effectively.

This guide is perfect for anyone feeling overwhelmed by traditional budgeting methods, those living paycheck-to-paycheck, or anyone who wants to build wealth without becoming a "finance geek." Readers will learn to set clear financial goals, eliminate debt, build emergency funds, and create a sustainable wealth-building system that fits on a single page.

## Core Insights

### The GPS Destination Principle
Financial success requires both clear goals (your destination) and a solid budgeting system (your GPS). Without knowing where you want to go financially, even the best budgeting tools won't help you reach your dreams. Most people lack both a clear financial destination and a reliable system to get there.

> "Imagine having an awesome GPS. It finds you the best routes every time, calculates every aspect of your trip, and gives you astonishingly accurate data on your ETA, obstacles, checkpoints, police—everything. But you don't know the destination you want to go to. You can have the best GPS, but without a clear destination in mind, it is useless."

**Quick Take**: Define your 1-year, 5-year, 10-year, and 20-year financial goals before creating any budget.

### Money as a Shape-Shifter
Money isn't inherently good or evil—it's a neutral tool that takes on the meaning we give it. Understanding this helps remove emotional barriers to effective money management and allows you to make rational financial decisions.

> "Money is neither good nor evil—in fact, money on some level is an illusion. It's a shape-shifter, either on paper or in a combination of zeros and ones in a computer system. That, and the emotions we attribute to it."

**Quick Take**: Treat money as a tool to be mastered, not as an emotional force to be feared or worshipped.

### The Modified 50/30/20 Framework
While the traditional 50/30/20 budget (50% essentials, 30% personal, 20% savings) is a good starting point, McKey recommends adjusting percentages based on your priorities. For those pursuing financial freedom early, consider aggressive savings rates like 50/10/40 or even 40/10/50.

> "If you want to build up for the future more, become aggressive with your savings (debt repayment and investing). I would say do a 50/10/40 or even 40/10/50 ratio in your early years."

**Quick Take**: Customize budget percentages to match your financial priorities, not someone else's formula.

### Caps Over Percentages
Instead of using flexible percentages that can lead to overspending, implement strict dollar caps for each spending category. This creates clear boundaries and prevents lifestyle inflation when income increases.

> "Why do I work with numbers instead of percentages? Because in my view, a percentage, say, 30% of your income for personal expenses, can trap you into overspending or spending money on useless crap that won't make you happy even in the short run."

**Quick Take**: Set specific dollar limits for each category and treat them as non-negotiable boundaries.

### The Three-Category System
Organize your entire financial life into three main groups: Net Disposable Income, Total Expenditure (with subcategories), and Total Savings. This simplifies tracking while maintaining comprehensive coverage of your financial life.

**Quick Take**: Group similar expenses together and assign each category a specific monthly cap that you never exceed.

## Action Items

1. **List all income streams and expenses** - Create comprehensive lists of everything coming in and going out monthly
2. **Set SMART financial goals** - Make goals Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, and Time-framed
3. **Choose your budget percentages** - Start with 50/30/20 or customize based on your priorities
4. **Set strict caps** - Convert percentages to specific dollar amounts and treat them as limits
5. **Build emergency fund** - Save 3-6 months of living expenses before aggressive investing
6. **Pay off high-interest debt** - Attack credit card and other expensive debt first
7. **Start investing early** - Use compound interest to build wealth over time

## Questions to Consider

- What does financial freedom look like for you in 1, 5, 10, and 20 years?
- Are you using money or is money using you?
- Which expenses are truly essential versus nice-to-have?
- What percentage of your income are you currently saving versus spending?
- If your income doubled tomorrow, would your lifestyle inflate or would your savings rate increase?

## Conclusion
"The 1-Page Budgeting Plan" is worth your time if you're tired of complex financial systems that never stick. McKey's straightforward approach cuts through the noise and delivers exactly what she promises—a simple, actionable system for gaining control of your finances. The biggest reason to read this book is its emphasis on psychological barriers to financial success, addressing the mindset issues that keep most people stuck in financial mediocrity.

The book's strength lies in its honesty about the compromises required for financial freedom while maintaining an optimistic, achievable tone. Rather than promising overnight wealth, it provides a realistic roadmap for gradual, sustainable financial improvement that anyone can follow. The single-page budget template alone is worth the price of admission, offering a practical tool that replaces dozens of complicated spreadsheets and apps.
