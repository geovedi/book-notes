# The Best New True Crime Stories: Small Towns
**Editor**: Mitzi Szereto | **Year**: 2020

## Summary
This chilling anthology shatters the myth that small towns are safe havens from the darkness found in big cities. Editor Mitzi Szereto brings together international writers who explore shocking true crimes that occurred in small communities across Australia, Ecuador, Italy, Wales, and Germany. The book reveals how intimate, close-knit environments can sometimes breed the most horrific violence, from serial killings and cannibalism to vigilante justice and mysterious deaths.

True crime enthusiasts and readers fascinated by human psychology will find compelling narratives that show how evil can flourish anywhere, regardless of community size or proximity. The book offers fresh perspectives on lesser-known cases while examining how small communities respond to, and are forever changed by, extraordinary criminal acts.

## Core Insights

### The Small Town Illusion vs. Reality
Small towns project an image of safety, where everyone knows their neighbors and doors remain unlocked. Yet as FBI statistics show, some rural areas have higher violent crime rates than major cities. This collection demonstrates that criminality follows human nature, not geography. The postcard-perfect settings described—Tuscan hills, Australian outback towns, Welsh communities—become backdrops for horrific crimes, proving that evil thrives in both isolation and intimacy.

> "The sad fact is, criminality can be found wherever human beings can be found. Be it spree killings, drug violence, sexual assault, vigilante justice, juvenile delinquency, property crime, robbery, or murder, bad things can and do happen in small towns all over the world."

**Quick Take**: Community size doesn't determine safety—human nature does. Small towns can actually be more dangerous because false security leads to lowered vigilance.

### The Double-Edged Sword of Community Intimacy
In small towns, everyone knows everyone's business—which can be both protective and dangerous. This intimacy creates a unique dynamic where crimes deeply affect the entire community, but also where secrets become impossible to keep. The Snowtown case shows how twelve murders went unnoticed in a town of 467 people, while the Italian murder of "La Bella Elvira" divided an entire village between supporting the accused and defending the victim.

**Quick Take**: Small-town connectivity means crimes have ripple effects that touch everyone, but it also means perpetrators have nowhere to hide their tracks indefinitely.

### Justice: Official vs. Community-Driven
Several stories explore what happens when formal justice systems fail or are distrusted by citizens. The Posorja, Ecuador case shows social media-fueled vigilantism when three alleged robbers were lynched based on false pedophile rumors. Similarly, "The Voodoo Preacher" story reveals how community members took justice into their own hands when official systems failed to address suspicious deaths. These cases raise uncomfortable questions about the line between justice and mob mentality.

**Quick Take**: When formal justice systems lose credibility, communities may revert to primitive forms of justice—often with tragic consequences.

### The Lasting Scars on Communities
True crime in small towns leaves permanent wounds that affect generations. Snowtown became synonymous with "bodies in barrels," struggling with whether to embrace dark tourism or escape its notoriety. The Italian village of Toiano was eventually abandoned altogether, unable to escape the ghost of "La Bella Elvira." These stories show that while big cities absorb and move past crimes, small communities can be permanently altered or even destroyed by single horrific events.

**Quick Take**: Small towns may never recover from infamous crimes—their very identities become intertwined with the tragedies that occurred there.

### International Perspectives Reveal Universal Patterns
Despite spanning multiple continents and cultures, these stories reveal striking similarities in how small communities respond to extraordinary crimes. Whether in Australia, Italy, Wales, Ecuador, or Germany, the patterns repeat: initial shock, community division, media fascination, and lasting stigma. This universality suggests that small-town dynamics transcend cultural boundaries when faced with evil in their midst.

**Quick Take**: The psychology of small-town crime response is remarkably consistent worldwide, revealing fundamental truths about human community behavior.

## Key Cases to Remember

- **Snowtown, Australia**: The "Bodies in the Barrels" murders—Australia's most prolific serial killing case
- **Posorja, Ecuador**: Social media-fueled lynching of three innocent people based on false rumors
- **Alexander City, Alabama**: The "Voodoo Preacher" case that attracted Harper Lee's attention
- **Toiano, Italy**: The unsolved murder of "La Bella Elvira" that divided an entire village
- **Carmarthen, Wales**: Victorian-era abortion mystery involving a doctor, dentist, and dairyman's daughter
- **Wüstefeld, Germany**: The case of Armin Meiwes, the cannibal who found his willing victim online

## Conclusion
**The Best New True Crime Stories: Small Towns** is essential reading for anyone who wants to understand the darker side of human nature in intimate settings. The book's strength lies in its international perspective, revealing that evil knows no geographical boundaries. These stories serve as cautionary tales about false security, the dangers of vigilantism, and the permanent scars that violent crimes leave on small communities.

While the content is often disturbing, it provides valuable insights into human psychology, community dynamics, and the myth of rural safety. Editor Mitzi Szereto has curated a collection that will change how you view small towns and remind you that the most terrifying monsters often wear familiar faces in familiar places.

**Worth your time?** Absolutely—especially for true crime fans looking beyond the usual famous cases to understand how criminal behavior manifests in communities where everyone supposedly knows everyone else. The book's lasting message is that evil thrives wherever humans exist, regardless of population size or postal code.
