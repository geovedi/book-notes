# Women Don't Owe You Pretty
**Author**: Florence Given | **Year**: 2020
**Category**: Philosophy & Critical Thinking

## Summary
A fierce feminist manifesto that dismantles the patriarchal beauty standards women are conditioned to uphold. Florence Given argues that "prettiness" is not a rent women pay for occupying space marked female, exposing how beauty standards function as a system of control rooted in racism, sexism, fatphobia, and transphobia. The book serves as both a wake-up call and practical guide for women to reclaim their autonomy, reject the male gaze, and build self-worth independent of external validation.

Perfect for young women questioning beauty standards, anyone unlearning internalized sexism, or those seeking to understand how beauty functions as currency in patriarchal systems. Readers will gain frameworks for identifying their own biases, setting boundaries, and building authentic self-worth.

## Core Insights

### Prettiness Is Not a Rent You Pay for Existing
The foundational premise that women don't owe beauty to anyone—not partners, coworkers, strangers, or society itself. Given exposes how beauty standards are constructed around whiteness, thinness, being non-disabled, and being cisgender, making "prettiness" a political tool of exclusion. Even more damningly, she reveals how desirability privilege is a double-edged sword: while it may afford certain opportunities, it also makes women targets for harassment and objectification.

> "Prettiness is not a rent you pay for occupying a space marked 'female'." – Erin McKean

**Quick Take**: Your body belongs to you alone. The purpose of your appearance is not to please others but to serve your own authentic expression.

### Your Self-Worth Should Never Be Tied to Male Validation
Given dismantles the conditioning that teaches women to measure their worth by male desire. She shares how she learned to count calories and say "no" to food before learning to set boundaries with people—a perfect metaphor for how women are taught to prioritize being objects of desire over having their needs met as human beings. The constant pursuit of male validation is described as a "bottomless pit" that will exhaust women all the way to their graves.

**Quick Take**: Stop chasing male validation and start building self-worth from within. The right people will choose you for your whole self, not your appearance.

### The Male Gaze and Internalized Sexism
The book examines how women internalize sexist standards and judge both themselves and other women by patriarchal standards. Given calls out the ways we police other women's bodies and choices—from aesthetic procedures to aging to expressing gender—and provides practical tools for catching and challenging these thoughts. She emphasizes that women are multifaceted beings capable of being more than one thing at once.

**Quick Take**: When you catch yourself judging other women, ask if their choices actually affect you. If not, move on with your life and focus on your own growth.

### "If It's Not a Fuck Yes, It's a No"
A powerful framework for decision-making and boundary-setting that applies to relationships, opportunities, and life choices. Given argues that women are socialized to settle for lukewarm enthusiasm rather than holding out for genuine enthusiasm and alignment. This "fuck yes" philosophy extends beyond dating to career opportunities, friendships, and personal commitments.

**Quick Take**: Stop settling for "maybe" and "fine." Pursue experiences and relationships that inspire genuine enthusiasm—the kind that makes you say "fuck yes!"

### Privilege Awareness and Accountability
The book emphasizes recognizing how beauty standards intersect with race, body size, ability, and gender identity. Given openly acknowledges her own privileges as slim, white, non-disabled, and cisgender, and encourages readers to examine their positions within systems of desirability. She stresses that having time and resources to deconstruct these biases is itself a privilege.

**Quick Take**: Acknowledge your privileges without shame, use them to challenge unjust systems, and diversify the media and voices you consume to expand your perspective.

### Reclaiming Your Time, Energy, and Attention
Given provides practical strategies for mental and emotional liberation, including stopping morning social media scrolling, protecting your energy, and refusing to comfort yourself with other women's perceived flaws. She emphasizes how patriarchy wants women to settle and feel small, making it revolutionary for women to take up space, pursue their ambitions, and center their own needs.

**Quick Take**: Your time and attention are your most valuable resources. Guard them fiercely and invest them in your growth rather than seeking external validation.

## Action Items
- **Audit your beauty routines**: Question which practices are for self-expression versus performance for others
- **Diversify your media**: Follow creators, authors, and artists from marginalized backgrounds
- **Practice the "fuck yes" rule**: Apply to relationships, opportunities, and commitments
- **Name your internalized sexism**: Give judgmental thoughts a name and tell them they don't belong
- **Check your privilege**: Regularly examine how your identity affords you unearned advantages
- **Set boundaries without guilt**: Practice saying "no" to requests that drain your energy
- **Stop morning scrolling**: Replace social media with activities that center your needs and growth

## Questions to Consider
- What beauty practices would you abandon if no one were watching?
- How different would your life look if you stopped seeking male validation?
- Where do you hold women to different standards than men?
- What privileges do you take for granted in how others perceive you?
- What would you do with the time and money you spend on performing femininity?

## Conclusion
This book is essential reading for anyone ready to dismantle the patriarchal conditioning that ties women's worth to their appearance. Given's combination of personal vulnerability, sharp analysis, and practical guidance makes complex feminist concepts accessible and actionable. While some readers might find the tone confrontational, that's precisely the point—growth requires discomfort. The book's greatest strength is how it connects abstract feminist theory to concrete daily decisions, from what we eat to who we follow online to how we set boundaries.

For women exhausted by the constant performance of femininity and anyone seeking to understand how beauty standards function as systems of control, this book offers both validation and a roadmap to liberation. It's not just about refusing to be pretty—it's about refusing to let external validation determine your worth, your choices, or your future.
