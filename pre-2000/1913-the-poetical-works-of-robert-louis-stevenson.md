# The Poetical Works of Robert Louis Stevenson
**Authors**: Robert Louis Stevenson | **Year**: 1913
**Category**: Fiction & Literature

## Summary
This comprehensive collection captures the full range of Robert Louis Stevenson's poetic genius, from the childlike wonder of "A Child's Garden of Verses" to the mature reflections of "Songs of Travel and Other Verses." Stevenson masterfully weaves together the ordinary and the extraordinary, finding magic in everyday childhood experiences while exploring deeper themes of nature, journeying, love, and mortality.

The collection showcases Stevenson's unique ability to see the world through multiple lenses—both as a child discovering the world for the first time and as an adult reflecting on life's profound mysteries. His accessible yet profound poetry appeals to readers of all ages, making him one of the most beloved poets in the English language.

Readers seeking to reconnect with childhood wonder, find beauty in everyday moments, or explore the deeper meanings of life through accessible poetry will discover endless treasures in this collection.

## Core Insights

### Childhood Wonder and Imagination
Stevenson's most famous works celebrate the magical perspective of childhood, where ordinary objects and experiences become extraordinary adventures. He captures how children transform their world through imagination—shadow play becomes mysterious companions, bedtimes become journeys to magical lands, and simple swings become flights above the countryside. His poetry reminds us that wonder is not just for children but a way of seeing the world that we can reclaim at any age.

> "How do you like to go up in a swing,
> Up in the air so blue?
> Oh, I do think it the pleasantest thing
> Ever a child can do!"

**Quick Take**: Reclaiming childlike wonder can transform ordinary experiences into extraordinary ones.

### The Poetry of Nature and Observation
Stevenson demonstrates remarkable attention to the natural world, capturing the essence of wind, moonlight, seasons, and landscapes with precise yet lyrical language. He doesn't just describe nature—he personifies it, giving voices to wind, shadow, and celestial bodies. His nature poetry teaches us to observe more carefully and find the hidden stories in the world around us.

> "I saw you toss the kites on high
> And blow the birds about the sky;
> And all around I heard you pass,
> Like ladies' skirts across the grass—
> O wind, a-blowing all day long,
> O wind, that sings so loud a song!"

**Why This Matters**: Deep observation of nature connects us to something larger than ourselves and awakens our senses to the world's hidden poetry.

### Journey and the Vagabond Spirit
Throughout his work, Stevenson celebrates freedom, travel, and the restless spirit of the wanderer. From literal journeys to metaphorical ones, his poems capture the human desire for exploration and independence. The vagabond spirit represents not just physical travel but a mindset of openness to new experiences and rejection of conventional constraints.

> "Give to me the life I love,
> Let the lave go by me,
> Give the jolly heaven above
> And the byway nigh me.
> Bed in the bush with stars to see,
> Bread I dip in the river—
> There's the life for a man like me,
> There's the life for ever."

**Quick Take**: True freedom comes not from escaping life but from embracing it fully on our own terms.

### Life, Death, and Acceptance
In his more mature works, Stevenson confronts mortality with remarkable peace and acceptance. His famous "Requiem" poem embodies a graceful approach to death that sees it not as an end but as a homecoming. This perspective transforms our relationship with mortality from fear to acceptance.

> "Under the wide and starry sky,
> Dig the grave and let me lie.
> Glad did I live and gladly die,
> And I laid me down with a will.
> This be the verse you grave for me:
> Here he lies where he longed to be;
> Home is the sailor, home from sea,
> And the hunter home from the hill."

**Why This Matters**: A life well-lived prepares us for a peaceful death, transforming endings into homecomings.

## Best Quotes

- "I have a little shadow that goes in and out with me,
  And what can be the use of him is more than I can see.
  He is very, very like me from the heels up to the head;
  And I see him jump before me, when I jump into my bed."

- "In winter I get up at night
  And dress by yellow candle-light.
  In summer, quite the other way,—
  I have to go to bed by day."

- "The moon has a face like the clock in the hall;
  She shines on thieves on the garden wall,
  On streets and fields and harbour quays,
  And birdies asleep in the forks of the trees."

- "The funniest thing about him is the way he likes to grow—
  Not at all like proper children, which is always very slow;
  For he sometimes shoots up taller like an india-rubber ball,
  And he sometimes gets so little that there's none of him at all."

- "Let the blow fall soon or late,
  Let what will be o'er me;
  Give the face of earth around
  And the road before me."

Robert Louis Stevenson's poetical works are a masterclass in finding the extraordinary within the ordinary. His accessible yet profound poetry offers something for every reader—whether you're seeking to reconnect with childhood wonder, find beauty in everyday moments, or explore life's deeper questions through the lens of gentle wisdom.
