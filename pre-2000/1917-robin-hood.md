# Robin Hood
**Authors**: Paul Creswick | **Year**: 1917

## Summary
This timeless tale follows Robin Hood, the legendary outlaw of Sherwood Forest, who begins as Robin Fitzooth, son of the King's Ranger of Locksley. After his father is killed by corrupt officials and his lands are seized, Robin is forced into outlawry, where he gathers a band of Merry Men to fight injustice and protect the common folk from tyrannical nobles and clergy. The story follows his journey from exiled youth to Earl of Huntingdon, his love for Maid Marian, his allegiance to King Richard the Lionheart, and ultimately his tragic betrayal and death.

Readers who love classic adventure stories, tales of justice versus corruption, and legendary heroes will find this a captivating journey through medieval England. The book explores themes of loyalty, honor, social justice, and the eternal struggle between freedom and tyranny.

## Core Insights

### The Making of an Outlaw Hero
Robin's transformation from law-abiding forester's son to legendary outlaw begins when his father is murdered by corrupt officials seeking his lands. Forced to flee, Robin discovers his true calling as protector of the oppressed. His skill with bow and sword, combined with his unwavering moral code, makes him the perfect champion of the common people against corrupt authority.

> "Remember always that your father is a proud man; and see, take those arrows of my own making and learn from them how to trim the hazel."

**Quick Take**: True leadership emerges not from titles or birthright, but from character forged in adversity and commitment to justice.

### The Brotherhood of Sherwood
Robin's band of Merry Men—including Little John, Friar Tuck, Will Scarlett, and others—represents a new kind of family bound by loyalty rather than blood. Each character brings unique skills and perspectives, creating a diverse community that thrives on mutual respect and shared values. This brotherhood becomes the foundation of Robin's resistance against tyranny.

**Quick Take**: The strongest communities are built on shared purpose and mutual protection, not social status or wealth.

### Love Amid Revolution
Robin's relationship with Maid Marian transcends romance to become a partnership in the fight for justice. Marian proves herself as brave and resourceful as any of Robin's men, challenging medieval gender roles and demonstrating that love and revolution can coexist beautifully.

**Quick Take**: True partnership means fighting together for what you believe, not just sharing moments of peace.

### The King's Justice
When King Richard returns from the Crusades, he recognizes Robin's nobility and grants him pardon and title. This pivotal moment shows that Robin never opposed true authority, only its corruption. Richard's wisdom in seeing the difference between law and justice proves essential to restoring order.

**Quick Take**: Legitimate authority serves justice; corrupt authority serves itself.

### The Final Betrayal
Despite his triumphs, Robin meets his end not in glorious battle but through treachery and manipulation. Wounded and betrayed by those who envied his fame, he faces death with the same courage that defined his life, choosing his own burial place with his final arrow.

> "Bury me, gossips, where my arrow hath fallen. There lay a green sod under my head and another beneath my feet, and let my bow be at my side."

**Quick Take**: How we face death defines us as much as how we lived.

## Key Turning Points

- **Father's Death**: The murder of Robin's father sets him on the path to outlawry and defines his lifelong quest for justice.
- **Meeting the Merry Men**: Each new member strengthens Robin's band and expands their mission of protecting the innocent.
- **King Richard's Return**: The true king's recognition validates Robin's cause and transforms him from outlaw to noble.
- **Marian's Rescue**: Robin's daring rescue of Maid Marian from forced marriage demonstrates his courage and love.
- **Final Betrayal**: Even heroes can fall to treachery, reminding us that vigilance is eternal.

## Why It Matters

This story explores timeless questions about justice, authority, and the price of freedom. Robin Hood's struggle against corrupt officials resonates across centuries, speaking to anyone who has witnessed abuse of power. His legend represents the hope that ordinary people can challenge injustice when they unite behind shared values and courageous leadership.

## Questions to Consider

- When does breaking the law become morally necessary?
- How do we distinguish between legitimate authority and corrupt power?
- What makes someone a true leader—title or character?
- Is violence ever justified in the pursuit of justice?
- How do we ensure that movements for justice don't become corrupted over time?

## Conclusion

This version of Robin Hood deserves your time for its rich character development and complex exploration of medieval politics. Unlike simplified retellings, Creswick's Robin is fully human—flawed, passionate, and ultimately tragic. The biggest reason to read it is to understand how legends are made: not through perfect heroes, but through imperfect people who choose courage over comfort when history demands it.

The story's power lies in its acknowledgment that even the greatest champions can fall to betrayal, yet their impact lives on through the ideals they inspire. Robin Hood's final message reminds us that while individual lives end, the struggle for justice continues through those who remember and carry forward the cause.
