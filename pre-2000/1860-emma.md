# Emma
**Original Authors**: Charlotte Brontë | **Year**: 1860 (posthumous)

## Summary
Emma is Charlotte Brontë's unfinished novel, consisting of only two chapters that were published posthumously in 1860. The story follows Mrs. Chalfont, a widow who becomes acquainted with a mysterious young girl named Matilda Fitzgibbon, who is enrolled at a local ladies' school. The girl arrives with fine clothes and claims to be the wealthy daughter of Conway Fitzgibbon, Esq. of May Park, Midland County. However, questions soon arise about her true identity when the school headmistress discovers that neither the estate nor the father exist.

This mysterious fragment offers readers a glimpse into Brontë's masterful character development and social commentary, centering on themes of class deception, identity, and the treatment of children in Victorian society. Those interested in literary history, Brontë's writing evolution, or unfinished literary mysteries will find this brief work compelling.

## Core Insights

### The Mystery of the False Heiress
The story centers around Matilda Fitzgibbon, a young girl who arrives at Miss Wilcox's ladies' school with all the trappings of wealth and aristocracy. Her father, supposedly Conway Fitzgibbon, presents himself as a gentleman and pays substantial fees for his daughter's education. Miss Wilcox, eager for both the income and social prestige, immediately favors the girl above all other pupils, giving her special privileges and treating her as a showpiece.

> "A favorite has no friends; and the observation of a gentleman, who about this time called at the Lodge and chanced to see Miss Fitzgibbon, was, 'That child looks consummately unhappy.'"

**Quick Take**: The novel explores how wealth and perceived status can blind people to obvious truths, especially when profit and social standing are involved.

### The Deception Revealed
When Christmas approaches, Miss Wilcox writes to the parents of all her pupils to arrange for the holidays. Her letter to Conway Fitzgibbon at May Park, Midland County, is returned with a devastating message: there is no such place as May Park in Midland County, nor is there any record of Conway Fitzgibbon. The elaborate deception begins to unravel.

> "Conway Fitzgibbon was a man of straw; May Park a house of cards. there was no vestige of such man or mansion in Midland County, or in any other shire in England."

**Quick Take**: The systematic investigation reveals how easily appearances can deceive when no one verifies the claims of those who present themselves as wealthy and aristocratic.

### The Child's True Nature
Throughout her time at the school, Matilda never quite fits in. Despite her fine clothes and privileged treatment, she remains isolated and unhappy. Other students reject her company, and even Miss Wilcox admits to feeling an unnatural aversion to the child, despite her efforts to treat her favorably. Matilda's behavior ranges from distant and insolent to mysterious, including episodes of sleepwalking and a near-fatal collapse.

> "I wonder what her real name is? Betty Hodge? Poll Smith? Hannah Jones?"

**Quick Take**: The child's inability to connect with others suggests she may be either a victim of the deception or perhaps unwittingly complicit in it.

## Key Questions to Consider
- Who really placed Matilda at the school, and what was their motivation?
- Is Matilda herself aware of the deception, or is she another victim?
- How does Mr. Ellin's role as investigator suggest he knows more than he reveals?
- What would Brontë have revealed about Matilda's true background had she completed the novel?

## Conclusion
Emma, though unfinished, showcases Charlotte Brontë's exceptional ability to create mystery and develop complex characters within just two chapters. The fragment offers a fascinating glimpse into what might have been another masterpiece from the author of Jane Eyre. The central mystery of Matilda's true identity and origins, combined with the social commentary on class distinctions and the treatment of children in Victorian society, makes this brief work compelling despite its incomplete nature.

Literary enthusiasts and Brontë scholars will find this fragment valuable for understanding her writing process and themes, while casual readers can appreciate the tantalizing mystery she created. Though we'll never know how Brontë intended to resolve Matilda's story, the existing chapters provide sufficient intrigue and character development to make it worthwhile reading for anyone interested in Victorian literature or the Brontë legacy.

The biggest reason to read Emma is to experience Brontë's masterful storytelling even in its embryonic form, and to speculate on one of literature's fascinating unfinished mysteries.
