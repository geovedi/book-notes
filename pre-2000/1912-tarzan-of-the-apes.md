# Tarzan of the Apes
**Authors**: Edgar Rice Burroughs | **Year**: 1912
**Category**: Fantasy & Science Fiction

## Summary
"Tarzan of the Apes" tells the extraordinary story of John Clayton, Lord Greystoke, who is born to English nobility marooned on the African coast. After his parents die, the infant is adopted and raised by a tribe of apes, becoming Tarzan—a unique being caught between two worlds. Through sheer determination and innate intelligence, Tarzan teaches himself to read from his parents' abandoned books, develops superior physical abilities, and eventually becomes the king of his jungle domain.

The novel takes a dramatic turn when Jane Porter and her expedition arrive in the jungle, forcing Tarzan to confront his human heritage and choose between his life among the apes and the possibilities of civilization. It's a gripping exploration of identity, nature versus nurture, and what it truly means to be human.

Perfect for readers who love adventure stories, survival tales, and thought-provoking examinations of human nature and society.

## Core Insights

### The Nature vs. Nurture Paradox
Tarzan's life represents one of literature's most fascinating experiments: what happens when a human child is raised by animals? Despite growing up among apes, Tarzan retains innate human characteristics—curiosity, reasoning ability, and the capacity for complex emotions. His journey demonstrates that while environment shapes us profoundly, some aspects of our nature remain ingrained.

> "In his veins, though, flowed the blood of the best of a race of mighty fighters, and back of this was the training of his short lifetime among the fierce brutes of the jungle."

**Quick Take**: Your environment may develop your skills, but your essential nature often finds ways to express itself.

### The Power of Self-Education
One of the most inspiring aspects of Tarzan's story is his journey of self-education. With only his parents' books as guides, he teaches himself to read and write, bridging the gap between his jungle existence and human knowledge. This remarkable achievement shows that the drive to learn and grow can overcome even the most challenging circumstances.

> "At the bottom of his little English heart beat the great desire to cover his nakedness with CLOTHES for he had learned from his picture books that all MEN were so covered, while MONKEYS and APES and every other living thing went naked."

**Quick Take**: True learning comes from within—resources are less important than determination and curiosity.

### Identity and Belonging
Tarzan's internal conflict between his ape upbringing and human heritage speaks to universal questions about identity and belonging. When he encounters Jane Porter and her companions, he must decide where he truly belongs, exploring whether our connections are determined by blood, environment, or choice.

> "Tarzan of the Apes... You called me by name at the cottage, but I do not seem to recall yours, though there is something very familiar about you. It is as though I had known you well under very different conditions a long time ago."

**Quick Take**: Belonging isn't about where you come from—it's about who you choose to be.

### Civilization Versus Natural Living
The novel contrasts the "civilized" world with Tarzan's natural jungle existence, questioning what we really mean by civilization. Tarzan possesses honor, loyalty, and wisdom despite his "savage" upbringing, while many supposedly civilized characters in the story act with brutality and selfishness.

**Quick Take**: True civilization is about character and values, not about clothes, language, or social conventions.

### The Universal Language of Courage and Sacrifice
Throughout the novel, Tarzan demonstrates extraordinary courage and self-sacrifice, particularly in his protection of Jane Porter and her party. These qualities transcend cultural boundaries and speak to universal human values.

**Quick Take**: The most human qualities—courage, compassion, sacrifice—can develop in any environment.

## Best Quotes

- "In his veins, though, flowed the blood of the best of a race of mighty fighters, and back of this was the training of his short lifetime among the fierce brutes of the jungle."
- "No human mother could have shown more unselfish and sacrificing devotion than did this poor, wild brute for the little orphaned waif whom fate had thrown into her keeping."
- "Tarzan of the Apes... I am just learning it, and while I understand it fairly well I speak it very poorly."

## Key Takeaways
- **Environment shapes but doesn't define us**: Our circumstances influence our development, but our essential nature and choices ultimately determine who we become.
- **Self-education is powerful**: The drive to learn and improve oneself can overcome even the most limited resources and circumstances.
- **Identity is complex and multifaceted**: We can belong to multiple worlds and draw strength from diverse aspects of our heritage and experience.
- **True civilization comes from within**: Character, honor, and compassion are more important than superficial markers of "civilized" society.

## Questions to Consider
- How much of who we are is determined by our upbringing versus our innate nature?
- What does it really mean to be "civilized"?
- Can someone be truly human without exposure to human society?
- How do we balance our different identities and heritages in forming our sense of self?
- Is isolation from society a disadvantage or an opportunity for authentic development?

## Conclusion
"Tarzan of the Apes" is more than just an adventure story—it's a profound exploration of what makes us human. While the action sequences and jungle encounters are thrilling, the novel's lasting power comes from its examination of identity, education, and the conflict between natural living and civilization.

This book is absolutely worth your time, especially if you're interested in classic adventure stories that tackle deeper philosophical questions. Tarzan's journey from jungle child to noble hero continues to captivate readers more than a century after its publication because it speaks to universal questions about human nature, belonging, and the search for identity.

The biggest reason to read it? Burroughs created a character who has become part of our cultural mythology, and understanding the original story provides insight into themes that continue to resonate in modern storytelling about outsiders, heroes, and what it means to find your place in the world.
