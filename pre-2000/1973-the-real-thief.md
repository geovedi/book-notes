# The Real Thief
**Authors**: William Steig | **Year**: 1973

## Summary
"The Real Thief" tells the story of Gawain, an honorable goose appointed as Chief Guard of the Royal Treasury by King Basil. When priceless treasures begin disappearing from the impenetrable treasury, all evidence points to Gawain as the only possible thief. Despite his protestations of innocence, even his closest friends and the King himself turn against him. Gawain is put on trial and found guilty based on circumstantial evidence, leading him to escape through a courtroom window and flee into exile.

The real culprit is Derek, a small mouse who discovered a tiny crevice in the treasury floor and began stealing treasure not out of malice, but from envy and a desire for beauty. As Derek watches the innocent Gawain suffer, his conscience torments him. After Gawain's escape, Derek continues stealing to prove Gawain's innocence, then returns all the treasure, and finally sets out to find Gawain and restore justice.

Perfect for readers aged 8-12 who enjoy moral fables about justice, friendship, and redemption, this story explores how good people can make terrible mistakes and how true courage means facing the consequences of one's actions.

## Fast-Track Story Guide

### The Setup: Trust and Honor
Gawain is a distinguished goose chosen by the beloved King Basil to guard the Royal Treasury. Despite his dreams of becoming an architect, Gawain accepts the post out of love and loyalty to his King. The treasury is seemingly impregnable—stone walls a foot thick, fortified oak interior, massive iron-banded door with four locks. Only King Basil and Gawain have keys.

### The Crime: Vanishing Treasures
When rubies, gold ducats, silver ornaments, and finally the priceless Kalikak diamond disappear, King Basil is distraught. Despite thorough investigations proving no break-in occurred, logic points to only one possible thief: the only other person with access. Even Gawain's fellow guards—Harvey, Garvey, and Wetmore—have no explanation for the mysterious disappearances.

### The Betrayal: Trial and Condemnation
In a trial that shocks the community, King Basil himself acts as judge. Despite Gawain's lifelong reputation for honor, the circumstantial evidence convinces everyone—even his closest friends—that he must be guilty. When Gawain suggests the King might have made a mistake, Basil's rage convinces the court of Gawain's guilt. Before sentencing can be imposed, the heartbroken Gawain escapes through a courthouse window and flies away in disgrace.

### The Truth: The Real Thief
Derek, a mouse living among oak roots, discovered an old mole tunnel leading to a tiny uncemented crevice in the treasury floor. Entranced by the treasure's beauty and envious of the King's wealth, he began stealing to decorate his humble home. As he watched Gawain's trial, Derek was terrified of confessing but tortured by guilt. His cowardice costs an innocent friend everything.

### The Redemption: Courage and Consequences
After Gawain's escape, Derek's conscience drives him to first steal more treasure to prove Gawain's innocence, then return everything anonymously. Finally, he searches for Gawain to confess personally. When they meet in the forest, both have suffered deeply—Gawain from betrayal, Derek from guilt. Their emotional reconciliation leads to forgiveness, and they return together to a kingdom desperate to make amends.

## Key Characters

- **Gawain**: An honorable goose with dreams of architecture whose trustworthability makes him both perfect for the treasury job and the perfect scapegoat
- **King Basil**: A fatherly bear who loves Gawain like a son but allows logic and anger to override his instincts
- **Derek**: A small mouse whose envy leads to catastrophic consequences but whose conscience ultimately saves him
- **The Kingdom**: A community that demonstrates how quickly good people can abandon their principles when faced with seemingly irrefutable evidence

## Best Quotes
- "I am an honorable goose. How you could judge me otherwise, I do not know. Perhaps our Maker knows. Certainly He knows how much I once loved you. But now I HATE each and every living one of you, and with all my heart, for seeing evil in me that is not there. Shame on the lot of you!"
- "The King makes no mistakes!"
- "I will never, from now to eternity, mistrust you again! I repent."
- "We all make mistakes."
- "I forgive you gladly, dear mouse. I can see how much you've suffered, and we both know what suffering is."

## Moral Lessons
- **The danger of circumstantial evidence**: How seemingly logical conclusions can be completely wrong
- **The weight of conscience**: How guilt affects even those who think they can hide their wrongdoing
- **The cost of cowardice**: How fear of consequences can harm the innocent more than the crime itself
- **The power of forgiveness**: True reconciliation requires both confession and forgiveness
- **The complexity of human (or animal) nature**: Good people can do bad things for complex reasons

## Questions to Consider
- Why did Derek steal from the treasury if he wasn't malicious?
- Should Derek have confessed earlier, even if it meant severe punishment?
- Why did Gawain's friends abandon him so easily despite knowing his character?
- Was King Basil more wrong to doubt Gawain or to act as judge in his own case?
- How does the story show that consequences affect everyone involved in wrongdoing?
- What does Gawain's dream of being an architect reveal about his character?
- Why did the community need Gawain to return even after the treasure was found?

## Why This Story Matters
"The Real Thief" is a masterful exploration of justice and morality that doesn't talk down to young readers. William Steig creates a sophisticated narrative where the villain is sympathetic, the hero is flawed, and the moral universe is complex. The book shows children that justice isn't always straightforward, that good people can make terrible mistakes, and that courage isn't the absence of fear but acting despite it.

The story's power comes from its emotional authenticity—Gawain's heartbreak at betrayal, Derek's tortured conscience, the King's devastated regret. These feelings are universally understood by anyone who has ever been wrongly accused, hidden a mistake, or regretted rushing to judgment. By the end, readers learn that true justice requires more than evidence—it requires courage, compassion, and the willingness to admit when we're wrong.
