# The Lust Killer
**Author**: Ann Rule | **Year**: 1983
**Category**: Horror & Thriller

## Summary
"The Lust Killer" chronicles the horrifying true story of Jerome Henry "Jerry" Brudos, one of Oregon's most notorious serial killers. Between 1968-1969, Brudos abducted, murdered, and mutilated four young women in the Salem-Portland area, driven by a lifelong fetish for women's shoes and underwear that escalated into extreme sexual violence and homicide.

Ann Rule, who would later become famous for her work tracking Ted Bundy, masterfully reconstructs the investigation led by Lieutenant Jim Stovall, which ultimately brought Brudos to justice. The book serves as both a gripping true crime narrative and a chilling exploration of how childhood trauma, sexual obsession, and psychopathy can combine to create a monster hidden behind a facade of normalcy.

**Who should read**: True crime enthusiasts, criminal psychology students, and those interested in understanding how serial predators operate. Readers will gain insight into criminal investigation techniques and the warning signs of dangerous sexual pathology.

## Core Insights

### The Making of a Monster
Jerome Brudos wasn't born a killer but was molded by early psychological trauma and rejection. As a child, he developed an intense fetish for women's shoes and underwear, stealing them from neighbors and hiding them from his domineering mother who shamed him for any sexual curiosity. His obsession with women's intimate apparel evolved from theft to violent sexual assault and eventually murder, with each crime becoming more brutal than the last.

> "He had all the filmy, lacy panties and bras that he'd stolen over a long time. He could never decide which was the best—sneaking into dark apartments and stealing the underwear while he could hear the women sighing in their sleep only a few feet away."

**Quick Take**: Understanding the progression from fetish to violence helps recognize how seemingly "harmless" obsessions can become dangerous warning signs when combined with deep-seated misogyny and violent tendencies.

### The Hunter's Pattern
Brudos carefully selected his victims—usually young, attractive women he could catch in vulnerable situations. He used various ruses to gain their trust, from fake car trouble to impersonating authority figures. Once he had them isolated, he would subject them to rape, torture, and eventually murder, often keeping their bodies for days to indulge his necrophilic fantasies.

> "One by one the young women vanished without a trace. Linda Slawson disappeared while trying to make a living selling encyclopedias door to door... College student Jan Whitney never completed her two-hour drive home on the freeway."

**Quick Take**: Many serial killers target victims who are transient or in isolated circumstances, making detection difficult. Awareness of one's surroundings and avoiding isolation with strangers can be life-saving.

### The Breaking Point
The investigation intensified after 19-year-old Karen Sprinker disappeared from a shopping center parking lot in broad daylight. Unlike previous victims who had more transient lifestyles, Sprinker was a well-connected college student whose disappearance couldn't be ignored. This led to the creation of a massive task force and eventually to Brudos' arrest when an attempted victim, Sharon Wood, escaped and provided a detailed description.

> "He had a pleasant surprise when he undressed her. Despite her boy's haircut and plain clothing, she was wearing wonderful underwear. A blue bra and slip and girdle, and beneath the girdle, bright red panties."

**Quick Take**: The more connected and visible the victim, the more likely law enforcement will devote resources to finding them. This reality highlights systemic biases in how missing persons cases are prioritized.

### The House of Horrors
When police finally searched Brudos' home, they discovered a chamber of horrors: his garage workshop where he tortured victims, his freezer containing body parts, and an extensive collection of women's underwear and shoes taken from numerous victims over the years. Most disturbing were the photographs Brudos took of himself with his victims' bodies, posing them in various states of dress with different shoes and undergarments.

> "He was pleased with himself. He had convinced Ned Rawls that he was brewing nitroglycerin in the basement, and scared him off easily enough."

**Quick Take**: Even the most seemingly normal family homes can hide dark secrets, and serial killers often maintain facades of normalcy while committing unspeakable acts nearby.

## Crime Scene Awareness
- Always be aware of your surroundings when approaching unfamiliar locations
- Trust your instincts if something feels wrong about a situation or person
- Never enter a private home with someone you don't know and trust completely
- If faced with abduction attempts, fight back immediately—survival depends on not being taken to a secondary location
- Report suspicious behavior to authorities, even if it seems minor

## Questions to Consider
- How did Brudos maintain a facade of normalcy while committing these horrific crimes?
- What role did early childhood trauma and sexual shame play in his development into a serial killer?
- Why do some fetishists remain non-violent while others, like Brudos, escalate to murder?
- How can law enforcement better identify patterns in missing persons cases before predators strike again?
- What warning signs might family members miss in someone developing dangerous sexual pathologies?

## Conclusion
"The Lust Killer" is a gripping but disturbing read that offers valuable insights into the mind of a sexual sadist and the investigators who brought him to justice. Rule's detailed account demonstrates how ordinary-seeming individuals can harbor extraordinary darkness, and how meticulous police work can ultimately unravel the most carefully concealed crimes.

The book is worth your time if you're interested in criminal psychology or true crime, but be prepared for graphic content and deeply disturbing subject matter. Its greatest value lies in understanding how predators operate and the importance of vigilance, awareness, and prompt reporting of suspicious activity. Brudos died in prison in 2006, serving multiple life sentences, never expressing genuine remorse for his crimes.
