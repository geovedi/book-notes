# The Getting of Wisdom
**Authors**: Henry Handel Richardson | **Year**: 1910
**Category**: Fiction & Literature

## Summary
"The Getting of Wisdom" follows Laura Rambotham, a sensitive and intelligent twelve-year-old girl sent to a prestigious Melbourne boarding school in colonial Australia. The novel tracks her difficult journey through the social and academic challenges of institutional education, her intense friendships, spiritual crises, and eventual emergence as a self-aware young woman. Laura struggles with authority, conformity, and her own unconventional nature, ultimately discovering that wisdom comes not from conventional success but from embracing one's unique perspective.

This classic coming-of-age story will resonate with anyone who felt like an outsider in their youth or questioned the value of traditional education. Readers who appreciate psychological depth, character-driven narratives, and explorations of female adolescence will find rich rewards in this pioneering Australian novel.

## Core Insights

### The Pain of Being Different
Laura enters boarding school as a "square peg" in a world of round holes, immediately marked by her unconventional behavior and sensitive nature. Her rebellious streak—cutting off her hair to make a bouquet, questioning authority, struggling with social conventions—sets her apart from both peers and teachers. This outsider perspective becomes both her greatest challenge and most valuable asset, allowing her to see through the hypocrisies of institutional life while suffering the loneliness of nonconformity.

> "She went out from school with the uncomfortable sense of being a square peg, which fitted into none of the round holes of her world."
**Quick Take**: Our differences may cause suffering, but they're often the source of our unique wisdom.

### The Double-Edged Sword of Intense Friendships
Laura's relationship with the beautiful and popular Evelyn Souttar becomes the central emotional drama of her school years. Their intense friendship borders on obsession, revealing both the transcendent power of deep connection and its dangers when balanced with other life aspects. Laura learns that even the closest bonds can't protect her from life's fundamental challenges, and that clinging too tightly can lead to devastating disappointments when circumstances change.

> "She had learned that, oftentimes, just those mortals who feel cramped and unsure in the conduct of everyday life, will find themselves to rights, with astounding ease, in that freer, more spacious world where no practical considerations hamper."
**Quick Take**: True friendship requires both fierce devotion and the wisdom to let go when necessary.

### The Crisis of Faith and Spiritual Independence
Laura's religious journey takes her from unquestioning belief to intense devotion, then through profound disillusionment. When she makes a desperate bargain with God to help her pass examinations—and then resorts to cheating when divine help seems insufficient—she experiences a complete collapse of faith. This spiritual death becomes a rebirth into intellectual independence, teaching her that wisdom requires questioning even our most cherished beliefs.

> "From this time forward, she would rival the rest in lukewarmness."
**Quick Take**: Real wisdom often begins when we stop asking God to solve our problems and start solving them ourselves.

### The Limits of Formal Education
Despite attending an expensive boarding school and enduring endless examinations, Laura's most valuable learning comes outside the classroom. The novel critiques educational systems that prioritize rote memorization and social conformity over genuine understanding and personal growth. Laura's ultimate "wisdom" comes not from passing exams but from accepting her unconventional nature and recognizing that different kinds of intelligence deserve different kinds of expression.

> "The wisdom she had got, the experience she was richer by, had, in the process of equipping her for life, merely seemed to disclose her unfitness."
**Quick Take**: Education should expand our minds, not shrink them to fit predetermined boxes.

### The Liberation of Self-Acceptance
In the novel's powerful final scene, Laura runs freely through the streets, abandoning all concern for social conventions. This physical liberation symbolizes her psychological breakthrough—accepting that she may never fit neatly into society's expectations but can find freedom in embracing her authentic self. The getting of wisdom, ultimately, is the realization that being yourself is more important than being what others want you to be.

> "She was off, had darted away into the leaden heat of the December morning, like an arrow from its bow, her head bent, her arms close to her sides, fleet-footed as a spaniel."
**Quick Take**: True freedom comes when you stop performing and start being.

## Takeaways
- **Embrace your unconventional nature**: What makes you different may ultimately be your greatest strength
- **Question institutional authority**: Education systems don't always know what's best for individual growth
- **Balance relationships wisely**: Intense friendships are precious but shouldn't eclipse your own development
- **Develop spiritual independence**: True wisdom comes from within, not from external validation
- **Value self-knowledge over social acceptance**: Being yourself is more valuable than fitting in

## Questions to Consider
- How do educational systems shape (or misshape) unconventional minds?
- What's the difference between formal education and genuine wisdom?
- How can intense friendships both help and hinder personal growth?
- What role does spiritual crisis play in developing independent thinking?
- When is nonconformity valuable, and when is it self-destructive?

## Conclusion
"The Getting of Wisdom" deserves your time if you appreciate psychologically complex coming-of-age stories that challenge conventional wisdom about education, socialization, and female development. Richardson's pioneering novel was remarkably ahead of its time in its critique of institutional education and its nuanced portrait of adolescent rebellion.

The biggest reason to read this book is its unflinching honesty about the difficult journey from childhood dependency to adult autonomy. Laura's struggles with authority, friendship, faith, and self-acceptance remain powerfully relevant over a century after publication. This isn't a comfortable novel about easy growth—it's a challenging exploration of how wisdom often comes through failure, disappointment, and the painful acceptance of one's own nature.

While the pacing may feel slow to readers accustomed to faster narratives, those who stick with Laura's journey will be rewarded with profound insights into the process of becoming oneself—no matter the cost.
