# Intellectual Property Law
**Author**: Lysander Spooner | **Year**: 1855 (Published 2019)
**Category**: Business & Finance

## Summary
Lysander Spooner's "Intellectual Property Law" is a radical philosophical treatise that argues for perpetual, absolute property rights in ideas based on natural law principles. Written in 1855 by the renowned American legal theorist and individualist anarchist, this work makes the groundbreaking case that intellectual property deserves the same - and often stronger - protection than physical property.

Spooner's central thesis challenges conventional wisdom by asserting that ideas are not only wealth but are the most valuable form of wealth, serving as the foundation of all human progress and happiness. He argues that denying perpetual property rights in ideas is equivalent to denying all property rights whatsoever, since even physical property rights ultimately derive from labor - which itself is incorporeal.

The book is essential reading for anyone interested in intellectual property law, libertarian philosophy, or the foundations of property rights. It provides a robust natural law defense of creators' rights that remains strikingly relevant in today's digital age, where questions about idea ownership and intellectual property have become increasingly urgent.

## Core Insights

### Ideas Are Wealth - The Most Valuable Form of Wealth
Spooner establishes that wealth includes "every conceivable object, idea, and sensation that can either contribute to, or constitute, the physical, intellectual, moral, or emotional well-being of man." He argues that ideas are not only wealth but often represent the most valuable form of wealth, as they guide all labor and creation of material wealth.

> "An idea, then, is wealth. It is equally wealth, whether it be regarded, as some ideas may be, simply as, in itself, an object of enjoyment, reflection, meditation, and thus a direct source of happiness; or whether it be regarded, as other ideas may be, simply as a means to be used for acquiring other wealth, intellectual, moral, affectional, or material."

> "The idea, after which a machine is fashioned, is as clearly wealth, as is the material of which the machine is composed. The idea is the life of the machine, without which, the machine would be inoperative, powerless, and incapable of producing wealth."

**Quick Take**: Ideas aren't just valuable - they're the foundation of all value. Without ideas, physical materials remain useless; with ideas, they become tools of human progress.

### Incorporeal Things Are Legitimate Property
Spooner systematically dismantles the argument that only physical things can be property. He demonstrates that countless incorporeal things - labor, reputation, health, liberty, even life itself - are universally recognized as property, making ideas equally legitimate subjects of ownership.

> "To deny the right of property in incorporeal things, is equivalent to denying the right of property in labor itself; in the products of labor; and even in those corporeal substances, that are acquired by labor."

> "The right of property being incorporeal, and being itself a subject of property, it demonstrates that the right of property may attach to still other incorporeal things; for it would be plainly absurd to say, that there could be an incorporeal right of property to a corporeal thing, but could be no incorporeal right of property to an incorporeal thing."

**Quick Take**: Property rights have never been limited to physical objects. Our entire legal and economic system already recognizes countless forms of incorporeal property, making intellectual property the logical extension of established principles.

### Knowledge Doesn't Transfer Ownership
One of Spooner's most powerful arguments is that communicating an idea doesn't transfer ownership rights, any more than showing someone your house transfers ownership of the house. He argues that mere possession or knowledge of an idea doesn't convey property rights without explicit consent.

> "The admission, therefore, that a man owns an idea, as property, while it is in his exclusive possession, is an admission that he owns it forever after, in whosesoever possession it may be, until he has consented to part, not merely with his exclusive possession, but also with his right of property in it."

> "If one man give to another the possession of a corporeal commodity, of so large value as a horse, a house, or a farm, without receiving any equivalent, and without specially making known that he also gives the right of property in it, the legal presumption clearly would be, that he did not intend to give the right of property."

**Quick Take**: Sharing knowledge shouldn't mean surrendering ownership. Just as letting someone inspect your house doesn't transfer ownership, letting someone learn your idea doesn't transfer your property rights in that idea.

### Perpetual Rights Are Essential for Progress
Spooner argues that only perpetual intellectual property rights can properly incentivize innovation and creativity. Limited terms, he contends, represent an arbitrary seizure of property that discourages the production of valuable ideas.

> "The conclusions, that follow from the principles now established, obviously are, that a man has a natural and absolute right—and if a natural and absolute, then necessarily a perpetual, right—of property, in the ideas, of which he is the discoverer or creator."

> "If men cannot be protected in bringing their valuable ideas into the market, they will either not produce them, or will keep them concealed as far as possible, and strive to realize some profit by using them as far as they can, in private."

**Quick Take**: Creators need the assurance of permanent rights to justify investing time and resources into developing valuable ideas. Temporary protections create uncertainty and discourage innovation.

## Best Quotes

- "Every thing—whether intellectual, moral, or material, however gross, or however subtile; whether tangible or intangible, perceptible or imperceptible, by our physical organs—of which the human mind can take cognizance, and which, either as a means, occasion, or end, can either contribute to, or of itself constitute, the well-being of man, is wealth."

- "The same principle governs in every department and variety of industry. An idea is every where and always the guide of labor, in the production and acquisition of wealth; and the idea, that guides labor, in the production or acquisition of wealth, is itself as obviously wealth, as is the labor, or as is any other instrumentality, agency, object, or thing whatever, whether material or immaterial, that aids in the production or acquisition of wealth."

- "The right of property itself is an incorporeality. The right of property is a mere incorporeal right of dominion, or control, over a thing. It is neither tangible by the hand, nor visible by the eye. It is a mere abstraction, existing only in contemplation of the mind. Yet this incorporeal right of dominion or control over a thing, is itself a subject of property—of ownership; one that is continually bought and sold in the market, independently of possession of the thing to which it relates."

- "But for the thought, that guides the hand, the commodity would not be produced; the labor of the hand would be fruitless, and therefore valueless."

- "It would be no more absurd or atrocious, in policy, or in law, to deprive a man of his right of property in a valuable material commodity, as a penalty for exhibiting or offering that commodity to the public, than it is to deprive a man of his right of property in a valuable idea, as a penalty for bringing that idea to the knowledge of the public."

## Key Takeaways
- Ideas represent the highest form of wealth and the foundation of all human progress
- Property rights naturally extend to incorporeal things, including ideas
- Communicating an idea doesn't transfer ownership rights without explicit consent
- Perpetual intellectual property rights are essential for incentivizing innovation
- Natural law provides stronger protection for creators than current statutory systems
- The right to property in ideas is identical in principle to the right to property in physical objects

## Conclusion
Spooner's "Intellectual Property Law" is a challenging and thought-provoking work that forces readers to reconsider fundamental assumptions about property, creativity, and rights. While his argument for perpetual rights may seem extreme by modern standards, his systematic defense of intellectual property based on natural law principles remains powerful and compelling.

The book's greatest strength is its rigorous logical foundation - Spooner builds his case step by step, starting with basic definitions of wealth and property, then extending these principles to intellectual creations. His arguments about the incorporeal nature of all property rights are particularly insightful and remain relevant to contemporary debates about digital assets and virtual property.

This work is essential reading for anyone serious about understanding the philosophical foundations of intellectual property. Whether you agree with Spooner's conclusions or not, his reasoning provides a robust framework for debating these crucial issues. In an age where intellectual property has become increasingly important yet increasingly contested, Spooner's natural law approach offers valuable insights that transcend the utilitarian arguments that dominate modern IP discourse.

The book is worth your time if you're interested in libertarian philosophy, property rights theory, or the fundamental justification for intellectual property protection. Spooner's arguments, though written over 160 years ago, speak directly to contemporary challenges in the digital age and provide a principled foundation for thinking about creators' rights in the 21st century.
