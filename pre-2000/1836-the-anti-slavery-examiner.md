# The Anti-Slavery Examiner
**Authors**: American Anti-Slavery Society | **Year**: 1836
**Category**: History & Biography

## Summary
The Anti-Slavery Examiner is a collection of powerful abolitionist pamphlets that formed the intellectual and moral foundation of the American anti-slavery movement. Published in 1836, this groundbreaking work combines constitutional analysis, biblical interpretation, and passionate appeals to human conscience to expose slavery as a profound violation of both divine and natural law. The pamphlets systematically dismantle pro-slavery arguments while exposing how slavery corrupts not only the enslaved but the slaveholders themselves, undermining the very foundations of American democracy and Christian morality.

This collection is essential reading for students of American history, political philosophy, and moral theology. It offers profound insights into how moral arguments can be wielded for social justice and demonstrates the power of principled resistance to systemic injustice. Readers will gain a deeper understanding of the intellectual forces that eventually led to the abolition of slavery and the timeless principles underlying human rights movements.

## Core Insights

### Slavery Reduces Humans to Property
The fundamental argument throughout the pamphlets is that slavery's core evil lies in transforming persons into things—sinking immortal beings into merchandise. This violation represents the ultimate form of theft: not merely taking what a person owns, but taking the person themselves. The authors argue that this reduction of human beings to articles of property strikes at the very foundation of morality, law, and human dignity.

> "ENSLAVING MEN IS REDUCING THEM TO ARTICLES OF PROPERTY, making free agents chattels, converting persons into things, sinking intelligence, accountability, immortality, into merchandise... MAN sunk to a thing! the intrinsic element, the principle of slavery; MEN sold, bartered, leased, mortgaged, bequeathed, invoiced, shipped in cargoes, stored as goods, taken on executions, and knocked off at public outcry!"

**Quick Take**: Whenever we treat people as mere means to an end rather than ends in themselves, we perpetuate the same fundamental injustice as slavery.

### The Bible Condemns Slavery
The authors systematically dismantle biblical justifications for slavery, arguing that Scripture explicitly condemns man-stealing with the death penalty—the same punishment as murder. They demonstrate that the Bible consistently separates humans from property, with God's highest law protecting the eternal distinction between persons and things created in God's image.

> "He that stealeth a man and selleth him, or if he be found in his hand, he shall surely be put to death... God's cherubim and flaming sword guarding the entrance to the Mosaic system!"

**Quick Take**: The Bible's consistent message is that humans bear God's image and therefore can never legitimately be owned by other humans.

### Slavery Destroys Constitutional Rights
The pamphlets expose how slavery undermines fundamental American liberties, particularly the right of petition and freedom of speech. The authors document how Congress passed gag rules preventing discussion of slavery, violating the Constitution and betraying American principles of free government.

> "When the right of petition shall be broken down—when no whisper shalt be heard in Congress in behalf of human rights—when the press shall be muzzled, and the freedom of speech destroyed by gag-laws, then will the slaveholders announce, that TRANQUILLITY IS RESTORED TO THE PUBLIC MIND!"

**Quick Take**: Any system that requires suppression of free speech and petition to survive is fundamentally incompatible with democratic government.

### All Rights Are Connected
The authors make the crucial point that the rights of slaveholders and non-slaveholders cannot be separated. When the government violates the rights of any group, everyone's rights are endangered. Either all people are free, or all are slaves together—there is no middle ground.

> "We must be all free, or all slaves together."

**Quick Take**: Injustice against any group threatens justice for all groups. The defense of others' rights is essential to protecting our own.

### Christianity Requires Immediate Emancipation
The collection includes powerful appeals to Christian women and all believers, arguing that true Christianity cannot coexist with slavery. The authors contend that Christian love and slaveholding are mutually exclusive, requiring believers to choose between their faith and the institution of slavery.

> "Like other unclean spirits, it 'hateth the light, neither cometh to the light, lest its deeds should be reproved.' Goaded to phrenzy in its conflicts with conscience and common sense, denied all quarter, and hunted from every covert, it breaks at last into the sacred enclosure, and courses up and down the Bible, 'seeking rest, and finding none.'"

**Quick Take**: True religious faith demands active opposition to systems that violate human dignity and divine principles.

## Best Quotes

- "The eighth commandment forbids the taking of any part of that which belongs to another. Slavery takes the whole. Does the same Bible which forbids the taking of any thing belonging to him, sanction the taking of every thing?"

- "If one man's title is valid, all are valid. If one is worthless, all are. To deny the validity of the slave's title is to deny the validity of his own; and yet in the act of making him a slave, the slaveholder asserts the validity of his own title, while he seizes him as his property who has the same title."

- "Slavery is the highest possible violation of the eighth commandment. To take from a man his earnings, is theft. But to take the earner, is compound, superlative, perpetual theft."

- "Whatever system sinks man from an END to a means, or in other words, whatever transforms him from an object of instrumentality into a mere instrumentality to an object, just so far makes him a slave."

- "The eternal distinction between a person and a thing, trampled under foot—the crowning distinction of all others—their centre and circumference—the source, the test, and the measure of their value."

## Action Items

- **Speak Truth to Power**: Follow the authors' example in using reasoned argument and moral appeals to challenge injustice, even when facing opposition.

- **Protect Free Speech**: Defend the rights of all people to petition government and speak freely, recognizing these rights as essential to democracy.

- **Recognize Human Dignity**: Treat every person as an end in themselves, never as a mere means to your own objectives or profit.

- **Stand in Solidarity**: Understand that rights are interconnected—defending the rights of others protects your own freedoms.

- **Apply Moral Principles**: Use consistent moral reasoning across all issues, avoiding special pleading for economically or socially convenient injustices.

## Conclusion

The Anti-Slavery Examiner is essential reading because it demonstrates how systematic moral argumentation can challenge and eventually overturn deeply entrenched injustice. These pamphlets show that the battle against slavery was not fought with weapons but with words—carefully reasoned, biblically grounded, and passionately articulated arguments that exposed slavery's fundamental incompatibility with American democracy, Christian morality, and human dignity.

The biggest reason to read this collection is its timeless relevance. The principles articulated—human rights, the connection between all freedoms, the responsibility to speak against injustice, and the moral imperative to treat people as ends rather than means—continue to illuminate contemporary struggles for justice. In an age when new forms of exploitation and oppression emerge, these 19th-century arguments provide a powerful framework for recognizing and resisting all violations of human dignity. The Anti-Slavery Examiner reminds us that moral consistency and courageous speaking of truth remain our most powerful weapons against injustice.
