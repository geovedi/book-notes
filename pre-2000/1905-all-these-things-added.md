# All These Things Added
**Authors**: James Allen | **Year**: 1905
**Category**: Spirituality & Religion

## Summary
"All These Things Added" is a profound spiritual guide that reveals how true peace and fulfillment come not from pursuing material possessions or worldly success, but from finding the "Kingdom of Heaven" within through righteousness and self-mastery. James Allen presents a timeless philosophy showing that when we stop seeking earthly pleasures and heavenly rewards, we discover the Temple of Righteousness and find the Kingdom of Heaven through self-surrender and love.

This book is ideal for spiritual seekers, those feeling discontent despite material success, and anyone looking to transform their life through inner wisdom rather than external achievement. Readers will learn how to overcome selfishness, transcend competitive thinking, and discover lasting peace through spiritual principles.

## Core Insights

### The Soul's Deep Hunger for Righteousness
Every human soul experiences an inexpressible hunger that material possessions and worldly success can never satisfy. This spiritual need drives us to seek fulfillment in various ways—some consciously, others unconsciously. While the majority mistakenly try to satisfy this hunger through wealth, status, and pleasure, those who understand its true nature recognize it as a longing for righteousness—the permanent principle that brings lasting peace.

> "Every soul, consciously or unconsciously, hungers for righteousness, and every soul seeks to gratify that hunger in its own particular way, and in accordance with its own particular state of knowledge."

**Quick Take**: Your deepest fulfillment won't come from external achievements but from discovering and embodying timeless principles of integrity, compassion, and love.

### Abundance Creates Competition, Not Scarcity
One of Allen's most counterintuitive insights is that competition doesn't arise from scarcity but from abundance. Birds fighting over abundant food illustrate how luxury and excess breed strife rather than contentment. Similarly, nations become more competitive as they grow richer, with people fighting more fiercely for necessities and luxuries when there's more than enough to go around.

> "It is not scarcity that produces competition, it is abundance; so that the richer and more luxurious a nation becomes, the keener and fiercer becomes the competition for securing the necessaries and luxuries of life."

**Quick Take**: The more you have, the more you tend to want—practice gratitude and contentment to break free from competitive thinking.

### Selfishness: The Root Cause of All Suffering
Allen identifies selfishness as the fundamental cause of all competition, strife, and suffering. This includes not just obvious forms like greed but also subtle manifestations like self-protection, ego attachment, and the desire to be right. Every attempt to reform external systems without addressing selfishness will ultimately fail, because the root cause remains untouched.

> "Selfishness, then, is the root cause of competition, the foundation on which all competitive systems rest, and the sustaining source of the competitive laws."

**Quick Take**: Focus on transforming your own character rather than trying to fix others or change external systems—inner work creates lasting outer change.

### The Three Gateways to Spiritual Freedom
Allen outlines a precise pathway to spiritual liberation through three "Gateways of Surrender": First, surrendering desires by mastering appetites and impulses; second, surrendering opinions by letting go of mental attachments and ego; and third, surrendering self-interest completely. Each gate requires conscious effort and brings new challenges, but passing through them leads to increasing freedom and wisdom.

> "On his way to that goal, he must pass through three Gateways of Surrender. The first is the Surrender of Desire; the second is the Surrender of Opinion; the third is the Surrender of Self."

**Quick Take**: True freedom comes progressively—start by controlling your desires, then your opinions, and finally your self-attachment.

### Living in the Divine Center
The ultimate secret to abundant life is discovering and abiding in the "Divine Center" within yourself—the changeless core that transcends time and death. Instead of living in the outer circumference of disturbances, cravings, and arguments, you can find refuge in your inner spiritual heart where peace, wisdom, and love reside eternally.

> "The secret of life, of abundant life, with its strength, its felicity, and its unbroken peace is to find the Divine Centre within oneself, and to live in and from that, instead of in that outer circumference of disturbances."

**Quick Take**: Your greatest power and peace come from accessing your inner spiritual center rather than reacting to external circumstances.

## Best Quotes

- "Blessed are they that mourn—that have come to the end of strife, and have found the pain and sorrow to which it leads; for unto them, and unto them only, can open the door which leads to the Kingdom of Peace."

- "The building of pleasant houses and the planting of beautiful gardens can never, of itself, constitute a Garden City unless its inhabitants have learned that self-sacrifice is better than self-protection, and have first established in their own hearts the Garden City of unselfish love."

- "Therefore take no thought, saying 'What shall we eat?' or, 'What shall we drink?' or, 'Wherewithal shall we be clothed?' For your heavenly Father knoweth ye have need of all these things. But seek ye first the Kingdom of God, and His Righteousness, and all these things shall be added unto you."

- "Forgoing self, the universe grows I": and he whose nature is compassion, wisdom and love cannot possibly need any protection; for those Principles themselves constitute the highest protection."

- "The redeemed sons of God, the glorified in body and spirit, are 'bought with a price,' and that price is the crucifixion of the personality, the death of self."

## Action Items

- **Practice Self-Examination**: Regularly examine your motives, desires, and thoughts to identify subtle forms of selfishness that may be driving your behavior.

- **Master Your Desires**: Begin with small acts of self-discipline—regulating eating, sleeping, and indulgent habits to build spiritual strength.

- **Surrender Opinions**: Practice letting go of the need to be right, defending your views, and judging others' perspectives.

- **Live in the Present**: Focus on embodying your highest ideals now rather than postponing spiritual growth to some future time.

- **Cultivate Compassion**: Actively work to understand and empathize with others, especially those you might normally compete with or judge.

## Questions to Consider

- What desires, opinions, or aspects of your self-identity are you clinging to that might be preventing true spiritual freedom?

- How has the pursuit of abundance actually increased your competitive feelings rather than bringing contentment?

- In what areas of your life are you trying to fix external problems while ignoring the internal selfishness that might be their root cause?

- What would it look like to live from your "Divine Center" rather than reacting to outer circumstances?

- Which of the Three Gateways of Surrender do you need to focus on right now in your spiritual journey?

## Conclusion
"All These Things Added" offers a profound roadmap for spiritual transformation that is as relevant today as when it was written over a century ago. Allen's central message—that true fulfillment comes from inner righteousness rather than outer achievement—challenges our culture's relentless focus on success and accumulation.

The book's greatest strength lies in its clear, systematic approach to spiritual growth. By identifying selfishness as the root cause of suffering and providing a specific pathway through the Three Gateways, Allen makes profound spiritual wisdom accessible and actionable. His insight about abundance creating competition rather than scarcity is particularly relevant in our age of unprecedented material wealth and psychological distress.

This book is definitely worth your time if you're feeling a sense of spiritual emptiness despite external success, or if you're seeking a deeper understanding of how inner transformation leads to outer peace. The biggest reason to read it is Allen's promise that when you stop seeking "things" and instead discover the Kingdom within, all these things will naturally be added to your life—not as the primary goal, but as the natural consequence of living in harmony with divine principles.

While some readers may find Allen's language formal and his spiritual concepts challenging, the book offers timeless wisdom that can transform your relationship with yourself, others, and the world around you. It's a powerful antidote to the competitive, achievement-driven mindset that dominates modern culture.
