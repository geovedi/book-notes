# The Glimpses of the Moon
**Authors**: Edith Wharton | **Year**: 1922
**Category**: Fiction & Literature

## Summary
"The Glimpses of the Moon" tells the story of Nick Lansing and Susy Branch, two attractive but impoverished members of New York's high society who marry for convenience with an unusual agreement: they'll stay together for one year, living off borrowed money and their friends' generosity, while each reserves the right to leave if a better financial prospect appears. Their honeymoon begins at a borrowed villa on Lake Como, where they initially find genuine happiness and companionship beyond their pragmatic arrangement.

As the year progresses, their unconventional marriage faces severe tests. Nick is offered a lucrative position as secretary to the wealthy Ellie Vanderlyn, while Susy is pursued by their friend Strefford, who unexpectedly inherits a title and fortune. Tensions mount as financial pressures and social temptations strain their relationship. The couple separates when Nick accepts the position with Ellie, believing Susy will inevitably choose wealth and status with Strefford. During their time apart, both achieve material success but find themselves hollow and incomplete without each other.

This novel will appeal to readers who enjoy sophisticated social commentary, complex character studies, and stories that explore the tension between romantic love and financial necessity in high society. Wharton fans will appreciate her sharp wit and psychological insight into the compromises people make for money and status.

## Fast-Track Story Guide

### The Setup: A Marriage of Convenience
Nick Lansing and Susy Branch, both penniless but socially connected, enter a pragmatic marriage agreement. They'll spend one year together living on borrowed luxury, each free to leave if a wealthy prospect appears. Their honeymoon at Strefford's borrowed villa on Lake Como begins as pure bliss, with moonlit nights, stolen cigars, and genuine emotional connection that neither expected from their arrangement.

**Key Characters Introduced**:
- **Nick Lansing**: An intelligent but impoverished writer with good social standing and romantic ideals
- **Susy Branch**: A beautiful, pragmatic socialite who has learned to survive by relying on wealthy friends
- **Strefford**: Their impecunious aristocratic friend who unexpectedly becomes Earl of Altringham
- **Ellie Vanderlyn**: A wealthy society woman who offers Nick a lucrative position

### The Turning Point: Moral and Financial Cracks
The marriage begins to unravel when Nick accepts a position as secretary to Ellie Vanderlyn, effectively becoming her paid companion. The arrangement requires Nick to facilitate Ellie's affair while maintaining social appearances—a compromise that damages his integrity and his marriage. Susy, meanwhile, grows closer to Strefford, who has suddenly inherited a fortune and title. The breaking point comes when Nick discovers Susy has pawned a friend's bracelet to cover expenses, confirming his fears that their poverty will force her into morally compromising situations.

> "The fates seem to have taken our future in hand, and spared us the trouble of unravelling it."
> _Original: "The fates seem to have taken our future in hand, and spared us the trouble of unravelling it."_

### The Separation: Success Without Happiness
The couple separates amicably, each achieving financial security but finding it hollow. Nick travels with Ellie as her secretary, while Susy spends time with the now-wealthy Strefford. Despite their material comfort and social success, both feel increasingly empty and incomplete without each other. Wharton masterfully contrasts the glittering social world with the internal loneliness of her protagonists.

### The Reconciliation: Love Over Wealth
The turning point comes when Nick and Susy meet again in Paris. Susy is caring for five children from another family (the Fulmers) while Nick has just earned $200 from his writing. Their reunion feels natural and necessary, despite the complications. Susy confesses she pawned Ellie Vanderlyn's bracelet to help cover expenses during their difficult time. This honest admission, rather than driving them apart, brings them together as they face their financial future with mutual understanding and renewed commitment.

> "We'll have to manage somehow. And we'll have to manage without... making the same kind of mistakes."
> _Original: "We'll have to manage somehow. And we'll have to manage without... making the same kind of mistakes."_

### The Resolution: A New Beginning
The novel ends with Nick and Susy reunited but facing a challenging future. They begin a "second honeymoon" in Fontainebleau with five children in tow, embracing a more authentic but less luxurious life together. The final image shows them standing together as clouds briefly reveal the moon—symbolizing that their love, like glimpses of the moon, may be intermittent but remains constant. Their journey from a marriage of convenience to genuine love represents Wharton's commentary on the possibility of finding authenticity within a superficial society.

## Takeaways

- True love often develops from unexpected beginnings and requires overcoming practical obstacles
- Financial pressure can test even the strongest relationships and force difficult moral choices
- Social status and material comfort cannot replace genuine emotional connection
- Honesty and mutual understanding are essential foundations for lasting relationships
- Sometimes losing everything material is necessary to gain what truly matters

## Conclusion
**Is this worth your time?** Absolutely. "The Glimpses of the Moon" showcases Edith Wharton at her finest—blending sharp social satire with deep psychological insight and genuine emotional depth. While the novel explores familiar themes of love versus money in high society, Wharton's nuanced character development and moral complexity elevate it beyond conventional romance.

**The biggest reason to read this book** is Wharton's masterful exploration of how love can blossom in the most unpromising circumstances. Nick and Susy's journey from a marriage of convenience to genuine partnership offers hope that authentic connection can survive—and even thrive despite—the pressures of social expectations and financial necessity. The ending suggests that glimpses of true happiness, like glimpses of the moon, may be fleeting but remain worth pursuing.

This novel particularly resonates for its honest portrayal of how financial struggles can test relationships and force difficult moral choices. Wharton doesn't offer easy answers but presents her characters' struggles with empathy and psychological depth, creating a story that feels both of its time and timeless in its understanding of human nature.
