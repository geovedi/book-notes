# Mornings in Mexico
**Authors**: D. H. Lawrence | **Year**: 1927 (Original publication), 2017 (This edition)
**Category**: Fiction & Literature

## Summary
"Mornings in Mexico" is D.H. Lawrence's vivid travel memoir capturing his experiences in a small Mexican town, where he observes daily life, native customs, and the profound relationship between people and their environment. Through eight beautifully crafted essays, Lawrence explores everything from the antics of pet parrots to ancient ceremonial dances, offering intimate glimpses into Mexican culture, landscape, and spiritual life.

This book is perfect for travelers, cultural enthusiasts, and readers who appreciate poetic, observational writing about place and people. You'll gain a deep appreciation for Mexico's rich traditions, its natural beauty, and Lawrence's unique ability to find the universal in the particular.

## Core Insights

### The Sacred in the Ordinary
Lawrence discovers profound meaning in everyday Mexican life, from the morning rituals in his garden patio to the complex social dynamics of the market place. He shows how Mexican culture seamlessly blends the practical with the spiritual, finding sacred significance in ordinary activities like corn grinding, flower buying, and even the mocking calls of parrots.

> "Still, it is morning, and it is Mexico. The sun shines. But then, during the winter, it always shines. It is pleasant to sit out of doors and write, just fresh enough and just warm enough."
**Quick Take**: True cultural understanding comes from observing daily life rather than grand tourist attractions.

### The Living Connection to Land and Tradition
Lawrence is deeply moved by how Mexican Indians maintain their ancient connection to the earth and their cultural traditions, even as modernity encroaches. He describes market days not just as commercial exchanges but as sacred gatherings where human contact matters more than money.

> "To buy and to sell, but above all, to commingle. In the old world, men make themselves two great excuses for coming together to a centre, and commingling freely in a mixed, unsuspicious host. Market and religion."
**Quick Take**: Traditional societies preserve wisdom through rituals that connect past and present.

### The Dance of Life and Death
The ceremonial dances Lawrence witnesses, particularly the Dance of the Sprouting Corn, reveal a sophisticated understanding of life's cycles. These aren't performances but living prayers that connect the community to cosmic forces, agricultural cycles, and the mysteries of germination and resurrection.

> "And the mystery of germination, not procreation, but putting forth, resurrection, life springing within the seed, is accomplished. The sky has its fire, its waters, its stars, its wandering electricity, its winds, its fingers of cold."
**Quick Take**: Ancient cultures often understood ecological and spiritual connections that modern society has lost.

### The Power of Observation
Lawrence's own method reveals the value of patient, attentive observation. By sitting quietly on his veranda and watching the parrots mock the dog, or following Indians to market, he discovers insights that would be missed by hurried travelers.

> "One says Mexico: one means, after all, one little town away South in the Republic: and in this little town, one rather crumbly adobe house built round two sides of a garden patio: and of this house, one spot on the deep, shady veranda facing inwards to the trees."
**Quick Take**: Deep cultural understanding requires stillness and attention to small details.

### The Cycles of Worlds
Lawrence challenges Western linear thinking with his fascination for Aztec cosmology and the concept of successive worlds. He sees in Mexican culture a more organic understanding of time, destruction, and renewal that contrasts sharply with European notions of progress.

> "I prefer to believe in what the Aztecs called Suns: that is, Worlds successively created and destroyed. The sun itself convulses, and the worlds go out like so many candles when somebody coughs in the middle of them."
**Quick Take**: Different cultures offer fundamentally different ways of understanding reality that can expand our own perspectives.

- "The straight course is hacked out in wounds, against the will of the world."

- "They are dressed in snow-white cotton, and they lift their knees in the Indian trot, following the ass where the woman sits perched between the huge baskets, her child tight in the rebozo, at the brown breast."

- "Mindless, without effort, under the hot sun, unceasing, yet never perspiring nor even breathing heavily, they dance on and on."

- "The centavos are an excuse. It is life. The contact, so short even, brisked her up."

## Key Takeaways

- **Slow down and observe**: Lawrence's insights come from patient watching rather than active seeking
- **Find sacredness in daily life**: Mexican culture shows how ordinary activities can have profound meaning
- **Respect traditional wisdom**: Ancient practices often contain sophisticated knowledge
- **Cultural immersion beats tourism**: Real understanding comes from living like locals, not just visiting
- **Nature and culture are interconnected**: Mexican traditions show deep awareness of ecological relationships

## Questions to Consider

- What can we learn from cultures that maintain strong connections to ancestral traditions?
- How does Lawrence's observational approach compare to modern travel writing?
- In what ways do our own cultural assumptions limit our understanding of other societies?
- How might the Mexican concept of time and cycles enrich our perspective on life?
