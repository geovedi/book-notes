# 1751-Treatise-on-Oratory

**Original Title**: Verhandeling over de redenvoering
**Authors**: Francis de la Fontaine | **Year**: 1751
**Category**: Art & Creativity

## Summary
This remarkable 18th-century Dutch treatise on oratory offers a comprehensive exploration of the art of public speaking and theatrical performance across Europe. Written by Francis de la Fontaine, this work provides both practical guidance for speakers and a fascinating survey of theatrical traditions from Italy to England. The author examines the essential connection between emotional authenticity and technical skill, arguing that true oratory requires both natural gifts and disciplined practice. Beyond instruction, the book serves as a valuable cultural document capturing the state of European theater and rhetorical practice at the dawn of the Enlightenment.

This guide will benefit public speakers, actors, theater enthusiasts, and anyone interested in 18th-century European culture. Readers will gain practical insights into effective communication while discovering how theatrical traditions shaped public discourse across different nations.

## Core Insights

### The Foundation of Effective Oratory

De la Fontaine establishes that true oratory begins with emotional authenticity—what he calls "Verrukking" (rapture or emotional transport). He argues that speakers must feel deeply what they wish to convey, as genuine emotion naturally produces the right tones, gestures, and expressions. This principle applies equally to preachers, lawyers, actors, and anyone who addresses an audience.

> "The art of oratory consists in adding to different speech the expression of movement, to make the force of your thoughts felt."

The author emphasizes that oratory is one of the most difficult arts because it requires the entire being—voice, eyes, gestures, and spirit—to work in harmony. Unlike other crafts where the hands can work while the mind wanders, oratory demands complete concentration of all faculties.

### The Three Pillars of Expression

De la Fontaine identifies three essential elements of effective delivery:

**1. The Voice**: Natural speech with appropriate modulation, avoiding artificial theatrics. He criticizes speakers who use affected tones, shouting, or unnatural elongation of words. The voice should rise and fall naturally with the emotional content.

**2. The Eyes**: The most expressive feature after the voice. Eyes can convey fear, shame, joy, sorrow, and countless emotions without words. The author strongly advises against speakers closing their eyes during delivery, as this cuts off a vital channel of communication.

**3. Gestures**: Natural movement of arms and body that flows from emotional engagement. He notes that some people naturally possess graceful gestures while others must work to minimize awkward movements. The key is that gestures should arise from genuine feeling rather than calculated affectation.

> "A perfect orator, preacher, or actor, deprived of the advantage of noble stature and attractive grace, loses much of his merit."

### Different Styles for Different Occasions

The treatise carefully distinguishes between three types of formal address:

**Sermons (Predikatie)**: Should be characterized by "Drift" (zeal/earnestness), with simple but compelling tones. The sacred subject matter requires dignity without theatrical excess. He criticizes preachers who use street language or inappropriate humor to gain attention.

**Eulogies (Lofreden)**: Dominated by "Verwondering" (wonder/awe), mixed with appropriate emotion. The speaker should elevate the deceased's virtues while inspiring listeners.

**Mourningspeeches (Rouwklacht)**: Governed by "Droefheyd" (sorrow), though it may incorporate elements of the other two tones when appropriate.

Each style requires its own emotional tone, though skilled speakers may blend them when the occasion demands.

### The European Theater Survey

The second half of the book provides a fascinating comparative study of theatrical traditions across major European nations:

**Italian Theater**: Known for passionate audiences who express approval loudly with "Viva!" and disapproval with "Va dentro!" (get out!). Italian theaters are architectural marvels with multiple tiers of boxes, seating up to 1,400 people. The author admires their versatility, noting how actors can switch from comedy to tragedy, speaking perfect Roman verses.

**Spanish Theater**: Produces enormous quantities of plays—Lope de Vega alone wrote 1,500 plays, 312 of which survive. Their unique "Autos Sacramentales" are allegorical religious plays where abstract concepts like Memory, Will, and the Church appear as characters. Theaters are nearly square with distinctive seating arrangements.

**French Theater**: The most refined and regulated, achieving perfection through strict adherence to classical rules. The author credits Molière with revolutionizing comedy, though he notes that French tragedy didn't reach its height until Corneille and Racine. French theaters maintain strict discipline—audience members face imprisonment for disrupting performances.

**English Theater**: Characterized by spectacular violence and supernatural elements. Shakespeare's plays feature multiple deaths, graveyards with real skulls, and intense emotional scenes. The author suggests this reflects the English character—contemplative and brooding, requiring shocking elements to stir them from deep thought.

**German Theater**: Still developing in 1751, heavily influenced by Dutch and French models. Their plays retain medieval elements with graphic depictions of martyrdom and torture. The author mentions a recent Passion play in Vienna that was so realistic it was banned for blasphemy.

**Dutch Theater**: Originates from "Redenrykers Kameren" (rhetorician chambers)—civic organizations dedicated to poetry and performance. These groups competed in elaborate contests and played a crucial role in developing Dutch literary culture. Dutch audiences prefer spectacular effects over subtle French-style refinement.

### Cultural Character Reflected in Theater

One of the most insightful aspects of de la Fontaine's analysis is how he connects theatrical styles to national character:

- **Italians**: Passionate and expressive, both in performance and audience response
- **Spanish**: Proud and secretive, with a taste for honor and religious allegory
- **French**: Intelligent, lively, and refined, valuing classical perfection
- **English**: Melancholy and intellectual, requiring violent stimulation
- **Germans**: Still finding their voice, transitioning from medieval to modern
- **Dutch**: Practical and direct, preferring spectacular effects over subtlety

This analysis demonstrates how theater serves as a mirror reflecting cultural values and temperament.

## Best Quotes

- "The art of oratory consists in adding to different speech the expression of movement, to make the force of your thoughts felt."
  _Original: "Die bestaet in te voegen by een verschillende Uytspraek d'uytdruckking der Beweging, om de kracht van uw Gedachten te doen gevoelen."_

- "Without the dumb speech of the eyes, words alone are not sufficient for expression."
  _Original: "zelf kan men zeggen dat zonder de stomme Spraek der Oogen de Woorden niet genoeg zyn tot d'uytdrukking."_

- "The art of a preacher consists in leading the senses of his listening assembly."
  _Original: "De Konst van een Predikant bestaet in de verleyding der zinnen van syn toehoorende Vergadering."_

- "Oratory is an art that requires that all the parts which the Divine Worker made for the composition of the human body be helpful to it."
  _Original: "De Redenvoering is een Konst die vereyscht dat allen de deelen die den goddelycke Werker tot de zaemenstelling van het menschelyk lichaem gemaekt heeft, aen de zelve behulpzaem zyn."_

- "Lof and Loon are the movers of spirits."
  _Original: "Lof en Loon zyn de beweegers der geesten."_

## Action Items

- **Practice emotional authenticity**: Before speaking, connect deeply with your message. Genuine feeling naturally produces appropriate expression in voice, eyes, and gestures.

- **Master gradual progression**: Begin speeches with moderate intensity and build naturally, saving your strongest vocal delivery for key moments rather than starting at maximum volume.

- **Study your audience**: Different audiences require different approaches. Consider whether your listeners need intellectual stimulation, emotional appeal, or practical benefit.

- **Read widely in multiple languages**: The author notes that Dutch is particularly rich in expressive words for tragedy, suggesting that multilingual knowledge enhances rhetorical capabilities.

- **Learn from observation**: Watch skilled speakers and actors, analyzing how they coordinate voice, eyes, and gestures to create unified emotional impact.

- **Avoid artificiality**: Reject exaggerated gestures, affected speech patterns, or theatrical tricks. Natural expression born from genuine feeling always proves more effective.

## Conclusion
De la Fontaine's "Treatise on Oratory" remains remarkably relevant despite being written over 270 years ago. His fundamental insight—that effective speaking requires the integration of emotional authenticity with technical skill—transcends historical context. The book serves as both a practical guide for anyone who speaks publicly and a fascinating window into 18th-century European culture.

What makes this work particularly valuable is its comprehensive scope, covering not just the mechanics of speech but also providing a detailed survey of theatrical traditions across Europe. De la Fontaine's observations about how national character shapes artistic preferences demonstrate sophisticated cultural understanding that feels surprisingly modern.

The treatise is worth your time not merely for its historical interest but for its practical wisdom about human communication. In an age of artificial amplification and digital mediation, his emphasis on authentic emotional connection reminds us that effective speaking ultimately depends on genuine human feeling expressed through natural means. His insights about the relationship between speaker and audience, the importance of cultural context, and the integration of mind, body, and spirit in communication remain as valuable today as they were in 1751.
