# The Crimes of Love
**Original Title**: Les Crimes de l'Amour | **Authors**: Marquis de Sade | **Year**: 1800

## Summary
A collection of five tragic tales that explore the destructive power of love and passion in 18th-century aristocratic society. Unlike Sade's more infamous works, these stories present a moralistic worldview where vice is ultimately punished and virtue frequently prevails. Written during his twelve-year imprisonment, these "heroic and tragic tales" represent Sade's attempt to rehabilitate his literary reputation while examining how love—intended to bring happiness—can lead to crime, betrayal, and death.

**Perfect for**: Readers interested in Gothic literature, moral philosophy, and the psychological complexity of passion, as well as those seeking a more accessible introduction to Sade's work outside his notorious erotic writings.

## Core Insights

### Love's Criminal Potential
Sade explores how love, supposedly society's most noble emotion, can transform into a destructive force that drives ordinary people to extraordinary crimes. Each story demonstrates the thin line between passion and obsession, between devotion and destruction. The collection asks why men "abuse everything" and why the emotion meant to produce happiness instead brings suffering and death.

> "...never, in fact, and this I repeat, never will I paint crime other than in the colours of Hell. I want it to be seen in all its nakedness, to be feared, to be lothed..."
> _Original: "...jamais, en effet, et je le répète, jamais je ne peindrai le crime que dans les couleurs de l'enfer. Je veux qu'on le voie dans toute sa nudité, qu'on le craigne, qu'on le haïsse..."_

**Quick Take**: Love's greatest danger isn't hatred but its capacity to make us justify any action in its name.

### The Arranged Marriage System as Crime
Sade, who was forced into an arranged marriage at twenty-three, attacks the 18th-century system that traded daughters like property. In "Faxelange" and "Dorgeville," he shows how parental greed and social pressure create the conditions for tragedy. Parents become "greedy" and daughters "ambitious" in a system that values wealth and status over genuine affection.

**Quick Take**: Society's institutionalized crimes against the heart create the perfect breeding ground for personal betrayals.

### Incest and Forbidden Desire
Several stories explore incest as the ultimate transgression, reflecting both classical literary traditions and contemporary fascination with "primitive" societies. In "Dorgeville," a virtuous man accidentally marries his sister, while in "Laurence and Antonio," a father attempts to seduce his son's betrothed. These stories examine how forbidden desires destroy families and social order.

**Quick Take**: The most dangerous crimes are those that threaten the fundamental boundaries that hold society together.

### Virtue vs. Vice: Sade's Moral Reversal
Unlike Sade's major novels where evil triumphs, these stories usually end with "Virtue Rewarded." Villains like Franlo, Rodrigo, and the Comtesse de Sancerre come to bad ends, suggesting either genuine moral evolution or strategic self-preservation. Yet even in this moralistic framework, Sade's psychological insight into human depravity remains profound.

> "I do not wish to make vice liked... I want them to detest them."
> _Original: "Je ne veux point faire aimer le vice... Je veux qu'elles le détestent."_

**Quick Take**: Even when trying to be moral, Sade cannot resist exploring the psychological depths of human corruption.

### Women as Central Characters
Despite Sade's personal misogyny, these stories are dominated by complex female characters who drive the action. From innocent victims like Faxelange to dangerous predators like the Comtesse de Sancerre, women possess psychological depth that often overshadows their male counterparts. The Comtesse is compared to Racine's Phèdre in her tragic complexity.

**Quick Take**: Sade's most compelling characters are the women he simultaneously fears and admires.

## The Five Stories Explained

### 1. Faxelange or The Wrongs of Ambition
A young woman forced to abandon her true love for a wealthy aristocrat discovers her new husband is actually a criminal who murders her companion. She eventually escapes with her former lover's help, but not before bearing her criminal husband's child and dying of consumption, becoming "a sad and unhappy example of the avarice of parents and the ambition of young girls."

### 2. Dorgeville or The Criminal through Virtue
A virtuous businessman rescues a fallen woman and her child, then unknowingly marries her (she's his long-lost sister). When she's arrested for their incestuous marriage and executed, Dorgeville retreats to a monastery, "unable to destroy within himself... the feelings of benevolence and pity which made up his fine soul, or the excessive love which consumed him."

### 3. Rodrigo or The Enchanted Tower: An Allegorical Tale
A Spanish king abuses young women, including Florinda, who dies from his assault. She returns from the dead in disguise, defeats him in combat, and reveals she's a woman—his ultimate humiliation. A revenge fantasy where justice comes from beyond the grave.

### 4. Laurence and Antonio: An Italian Story
Set in Renaissance Italy, a father tries to seduce his son's betrothed while the son is away at war. The scheme fails, but when the son discovers his beloved in what appears to be an adulterous situation, tragedy ensues. A study in how jealousy and misunderstanding can destroy even the strongest love.

### 5. The Comtesse de Sancerre or Her Daughter's Rival
A noblewoman falls in love with her own daughter's fiancé. Her jealousy and desire lead her to manipulate events, ultimately causing her daughter's death and her lover's suicide. Compared to Phèdre, she represents destructive passion that cannot be contained by social convention.

## Best Quotes

- "Why does love, which was intended to produce happiness, bring about crimes?"
  _From Edward Young's Night Thoughts, which Sade used as an epigraph_

- "Reality among us continues to outdo fiction"
  _From Le Journal de Paris review, 1800_

- "These horrible depictions of crime can only make it more odious; they are therefore useless and dangerous"
  _Critic Villeterque's condemnation of Sade's work_

- "They are governed by their virtues. Neither laws nor sovereigns are needed for those who know no vices"
  _From Rodrigo, describing ideal governance_

## Questions to Consider

- Can true love exist within a system that treats marriage as economic transaction?
- Why do we find stories of virtue punished more compelling than virtue rewarded?
- Is Sade's moralism genuine or merely strategic self-preservation?
- How do these stories reflect the psychological insights gained from his long imprisonment?
- What does Sade's fascination with incest reveal about 18th-century anxieties about social boundaries?

## Conclusion
**Is this worth your time?** Absolutely, but not for the reasons you might expect. This isn't the transgressive, shocking Sade of legend, but a surprisingly moralistic writer exploring tragic themes with psychological depth. The stories function as Gothic cautionary tales about love's destructive potential when corrupted by social injustice and human weakness.

**The biggest reason to read it**: You'll discover a more complex, literary Sade who deserves recognition beyond his scandalous reputation. These stories demonstrate how even the most infamous minds can produce profound insights into human nature when forced to confront their own morality through the crucible of suffering and confinement. For readers interested in the evolution of Gothic literature and the psychology of passion, this collection offers invaluable perspective on one of history's most misunderstood writers.
