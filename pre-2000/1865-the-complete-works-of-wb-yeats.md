# 2015 The Complete Works of W.B. Yeats
**Authors**: W.B. Yeats | **Year**: 1865-1939 (Collected Works)
**Category**: Fiction & Literature

## Summary
This comprehensive collection spans Yeats' entire poetic career from his early romantic period to his later mystically-charged works. The volume includes major poetry collections like "The Wanderings of Oisin," "The Wind Among the Reeds," "The Tower," and "The Winding Stair," plus his complete dramatic works and prose. Yeats transforms from Ireland's romantic nationalist poet to one of the most profound and complex voices of 20th-century literature, exploring Irish mythology, spiritual mysticism, politics, love, and the human condition.

This collection offers readers a complete journey through one of literature's greatest minds, perfect for poetry lovers, those interested in Irish culture, and anyone seeking to understand how art evolves across a lifetime. You'll gain access to the evolution of modern poetry and Irish cultural identity through Yeats' masterful craft.

## Core Insights

### The Irish Mythological Foundation
Yeats built his early poetry on Irish folklore and mythology, creating a national literary identity. He drew from ancient Irish legends, fairy tales, and Gaelic traditions to establish Ireland's place in world literature. This wasn't mere decoration – Yeats believed these ancient stories contained essential truths about the Irish psyche and could help forge cultural independence from England.

> "I have spread my dreams under your feet; Tread softly because you tread on my dreams."
> _From "Aedh Wishes for the Cloths of Heaven"_

**Quick Take**: Your cultural heritage holds power for transformation – don't just preserve it, make it revolutionary.

### The Gyre: Cosmic Cycles and Historical Pattern
Yeats developed the concept of the gyre – a spiral or vortex that represents how history moves in 2,000-year cycles. This philosophical system explains the rise and fall of civilizations, suggesting that when one historical cycle reaches its end, its opposite begins. "The Second Coming" famously applies this theory to post-World War I Europe.

> "Turning and turning in the widening gyre / The falcon cannot hear the falconer; / Things fall apart; the centre cannot hold; / Mere anarchy is loosed upon the world..."

**Quick Take**: History repeats itself in spiraling patterns – understanding cycles helps you navigate chaotic transitions.

### Spiritual Evolution: From Romantic to Mystic
Yeats' poetry shows a remarkable evolution from romantic nationalism to complex spiritual mysticism. Influenced by his wife Georgie's automatic writing and his study of occult systems, he developed a sophisticated symbolic system connecting personal experience with universal forces. This journey shows how spiritual searching can deepen rather than diminish artistic power.

**Quick Take**: Creative evolution requires embracing mystery – your greatest growth may come from what you cannot fully explain.

### Politics Meets Personal: The Revolutionary Spirit
"Easter 1916" demonstrates Yeats' unique ability to weave political events with personal reflection and mystical insight. He captures the transformation of ordinary acquaintances into revolutionary martyrs, coining the paradoxical phrase "A terrible beauty is born." This poem shows how personal relationships intersect with historical forces.

> "All changed, changed utterly: / A terrible beauty is born."

**Quick Take**: History is made by people you know – revolutionary change comes from familiar faces transformed by conviction.

### Art as Immortality: Byzantium and Eternal Creation
In "Sailing to Byzantium," Yeasts explores art's power to transcend mortality and decay. The poem presents a journey from natural cycles of birth and death to the eternal realm of artistic creation. Byzantium represents a perfected civilization where art preserves human consciousness beyond physical limitations.

> "An aged man is but a paltry thing, / A tattered coat upon a stick, unless / Soul clap its hands and sing, and louder sing"

**Quick Take**: Art offers immortality through consciousness rather than physical preservation.

## Essential Reading Path

### 1. Early Romantic Period (1889-1899)
Start with "The Stolen Child," "Down by the Salley Gardens," "The Lake Isle of Innisfree," and "When You Are Old" – accessible poems showcasing Yeats' romantic nationalism and lyrical mastery.

### 2. Middle Period (1900-1917)
Progress to "The Tower" collection and political poems like "Easter 1916" – shows development toward complex symbolism and political engagement.

### 3. Late Mystical Period (1918-1939)
End with "Sailing to Byzantium," "The Second Coming," and "Leda and the Swan" – represents Yeats at his most profound, combining spiritual insight with technical brilliance.

## Key Themes to Track

- **Nature vs. Civilization**: Rural Irish landscapes representing spiritual purity vs. urban modernity
- **Time and Mortality**: Aging, memory, and the search for permanence
- **Love and Longing**: Both romantic and mystical dimensions of desire
- **Irish Identity**: The struggle for cultural and political independence
- **Spiritual Evolution**: The journey from material to mystical consciousness

- How does Yeats' mystical system inform his view of historical cycles?
- What role does Irish landscape play in Yeats' spiritual and political vision?
- How does the concept of "the tower" function as both physical location and psychological symbol?

## Conclusion
Is this worth your time? Absolutely. Yeats offers something rare: the complete evolution of a genius from romantic youth to profound old age. His combination of musical lyricism, intellectual depth, and spiritual searching creates poetry that speaks across centuries. The biggest reason to read Yeats is his demonstration that poetry can simultaneously be politically relevant, spiritually profound, and technically brilliant.

For busy readers, start with 5-10 famous poems rather than attempting the entire collection. "The Lake Isle of Innisfree," "When You Are Old," "Easter 1916," "The Second Coming," and "Sailing to Byzantium" provide a perfect introduction to his range and development. From there, you can explore specific periods that interest you most.

**Reading time for core poems**: 30-45 minutes
**Full collection commitment**: 20+ hours of intensive reading
**Best approach**: Start with selected masterpieces, then explore periods that resonate most with your interests
