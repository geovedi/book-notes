# Assassin's Creed Forsaken

**Original Title**: Assassin's Creed Forsaken
**Author**: Oliver Bowden | **Year**: 2012

## Summary

"Assassin's Creed Forsaken" tells the origin story of Haytham Kenway, father of Connor Kenway from Assassin's Creed III, tracing his journey from childhood tragedy to his transformation into a Templar Grand Master. Set against the backdrop of 18th century historical events, the novel explores themes of family, betrayal, ideology, and the eternal conflict between Assassins and Templars.

The story begins with young Haytham Kenway witnessing the brutal murder of his father and the kidnapping of his sister Jenny, an event that shatters his privileged London childhood and sets him on a path of vengeance. Taken under the wing of the mysterious Reginald Birch, Haytham is trained in combat and indoctrinated into the Templar Order, believing they seek to bring peace and order to a chaotic world. As he rises through the ranks and becomes Grand Master, Haytham increasingly questions his loyalty and the true nature of his mission, culminating in a tragic confrontation with his own son that bridges the ideological divide between Assassin and Templar philosophies.

This novel provides essential backstory for fans of the Assassin's Creed video game series, particularly those interested in the complex relationship between father and son that drives the narrative of Assassin's Creed III. It explores moral ambiguity, the cost of ideology, and how seemingly noble intentions can lead to devastating consequences.

## Core Insights

### The Transformative Power of Childhood Trauma
Haytham's journey begins with the violent loss of his father Edward Kenway, a former pirate turned aristocrat who is murdered in their home. This traumatic event not only robs him of his innocence but fundamentally shapes his worldview and future allegiances. The kidnapping of his sister Jenny adds another layer of loss that fuels his quest for revenge and justice.

> "He spat and beckoned me forward with one hand, rolling the blade in the other. 'Come on, Assassin,' he goaded me. 'Come be a warrior for the first time. Come see what it feels like. Come on, boy. Be a man.'"

**Quick Take**: Early childhood experiences, particularly traumatic ones, can profoundly influence our life choices, allegiances, and the paths we choose to follow.

### The Seductive Appeal of Order vs. Freedom
The novel explores the fundamental ideological divide between the Templars (who seek control and order for humanity's own good) and the Assassins (who champion free will despite its dangers). Haytham's journey from Assassin family roots to Templar leadership represents a compelling exploration of why different people choose different philosophical approaches to society's problems.

> "The people never have the power, only the illusion of it. And here's the real secret: they don't want it. The responsibility is too great to bear. It's why they're so quick to fall in line as soon as someone takes charge. They yearn for it."

**Quick Take**: The tension between security and freedom remains one of society's fundamental dilemmas, with reasonable arguments existing on both sides.

### Father-Son Relationships and Legacy
The novel culminates in the tragic confrontation between Haytham and his son Connor, representing not just a family drama but the clash of ideologies. Their final battle highlights how legacy, both genetic and ideological, can transcend personal relationships and lead to inevitable conflict.

> "No, son. We are enemies. And one of us must die... Even when your kind appears to triumph... still we rise again. Do you know why? It is because the Order is born of a realization. We require no creed. No indoctrination by desperate old men. All we need is that the world be as it is."

**Quick Take**: Family bonds can be both our strongest connections and our deepest sources of conflict, especially when ideological differences divide us.

### The Moral Complexity of "Good" vs. "Evil"
"Forsaken" excels at showing how both Assassins and Templars believe they are working toward humanity's benefit, just through different means. Haytham's internal conflict demonstrates that the line between hero and villain often depends on perspective rather than absolute morality.

> "I should have killed you long ago," he said softly as I watched the life ebb out of him. "I'm proud of you in a way. You have shown conviction. Strength. Courage. These are noble traits."

**Quick Take**: Understanding the motivations and perspectives of those we disagree with is essential for recognizing moral complexity in real-world conflicts.

### The Historical Weight of Personal Choices
Set against major historical events including colonial conflicts and revolutions, the novel shows how individual lives intersect with broader historical forces. Haytham's choices affect not just his personal destiny but the course of the eternal Assassin-Templar conflict.

**Quick Take**: Our individual choices, while seeming small in the grand scheme of history, can have ripple effects that influence far larger outcomes.

## Key Plot Points

- **Childhood Tragedy**: Young Haytham witnesses his father's murder and sister's kidnapping, setting him on a path of revenge
- **Templar Indoctrination**: Under Reginald Birch's guidance, Haytham is trained as a Templar and rises through their ranks
- **American Colonial Conflict**: Haytham travels to the American colonies, seeking revenge and pursuing Templar objectives
- **Search for the Precursor Site**: The race to find ancient First Civilization technology drives much of the plot
- **Confrontation with Connor**: The inevitable battle between father and son representing opposing ideologies
- **Sacrifice and Legacy**: Haytham's death becomes a pivotal moment in the ongoing Assassin-Templar war

## Characters

- **Haytham Kenway**: The protagonist, transformed from privileged child to Templar Grand Master
- **Connor Kenway**: Haytham's son and eventual nemesis, an Assassin fighting for colonial freedom
- **Jenny Kenway**: Haytham's sister, kidnapped in childhood and later reunited under complicated circumstances
- **Edward Kenway**: Haytham's father, former pirate killed in the opening chapters
- **Reginald Birch**: Haytham's mentor who guides him into the Templar Order
- **Charles Lee**: Haytham's right-hand man and eventual antagonist in the colonial conflict

## Questions to Consider

1. Can order and freedom truly coexist, or must one always dominate the other?
2. How do childhood traumas shape our adult decisions and allegiances?
3. Is it possible to bridge ideological divides when they're rooted in fundamental beliefs about human nature?
4. What responsibility do we have for the consequences of our well-intentioned actions?
5. Can we ever truly escape the legacy of our family and upbringing?

## Conclusion

"Assassin's Creed Forsaken" is worth your time if you're interested in complex character studies that explore moral ambiguity within an action-packed historical setting. The novel succeeds brilliantly at humanizing the "villain" side of the Assassin's Creed conflict while maintaining the franchise's themes of free will versus control.

The biggest reason to read this book is its nuanced exploration of how good people can find themselves on opposing sides of fundamental conflicts. Haytham's journey from innocent child to committed ideologue provides a compelling character arc that enriches the entire Assassin's Creed narrative universe. While the novel works best as backstory for game players, it stands as a solid historical fiction exploring timeless questions about ideology, family, and the price of conviction.

The book's greatest strength lies in its refusal to present simple answers, instead embracing the complexity of its themes and characters. By the end, readers may find themselves questioning not just Haytham's choices, but their own beliefs about power, freedom, and human nature.
