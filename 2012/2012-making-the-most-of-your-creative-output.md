# Making the Most of Your Creative Output
**Authors**: Ian Shipley | **Year**: 2012

## Summary
This practical business guide serves as a roadmap for visual artists, photographers, writers, and designers looking to transform their creative talents into viable commercial ventures. Shipley draws from his extensive experience in music, photography, and creative licensing to demystify the business side of creativity, offering real-world strategies for protecting, promoting, and profiting from artistic work.

**Who should read this**: Visual artists, photographers, writers, designers, and other creative professionals who want to monetize their work without losing creative control. You'll learn how to navigate licensing deals, understand publishing contracts, build sustainable income streams, and make informed decisions about when and how to commercialize your artistic output.

## Core Insights

### Understanding Your Creative Motivation
Before diving into the business aspects, it's crucial to understand why you create. Some people create purely for the joy and escape it provides from daily life. The mid-1900s mining art collectives in northern England exemplified this - they created for pure self-expression and camaraderie. Others see creativity as a potential escape from traditional employment, dreaming of that idyllic log cabin studio where they can create on their own terms.

> "A lot of people see their creativity as an escape route to a new life, one free of the clocking in at work, answering to their boss or that daily grind called commuting."

**Quick Take**: Honest assessment of your motivation - whether you seek creative freedom, recognition, or commercial success - will guide every business decision you make.

### Building Your Platform Gradually
Rather than waiting for a major publishing deal, build your platform systematically. Start with local clubs, exhibitions, and your own website. Local art groups provide valuable feedback and your first sales opportunities. When displaying work in venues like hotels or restaurants, understand that while exposure is valuable, direct sales may be limited.

> "More importantly is the fact that other people actually 'get' what you are doing and can provide you with justification for those long lonely hours in your studio."

**Quick Win**: Join local creative clubs and participate in exhibitions to gain honest feedback and your first paying customers.

### The Self-Publishing Path
Self-publishing offers complete creative control and higher profit margins. Artists can create limited edition prints (genuinely limited quantities, not "limited to orders placed") and build relationships with galleries and collectors directly. Writers can produce ebooks or print-on-demand books, maintaining rights to all subsidiary markets.

For artists, the financial model works like this: produce limited edition prints (typically 150-500 signed, numbered pieces) sold through galleries for hundreds to thousands of dollars each. Once you establish a collector base, original works can command substantially higher prices.

> "Self publishing can be lucrative especially for artists and sculpture artists, they produce the original and then have a (usually) limited number of reproductions produced."

**Quick Win**: Start with small production runs to test demand while maintaining scarcity value.

### Understanding Publishing Deal Pitfalls
Traditional publishing deals can be a double-edged sword. Be wary of contracts that include "worldwide rights" for limited print runs, or clauses that assign merchandising and other subsidiary rights to the publisher. These clauses can severely limit your future earning potential.

Example: If you create a book with unique characters and sign away merchandising rights, when toy companies want to produce plush toys, you'll only receive a fraction of the revenue (perhaps 40% of what the publisher gets).

> "Remember this the manufacturer film rights people will only pay a pre determined amount of money regardless of how many people you have in the food chain before you get your net amount."

**Strategy**: Negotiate territory-specific, time-limited contracts. Keep separate rights for different media and territories.

### Licensing as Revenue Stream
Licensing offers one of the simplest business models for creative professionals. You retain ownership while companies pay to reproduce your designs on their products. There are two main approaches:

**Image Library Model**: Submit work to stock libraries that handle all licensing. You receive one-time fees but no ongoing relationship or recognition.

**Direct Licensing Model**: Build relationships with manufacturers for ongoing royalty-based deals. Typical royalty rates range from 4-14% of wholesale price, depending on uniqueness and market recognition.

> "For many designers and creatives the key to getting a 'licence' deal is to be able to present themselves as having a consistent output and style."

**Quick Win**: Develop a consistent body of work in a recognizable style before approaching licensing partners.

### Choosing Between Agents and Managers
**Managers** handle your entire career development and business strategy, typically earning 25-30% of total revenue. **Agents** focus specifically on licensing deals and work on commission.

When evaluating potential partners, ask blunt questions about their experience, current client roster, and specific plans for your work. Be cautious of agents who simply collect properties and mass-market them without strategy.

> "If I earn £25K from this project that means you have earned £75k, which is £75k more than you are earning now."

**Strategy**: Choose partners who understand your specific creative field and have proven track records of success with similar artists.

## Action Items

- **Define Your Creative Purpose**: Clarify whether you're creating for personal fulfillment, recognition, or commercial success before making business decisions.

- **Build Your Portfolio**: Create a consistent body of work showcasing your unique style before approaching business partners.

- **Start Local**: Join creative clubs, participate in exhibitions, and build a local following before seeking wider distribution.

- **Protect Your Rights**: Read terms of service carefully before uploading work to sharing sites or social media platforms.

- **Negotiate Smart Contracts**: Keep deals territory-specific and time-limited. Never sign away all media rights indefinitely.

- **Diversify Revenue Streams**: Combine direct sales, licensing deals, and limited edition reproductions for stable income.

- **Build Industry Knowledge**: Understand royalty rates, contract terms, and industry standards before negotiating deals.

- **Choose Partners Wisely**: Select agents or managers with proven experience in your specific creative field.

## Questions to Consider

- What is your primary motivation for creating - artistic freedom, recognition, or financial success?
- How much creative control are you willing to sacrifice for commercial opportunities?
- Do you prefer the consistency of ongoing relationships or the variety of working with multiple partners?
- What is your minimum acceptable income level before pursuing creative work full-time?
- How will you measure success beyond just financial metrics?
- Are you prepared for the business administration that comes with commercial success?

## Conclusion
This book provides essential business intelligence for creatives who want to monetize their work without losing their artistic soul. Shipley's practical approach demystifies the complex world of publishing contracts, licensing deals, and gallery relationships.

**Is it worth your time?** Absolutely. If you're serious about generating income from your creative work, this guide will save you from costly mistakes and help you build sustainable revenue streams. The real value lies in Shipley's honest assessment of the creative business landscape - he doesn't promise overnight success but provides realistic strategies for long-term viability.

**Biggest takeaway**: You don't have to choose between artistic integrity and commercial success. With careful contract negotiation and strategic partnerships, you can maintain creative control while building a profitable creative business. The key is understanding that you're not just creating art - you're building a business around your unique creative vision.
