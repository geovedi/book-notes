# Let's Pretend This Never Happened
**Authors**: Jenny Lawson | **Year**: 2012
**Category**: Fiction & Literature

## Summary
Let's Pretend This Never Happened is a hilarious and deeply personal memoir about Jenny Lawson's bizarre childhood growing up in rural Texas with a taxidermist father and an endless parade of eccentric family members. The book transforms the author's most embarrassing, traumatic, and absurd experiences into comedy gold, showing how the moments we want to forget are often the ones that shape us most profoundly.

This memoir will appeal to anyone who has ever felt like the odd one out, struggled with anxiety, or simply enjoys dark, irreverent humor about life's most ridiculous moments. Readers will find both laughter and validation in Lawson's celebration of imperfection and her refusal to be defined by society's expectations of normalcy.

## Core Insights

### The Beauty in Being Broken
Lawson reveals how our most embarrassing and traumatic moments become the foundation of our identity. From being thrown from a moving car as a child to wearing a dead deer as an accidental sweater, she shows that our reactions to life's absurdities define us more than the events themselves. Her journey from anxious child to successful humorist demonstrates that embracing our "brokenness" rather than hiding it creates authentic connection and unexpected strength.

> "This book is a love letter to my family. It's about the surprising discovery that the most terribly human moments—the ones we want to pretend never happened—are the very same moments that make us who we are today."

**Quick Take**: Your weirdness is your superpower, not your weakness.

### Family as Both Blessing and Therapy Session
Growing up with a father who wakes his daughters with a dead squirrel hand puppet ("Stanley, the Magical Talking Squirrel") and a grandmother who encourages dangerous adventures with "Don't tell your parents" as her catchphrase, Lawson shows that families don't have to be perfect to be valuable. Her parents' combination of neglect, adventure, and unconditional acceptance created both trauma and resilience in equal measure.

> "My father was perpetually disappointed by our lack of trust, but I reminded him that just last week he'd brought his own mother a box he'd filled with an angry live snake."

**Quick Take**: The same family experiences that drive you crazy may also be your best material.

### Rural Texas as Character Development Laboratory
Wall, Texas—a tiny town with more cotton than people, poisonous well water, and armadillo racing championships—serves as both setting and catalyst for Lawson's unique perspective. The absurdity of rural life, where sharing a swimming pool with pigs and filing cabinets as chicken coops are normal, forces creativity and adaptation that city life could never provide.

> "If you compared the Wall, Texas, of today with the Wall, Texas, of my childhood, you would hardly recognize it, because the Wall, Texas, of today has a gas station."

**Quick Take**: Sometimes the strangest environments produce the most interesting people.

## Best Quotes
- "You are defined not by life's imperfect moments, but by your reaction to them."
- "I thank my family for teaching me that lesson. In spades."
- "What doesn't kill you makes you stronger. My mom was a big proponent of this theory, almost to the point where she seemed to be daring the world to kill us."
- "My father was a total softy. Unless he was inadvertently killing the mother of a bunch of baby raccoons. Then you'd better stand the fuck back, because you're totally going to get blood on you."
- "There is joy in embracing—rather than running screaming from—the utter absurdity of life."

## Action Items
- **Embrace your embarrassing stories**: Instead of hiding your most awkward moments, learn to laugh at them and share them—they're often your most relatable experiences.
- **Find humor in trauma**: Look for ways to reframe painful memories through comedy, not to dismiss the pain but to reclaim your power over it.
- **Celebrate your weirdness**: Stop trying to be normal and recognize that your unique experiences and perspectives make you interesting.
- **Practice radical self-acceptance**: Accept that anxiety, imperfection, and eccentricities are part of what makes you human and worthy of love.

## Questions to Consider
- How might your most embarrassing childhood stories actually be your greatest strengths?
- What "normal" things do you pretend to understand that you actually find completely bizarre?
- If you wrote honestly about your family, what would be the most shocking chapter?
- How has your relationship with anxiety and mental health shaped your sense of humor?
- What parts of yourself do you hide because you're afraid others will think you're "too much"?

## Conclusion
Let's Pretend This Never Happened is worth your time if you appreciate dark humor, irreverent storytelling, and memoirs that refuse to sentimentalize the past. Lawson's distinctive voice—equal parts confessional, comedic, and surprisingly wise—makes even the most disturbing stories hilarious and cathartic. The biggest reason to read this book is not just for the laughs (though there are plenty), but for the profound message that our imperfections and scars are not things to be hidden but celebrated as essential parts of our humanity.

Lawson proves that sometimes the most healing thing we can do is look at our most painful experiences and say, "You know what? That's actually pretty funny now."
