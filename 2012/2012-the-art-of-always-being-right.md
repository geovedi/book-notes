# The Art of Always Being Right
**Authors**: Arthur Schopenhauer, A.C. Grayling | **Year**: 2012
**Category**: Philosophy & Critical Thinking

## Summary
This revealing guide exposes the 38 cunning rhetorical tricks used by politicians, lawyers, negotiators, and debaters to win arguments regardless of truth. Originally written in 1830 by philosopher Arthur Schopenhauer as a satirical take on human nature's tendency to prioritize victory over truth, this edition includes insightful commentary from A.C. Grayling. The book serves as both a manual for those who want to win at all costs and, more importantly, a defense manual for recognizing and countering manipulation in everyday discourse.

This is essential reading for professionals who regularly engage in debates, negotiations, or public discourse, as well as anyone who wants to develop intellectual self-defense against the sophisticated tactics of persuasion that pervade modern media, politics, and business.

## Core Insights

### The Victory-over-Truth Principle
Schopenhauer's central thesis is that most people argue not to discover truth but to win. Human nature's innate vanity and stubbornness make us cling to our positions even when confronted with superior reasoning. This creates a landscape where rhetorical skill often triumphs over objective correctness, making knowledge of these tactics essential for anyone engaged in intellectual combat.

> "In debating we must put objective truth aside, or rather, we must regard it as an accidental circumstance, and look only to the defence of our own position and the refutation of our opponent's."
> _Original: "In debating we must put objective truth aside, or rather, we must regard it as an accidental circumstance, and look only to the defence of our own position and the refutation of our opponent's."_

**Quick Take**: Recognize that most arguments are battles, not truth-seeking missions. Adjust your expectations accordingly.

### The Ad Hominem Arsenal
Schopenhauer catalogs personal attack strategies that shift focus from arguments to individuals. These include insults, questioning credentials, implying incompetence, and attacking character rather than ideas. The effectiveness of these tactics lies in their ability to undermine opponents without engaging with their actual points.

> "It consists in passing from the subject of dispute, as from a lost game, to the disputant himself, and in some way attacking his person."
> _Original: "It consists in passing from the subject of dispute, as from a lost game, to the disputant himself, and in some way attacking his person."_

**Quick Take**: When attacked personally, calmly redirect: "That has no bearing on the point in dispute" and return to the argument.

### The Strategic Confusion Technique
 overwhelming opponents with rapid-fire questions, complex terminology, and convoluted logic creates confusion that masks the weakness of your position. This tactic works by exhausting your opponent's mental resources, making them unable to effectively track and respond to all points.

> "Ask your opponent a great many wide-reaching questions at once, so as to hide what you want to get admitted."
> _Original: "Ask your opponent a great many wide-reaching questions at once, so as to hide what you want to get admitted."_

**Quick Take**: If overwhelmed, pause and say: "Let's address one point at a time" to regain control of the debate structure.

### The Authority Appeal
This classic tactic substitutes citing authorities for actual reasoning. The trick works because most people respect credentials and will accept arguments from perceived experts without examining the substance. Advanced versions include quoting out of context or inventing authorities entirely.

> "Every man prefers belief to the exercise of judgement."
> _Original: "Every man prefers belief to the exercise of judgement."_

**Quick Take**: Always ask: "What is the actual evidence behind this authority's claim?" to force substance over status.

### The Emotional Manipulation Strategy
Making opponents angry or appealing to their self-interest bypasses rational analysis entirely. When emotions run high, logical thinking decreases dramatically, making victory through manipulation rather than merit much easier to achieve.

> "For when he is angry he is incapable of judging aright and perceiving where his advantage lies."
> _Original: "For when he is angry he is incapable of judging aright and perceiving where his advantage lies."_

**Quick Take**: Recognize emotional manipulation attempts and mentally note: "This tactic means my argument is strong enough to require diversion."

## Key Defensive Strategies

### Recognize the 38 Tactics
Schopenhauer's comprehensive list includes: exaggerating opponent's claims, using homonyms to confuse, making false generalizations, demanding proof of negatives, changing the subject when losing, appealing to popular opinion, using bombast to intimidate, and ultimately resorting to personal attacks when all else fails.

### The Truth-Seeking Alternative
Rather than engaging in verbal combat, Schopenhauer ultimately recommends avoiding arguments with those who lack intellectual integrity. True debate requires mutual respect for truth and willingness to change positions when presented with superior evidence.

> "The only safe rule, therefore, is not to dispute with the first person you meet, but only with those of your acquaintance of whom you know that they possess sufficient intelligence and self-respect not to advance absurdities."
> _Original: "The only safe rule, therefore, is not to dispute with the first person you meet, but only with those of your acquaintance of whom you know that they possess sufficient intelligence and self-respect not to advance absurdities."_

**Quick Take**: Quality of intellectual opponents matters more than quantity.

## Practical Applications

### For Negotiators and Business Leaders
- Identify when opponents use diversion tactics to avoid addressing weak points
- Use the "turn the tables" technique when opponents attack your motives
- Recognize bombast and jargon as potential cover for weak arguments

### For Students and Academics
- Watch for appeals to authority that substitute for actual evidence
- Question generalizations and demand specific examples
- Develop the ability to identify when arguments are being extended beyond reasonable limits

### For Public Communicators
- Frame your positions using favorable terminology before opponents can define them
- Control debate boundaries by carefully limiting the scope of discussion
- Use the "particular cases" technique to build momentum toward your desired conclusions
