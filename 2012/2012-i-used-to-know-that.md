# I Used to Know That: Science
**Authors**: Marianne Taylor, Caroline Taggart | **Year**: 2012
**Category**: Science & Nature

## Summary
"I Used to Know That: Science" is a refresher course in all the science fundamentals you learned (or should have learned) in school but have since forgotten. Written in an accessible, conversational style, the book covers physics, chemistry, and biology - the science trinity - explaining complex concepts in straightforward language without overwhelming technical jargon or mind-bending mathematics.

The book targets adults who want to reconnect with scientific knowledge they may have half-remembered from school, or those who want to understand the scientific principles that govern their daily lives. It bridges the gap between forgotten classroom knowledge and practical, everyday understanding, making science accessible and relevant to modern life.

Perfect for curious readers who want to understand how the world works, parents helping with homework, or anyone who wants to feel more confident discussing scientific topics. Readers will gain a solid foundation in basic science principles and the confidence to engage with science-related issues in the news and their daily lives.

## Core Insights

### The Science Trinity: Interconnected Knowledge
Science education traditionally separates physics, chemistry, and biology, but these disciplines are deeply interconnected. Physics studies fundamental forces and particles - the very building blocks of matter. Chemistry emerges when those particles form atoms and molecules, dealing with how different elements combine and interact. Biology builds on chemistry, studying how those molecules form the complex systems of living organisms. This progression from subatomic particles to living ecosystems shows how scientific knowledge builds upon itself.

> "Once you have assembled enough subatomic particles to make an actual atom, you enter the realm of chemistry. This science deals with the properties and behaviour of atoms and molecules... And so chemistry segues effortlessly (well, more or less) into biology, the study of living things."

**Quick Take**: You can't fully understand biology without chemistry, and you can't understand chemistry without physics - they're all parts of the same magnificent puzzle.

### Science vs. Pseudoscience: The Critical Difference
The scientific method follows a rigorous process: propose a hypothesis, make predictions based on that hypothesis, then test those predictions to see if the hypothesis can be proven false. This approach - focused on falsifiability - distinguishes genuine science from pseudoscience. Scientific claims must be testable and potentially disprovable, while pseudoscientific claims often can't be falsified regardless of evidence.

> "Pseudoscience covers things that look science-like but don't fit the scientific method... Pseudoscience also describes things which are claimed to be true but for which convincing evidence has not been found."

**Quick Take**: The willingness to be proven wrong is what separates science from belief systems - real science embraces challenges to its theories.

### E=mc²: The Universe's Most Famous Equation
Einstein's legendary formula isn't as intimidating as it appears - it simply states that matter and energy are different versions of the same thing. This relationship explains nuclear power: when atoms split (fission), the resulting products weigh slightly less than the original material because the "missing mass" has been converted into enormous energy. This principle underlies both nuclear power plants and nuclear weapons, demonstrating how a simple mathematical relationship can change the world.

> "E=mc² Bet you weren't expecting to see this quite so soon. This most famous formula, from Einstein's Theory of Special Relativity and first proposed in 1905, is not as scary as you might think, however. It just means that matter (any kind of physical substance) and energy are different versions of the same thing."

**Quick Take**: Mass and energy are interchangeable - understanding this relationship unlocks the secrets of nuclear power and the universe itself.

### The Periodic Table: Nature's Building Blocks
The periodic table isn't just a random collection of elements - it's organized so that elements with similar properties form columns, revealing recurring patterns in nature. Elements combine to form stable compounds because atoms seek to complete their electron shells, either by sharing electrons (covalent bonds) or transferring them (ionic bonds). This fundamental drive for stability creates everything from table salt to DNA.

> "All of which gives rise to the best (if not the only) chemistry joke of all time. Two hydrogen atoms are walking down the road, when one stops dead with a look of great anxiety. The other says, 'Hey, what's wrong?' The first says 'I lost my electron!' The second says, 'Oh, no! Are you sure?' The first replies, 'Yes. I'm positive'."

**Quick Take**: Chemistry is essentially the story of atoms trying to achieve stability by filling their outer electron shells - everything from salt formation to life itself follows this principle.

### The Human Body: An Engineering Marvel
The human body operates like a perfectly engineered city, with specialized systems working together seamlessly. The cardiovascular system acts as transportation, delivering oxygen and nutrients while removing waste. The skeletal system provides structure and protection while manufacturing blood cells. Muscles convert chemical energy into movement, and skin serves as both protective barrier and environmental interface. These systems demonstrate nature's incredible ability to create complex, self-regulating machines.

> "A human body is rather like a city. The organs are like buildings, some used for storage, some for production, some for processing. The 'workers' and the 'products' - blood cells, hormones, molecules of food and so on - are like vehicles, travelling from organ to organ as required via our circulatory systems."

**Quick Take**: Your body is an incredibly efficient, self-repairing machine that continuously balances multiple complex systems to keep you alive and functioning.

## Conclusion
"I Used to Know That: Science" succeeds brilliantly at making science accessible without dumbing it down. The book's conversational tone and real-world examples transform potentially intimidating topics into fascinating stories about how our world works. Rather than presenting science as a collection of facts to memorize, it reveals science as an ongoing process of discovery and understanding.

This book is absolutely worth your time if you've ever felt scientifically illiterate or wanted to understand the world around you better. The biggest reason to read it isn't just to recover forgotten knowledge, but to gain confidence in your ability to understand and engage with scientific concepts that affect your daily life. In an age where scientific literacy matters more than ever, this book provides the perfect foundation for becoming a more informed, curious, and engaged citizen of the universe.

Whether you're helping kids with homework, trying to understand climate science, or simply want to satisfy your curiosity about how things work, this book delivers maximum insight with minimum intimidation - exactly what busy readers need in our information-rich world.
