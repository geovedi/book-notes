# 2012-Motivating Beyond Money

**Authors**: Rus Slater | **Year**: 2012
**Category**: Business & Finance

## Summary

"Motivating Beyond Money" tackles a common management dilemma: how to keep good performers engaged when financial rewards are limited or unavailable. Slater argues that managers often overestimate money's motivational power while underutilizing far more effective non-financial incentives. The book reveals that employees consistently rank interesting work, appreciation, and involvement above salary in what drives their performance, yet managers continue to focus primarily on monetary compensation.

This guide is essential for managers, team leaders, and business owners who want to boost team performance without breaking the budget. Readers will learn practical, immediately applicable techniques for empowering employees, showing authentic appreciation, and creating engaging work environments that retain top talent. The strategies work equally well in large corporations and small businesses, making this valuable for anyone responsible for managing people.

## Core Insights

### The Motivation Gap: What Managers Think vs. What Employees Want

Research by Kovacs et al at George Mason University revealed striking disconnects between management perceptions and employee reality. While managers ranked money as the #1 motivator, employees ranked it sixth. Managers placed job security second, but employees prioritized feeling appreciated by their managers. This gap exists because managers tend to project their own assumptions onto their teams rather than understanding what truly drives individual performance.

> "Overall this shows that people are generally more motivated by the intangible things rather than the material, the good news about this is that most of the intangible things are completely within every manager's gift."

**Quick Take**: Stop assuming you know what motivates your team. The most powerful motivational tools cost nothing but attention and understanding.

### Five Pillars of Non-Financial Motivation

Slater outlines five research-backed areas that truly drive employee engagement: 1) Interesting work through empowerment and challenges, 2) Genuine appreciation and recognition, 3) Personal involvement and consultation, 4) Job/income security, and 5) Fair compensation (not necessarily high compensation). The key is understanding that good performers—defined as anyone meeting expectations—need consistent attention, not just the problem employees or superstar talents.

> "What we should be doing is concentrating at least as much effort on our good performers as we are on the underachievers; and when we say 'good' performers we aren't just talking about the stars; we are talking about the people who we appraise in our annual Performance Report as 'meeting expectations'."

**Quick Take**: The silent majority of solid performers often gets the least management attention, leading to disengagement and turnover.

### The Art of Authentic Appreciation

Effective appreciation goes far beyond formal "Employee of the Month" programs, which Slater warns often become meaningless or politically motivated. Instead, he advocates for immediate, specific recognition of good work. The key is personalization—some employees thrive on public praise while others prefer quiet acknowledgment. Cultural considerations also matter; in some cultures, overt appreciation can be perceived as sarcasm or insincerity.

> "Catch people doing things right: every time someone does something right or well, say a thank you and a well done…it isn't rocket science and it is good manners."

**Quick Take**: Consistently notice and verbally acknowledge good work in the moment, tailoring your approach to each employee's personality and cultural background.

### Empowerment Through Autonomy and Challenge

Giving employees control over their workday creates immediate engagement. Whether it's allowing truck drivers to choose their routes, letting shop assistants process refunds without manager approval, or giving teams decision-making authority, autonomy builds ownership. For roles with inherent limitations, multi-skilling and job rotation provide variety while creating organizational flexibility. Challenges can range from special projects to mentoring opportunities to cross-department assignments.

> "When Pat Carrigan became the boss of a factory in General Motors, it was already destined for closure and all the staff for redundancy, she immediately set about empowering people and productivity actually rose!"

**Quick Take**: Start small with autonomy and watch how quickly employees take ownership when trusted with meaningful decisions.

### Strategic Engagement and Communication

Engagement differs from empowerment—it involves consulting employees on broader decisions and keeping them informed about organizational matters. Slater recommends "cascade communication" rather than mass emails, ensuring information flows appropriately through management levels. Most importantly, involve good performers in improvement discussions; they often have the most valuable insights because they live the processes daily.

> "Staff are a heck of a lot brighter than many managers give them credit for and just taking an afternoon to get the people who do the job day-to-day to suggest ways to improve, usually produces a lot of really valuable ideas."

**Quick Take**: Your employees already know how to fix many organizational problems—ask them and actually implement their suggestions.

## Best Quotes

- "Traditionally we spend a lot of management effort focusing on pulling the low performers up by their bootstraps whilst we let the good performers get on with it. This is a completely understandable but counterproductive strategy."

- "In every job that's to be done there is an element of fun, find the fun and snap, the job's a game."

- "Beware of any formal appreciation programme that is 'fun'…being presented with a framed certificate by someone dressed as a chicken may be fun to some people but it is the absolute kiss-of-death to many as it belittles the appreciation to a side-show."

- "If an idea is successful and it improves things then try to get some sort of longer term recognition for the individual or people responsible...NEVER, EVER try to steal the credit, or even passively allow others to think that you are the creative genius."

- "The phrase 'job security' is a bit of a misnomer; when people talk about job security what they really mean in income security."

## Action Items

- **Conduct a motivation audit**: Ask your team what truly motivates them, then compare their answers with what you think motivates them
- **Implement daily recognition**: Make it a habit to catch at least three people doing things right every day
- **Create an empowerment inventory**: Identify three areas where you can give team members more autonomy immediately
- **Start improvement conversations**: Schedule dedicated brainstorming sessions with front-line employees about process improvements
- **Review your appreciation style**: Ensure recognition methods match individual preferences and cultural backgrounds

## Questions to Consider

- How much time do you spend with your good performers versus underperformers?
- What assumptions are you making about what motivates your team?
- Where could you give employees more control without creating chaos?
- How do you currently show appreciation, and is it working?
- What information are you withholding that could help your team feel more involved?

## Conclusion

"Motivating Beyond Money" delivers a powerful reminder that the most effective management tools cost nothing but attention and intention. Slater's practical approach makes it immediately actionable—any manager can start implementing these techniques tomorrow. The book's strength lies in its focus on the silent majority of solid performers who keep organizations running but often receive the least leadership attention.

The biggest reason to read this is its timing relevance: in today's economic uncertainty, managers need ways to maintain morale and performance when budgets are tight. Slater provides a roadmap to create workplaces where people want to work, not just places where people need to work. For any manager who's ever said "I can't motivate my team without more money," this book provides the definitive answer: yes, you can, and here's exactly how.
