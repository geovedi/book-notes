# Bulgarian Turks (1878-2008)
**Original Title**: Bulgaristan Türkleri (1878-2008)
**Authors**: Bilâl N. Şimşir | **Year**: 2012
**Category**: History & Biography 

## Summary

This comprehensive historical work chronicles the struggle of Turkish minorities in Bulgaria from the country's liberation in 1878 through 2008, documenting systematic assimilation policies, cultural resistance, and mass migrations. Written by a Turkish diplomat who personally participated in efforts to protect Bulgarian Turks, the book combines scholarly research with first-hand diplomatic experience to reveal how Bulgaria attempted to erase Turkish identity through forced name changes, educational suppression, and economic marginalization.

Readers interested in minority rights, Balkan history, and cultural preservation will find compelling insights into how a community of over one million people maintained their identity despite decades of state-sponsored assimilation campaigns that peaked in the 1980s.

## Core Insights

### The Revival Process: Systematic Cultural Erasure (1878-1944)

Following Bulgaria's liberation from Ottoman rule, Turkish communities transformed from a ruling majority to a persecuted minority. The new Bulgarian state immediately implemented policies to dispossess Turks of their economic base - a "soil revolution" where 70% of Turkish-owned agricultural lands were seized and redistributed to ethnic Bulgarians. Turkish educational institutions, numbering 2,700 schools in 1875, were systematically reduced through arson, looting, and discriminatory policies. By the 1930s, under fascist influences, Turkish-language newspapers were forced to close, their editors imprisoned, and writers labeled as "Kemalists" facing persecution.

> "Bulgarian fascism had begun to respond to the strengthened Turkish press. One after another, Turkish newspapers were suffocated."
> _Original: "Bulgar faşizminin Türk basınına karşı tepkisi başlamıştır. Arka arkaya Türk gazetelerinin boğulduğu bir dönem yaşanmıştır."_

**Quick Take**: Economic dispossession combined with cultural suppression created the foundation for future assimilation policies, establishing a pattern of treating Turkish identity as threatening to Bulgarian nationhood.

### Communist Assimilation: The "Bulgarianization" Campaign (1944-1984)

The communist regime initially appeared supportive, permitting Turkish-language publications including Yeni Işık newspaper and supporting Turkish literature. However, this masked a systematic strategy of cultural absorption. The state promoted a "socialist in content, national in form" approach - maintaining Turkish language while filling it with communist ideology. By the 1960s, Turkish schools were merged with Bulgarian institutions, Turkish reduced to a foreign elective subject, and eventually banned altogether. The regime implemented population redistribution policies, scattering Turkish communities to break their cultural cohesion.

> "The collectivization of agricultural lands between 1949-1956 hurt Turks the most. Because 80% of Turks were small farmers."
> _Original: "1949-1956 yıllarında Bulgaristan'da bütün topraklar kolektifleştirildi. Bu, en çok Türkleri ezdi. Çünkü Türklerin yüzde 80 kadarı küçük çiftçiydi."_

**Quick Take**: The communist strategy proved more insidious than overt repression - it maintained the illusion of cultural tolerance while systematically eroding the foundations of Turkish identity through education and economic control.

### The Revival Process: Forced Name Changes (1984-1989)

The culmination of assimilation policies came in winter 1984-85 when Bulgaria launched its most aggressive campaign - the forced changing of Turkish names to Bulgarian ones. This "Revival Process" aimed to erase Turkish identity entirely, with the government declaring by early 1985 that "no Turks remained in Bulgaria." The campaign was brutally enforced, resulting in deaths of those who resisted, including three memorialized Turks from Gruevo village. This triggered international condemnation and led Turkey to initiate diplomatic protests, transforming the issue into a "national cause" supported across Turkish society.

> "The Bulgarian Government, with an unimaginable stance, attempted to erase the Turkish minority from history. The powerful Turkish minority in Bulgaria could not silently bow to this."
> _Original: "Bulgar Hükümeti, akıl almaz bir tutumla Bulgaristan Türklerini tarihten silmeye kalkışmıştı. Bulgaristan'daki büyük Türk azınlığı buna sessizce boyun eğemezdi."_

**Quick Take**: The name-change campaign represented the ultimate expression of cultural genocide, seeking to eliminate not just Turkish culture but the very markers of Turkish identity, yet it ultimately galvanized resistance both within Bulgaria and internationally.

### Resistance and Liberation: The Great Migration (1989)

Despite decades of repression, Turkish resistance persisted underground. In May 1989, Bulgarians Turks rose up, demonstrating that assimilation had failed. The regime's collapse led to the opening of borders, triggering one of the largest mass migrations in modern Balkan history - 350,000 Turks fled to Turkey within 70 days. This "Great Exodus" disproved Bulgaria's claims that no Turks remained and represented both a tragedy of forced displacement and a triumph of cultural survival. The migration revealed the failure of decades of assimilation policies and demonstrated the resilience of Turkish identity.

> "Finally Bulgarian Turks exploded. They broke the dams and shattered the stones. Bulgaria shook. The Iron Curtain tore. The border opened. A massive migration flow from Bulgaria to Turkey began."
> _Original: "Sonunda Bulgaristan Türkleri patladı. Bendleri yıkıp taştı. Bulgaristan sarsıldı. Demir Perde yırtıldı. Sınır açıldı. Bulgaristan'dan Türkiye'ye muazzam bir göç akını oldu."_

**Quick Take**: The mass migration demonstrated that cultural identity cannot be erased by state force, and that repression ultimately strengthens resistance by unifying persecuted communities.

## Key Takeaways

- **Cultural resilience**: Despite 130 years of systematic assimilation policies, Turkish identity in Bulgaria survived through family traditions, religious practices, and community solidarity.

- **Economic warfare**: The dispossession of Turkish lands and businesses was a primary tool of assimilation, demonstrating how economic control facilitates cultural suppression.

- **International pressure matters**: Turkey's diplomatic protests and international attention were crucial in eventually forcing Bulgaria to moderate its assimilation policies.

- **Education as battlefield**: The struggle over Turkish-language education reveals how control of schools becomes critical in cultural preservation efforts.

- **Migration as survival**: The mass exodus of 1989 represented both the failure of assimilation policies and the extreme measures communities will take to preserve their identity.

## Conclusion

This book is essential reading for understanding how minority communities resist cultural erasure and the limits of state power in suppressing identity. Şimşir's unique perspective as both diplomat and advocate provides unprecedented insight into one of the most systematic assimilation campaigns in modern European history. The work demonstrates that while states can dispossess, oppress, and attempt to erase cultures, identity rooted in family, faith, and community ultimately survives through determined resistance.

The biggest reason to read this book is its powerful testament to cultural resilience - showing how a community of over one million people maintained their Turkish identity despite 130 years of policies designed to erase them. It's both a warning about the dangers of ethnic nationalism and inspiration about human capacity to preserve culture against impossible odds. The book reminds us that identity cannot be legislated away and that the human spirit's need for cultural belonging persists even through the darkest periods of repression.
