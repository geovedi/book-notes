# Steal Like an Artist
**Author**: Austin Kleon | **Year**: 2012
**Category**: Art & Creativity

## Summary
"Steal Like an Artist" is a refreshing manifesto for creativity in the digital age that demolishes the myth of originality. Austin Kleon argues that all creative work builds on what came before—nothing is completely original. Through 10 transformative principles, he shows how to embrace influence, find your voice by copying heroes, and develop a sustainable creative practice. The book is perfect for artists, writers, entrepreneurs, and anyone seeking to inject more creativity into their life and work.

This guide will benefit anyone feeling stuck creatively, struggling with impostor syndrome, or overwhelmed by the pressure to be original. You'll learn practical strategies for finding inspiration, building creative habits, and sharing your work with confidence.

## Core Insights

### 1. Steal Like an Artist
Nothing is completely original—all creative work builds on what came before. Instead of trying to create something from nothing, embrace influence by collecting ideas that resonate with you. The key is to study your heroes' thinking, not just copy their style. You become a unique remix of everything you choose to let into your life.

> "Every new idea is just a mashup or a remix of one or more previous ideas."

**Quick Take**: Build your creative family tree by studying one thinker you love, then finding the three people they loved, and climbing that tree as far as you can.

### 2. Don't Wait Until You Know Who You Are to Get Started
You discover who you are through the act of making things, not by thinking about it. Everyone experiences impostor syndrome—even successful creatives feel like phonies. The solution is to "fake it 'til you make it" by starting now and learning through doing.

> "If I'd waited to know who I was or what I was about before I started 'being creative,' well, I'd still be sitting around trying to figure myself out instead of making things."

**Quick Take**: Start copying your heroes' work to learn their craft—this is how you'll eventually find your own voice through your imperfect attempts to imitate.

### 3. Write the Book You Want to Read
Create work that you wish existed in the world. The best creative motivation comes from being a fan who wants more. Instead of asking "What should I write?" ask "What would make a better story?" Make the art you want to see, start the business you want to run, build the products you want to use.

> "Write the kind of story you like best—write the story you want to read."

**Quick Take**: Think about your favorite work and ask what your heroes missed or could have done better—then go make that.

### 4. Use Your Hands
Our bodies are just as important as our brains in the creative process. Analog activities—drawing, writing by hand, making physical things—engage different parts of your mind than digital work. Set up separate analog and digital workstations, and use your body to kickstart your brain.

> "In the digital age, don't forget to use your digits! Your hands are the original digital devices."

**Quick Take**: Keep a notebook and pen with you always. When you're stuck, go analog—draw, cut paper, move your body.

### 5. Side Projects and Hobbies Are Important
The work you do while procrastinating is often your most important. Side projects remove pressure and allow for pure exploration, often leading to breakthroughs. Keep multiple passions alive—they'll eventually start talking to each other in unexpected ways. Don't worry about unity; what unifies your work is that you made it.

> "The work you do while you procrastinate is probably the work you should be doing for the rest of your life."

**Quick Take**: Have hobbies that give but don't take—creative pursuits just for you, without pressure to make money or get famous.

### 6. The Secret: Do Good Work and Share It with People
The formula for getting discovered is simple: do good work and share it with people. Step one (do good work) takes time—make stuff daily, fail, get better. Step two (share it) is easier than ever through the internet. Share your process, not just finished products. Wonder at something, then invite others to wonder with you.

> "Step 1: Wonder at something. Step 2: Invite others to wonder with you."

**Quick Take**: Put your stuff on the internet. Show glimpses of your process. Having an audience keeps you accountable and inspired.

### 7. Geography Is No Longer Our Master
You don't need to move to a creative hub to connect with your tribe. The internet allows you to build your own world and community regardless of location. However, physical space still matters—create solitude and "temporary captivity" for focused work. Eventually leave home to see the world with fresh eyes.

> "You don't have to live anywhere other than the place you are to start connecting with the world you want to be in."

**Quick Take**: Create your own creative world wherever you are—surround yourself with inspiring objects and find pockets of solitude.

### 8. Be Nice (The World Is a Small Town)
In our hyperconnected world, kindness and generosity are career assets. The golden rule is more important than ever—if you talk about someone online, they'll find out. Say nice things about people, ignore your enemies, and surround yourself with those smarter than you.

> "The best way to vanquish your enemies on the Internet? Ignore them. The best way to make friends on the Internet? Say nice things about them."

**Quick Take**: If you find yourself the most talented person in the room, find another room.

### 9. Be Boring (It's the Only Way to Get Work Done)
Creative productivity requires routine, discipline, and taking care of yourself. The romantic image of the wild creative genius is dangerous—it burns people out. Eat breakfast, get sleep, exercise. Manage your finances wisely. A day job provides freedom from financial stress and daily structure. Keep a calendar to track your work and don't break the chain.

> "Be regular and orderly in your life, so that you may be violent and original in your work."

**Quick Take**: Use the Seinfeld method: get a wall calendar, mark an X for each day you work, and don't break the chain.

### 10. Creativity Is Subtraction
In an age of infinite possibilities, creativity thrives on constraints. Limitations force innovation and focus. Dr. Seuss wrote "Green Eggs and Ham" with only 50 different words because his editor bet him he couldn't. Often, what makes art interesting is what the artist chose to leave out.

> "What we respond to in any work of art is the artist's struggle against his or her limitations."

**Quick Take**: Place creative constraints on yourself—work with limited time, materials, or tools to spark innovation.

## Best Quotes

- "We were kids without fathers, so we found our fathers on wax and on the streets and in history. We got to pick and choose the ancestors who would inspire the world we were going to make for ourselves."
- "You start out as a phony and become real."
- "If you copy from one author, it's plagiarism, but if you copy from many, it's research."
- "It is our failure to become our perceived ideal that ultimately defines us and makes us unique."

## Action Items
- Start a swipe file or "morgue file" to collect things that inspire you
- Carry a notebook everywhere and use it daily
- Set up both analog and digital workspaces
- Begin sharing your work online, even small pieces of your process
- Identify your creative heroes and study their influences
- Schedule regular time for creative work and don't break the chain
- Find a day job that supports rather than drains your creative energy
- Practice productive procrastination through side projects
- Be intentional about what you leave out of your work and life
- Write public fan letters to creators you admire

## Questions to Consider
- Who are your creative heroes, and who influenced them?
- What work do you wish existed that you could create?
- How can you use constraints to spark your creativity?
- What boring routines could actually support your creative work?
- Who are the smartest people in your field, and how can you learn from them?
- What should you subtract from your life to focus on what matters?

## Conclusion
"Steal Like an Artist" is essential reading for anyone who wants to live a more creative life. Kleon demolishes the paralyzing myth of originality and replaces it with a liberating framework for creative practice. The book's biggest strength is its permission-giving nature—you're allowed to copy, to fail, to be boring, to steal from your heroes.

The practical strategies are immediately applicable, and the conversational tone makes complex ideas accessible. At just over 100 pages, it delivers maximum value with minimum fluff. This isn't just a book to read once—it's a manual to return to whenever you feel creatively stuck or need a reminder that creativity isn't magic, it's work.

**Worth your time?** Absolutely. If you make anything—or want to—this book will change how you think about creativity and give you the tools to start now, not when you feel "ready."
