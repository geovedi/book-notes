# Beauty and Two Beasts
**Authors**: A. Anders | **Year**: 2017

## Summary
Beauty and Two Beasts is a bisexual erotic romance novel that reimagines the classic Beauty and the Beast fairytale with MMF (male-male-female) themes. The story follows Belle, a young village woman who becomes entangled in a complex love triangle after her fiancé Trudeau tragically dies in a wolf attack. Searching for her missing father who disappears selling clocks, Belle finds herself at a mysterious castle where she discovers the Beast - a cursed nobleman - and Prince Renaud, who are actually two sides of the same person.

This novel will appeal to readers who enjoy LGBTQ+ romance, fairytale retellings, and stories exploring themes of self-acceptance, love in all its forms, and the complexity of human relationships. It's particularly suited for readers interested in polyamorous and bisexual romance narratives.

## Core Insights

### The Duality of Love and Identity
At the heart of the story lies the theme that love requires accepting all parts of ourselves and others. The curse that splits Prince Renaud into the Beast and the Prince represents internal conflict and the struggle to accept different aspects of one's identity. Belle's journey demonstrates that true love embraces complexity rather than seeking simple, one-dimensional connections.

**Quick Take**: The novel suggests that healing comes from integration, not elimination - we must learn to love all parts of ourselves and others.

### Redefining Fairytale Romance
This retelling subverts traditional fairytale tropes by introducing bisexual and polyamorous elements. Unlike classic tales where the heroine chooses between suitors, Belle finds herself drawn to both manifestations of the same person, challenging conventional romance narratives.

**Quick Take**: Love doesn't always follow predictable patterns - sometimes the most fulfilling connections require redefining our understanding of relationships.

### The Power of Vulnerability
Both the Beast and Prince Renaud must confront their fears and insecurities to break the curse. The Beast represents raw emotion and physical presence, while the Prince embodies social grace and external beauty. Their transformation requires embracing vulnerability and admitting their true feelings.

**Quick Take**: True strength lies not in perfection but in the courage to be vulnerable and authentic.

### Community and Belonging
The enchanted castle staff, all cursed alongside the Prince, create a found family that supports Belle and the healing process. Their loyalty and patience demonstrate how community can provide the foundation for personal transformation and acceptance.

**Quick Take**: Healing rarely happens in isolation - supportive relationships and community acceptance are essential for growth.

## Key Plot Points

### The Tragic Beginning
Belle's story starts with the loss of her fiancé Trudeau to wolves in the Dark Forest, followed by her father's mysterious disappearance while selling clocks in the city. These tragedies force Belle to grow beyond her sheltered village life.

### The Castle Discovery
Fleeing from wolves, Belle discovers an enchanted castle where she meets both the Beast and Prince Renaud, initially believing them to be separate individuals. She learns they are cursed and that love may break the spell.

### The Love Triangle
Belle develops feelings for both manifestations - the caring, protective Beast and the charming, vulnerable Prince. Her connections with both deepen the complexity of the curse and its potential resolution.

### The Truth Revealed
Belle discovers that the Beast and Prince Renaud are two sides of the same person, cursed by a witch after the Prince's father discovered his relationship with another man (Captain Bernard). The curse can only be broken through self-love and acceptance.

## Character Development

### Belle
Transforms from an innocent village girl to someone who understands the complexity of love and identity. Her journey challenges her preconceptions about relationships and teaches her to look beyond surface appearances.

### The Beast/Prince Renaud
Represents the internal struggle between different aspects of identity. The Beast embodies raw emotion and physical presence, while the Prince represents social acceptability and external beauty. Their integration represents wholeness.

## Conclusion
Beauty and Two Beasts offers a fresh take on a classic fairytale by incorporating bisexual themes and exploring the complexity of love beyond conventional boundaries. While containing explicit content, the novel uses these elements to examine deeper themes of identity, acceptance, and the courage required to love authentically.

The book is worth reading for those interested in LGBTQ+ romance that challenges traditional narratives and explores the multifaceted nature of love and attraction. Its biggest strength lies in how it uses fantasy elements to examine real human experiences of identity struggle and the journey toward self-acceptance. The novel's interpretation of the Beauty and the Beast story provides meaningful commentary on integration, healing, and the courage required to love all parts of ourselves and others.
