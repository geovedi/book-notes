# Think Small
**Authors**: Owain Service, Rory Gallagher | **Year**: 2017
**Category**: Psychology & Mental Health

## Summary
Think Small presents a revolutionary framework for achieving your goals by focusing on the small, manageable steps rather than getting overwhelmed by ambitious objectives. Written by two experts from the UK's Behavioural Insights Team (the "Nudge Unit"), this book translates decades of behavioral science research into practical strategies for personal and professional success.

This guide is perfect for anyone who has ever set ambitious goals only to lose motivation or get sidetracked by life's distractions. Whether you're looking to advance your career, improve your health, learn new skills, or help others achieve their potential, you'll discover evidence-based techniques that make success more attainable.

## Core Insights

### The Seven-Step Framework for Success
Think Small is built around a powerful behavioral scaffolding framework with seven core elements. Rather than treating these as rigid rules, think of them as the support poles and joints that will hold your goal structure steady as you build toward success. The more supports you put in place, the stronger your foundation becomes.

### Step 1: Set - Choose Goals That Matter
The first mistake most people make is setting the wrong goals. Research shows we're notoriously bad at predicting what will make us happy. Instead of chasing more money or material possessions, focus on five proven wellbeing boosters:

- **Strengthening social relationships** - People with strong social connections are 50% more likely to survive over time than those with poor social ties. Social isolation has the same mortality impact as smoking 15 cigarettes daily.
- **Getting healthy and active** - Better health correlates strongly with life satisfaction, and happier people tend to be healthier too. Regular exercise can be as effective as medication for mild depression.
- **Learning something new** - Continuous learning throughout life improves self-esteem, life satisfaction, and optimism. Learning a new skill often combines multiple wellbeing benefits.
- **Being more curious** - Actively noticing and savoring your surroundings (mindfulness) can significantly boost wellbeing. Experiences bring more happiness than material goods.
- **Giving to others** - Spending money or time on others makes you happier than spending on yourself. Giving triggers oxytocin release and reduces mortality rates.

> "If money doesn't make you happy, you probably aren't spending it right."
> _Research finding from behavioral science studies_

**Quick Take**: Before setting any goal, ask yourself: "Will this genuinely improve my wellbeing or someone else's?" Choose goals that align with these five evidence-based happiness factors.

### Step 2: Plan - Make It Actionable
Once you've chosen the right goal, break it down into concrete, manageable steps. The key is creating an actionable plan that becomes automatic over time.

> "The learners aren't told to dream big and then assume that everything will follow. Unfortunately, life doesn't work like that. Instead they are told that if you want to achieve your objectives in life, you need to start by being clear about what those objectives are, before thinking about the small steps required to get there."

**Quick Take**: Transform vague intentions into specific, scheduled actions. Link new habits to existing routines to make them automatic.

### Step 3: Commit - Make It Binding
Humans have limited cognitive bandwidth and willpower. The solution? Put systems in place that commit your future self to follow through, even when motivation fades.

- Make your goals public and write them down
- Appoint commitment referees who will check on your progress
- Create consequences for failure (like donating to rival charities)

**Quick Take**: Use your reflective mind today to lock in commitments that your automatic mind will follow tomorrow.

### Step 4: Reward - Design Smart Incentives
Not all rewards are created equal. The most effective rewards build good habits rather than just providing temporary motivation.

- Put something meaningful at stake for headline objectives
- Use small, immediate rewards to reinforce habit formation
- Beware of backfire effects—sometimes adding rewards to enjoyable activities reduces motivation

**Quick Take**: Design rewards that strengthen desired behaviors rather than undermining them.

### Step 5: Share - Leverage Social Support
Success rarely happens in isolation. Drawing on others' support multiplies your chances of achieving your goals.

- Ask for specific help from friends, family, or colleagues
- Tap into social networks that share your objectives
- Join groups with similar goals for mutual accountability

**Quick Take**: Surround yourself with people who support and accelerate your progress toward your goals.

### Step 6: Feedback - Measure What Matters
You can't improve what you don't measure. Effective feedback systems keep you oriented toward your target.

- Track your progress relative to your goal
- Make feedback timely, specific, and actionable
- Compare yourself to others for motivation and learning

**Quick Take**: Create systems that show you exactly where you stand and what you need to do next.

### Step 7: Stick - Build Lasting Habits
The final challenge is maintaining momentum when initial enthusiasm fades. This requires deliberate practice and continuous learning.

- Practice with focus and effort, not just going through the motions
- Test different approaches and learn from failures
- Reflect on progress and celebrate achievements along the way

**Quick Take**: Success comes from deliberate refinement of your approach, not just stubborn persistence.

## Action Items

- **Assess your current goals** against the five wellbeing factors and realign if necessary
- **Choose one primary goal** to focus on rather than trying to change everything at once
- **Break your goal into specific steps** with clear targets and deadlines
- **Schedule your first action** and link it to an existing routine
- **Find a commitment partner** who will hold you accountable
- **Set up a simple tracking system** to monitor progress
- **Plan small rewards** for completing key milestones
- **Join a group or community** working toward similar objectives

## Questions to Consider

- What goal would genuinely improve my wellbeing rather than just looking impressive to others?
- Which of my current goals are competing for my limited attention and cognitive resources?
- What small changes could I make to my environment to support my goals automatically?
- Who in my life could provide the most valuable support and accountability?
- How can I measure progress in ways that motivate rather than discourage me?
- What systems can I put in place today that my future self will thank me for?

## Conclusion

Think Small is definitely worth your time if you're serious about achieving meaningful goals without burning out or losing momentum. The biggest strength of this book is its foundation in rigorous behavioral science combined with practical, tested strategies from real-world government programs.

The core insight is profound: our brains have two systems—fast and automatic, slow and reflective—and we need to work with both, not against them. Success comes from using our slow system to design systems and habits that our fast system can follow automatically when willpower inevitably fades.

What makes this approach powerful is that it's not about having more willpower or motivation—it's about designing smarter systems. The behavioral scaffolding metaphor is perfect: just as construction scaffolding supports buildings during their vulnerable early stages, these techniques support you during the challenging process of building new habits and achieving meaningful goals.

The evidence-based approach, drawn from the UK government's Nudge Unit and extensive psychological research, gives these strategies credibility that most self-help books lack. This isn't just theory—it's been tested in real-world settings with millions of people, from job centers to tax offices to educational institutions.

If you're tired of setting ambitious goals only to watch them fizzle out, Think Small offers the blueprint for making your aspirations achievable through careful attention to the small details that matter most.
