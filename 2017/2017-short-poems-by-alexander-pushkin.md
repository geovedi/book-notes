# 2017 Short Poems by Alexander Pushkin
**Original Title**: Короткие стихи (Korotkiye stikhi) | **Authors**: Alexander Pushkin | **Year**: 2017 (Delphi Classics Edition)
**Category**: Fiction & Literature

## Summary
This collection showcases Alexander Pushkin, Russia's greatest poet and founder of modern Russian literature, through his finest lyrical poems. Born in 1799 into nobility, Pushkin published his first poem at fifteen and was recognized as a poetic genius by graduation. Though censored and exiled by the Tsar's regime, he developed a nuanced language that influenced Russian literature like Shakespeare influenced English.

This volume presents Pushkin's remarkable range: passionate love poems, political commentary, philosophical reflections, and personal explorations. The collection includes his most famous poem "To K—," considered the most beloved Russian poem ever written, alongside autobiographical verses, nature poems, and meditations on freedom, fame, and mortality. Pushkin's poetry combines classical elegance with raw emotional honesty, making him accessible yet profound.

Perfect for poetry lovers, those interested in Russian literature, and readers seeking timeless reflections on love, freedom, and the human condition. You'll gain insight into why Pushkin remains Russia's national poet and cultural icon.

## Core Insights

### The Transformative Power of Love
Pushkin's love poems reveal love as both devastating and transcendent force that awakens the soul. In his most famous poem "To K—," he describes how a fleeting encounter with Anna Kern transforms him from darkness to spiritual rebirth, bringing back "faith and inspiration, and the tears and life and love." His poetry shows how love operates beyond rational control, acting as both salvation and torment.

> "I still recall the marvellous moment: When you appeared before my gaze Like a ghost, like a fleeting spirit, Like soul of the purest grace."
> _Original: "Я помню чудное мгновенье: Передо мной явилась ты, Как мимолетное виденье, Как гений чистой красоты."_

**Quick Take**: Love, for Pushkin, is not mere emotion but spiritual revelation that makes life worth living.

### The Poet as Truth-Teller and Outcast
Pushkin explores the poet's role in society as both prophet and pariah. In "The Poet," he describes the transformation from ordinary person to inspired servant of Apollo, who must abandon worldly concerns to serve higher truths. In "The Unwashed," he confronts critics who demand practical value from art, defending poetry's autonomous spiritual purpose.

> "Poet, not popular applause shalt thou prize! Of raptured praise shall pass the momentary noise; The fool's judgment hear thou shalt, and the cold mob's laughter — Calm stand, and firm be, and — sober!"
> _Original: "Поэт! не дорожи любовью народной! Восторженных похвал пройдет минутный шум..."

**Quick Take**: True poetry serves eternal truths, not popular opinion or practical utility.

### Freedom as Spiritual Necessity
Throughout his work, Pushkin celebrates freedom—personal, political, and artistic—as essential to human dignity. Despite censorship and exile, he maintained unwavering commitment to individual liberty and creative independence. His poem "To the Calumniators of Russia" defiantly defends Russian sovereignty against foreign criticism while implicitly criticizing his own government's constraints.

> "Not dear I prize high-sounding rights by which is turned more head than one... Other, better freedom is my need.... To depend on rulers, or the mob — Is not all the same it?"
> _Original: "Многие мне, как я, не надобны права, И мало, право мне, иного не надобно..."_

**Quick Take**: Freedom isn't political theory but lived experience of authentic selfhood.

### The Creative Process and Inspiration
Pushkin provides intimate glimpses into artistic creation, particularly in his autobiographical poems. He describes the Muse as both divine messenger and intimate companion who "handed me the seven-stemmed flute" and transformed his life. His poetry reveals creation as mysterious collaboration between human craft and divine inspiration.

> "In the days of my youth she was fond of me, And the seven-stemmed flute she handed me. To me with smile she listened; and already gently Along the openings echoing of the woods Was playing I with fingers tender."
> _Original: "В мои юношеские годы она любила меня, И семиструнную мне дала она лиру. Ей улыбкой я обязан, и с тех пор я пою..."_

**Quick Take**: Art emerges from the intersection of personal experience and transcendent inspiration.

### Mortality and Legacy
Pushkin confronts death directly, contemplating both physical mortality and artistic immortality. In "My Monument," he claims victory over death through poetry that will outlast stone monuments and reach future generations. His acceptance of mortality paradoxically strengthens his creative urgency and commitment to truth.

> "A Monument not hand-made I have for me erected; The path to it well-trodden will not overgrow; Risen higher has it with unbending head Than the monument of Alexander."
> _Original: "Я памятник себе воздвиг нерукотворный, К нему не зарастет народная тропа, Вознесся выше он главою непокорной Александрийского столпа."_

**Quick Take**: True immortality comes not from physical monuments but from enduring influence on human hearts and minds.

## Best Quotes

- "I have outlived my every wish, Each dear dream seen rudely broken, And naught remains but woe and plaint, Sole heritage of vacant heart."
  _Original: "Я пережил свои желанья, Я разлюбил свои мечты; Оставлены мне страданья, Плоды суетной души."_

- "Blessed who to himself has kept His creation highest of the soul, And from his fellows as from the graves Expected not appreciation!"
  _Original: "Счастлив, кто в совести чистой, Душу красит твореньем, И спит сладко, и мертвецы не тревожат его движение."_

- "The extinguished joy of crazy years On me rests heavy, like dull debauch. But of by-gone days the grief, like wine In my soul the older, the stronger 't grows."
  _Original: "Погасшее веселье безумных лет Тяжелой сказкой мне легло на сердце, И вином грусти, как вином старым, Усилилась в душе моей болезнь."_

- "Life, — does it disappoint thee? Grieve not, nor be angry thou! In days of sorrow gentle be: Come shall, believe, the joyful day."
  _Original: "Что жизнь? — она разочаровывает, Не тоскуй, не гневайся ты! В дни печали будь кроток: Придет, верь, день веселый."_

- "Thus peace shall bless my parting hour, The genius of Death shall come, And whisper, knocking at the door, 'The dwelling of the shades awaits thee!'"
  _Original: "И будет мирный час прощанья, И придет смерти гений, И постучится тихо в двери, 'Обитель теней ждет тебя!'"_

## Conclusion
This collection reveals why Pushkin remains Russia's national poet and literary foundation. His poetry combines classical refinement with passionate immediacy, creating work that feels both timeless and urgently personal. Whether celebrating love, confronting death, or defending artistic integrity, Pushkin writes with clarity, emotional honesty, and technical mastery that transcends cultural boundaries.

The biggest reason to read Pushkin is his unique ability to transform personal experience into universal insight while maintaining musical beauty and intellectual depth. For poetry lovers, he offers masterful technical skill combined with emotional authenticity. For those interested in Russian culture, he provides essential understanding of Russia's literary soul. For anyone seeking wisdom about love, freedom, and mortality, he speaks across centuries with remarkable relevance and grace.
