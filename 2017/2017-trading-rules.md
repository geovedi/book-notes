# Trading Rules: A Practical Guide to Successful Trading
**Authors**: Patt Tomas | **Year**: 2017
**Category**: Business & Finance

## Summary
This book presents 10 essential trading rules that separate successful traders from average ones. The core premise is that discipline and proper risk management matter more than complex strategies or market predictions. The book emphasizes that trading success comes from following established rules consistently, managing emotions, and protecting capital. It's designed for beginner to intermediate traders looking to build a strong foundation in trading discipline and risk management.

## Core Insights

### 1. Prioritize Profits Over Being Right
Successful traders don't care about being right—they care about making money. When a trade isn't working, they exit immediately rather than holding onto losing positions to avoid admitting they were wrong. The most dangerous trading mistake is staying in a losing trade due to ego. Remember: holding a losing stock because you "think you're right" is a recipe for disaster.

> "If you are holding a losing trade because you think your prediction is correct and should pay off, GET OUT IMMEDIATELY."

**Quick Take**: Your ego costs you money; admitting mistakes quickly preserves capital.

### 2. Cut Losses Short, Let Winners Run
This Wall Street wisdom is fundamental to trading success. Most traders do the opposite—they hold losers too long hoping for recovery and sell winners too quickly for small profits. Since statistically 7 out of 10 trades result in losses, your winning trades must be large enough to cover your losing ones. Use technical analysis tools like support/resistance levels, moving averages, and indicators like RSI to determine when to exit winning trades.

> "Somehow the majority of traders are more comfortable holding losing positions for months compared to hold in winning positions for even just the week."

**Quick Take**: Small losses are acceptable; small wins are not. Your winners need to run 3x longer than your losers.

### 3. Develop a Comprehensive Trading Plan
"Wanting to make money" is not a trading plan. A real plan includes: your trading style (day trader, swing trader, or investor), risk/reward ratio calculations, position sizing rules, market and sector analysis, and clear entry/exit criteria. Your plan must specify exactly how much you're willing to risk (typically aiming for 1:3 risk/reward ratios) and when you'll exit both winning and losing positions.

**Quick Take**: Plan every trade before you enter; never trade without predetermined exit points.

### 4. Understand Position Sizing and Risk Management
Keep position sizes small, especially when starting. For accounts under $5,000, risk no more than 1/8 of your account on any single trade ($625 maximum). As your account grows, adjust your unit fractions: 1/12 for $10K-$100K accounts, 1/16 for $100K-$1M accounts, and 1/28 for accounts over $1M. Start with partial positions and add more only if the trade moves in your favor.

**Quick Take**: Small positions reduce emotional pressure and allow for better decision-making.

### 5. Trade With the Market and Sector Trends
When major indices are down, 80% of stocks also move down. Before buying stocks, ensure major indices (S&P 500, Nasdaq) and the relevant sector ETFs are in uptrends. The nine major sectors to monitor are: Materials (XLB), Energy (XLE), Financial (XLF), Industrial (XLI), Technology (XLK), Consumer Staples (XLP), Utilities (XLU), Consumer Discretionary (XLY), and Healthcare (XLV).

**Quick Take**: Don't fight the trend—80% of stocks follow their sector and market direction.

### 6. Choose Tradable Stocks Wisely
Avoid famous stocks like Apple, Google, and Amazon where you're competing against the world's best traders. Instead, look for lesser-known stocks under $50 per share with average daily volume between 300,000 and 30 million shares. Use bid-ask spread to filter out illiquid stocks (avoid spreads larger than $0.06 per share). Scanning tools can help you narrow thousands of stocks down to 10-15 promising candidates.

**Quick Take**: Trade unknown stocks with good liquidity where you have an edge over institutional traders.

### 7. Never Chase Trades or Average Down on Losers
If you miss a trade, don't chase it at higher prices. The market will be there tomorrow. Similarly, never average down on losing trades by buying more shares—this is throwing good money after bad. The only time averaging down makes sense is for long-term investors in fundamentally sound companies during temporary pullbacks, not for trading.

> "When in doubt get out."

**Quick Take**: Missed opportunities are better than losing money by chasing trades.

### 8. Maintain a Trading Journal
Record every trade with details about your thought process, emotional state, and execution. Your journal should answer: What was your strategy? Did you follow your plan? Why did the trade succeed or fail? This documentation helps you identify patterns, eliminate emotional trading, and refine your strategy over time.

**Quick Take**: A trading journal transforms random trading into systematic improvement.

### 9. Master Chart Reading
Charts are the language of the market. Focus on candlestick charts which provide more complete information than bar charts. Learn key reversal patterns (bullish/bearish engulfing, shooting stars, harami), trend formations, and basic indicators like volume, moving averages, and RSI. Practice reading the right side of the chart (future projections) rather than just analyzing the left side (past price action).

**Quick Take**: Chart reading ability directly correlates with trading success.

### 10. Keep Charts Simple
Avoid indicator overload. While some tools are helpful (volume, stochastic, RSI, OBV), too many indicators create confusion and delay decision-making. Professional traders often use clean charts with just price action and key support/resistance levels. The goal is clarity, not complexity.

**Quick Take**: Simple charts with few quality indicators outperform complex, cluttered charts.

## Conclusion
This book provides a solid foundation for trading success by emphasizing what truly matters: discipline, risk management, and emotional control. The 10 rules aren't complex strategies but fundamental principles that separate profitable traders from losing ones. While experienced traders might find some concepts basic, the emphasis on following rules consistently rather than seeking market predictions is valuable for traders at all levels. The book's strength lies in its focus on practical risk management and the psychological aspects of trading—the areas where most traders fail. If you're struggling with consistent losses or emotional trading, implementing these rules could transform your trading results. The biggest reason to read this book is to understand that trading success isn't about being smart or right—it's about being disciplined and protecting your capital.
