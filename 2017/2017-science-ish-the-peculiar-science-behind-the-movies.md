# Science(ish): The Peculiar Science Behind the Movies
**Authors**: Rick Edwards, Dr Michael Brooks | **Year**: 2017

## Summary
Science(ish) explores the real scientific principles behind popular science fiction movies, examining how Hollywood's imaginative storytelling intersects with actual scientific research. The book analyzes nine iconic films—from The Martian to The Matrix—using them as accessible entry points to discuss complex topics like space travel, genetic engineering, artificial intelligence, and the search for extraterrestrial life. Written in an entertaining conversational format between a TV presenter and a quantum physicist, the book makes sophisticated scientific concepts digestible without sacrificing accuracy.

Perfect for curious movie lovers and science enthusiasts alike, this book reveals that many seemingly far-fetched movie plots are grounded in real scientific research. Readers will gain a deeper appreciation for both cinema and science, understanding how fiction often precedes and inspires real innovation. The conversational tone and humor make complex topics approachable, while the rigorous scientific analysis ensures legitimate learning.

## Core Insights

### Space Colonization: The Martian Reality
NASA and private companies are actively working toward Mars colonization, but the challenges are immense. The Martian's depiction of using human waste for fertilizer and water extraction from atmospheric humidity is scientifically accurate. However, the massive dust storms shown in the film wouldn't actually pose much danger due to Mars's thin atmosphere—only 1% of Earth's atmospheric pressure.

**Quick Take**: Mars colonization is technically plausible but remains extraordinarily expensive and dangerous. The biggest hurdles aren't technological but rather biological: radiation exposure, bone density loss, and psychological effects of isolation.

### Black Holes: Interstellar's Scientific Breakthrough
Christopher Nolan's collaboration with physicist Kip Thorne produced the first scientifically accurate visualization of a black hole. The film's depiction of gravitational time dilation—where one hour equals seven years near a massive black hole—is based on real Einsteinian physics. The groundbreaking CGI used for the movie actually generated new scientific insights published in peer-reviewed journals.

> "Interstellar has literally given us a new scientific view of what a black hole is like."

**Quick Take**: Science fiction can drive real scientific discovery when creators collaborate with experts rather than treating scientific accuracy as optional.

### Genetic Engineering: Beyond Jurassic Park
While dinosaur de-extinction remains theoretically possible, the ethical and practical challenges are monumental. We've successfully extracted and sequenced DNA from extinct species like woolly mammoths, but DNA degrades over time—making 65-million-year-old dinosaur DNA essentially unrecoverable. The greater scientific focus is on "de-extinction" of recently extinct species and using CRISPR technology to prevent endangered species extinction.

**Quick Take**: Genetic engineering capabilities are advancing faster than our ethical frameworks for using them, creating urgent questions about what species we should bring back and why.

### Time Travel: Scientific Possibilities
Time travel to the future is already proven through Einstein's theory of relativity—astronauts age slightly slower than Earth-bound people due to gravitational effects. However, backward time travel faces the "grandfather paradox" and would violate fundamental laws of causality. Some theoretical physicists propose closed time-like curves or wormholes as potential mechanisms, but these remain purely speculative.

**Quick Take**: While time travel to the future happens constantly on a small scale, meaningful time manipulation remains firmly in theoretical physics rather than engineering possibility.

### Pandemics: The 28 Days Later Warning
Viral pandemics represent one of the most realistic existential threats explored in cinema. The rapid spread depicted in films like 28 Days Later and Contagion accurately reflects how modern transportation networks could accelerate global outbreak. Scientists emphasize that we're overdue for a major pandemic, and COVID-19 proved that our healthcare systems remain vulnerable to novel pathogens.

**Quick Take**: Viral pandemic preparedness requires international cooperation and sustained investment in research—not just response capabilities.

### Artificial Intelligence: Beyond Human Comprehension
The leap from current AI to truly conscious machines represents what futurists call "technological singularity"—a point where AI improvement becomes self-perpetuating and potentially beyond human control. Unlike movie portrayals of suddenly sentient robots, real AI development is more likely to produce intelligence fundamentally different from human cognition.

**Quick Take**: The real AI challenge isn't preventing robot uprisings but ensuring that artificial intelligence systems remain aligned with human values as they become increasingly powerful.

### Simulation Theory: The Matrix Hypothesis
The idea that we might be living in a computer simulation has moved from science fiction to serious philosophical consideration. Some physicists point to the universe's mathematical structure and the Planck scale's pixel-like nature as potential evidence. However, critics note that simulation theory is fundamentally untestable and therefore unfalsifiable.

**Quick Take**: Whether we're in a simulation or not doesn't change how we should live our lives, but the thought experiment reveals profound questions about the nature of consciousness and reality.

## Action Items

- **Apply scientific thinking to everyday problems**: Use the scientific method of observation, hypothesis, and testing in personal and professional decisions

- **Support science education and research**: Advocate for better science funding and education, particularly in emerging fields like AI and genetic engineering

- **Develop critical media literacy**: Practice distinguishing between scientifically accurate science fiction and pure fantasy

- **Stay informed about technological ethics**: Follow developments in AI, genetic engineering, and space exploration to understand their societal implications

- **Cultivate scientific curiosity**: Ask questions about how things work and seek evidence-based answers rather than accepting explanations at face value

## Questions to Consider

- If we discovered intelligent alien life tomorrow, how should humanity respond, and what would this mean for our understanding of our place in the universe?

- As AI becomes more sophisticated, what rights and protections should artificial consciousness have, if any?

- Should we pursue de-extinction of species like the woolly mammoth, or would this divert resources from preventing current extinctions?

- If simulation theory were proven true, how should this knowledge change how we live our lives?

- What responsibilities do we have to prevent existential threats like pandemics or climate change, and how do we balance individual freedoms with collective survival?

## Conclusion
Science(ish) is absolutely worth your time if you enjoy either science fiction or popular science—but it's essential if you love both. Edwards and Brooks have created something rare: a book that's genuinely entertaining while maintaining scientific rigor. The biggest reason to read it is the unique perspective it offers on how science fiction influences real scientific research and vice versa.

What makes this book exceptional is its perfect balance of accessibility and depth. The conversational format prevents the material from becoming dry, while Brooks's scientific expertise ensures accuracy. Each chapter works as a standalone exploration of a fascinating topic, but together they create a comprehensive overview of humanity's biggest scientific questions and challenges.

The book's ultimate value lies in how it changes how you watch movies—you'll never again see science fiction as mere entertainment, but as a conversation with real scientific possibility. That shift in perspective alone makes Science(ish) an enlightening read that will enhance both your movie nights and your understanding of the universe.
