# Michael Faraday and the Electrical Century
**Author**: Iwan Morus | **Year**: 2017
**Category**: Science & Nature

## Summary
"Michael Faraday and the Electrical Century" explores the remarkable journey of Michael Faraday, a working-class bookbinder who rose to become one of England's greatest natural philosophers and the pivotal figure in the 19th-century electrical revolution. The book chronicles Faraday's transformation from humble apprentice to scientific pioneer, detailing his groundbreaking discoveries in electromagnetism and induction while situating his work within the broader social, cultural, and political context of Victorian Britain.

This biography offers readers more than just scientific achievement—it reveals how Faraday navigated the treacherous social hierarchies of early 19th-century science, his complex relationship with mentor Humphry Davy, and his role in shaping electricity from a radical, dangerous pursuit into a respectable science that would transform modern society.

Perfect for history enthusiasts, science students, and general readers interested in how scientific revolutions unfold within their social contexts, this book provides a compelling portrait of the man whose discoveries laid the foundation for our electrical age.

## Core Insights

### The Working-Class Revolutionary
Michael Faraday's story is extraordinary not just for his scientific genius, but for his social journey. Born in 1791 to a poor London blacksmith, Faraday began his working life as a bookbinder's apprentice with minimal formal education. His passion for learning led him to attend scientific lectures in his spare time, where he meticulously took notes and recreated experiments. This self-education, combined with his exceptional manual skills and experimental talents, eventually secured him a position at the Royal Institution as laboratory assistant to the celebrated Humphry Davy. Faraday's rise from the working class to the pinnacle of British science challenged the era's rigid social boundaries and demonstrated that genius could emerge from any background.

> "I was never able to make a fact my own without seeing it; if Grove, or Wheatstone, or Gassiot, or any other told me a new fact & wanted my opinion, either of its value, or the cause, or the evidence it could give in any subject, I never could say anything until I had seen the fact."
**Quick Take**: Faraday's commitment to hands-on experimentation and direct observation of nature became his scientific signature and key to success.

### Electromagnetic Revolution
Faraday's 1821 discovery of electromagnetic rotation—demonstrating that a current-carrying wire could rotate around a magnet—was his first major breakthrough. This discovery almost ended his career when he was accused of stealing ideas from William Hyde Wollaston, a more established scientist. The controversy reveals how scientific discovery was intertwined with social status and propriety in early 19th-century Britain. Faraday's determination to prove his independence and originality became a driving force throughout his career.

**Quick Take**: Scientific breakthroughs often come with social and political challenges that must be navigated as carefully as the experimental work itself.

### The Nature of Scientific Mentorship
The complex relationship between Faraday and his mentor Humphry Davy illustrates the tensions inherent in scientific apprenticeship. Davy provided Faraday with unparalleled opportunities, training, and institutional support, but also viewed his assistant's work as his intellectual property. The relationship deteriorated as Faraday gained independence and recognition, culminating in Davy's opposition to Faraday's election to the Royal Society. This story reveals how scientific patronage operated in an era when the lines between master and apprentice, employer and employee, were deeply contested.

**Quick Take**: Mentor relationships in science can be both transformative and fraught with tension as protégés develop their own independent contributions.

### Electricity as Radical Science
Before Faraday helped legitimize it, electricity was considered dangerous and radical—associated with political subversion, atheism, and controversial experiments like galvanic stimulation of corpses (the real-life inspiration for Frankenstein). The book explores how figures like Andrew Crosse created "electrical insects" through experiments, sparking fears that scientists were playing God. Faraday's task was not just making discoveries, but making electricity respectable by separating it from its radical associations and establishing it as proper science.

> "Electricity was the science of atheists, materialists, political radicals and revolutionaries. According to some of its critics, electricity was even to blame for the French Revolution."
**Quick Take**: Scientific respectability often depends on separating knowledge from controversial social and political associations.

### Experimental Philosophy as Performance
At the Royal Institution, Faraday pioneered the art of scientific performance. His famous Christmas lectures drew fashionable London audiences eager to witness nature's wonders. Faraday developed a distinctive lecturing style that made complex experiments accessible while maintaining scientific rigor. He carefully orchestrated demonstrations to make it seem as if "nature herself" was speaking directly to the audience, minimizing both his own role and the visible machinery. This theatrical approach helped make science a form of popular entertainment and education.

**Quick Take**: Effective science communication requires balancing accessibility, entertainment value, and scientific accuracy.

### Field Theory and Physical Intuition
Faraday's most profound theoretical contribution was his concept of "lines of force" in space—the idea that electromagnetic phenomena occurred in the spaces around objects rather than within them. This field theory, represented visually through lines of force emanating from magnets and electrical charges, was revolutionary. Most contemporaries dismissed Faraday's ideas as mathematically unsophisticated, but they later formed the foundation for James Clerk Maxwell's electromagnetic theory and modern physics. Faraday trusted his physical intuition over mathematical formalism.

**Quick Take**: Revolutionary scientific ideas often emerge from visual and physical intuition before they can be expressed in formal mathematical terms.

### Religion and Science
Faraday was a devout member of the Sandemanian church, a small, exclusive Christian sect that emphasized literal interpretation of scripture and separation from mainstream society. His religious beliefs informed his scientific practice—he saw nature as God's book to be read literally, and his belief in conservation of force reflected his conviction that only God could destroy what He had created. This private religious commitment existed in tension with his very public scientific career and helped him maintain critical distance from fashionable London society.

**Quick Take**: Scientific practice can be deeply informed by religious belief, and the relationship between faith and science is often more complex than simple opposition.

## Key Takeaways

- **Social mobility through science**: Faraday's journey shows how scientific talent could transcend class barriers in 19th-century Britain, though not without significant struggle
- **Experimental persistence**: Faraday's methodical, patient approach to experimentation—often repeating trials for months—was crucial to his discoveries
- **Scientific communication matters**: Faraday's skill at making complex ideas accessible helped establish electricity as legitimate science
- **Independence and originality**: Learning to assert intellectual independence from mentors is essential for scientific development
- **Field theory revolution**: Faraday's concept of electromagnetic fields laid groundwork for modern physics
- **Work-life balance**: Faraday maintained scientific productivity while managing heavy administrative duties and religious commitments

## Conclusion
"Michael Faraday and the Electrical Century" is essential reading for anyone interested in how science actually happens—not as a smooth progression of discoveries, but as a messy, socially embedded process involving ambition, mentorship, performance, and politics. Faraday's story reminds us that scientific genius can emerge from unexpected places and that the most revolutionary ideas often require both exceptional experimental skill and the courage to challenge established thinking.

The book's greatest strength is showing how Faraday's personal struggles with class, recognition, and intellectual independence shaped his scientific contributions. His journey from bookbinder to national treasure illustrates both the possibilities and limitations of social mobility in 19th-century Britain. This isn't just a story about scientific discovery—it's about how one man's determination to understand nature helped create the electrical age while transforming what it meant to be a scientist.

**Is it worth your time?** Absolutely. This book offers more than scientific biography—it provides a window into how scientific revolutions unfold within their social contexts, making it valuable for understanding innovation in any field.
