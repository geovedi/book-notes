# 2017 Romance Classics Collection Vol 1
**Authors**: John Cleland, Daniel Defoe, Henry Fielding, Gustave Flaubert, D.H. Lawrence, Vatsyayana, Leopold von Sacher-Masoch | **Year**: 2017
**Category**: Romance & Relationships

## Summary
This provocative collection brings together seven controversial works that pushed boundaries of literature, sexuality, and social convention across three centuries. From 18th-century erotic memoirs to 20th-century modernist explorations of desire, these works share a common thread: they were all considered scandalous, often banned, and frequently challenged prevailing notions of morality, gender roles, and sexual freedom. Each work uses explicit content not merely for shock value, but as a vehicle for deeper social commentary, psychological insight, and philosophical questioning of human nature.

Readers interested in literary history, censorship studies, and the evolution of sexual expression in Western literature will find this collection invaluable. These works demonstrate how literature has served as a battleground for debates about personal freedom, social constraints, and the human condition.

## Core Insights

### Fanny Hill: Memoirs of a Woman of Pleasure (1749)
John Cleland's pioneering erotic novel follows Frances "Fanny" Hill, a country orphan who falls into prostitution in 18th-century London. Rather than mere titillation, the work offers a surprisingly nuanced psychological portrait of a woman navigating sexual commerce with agency and wit. Fanny's journey from innocent victim to empowered sexual professional subverts typical narratives of female victimhood, presenting a character who finds pleasure and profit in her circumstances while maintaining essential humanity.

> "Truth! stark, naked truth, is the word; and I will not so much as take the pains to bestow the strip of a gauze wrapper on it, but paint situations such as they actually rose to me in nature."

**Quick Take**: This work established the template for the erotic novel as literary form, proving that sexual content could coexist with genuine character development and social commentary.

### Moll Flanders (1722)
Daniel Defoe's masterpiece follows the life of a woman born in Newgate prison who survives through wit, charm, and criminal enterprise. Moll's journey through multiple marriages, theft, and prostitution becomes a trenchant critique of 18th-century English society's limited options for women. Her eventual redemption in America offers hope while acknowledging the systemic barriers that forced her into criminality.

> "The pen employed in finishing her story, and making it what you now see it to be, has had no little difficulty to put it into a dress fit to be seen."

**Quick Take**: Defoe creates the first fully realized female criminal protagonist in English literature, using her story to expose society's hypocrisy and the economic desperation driving women to crime.

### The History of Tom Jones, A Foundling (1749)
Henry Fielding's comic epic follows the romantic and sexual adventures of the foundling Tom Jones. While less sexually explicit than other works in this collection, Fielding's frank treatment of sexuality, desire, and human passion was revolutionary for its time. The novel celebrates physical pleasure as natural while embedding it in a sophisticated moral framework that examines virtue, hypocrisy, and social class.

> "I have endeavoured to laugh mankind out of their favourite follies and vices."

**Quick Take**: Fielding established that literary merit and sexual frankness could coexist, paving the way for future authors to explore human desire without sacrificing artistic ambition.

### Madame Bovary (1856)
Gustave Flaubert's groundbreaking novel portrays Emma Bovary, whose romantic ideals and sexual desires clash with provincial French reality. Her affairs and spending become desperate attempts to escape the confines of bourgeois marriage and female limited agency. Flaubert's precise, clinical descriptions of sexual situations shocked 19th-century readers while establishing modern literary realism.

> "She wanted to die, and she wanted to live in Paris."

**Quick Take**: This work established the psychological novel and transformed sexual desire from mere plot device into window on character psychology and social critique.

### Lady Chatterley's Lover (1928)
D.H. Lawrence's controversial novel explores Constance Chatterley's affair with gamekeeper Oliver Mellors after her husband's wartime injury leaves him impotent. Their passionate sexual relationship becomes a rebellion against industrial modernity, class barriers, and emotional sterility. Lawrence's explicit descriptions sparked landmark obscenity trials that helped liberalize publishing standards.

> "Ours is essentially a tragic age, so we refuse to take it tragically. The cataclysm has happened, we are among the ruins, we start to build up new little habitats."

**Quick Take**: Lawrence used sexual explicitness to argue for spiritual and physical wholeness, making sexuality the battlefield for debates about modernity, nature, and human connection.

### The Kama Sutra (400)
Vatsyayana's ancient Indian treatise on love, sexuality, and pleasure represents the world's oldest comprehensive guide to sexual fulfillment. Far from being merely a manual of sexual positions, it encompasses philosophy, psychology, and social conduct, presenting sexuality as integral to human flourishing and spiritual development. Its systematic approach to pleasure shocked Western readers while offering sophisticated understanding of human desire.

> "There are to be found both in Sanscrit poetry and in the Sanscrit drama a certain amount of poetical sentiment and romance, which have, in every country and in every language, thrown an immortal halo round the subject."

**Quick Take**: This work demonstrates that sophisticated sexual philosophy predates Western traditions by millennia, challenging assumptions about sexual morality as cultural universal.

### Venus in Furs (1906)
Leopold von Sacher-Masoch's novella explores the psychological dynamics of dominance and submission through Severin's relationship with Wanda von Dunajew. His desire to be enslaved and humiliated by her becomes a vehicle for examining power, gender roles, and the nature of desire itself. The work gave its name to "masochism" as psychological concept while raising profound questions about freedom and submission.

> "Suppose that I were your slave, your servant, your property—would you be able to punish me for the slightest fault?"

**Quick Take**: This work established masochism as subject for serious literary exploration, using extreme scenarios to examine universal questions about power, love, and human psychology.

## Key Takeaways

- Sexual literature has historically served as vehicle for broader social commentary and philosophical exploration
- Censorship battles over these works helped establish modern free expression principles
- Each work uses explicit content to examine different aspects: power dynamics, gender inequality, class conflict, spiritual alienation
- These novels collectively document evolving understanding of human psychology and sexual fulfillment
- Literary merit and sexual content are not mutually exclusive—these works demonstrate sophisticated artistic achievement alongside frank sexual exploration

## Conclusion
This collection is essential reading for understanding how literature has challenged social boundaries and expanded discussions of human desire. While some works may seem dated in their social assumptions, they collectively document humanity's ongoing struggle to understand sexual fulfillment as integral to human experience. These are not merely "dirty books" but serious literary works that changed how we think about desire, relationships, and the boundaries of acceptable discourse.
