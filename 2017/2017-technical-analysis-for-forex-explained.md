# Technical Analysis for Forex Explained
**Authors**: Wayne Walker | **Year**: 2017

## Summary
"Technical Analysis for Forex Explained" is a practical guide that equips traders with the essential tools and strategies to profit from foreign exchange markets using technical analysis. The book covers everything from basic forex concepts to advanced trading tactics, making it accessible for beginners while providing valuable insights for experienced traders.

This book is ideal for aspiring forex traders, those transitioning from demo to live trading, and anyone seeking to understand how technical indicators can identify profitable trading opportunities in the world's most liquid financial market.

## Core Insights

### Understanding the Forex Market
The foreign exchange market is the world's most liquid market with over $4 trillion in daily turnover, operating 24/5 across major financial centers including London, New York, Tokyo, and Singapore. Unlike stock exchanges, forex is traded over-the-counter (OTC), meaning there's no central exchange and trading rules are determined by counterparties. The market moves based on economic data, rumors, and geopolitical events, offering traders the ability to profit from both rising and falling prices through margin trading with leverage.

> "The key point to making money with technical analysis is identifying the trend and trading along with it. Trends reveal to you where prices are most likely to head in the future."

**Quick Take**: Focus on major currency pairs (EUR/USD, USD/JPY, GBP/USD, USD/CHF) as they offer the tightest spreads and highest liquidity, reducing trading costs and improving profit potential.

### Trend Analysis Principles
Successful forex trading begins with identifying market trends. Uptrends form higher highs and higher lows, downtrends create lower highs and lower lows, while sideways trends show price levels trading in ranges. The fundamental rule is to trade with the trend, not against it. Trends rarely move straight up or down but typically advance in one direction before temporarily retracing, then continuing the original movement. Candlestick charts visually represent these movements, showing the open, high, low, and close prices for each time period.

> "Trends reveal to you where prices are most likely to head in the future. If the trend of a currency pair is heading up, then you need to buy the currency pair to make money."

**Quick Take**: Master identifying higher highs/higher lows for uptrends and lower highs/lower lows for downtrends across multiple timeframes before executing any trades.

### Essential Technical Indicators
Three core technical indicators form the foundation of profitable forex trading. Moving averages help identify trends by smoothing price data over set periods. A shorter moving average crossing above a longer one signals an uptrend (golden cross), while crossing below signals a downtrend. The Relative Strength Index (RSI) identifies overbought (above 70) and oversold (below 30) conditions, serving as a leading indicator. Bollinger Bands measure volatility and define trading channels, with prices typically remaining between upper and lower bands, making band touches potential reversal points.

> "Readings below thirty indicate that the market maybe oversold and when you see or hear the term oversold it mean excessive selling. Readings above seventy indicate that the market maybe overbought, excessive buying."

**Quick Take**: Use RSI settings of 4-7 for day trading, 7 for swing trading, and default Bollinger Band settings (20:2) for optimal performance across most currency pairs.

### Chart Patterns and Trading Signals
Double tops and double bottoms are powerful reversal patterns that signal trend changes. A double bottom forms after a downtrend when price hits support levels twice without breaking lower, indicating a potential uptrend. The neckline drawn between the two bottoms serves as an entry point for long positions. Conversely, a double top forms after an uptrend when price hits resistance twice without breaking higher, suggesting a potential downtrend. The neckline between tops becomes an entry point for short positions.

> "With a double bottom, you could think of placing your long entry order above the 'neckline' because you are expecting the trend to change upwards."

**Quick Take**: Wait for confirmation of pattern completion before entering trades, and always place stop-loss orders just below the lows (for longs) or above the highs (for shorts) of the pattern formation.

### Technical vs Fundamental Analysis
The choice between technical and fundamental analysis depends on your trading timeframe. Day traders and scalpers (positions lasting seconds to minutes) rely primarily on technical analysis, while long-term investors (positions held for years) focus on fundamental factors like economic data, company performance, and market conditions. Short-term traders should monitor key economic reports including central bank decisions (FOMC, ECB, BOE), Consumer Price Index (CPI), Nonfarm Payrolls, and Housing Starts, as unexpected deviations from market expectations create significant trading opportunities.

> "In reality there is not this competition between technical or fundamental, what I say is it really boils down to your time frame."

**Quick Take**: Align your analysis method with your trading horizon—use technical indicators for day trading and economic calendars for swing to position trading.

### Quick Trade Strategy Implementation
Effective trading begins with selecting the appropriate timeframe based on your trading style. Day traders should use 1-hour charts for market overview and 15-30 minute charts for trade execution, while swing traders can use longer timeframes. Within the Bollinger Bands channel, price near the top band suggests potential reversal (sell opportunity), while price near the bottom suggests potential reversal (buy opportunity). Ideal buy signals occur when RSI rises from oversold levels (30-40) and price is near the bottom band, while sell signals happen when RSI falls from overbought levels (70-80) and price is near the top band.

> "When faced with grey areas/undecided I suggest that you use buy or sell stop orders. Orders are NOT trades so no money is at risk until they are executed."

**Quick Take**: Use stop orders instead of market orders in uncertain conditions to reduce slippage risk and only enter trades when multiple indicators confirm the same direction.

### Risk Management and Trading Psychology
The five primary reasons traders lose money are unrealistic expectations, no trading plan, excessive risk, confusing trading with investing, and over/under-trading. Successful traders use low leverage to survive bad days, follow the "no cash, no trading" golden rule, and implement scaling in/out strategies. They focus on 2-5 major currency pairs rather than trying to master dozens, set daily profit/loss limits, and remove losing positions quickly using stop-losses typically set at 15-25 pips.

> "Failing to plan is planning to fail."

**Quick Take**: Risk no more than 2-5% of your account on any single trade, use stop-losses religiously, and have a written trading plan that defines entry, exit, and position sizing rules before placing any trades.

### Demo to Live Trading Transition
The critical transition from demo to live trading requires three key elements. First, practice with realistic account balances that match your intended live account amount to internalize the emotional and psychological impact of gains and losses. Second, use realistic trade sizes appropriate for your account balance. Third, achieve consistent profitability in demo trading before risking real capital. Success in demo trading doesn't guarantee live trading success, but failure in demo trading almost guarantees live trading failure.

> "When you are trading you don't need to make money every day, but at the end of the week or in general you should end up in the black."

**Quick Take**: Demo trade for at least 1-2 months with the exact account size and position sizes you plan to use live, and only transition after achieving consistent monthly profits.

## Action Items
- Practice identifying trend patterns on historical charts before risking real capital
- Set up demo trading accounts with realistic balances matching your intended live account size
- Master the three core indicators: Moving averages, RSI, and Bollinger Bands
- Create a written trading plan with specific entry, exit, and risk management rules
- Focus on 2-5 major currency pairs with tight spreads rather than trying to trade everything
- Implement the 2% risk rule and never risk more than you can afford to lose
- Keep a trading journal to track performance and identify improvement areas

## Conclusion
"Technical Analysis for Forex Explained" delivers exactly what its title promises—a clear, practical guide to using technical analysis for forex trading. Wayne Walker provides a solid foundation of both theoretical knowledge and actionable strategies that new traders can implement immediately while offering valuable insights for experienced traders looking to refine their approach.

The book's greatest strength lies in its balanced perspective, acknowledging that technical analysis is just one tool in a trader's arsenal and emphasizing the critical importance of risk management, realistic expectations, and proper psychological preparation. The quick trade guide and transition from demo to live trading chapters alone make this book worth reading for anyone serious about forex trading.

While not a comprehensive encyclopedia of every technical indicator known to traders, it focuses on the most effective tools and strategies that actually work in real-world trading. For traders willing to put in the practice time and maintain discipline, this book provides an excellent roadmap to potentially profitable forex trading.
