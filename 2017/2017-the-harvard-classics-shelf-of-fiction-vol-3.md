# The Harvard Classics Shelf of Fiction Vol. 3
**Authors**: Laurence Sterne, Jane Austen | **Year**: 1768, 1813

## Summary
This volume brings together two masterpieces of English literature that represent contrasting approaches to storytelling and human nature. Laurence Sterne's "A Sentimental Journey" offers a whimsical, introspective travel narrative that follows Yorick through France and Italy, focusing on emotional encounters and philosophical musings rather than conventional adventures. Jane Austen's "Pride and Prejudice" presents a sharp, satirical examination of manners, marriage, and social class in Regency England, centering on the headstrong Elizabeth Bennet and her complicated relationship with the proud Mr. Darcy.

Readers seeking to understand the evolution of the English novel will find this collection invaluable, while lovers of character-driven stories will appreciate both works' deep psychological insight and wit. Those interested in social commentary, human nature, and the art of storytelling will discover two complementary perspectives that continue to influence literature today.

## Core Insights

### Sentimental Journey: The Art of Emotional Travel
**Laurence Sterne's revolutionary approach transforms travel writing from mere observation to emotional exploration.** Rather than cataloging sights and landmarks, Sterne focuses on the feelings and connections that travel evokes, creating a new genre that prioritizes sentiment over scenery. His encounter with the monk demonstrates how brief interactions can reveal profound truths about human nature and our own moral failings.

> "I had predetermined not to give him a single sou; and accordingly, I put my purse into my pocket—buttoned it—set myself a little more upon my centre, and advanced up gravely to him"
> _Original: "I had predetermined not to give him a single sous; and accordingly, I put my purse into my pocket—buttoned it—set myself a little more upon my centre, and advanced up gravely to him"_

**Quick Take**: True travel isn't about places visited but about how those places transform our emotional landscape and self-understanding.

### Pride and Prejudice: First Impressions and Lasting Truths
**Austen masterfully demonstrates how initial judgments often mask deeper truths about character and motivation.** The famous opening line immediately establishes her satirical approach to social conventions, while the relationship between Elizabeth and Darcy becomes a vehicle for exploring how pride and prejudice blind us to genuine worth.

> "It is a truth universally acknowledged, that a single man in possession of a good fortune, must be in want of a wife."
> _Original: "It is a truth universally acknowledged, that a single man in possession of a good fortune, must be in want of a wife."_

**Quick Take**: Our judgments of others often reveal more about our own limitations than about the people we're judging.

### The Power of Self-Reflection
**Both authors champion introspection as the path to wisdom and growth.** Sterne's narrator constantly analyzes his own reactions and motivations, while Elizabeth Bennet's journey from hasty judgment to self-awareness forms the backbone of Austen's narrative. Both works suggest that understanding ourselves is prerequisite to understanding others.

> "I have behaved very ill, said I within myself; but I have only just set out upon my travels; and shall learn better manners as I get along."
> _Original: "I have behaved very ill, said I within myself; but I have only just set out upon my travels; and shall learn better manners as I get along."_

**Quick Take**: The most valuable journeys are those that take us inward rather than outward.

### Social Commentary Through Individual Stories
**Both novels use personal narratives to critique broader social structures.** Sterne exposes the hypocrisy of charitable institutions through his monk encounter, while Austen dismantles the marriage market and class prejudices through the Bennet family's struggles.

> "My dear Mr. Bennet," said his lady to him one day, "have you heard that Netherfield Park is let at last?"
> _Original: "My dear Mr. Bennet," said his lady to him one day, "have you heard that Netherfield Park is let at last?"_

**Quick Take**: Individual stories often reveal societal truths more powerfully than direct criticism.

## Key Takeaways

- **Travel as self-discovery**: Both works suggest that journeys—literal or emotional—are primarily opportunities for self-understanding rather than external achievement.
- **Question first impressions**: Initial judgments are often unreliable; true understanding requires time, reflection, and willingness to admit error.
- **Social critique through character**: Complex characters reveal societal truths more effectively than direct social commentary.
- **Emotional intelligence over intellectual rigor**: Both authors value feeling and empathy over cold rationality or conventional success.
- **The value of self-awareness**: Personal growth begins with honest self-examination and willingness to change.

## Conclusion

This volume offers readers two complementary approaches to understanding human nature and society. Sterne's experimental, emotionally-driven narrative pushes the boundaries of what fiction can do, while Austen's polished social commentary demonstrates how traditional forms can be used to revolutionary effect. Together they represent the range and possibility of the English novel at its formative stage.

**Worth your time?** Absolutely, especially if you're interested in the development of the novel or enjoy character-driven stories that combine wit with psychological depth. The biggest reason to read these works is their timeless exploration of how we understand ourselves and others—making them as relevant today as when they were written. Both books reward multiple readings and continue to offer new insights with each encounter.
