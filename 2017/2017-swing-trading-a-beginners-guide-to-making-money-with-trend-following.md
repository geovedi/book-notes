# 2017-Swing Trading: A Beginner's Guide to Making Money with Trend Following
**Authors**: George Pain | **Year**: 2017
**Category**: Business & Finance

## Summary

Swing Trading: A Beginner's Guide to Making Money with Trend Following" is a practical introduction to swing trading strategies for stock market beginners. The book explains how traders can profit from short-to-medium-term price movements (holding positions for several days to weeks) by identifying and riding market trends. Unlike long-term investing, swing trading focuses on capturing price differentials through technical analysis and strategic timing. The author emphasizes risk management, trend identification, and systematic approaches to trading that can be implemented part-time alongside other activities.

This guide is ideal for aspiring traders who want to understand the fundamentals of swing trading without overwhelming complexity. Readers will learn practical strategies for identifying trends, managing risks, and capitalizing on market opportunities like IPOs and earnings announcements.

## Core Insights

### Understanding Swing Trading vs. Investing
Swing trading differs fundamentally from investing in mindset and approach. While investing focuses on long-term wealth accumulation through fundamental analysis, swing trading targets short-term price movements using technical analysis. Swing traders seek to maximize profits from price differentials rather than holding assets for capital appreciation. This distinction is crucial because approaching swing trading with an investor's mindset often leads to missed opportunities by holding positions too long. The key is buying on downswings when prices are about to turn up and selling on upswings when prices are about to turn down.

> "In trading, your desire is to maximize profit. In investing, your desire is to maximize wealth. Thus, although your ultimate aim could be becoming wealthy, trading and investing are two distinct approaches."

**Quick Take**: Success requires a trader's mindset focused on price movements, not long-term company fundamentals.

### Risk Management and Strategy
Effective risk management separates successful traders from failures. The book outlines three risk mindset categories: risk-averse, risk takers, and risk-neutral, noting that swing trading favors risk takers. Essential risk management tools include stop-loss orders, options contracts (calls and puts), and position sizing. A solid risk strategy considers investment time horizon, alternative income streams, persistence levels, and asset allocation matching risk tolerance. The golden rule is preserving capital during losing periods by reducing position sizes.

> "What differentiates a shrewd trader from an ordinary trader is the ability to know, understand and appreciate risk."

**Quick Take**: Implement systematic risk management rules before entering any trade to protect your capital.

### Trend Following Fundamentals
Trend following involves identifying and riding market movements rather than predicting specific price levels. Trends are classified by direction (uptrend with higher highs, downtrend with lower lows) and duration (primary trends lasting months to years, secondary trends lasting weeks to months, short-term trends lasting days to weeks). Key technical indicators include moving averages (50-day, 100-day, 200-day), MACD for momentum, RSI for overbought/oversold conditions, and OBV for volume confirmation. Successful trend followers wait for trends to establish themselves before entering positions.

> "Trend followers enter the market after a trend properly establishes itself on the hope that it will persist along its path for a significantly long time."

**Quick Take**: Focus on identifying established trends and using technical indicators for confirmation rather than trying to predict market movements.

### Breakout Trading Strategies
Breakouts occur when prices move outside established support/resistance ranges with increased volume, often initiating major price trends. Successful breakout trading requires identifying securities with strong resistance/support levels, waiting for confirmed breakouts, setting realistic objectives, and understanding when patterns fail. Key breakout indicators include MACD for momentum, RSI for reversal confirmation, and volume indicators like VWMA. The book emphasizes waiting until near market close to confirm breakouts rather than jumping in prematurely.

> "Everything has its own limits. If you plan to succeed, you must be able to establish the operational limits within which to perform."

**Quick Take**: Patiently wait for confirmed breakouts with volume support before entering positions.

### Bear Market Profit Strategies
Bear markets offer unique profit opportunities for prepared traders. Unlike bull markets with clear trends, bear markets often favor range trading over trend trading. Profit strategies include short selling bad stocks (which fall faster than good stocks), hunting for dividends from stable companies, using covered calls for income, purchasing call options near market bottoms, and rotating sectors based on economic conditions. The key is understanding that bearish trading requires different skills and mindset than bullish trading.

> "Bear markets offer better chances of making huge profits as most bullish herds run away from it, thus leaving you with plenty of space and tons of opportunities."

**Quick Take**: Master bear-specific strategies like short selling and range trading to profit when others are selling.

## Action Items

- **Start paper trading**: Practice swing trading strategies with virtual money before risking real capital
- **Develop a trading plan**: Create written rules for entry, exit, and risk management
- **Master technical indicators**: Focus on moving averages, MACD, RSI, and volume analysis
- **Build a watchlist**: Identify 10-20 stocks that show good swing trading characteristics
- **Set up risk management**: Always use stop-loss orders and limit position sizes
- **Track earnings announcements**: Use PEAD (Post-Earnings Announcement Drift) for trading opportunities
- **Practice patience**: Wait for high-probability setups rather than forcing trades

## Questions to Consider

- What is your personal risk tolerance level, and does it align with swing trading requirements?
- How much time can you realistically dedicate to market monitoring each week?
- Do you have the psychological discipline to follow systematic rules without emotional interference?
- What capital amount can you afford to risk without affecting your essential living expenses?
- Are you prepared for the learning curve, including inevitable initial losses?

## Conclusion

This book provides a solid foundation for beginners interested in swing trading, offering practical strategies without overwhelming complexity. Its strength lies in clear explanations of core concepts like trend following, risk management, and market psychology. The book is worth your time if you're new to trading and want a straightforward introduction to swing trading strategies that can be implemented part-time.

The biggest reason to read this book is its emphasis on practical risk management and systematic approaches rather than get-rich-quick promises. While the content could benefit from more detailed examples and advanced strategies, it serves as an excellent starting point for understanding the mindset and basic techniques needed for swing trading success.
