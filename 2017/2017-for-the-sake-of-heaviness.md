# For the Sake of Heaviness: The History of Metal Blade Records
**Authors**: Brian Slagel, Mark Eglinton | **Year**: 2017

## Summary
"For the Sake of Heaviness" chronicles the remarkable 35-year journey of Metal Blade Records, the most important independent heavy metal label in music history. Founded by Brian Slagel—a passionate metal fan who started as a record store clerk and fanzine publisher—the label launched the careers of Metallica, Slayer, and countless other metal bands while staying true to its underground roots through changing musical landscapes.

This book serves as both a personal memoir and an essential chapter in heavy metal history, showing how one fan's dedication to music he loved accidentally created an institution that would shape the genre for decades. It's a testament to the power of authenticity, passion, and believing in great music when nobody else will.

Music industry professionals, metal fans, and entrepreneurs will discover how a label operated from a mother's garage became a metal powerhouse while maintaining its independence and integrity. Readers learn valuable lessons about building lasting businesses based on passion rather than profit, and how the DIY ethos can create cultural movements that outlast trends.

## Core Insights

### The Accidental Entrepreneur: From Fan to Founder

Brian Slagel never planned to start a record label. He was just a metal-obsessed teenager working at an LA record store, publishing a fanzine called "New Heavy Metal Revue," and trading tapes with fellow fans worldwide. The label was born from a simple desire to promote the local LA metal scene that wasn't getting attention. His first release, the "Metal Massacre" compilation in 1982, was meant to showcase local bands—including a then-unknown Metallica, who got on the record when Slagel told Lars Ulrich "if you form a band, I'll give you a slot."

> "I was still just trying to help out the local scene. I didn't think I'd ever own a record label; I was still way more into the journalistic aspect of what I was involved in."

**Quick Take**: The most successful businesses often emerge from genuine passion rather than strategic planning. When you're solving real problems you care about, opportunities naturally follow.

### Metal Blade as Family: The Community Model

Throughout its history, Metal Blade has operated more like a family than a corporation. Slagel signed bands he genuinely loved, supported them through ups and downs, and maintained relationships even when artists left for bigger deals. This created incredible loyalty—many bands returned to Metal Blade after major label experiences, and artists who never signed with the label still speak reverently about Slagel's influence.

The family extended beyond musicians to include employees, many of whom stayed for decades, and even fans who became lifelong friends. This community approach helped Metal Blade survive industry upheavals that destroyed larger, more impersonal labels.

> "Metal Blade is and always will be a family. Whether they've recorded one album for the label or fifteen, they are all significant."

**Quick Take**: Building a business around genuine relationships and shared values creates resilience that money can't buy.

### Adaptability Through Musical Revolutions

Metal Blade survived multiple dramatic shifts in heavy metal: from the NWOBHM explosion to glam metal's dominance, through thrash's rise, death metal's emergence, metalcore's popularity, and beyond. While other labels either went out of business or sold out, Metal Blade adapted by staying open to new subgenres while maintaining its core identity as a home for quality heavy music.

Slagel's formula was simple: sign bands he genuinely loved regardless of commercial trends. This meant releasing everything from traditional metal to extreme death metal, from melodic power metal to modern metalcore. The diversity became a strength rather than a weakness.

> "Through it all, Metal Blade has adapted and rolled with every punch the business has thrown, releasing in excess of a thousand albums over these thirty-five years."

**Quick Take**: In changing markets, doubling down on your core values while staying open to evolution is more sustainable than chasing trends.

### Independence as Creative Freedom

Despite numerous opportunities to sell to major labels, Metal Blade remained independent, allowing Slagel to make decisions based on musical merit rather than commercial expectations. This independence enabled unique signings that bigger labels wouldn't touch, experimental projects, and the freedom to say "yes" to interesting opportunities—like releasing comedy albums by metal-loving comedians or starting a sports-and-metal podcast.

The independence also meant Metal Blade could take chances on developing artists over multiple albums, allowing bands to grow without the pressure of immediate commercial success. This patient approach cultivated many long-term careers.

> "A huge part of the appeal for me has always been to have the freedom to say 'yes' to left-field ideas. If we were owned by somebody else, or had a partner, we probably wouldn't be able to have these kinds of adventures."

**Quick Take**: Creative freedom isn't just about artistic expression—it's a competitive advantage that allows you to seize opportunities more nimble competitors can't.

### The Curation Principle: Taste as a Business Model

Metal Blade's success ultimately came down to Slagel's consistently good taste in heavy metal. He signed what he loved, and his ear for quality became the label's brand. Fans learned to trust Metal Blade releases because they knew Slagel wouldn't put his name on anything he didn't genuinely believe in.

This curation principle extended beyond new artists to include reissuing classic metal albums that had fallen out of print, preserving metal history for future generations. The label became both a forward-looking tastemaker and a guardian of metal's heritage.

> "Brian is the true vanguard of heavy metal. He loves it. He supports it. He really does live it. He doesn't foghorn what he does or what he has done. He just does it."

**Quick Take**: In an era of infinite content, trusted curation becomes increasingly valuable. Your personal taste can become a sustainable business model when consistently applied.

## Action Items

- **Follow your obsessions**: Slagel's success came from pursuing what he genuinely loved rather than what was commercially viable. What are you passionate enough about to build something around?

- **Build community before business**: Metal Blade succeeded because Slagel prioritized relationships and scene-building over immediate profits. Focus on serving your community first.

- **Stay independent**: Resist the temptation to sell out or compromise your values. Creative freedom often pays dividends in ways money can't measure.

- **Adapt without losing identity**: Metal Blade survived by embracing new metal subgenres while maintaining its core mission. Change can strengthen rather than dilute your brand.

- **Trust your taste**: Your personal preferences and judgment can become a valuable asset if applied consistently and honestly.

- How can you build business relationships that feel more like family than transactions?

## Conclusion

"For the Sake of Heaviness" is essential reading for anyone interested in how independent culture thrives in corporate-dominated industries. Slagel's story demonstrates that authenticity, patience, and unwavering dedication to quality can build lasting institutions—even in seemingly hostile environments.

The book is worth your time if you're a music fan interested in behind-the-scenes stories, an entrepreneur seeking inspiration for building value-driven businesses, or anyone who believes that staying true to your vision is more important than chasing trends. Metal Blade's 35-year survival in the brutal music industry proves that sometimes the most radical act is simply refusing to compromise on what matters most.

Read this to understand how one fan's accidental entrepreneurship helped shape heavy metal history, and to discover lessons in building lasting cultural institutions that any industry can apply.
