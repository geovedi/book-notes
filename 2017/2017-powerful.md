# 2017-Powerful
**Authors**: Patty McCord | **Year**: 2017
**Category**: Business & Finance

## Summary
"Powerful" is a revolutionary guide to building a high-performance workplace culture that thrives on freedom and responsibility, written by the co-creator of Netflix's legendary culture deck. McCord challenges conventional HR wisdom, arguing that the traditional systems of managing people—policies, procedures, performance reviews, and perks—are outdated and counterproductive in today's rapidly changing business environment. Instead, she proposes treating employees like adults, hiring only high performers, practicing radical honesty, and creating a culture where everyone understands the business deeply.

This book is essential for leaders who want to build nimble, high-performing teams that can adapt quickly to change. Readers will learn how to strip away bureaucracy, foster open communication, and create an environment where talented people can do their best work without unnecessary constraints.

## Core Insights

### Treat People Like Adults: The Power of Freedom and Responsibility
The most fundamental shift in thinking required is to stop treating employees like children who need constant supervision, incentives, and rules. The elaborate systems of management developed in the 20th century are completely inadequate for today's fast-paced business environment.

At Netflix, they discovered that when they eliminated policies like vacation limits and expense reports—and simply told people to use good judgment—people didn't abuse the freedom. They took reasonable time off and spent company money responsibly. The key insight is that people walk in the door with power; a company's job isn't to empower them but to create conditions where they can exercise that power.

**Quick Take**: Start small by eliminating one policy and replacing it with trust in your team's judgment.

### Every Employee Must Understand the Business
One of the most critical gaps in most companies is that employees don't truly understand how the business works, what challenges it faces, or how their work contributes to success. McCord argues that clear, continuous communication about business context is more valuable than formal training programs.

At Netflix, they created "new employee college" where department heads presented detailed information about their part of the business, including metrics and deliverables. This gave everyone a holistic view and encouraged questions at all levels. When people understand the business deeply, they can make better decisions and contribute more effectively.

> "If you stop any employee, at any level of the company, in the break room or the elevator and ask what are the five most important things the company is working on for the next six months, that person should be able to tell you, rapid fire."

**Quick Take**: Make business literacy your top priority—share financials, challenges, and strategy openly with everyone.

### Practice Radical Honesty
Humans hate being lied to and spun, yet most companies avoid telling the truth about performance and business challenges. McCord advocates for face-to-face honesty, delivered respectfully but directly. This includes telling people when their performance isn't meeting expectations and being transparent about business problems.

Netflix implemented practices like "Start, Stop, Continue" feedback sessions where team members told each other what to start doing, stop doing, and continue doing—sometimes in front of groups. While uncomfortable at first, people learned to appreciate the clarity and opportunity to improve.

**Quick Take**: Practice giving specific, actionable feedback and encourage honest dialogue throughout your organization.

### Build the Company You Want to Be
Focus relentlessly on the future and build the company now that you want to be then. This means proactively ensuring you have the right people with the right skills for the challenges ahead, not just for today's needs.

Netflix constantly evolved its approach as the business changed—from DVD rental to streaming to original content production. They weren't afraid to make tough changes quickly when the business demanded it, even when those changes were unpopular.

**Quick Take**: Regularly assess whether your team has the skills needed for future challenges and make changes proactively.

### Have the Right Person in Every Position
Talent density is crucial—having only high performers creates an environment where excellence is the norm. The best perk you can give employees is excellent colleagues to work alongside. This means being ruthless about hiring only exceptional people and being willing to let go of those who aren't the right fit.

McCord developed a simple algorithm for personnel decisions: "Is what this person loves to do, that they're extraordinarily good at doing, something we need someone to be great at?" This takes emotion out of decisions and focuses on alignment between passion, skill, and business need.

**Quick Take**: Use the passion-skill-need alignment test for every person on your team.

### Pay People What They're Worth to You
Compensation should be a judgment call based on what someone is worth to your company, not tied to rigid formulas or market rates. Netflix's approach was to pay top-of-market for exceptional performers, understanding that keeping a great person was more valuable than saving money on salary.

They eliminated annual bonuses and formal performance reviews tied to compensation, instead focusing on paying people well throughout the year and having honest conversations about performance when needed.

**Quick Take**: Pay your best people exceptionally well and don't use compensation as a motivation tool.

### Master the Art of Good Good-byes
Sometimes people need to move on—for their sake and the company's. The key is to make these transitions quickly, honestly, and supportively. Rather than lengthy performance improvement plans that often prove incompetence rather than develop skills, have honest conversations when someone isn't the right fit.

The goal is to be a great place to be from, not just a great place to work at. This means actively helping people find new opportunities when they leave, providing honest references, and maintaining good relationships.

**Quick Take**: Use frequent "ten-game check-ins" instead of annual reviews to address performance issues promptly.

## Best Quotes
- "People have power. A company's job isn't to empower people; it's to remind people that they walk in the door with power and to create the conditions for them to exercise it."
- "The greatest motivation is contributing to success."
- "Being given a great problem to tackle and the right colleagues to tackle it with is the best incentive of all."
- "If you don't tell your people about how the business is doing and the problems being confronted—good, bad, and ugly—then they will get that information somewhere else, and it will often be misinformation."
- "Telling the truth about perceived problems, in a timely fashion and face to face, is the single most effective way to solve problems."

## Action Items
- Eliminate one unnecessary policy or procedure this month and replace it with trust in your team's judgment
- Start sharing business metrics, challenges, and strategy openly with your entire team
- Practice giving direct, specific feedback to at least one person this week
- Assess each team member using the passion-skill-need alignment test
- Replace annual performance reviews with frequent, informal check-ins
- Have honest conversations with team members who may not be in the right role

## Questions to Consider
- How many of your current policies and procedures could be eliminated without harming the business?
- Do your employees truly understand how your business makes money and what challenges it faces?
- Are you having honest conversations about performance issues promptly, or waiting for formal review periods?
- Is everyone on your team doing work they're passionate about and great at?
- Are you building a network to help team members find great opportunities when they need to move on?

## Conclusion
"Powerful" is worth your time if you're tired of bureaucratic HR practices that don't drive results and want to build a culture of high performance and trust. The biggest reason to read it is that it challenges everything you think you know about managing people and offers a proven alternative that actually works.

McCord's approach isn't easy—it requires courage to be honest, discipline to maintain high standards, and willingness to do things differently from everyone else. But the results speak for themselves: a Netflix culture that enabled the company to transform from DVD rental to streaming giant to major content producer. The core message is simple but profound: treat people like adults, be radically honest, and focus on building teams that can tackle any challenge. In a world of constant change, that's not just nice to have—it's essential for survival and success.
