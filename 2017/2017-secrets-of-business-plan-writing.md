# Secrets of Business Plan Writing
**Authors**: Andre Besedin | **Year**: 2017
**Category**: Business & Finance

## Summary
This practical guide demystifies the business planning process by providing a comprehensive framework for creating effective business plans that serve both as internal roadmaps and external financing tools. The book approaches business planning as a dynamic, living document that should guide business operations while securing necessary funding.

The book is designed for entrepreneurs, small business owners, and managers who need to create professional business plans either for internal strategic guidance or external financing purposes. Readers will learn how to structure compelling plans that address the key concerns of investors and lenders while maintaining practical relevance for day-to-day business operations.

## Core Insights

### The Dual Purpose of Business Plans
A business plan serves two fundamental purposes: it acts as your business blueprint and operational guide, while simultaneously serving as a critical document for securing financing. The book emphasizes that a successful plan must balance both internal guidance needs with external stakeholder requirements. Your plan should be brief yet comprehensive, using the "keyword approach" - answering Who, What, Where, When, Why, How, and How Much for each section.

> "A business plan is a comprehensive, written description of the business of an enterprise. It is an in-depth report on a company's management, products, production systems, organization, marketing strategy, markets and customers."

**Quick Take**: Start with keywords to ensure you cover all essential aspects while keeping your plan focused and actionable.

### Three Types of Business Plans
The book categorizes business plans into three distinct types based on business stage and financing needs. Summary Plans (10-15 pages) work best for early-stage businesses testing investment waters. Full Business Plans (20-40 pages) are the standard for serious financing requests, providing detailed operations and projections. Operational Business Plans (40-100+ pages) serve as comprehensive internal management tools for established companies.

> "There are no settled rules. Your plan's length will depend, to a great extent, on what you need it to fulfill and how modern and sophisticated your organization's operations will be."

**Quick Take**: Match your plan's depth to your specific needs - don't over-document for early-stage ventures, but don't under-prepare for serious financing requests.

### The Eight Essential Components
Every effective business plan must contain eight key sections: Executive Summary, Background, Products and Services, Markets and Clients, Business Operations and Organization, Human Resources, Legal Framework, and Financial Planning. The Executive Summary is arguably the most critical section, providing a high-level overview that determines whether readers will continue.

> "The executive summary is arguably the most vital single piece of your report. It gives an abnormal level overview of the reason for the business plan, the fundamental features, and the financial resources required."

**Quick Take**: Write your Executive Summary last, after completing all other sections, to ensure it accurately captures your complete business vision.

### Financial Planning Framework
The financial section must include four critical documents: Cash Flow Statement (budget), Three-Year Income Projection, Break-even Analysis, and Balance Sheet. The Cash Flow Statement is particularly crucial as it projects actual money movement rather than just accounting profits, helping identify when cash will be needed to cover operations.

> "The cash flow statement identifies when money is expected to be getting, how much money will be gained, when money must be spent to pay bills and debts, and how much money will be expected to pay costs."

**Quick Take**: Focus on cash flow projections rather than just profit projections - many profitable businesses fail due to cash flow timing issues.

### Risk Management and Sensitivity Analysis
The book emphasizes conducting a SWOT (Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats) analysis during planning. This helps organize thoughts, analyze risks, and develop strategies. Additionally, anticipating potential problems and preparing contingency plans demonstrates business maturity to investors.

> "A SWOT analysis will help you in organizing your thoughts, analyzing your dangers, and setting up your strategy. Build on your strengths, address your weaknesses, exploit opportunities, and develop strategies to deal with threats."

**Quick Take**: Address risks proactively in your plan - investors prefer businesses that anticipate challenges rather than pretending they don't exist.

## Action Items

- Use the keyword approach (Who, What, Where, When, Why, How, How Much) to ensure comprehensive coverage
- Prepare a thorough competitor analysis worksheet before writing your marketing section
- Create location analysis worksheets covering 20 key factors including traffic patterns, utilities, and labor costs
- Build your financial documents in sequence: Cash Flow → Income Projection → Break-even → Balance Sheet
- Develop contingency plans for best-case and worst-case scenarios
- Include supporting documents but distribute them strategically based on confidentiality needs
- Schedule quarterly reviews to compare projections with actual performance

## Questions to Consider

- What type of business plan (summary, full, or operational) best matches my current business stage and funding needs?
- How can I present my business plan to different audiences (investors, lenders, employees) while maintaining core consistency?
- What industry-specific trends and competitive forces will most impact my business over the next 3-5 years?
- How detailed should my financial projections be to demonstrate credibility without overwhelming complexity?
- What risks have I not adequately addressed in my planning, and how can I develop effective contingency strategies?

## Conclusion
This book delivers exceptional value for anyone needing to create a professional business plan. Its greatest strength is the balance between comprehensive structure and practical guidance, providing specific templates and worksheets while maintaining strategic focus. The systematic approach ensures all critical components are covered without getting bogged down in unnecessary detail.

The most compelling reason to read this guide is its emphasis on business plans as living documents rather than static reports. The book excels at showing how proper planning simultaneously guides operations and secures financing - a dual purpose that many other business planning books fail to adequately address. For entrepreneurs and small business owners, the practical frameworks and real-world examples make complex financial planning accessible and actionable.
