# 2017-Procrastination Self-Assessment Toolkit
**Authors**: Mira Jelica | **Year**: 2017
**Category**: Self-Help & Personal Development

## Summary
This practical toolkit serves as a diagnostic and solution-oriented guide for understanding and overcoming procrastination. The book begins with a comprehensive 10-question self-assessment quiz that helps readers identify their procrastination patterns, followed by detailed categorization into three main procrastinator types. It then provides targeted solutions based on the specific fears and pain points that drive procrastination behaviors.

This resource is perfect for anyone who struggles with delaying tasks and wants to understand the psychological drivers behind their procrastination. Readers will gain clarity about their specific procrastination style and learn practical strategies to overcome their time-wasting tendencies.

## Core Insights

### The Procrastination Self-Assessment Framework
The book opens with a revealing 10-question quiz that helps readers honestly evaluate their procrastination habits. Questions cover deadline management, emotional responses to tasks, planning tendencies, stress levels, and daily time-wasting patterns. The scoring system categorizes respondents into three levels: Serial Procrastinators (mostly A answers), Mild Procrastinators (mostly B answers), and Non-Procrastinators (mostly C answers). This initial assessment provides crucial self-awareness that forms the foundation for meaningful change.

> "The fact is that we all procrastinate at some time or another. You might even be procrastinating right now by taking an online quiz instead of focusing on something else you really need to be doing."

**Quick Take**: Self-awareness is the first step toward overcoming procrastination. Without understanding your patterns, any attempts at improvement will be ineffective.

### Type 1: The Rush Seeker
Rush seekers are thrill-chasers who believe they "work best under pressure" but are actually seeking the adrenaline rush that comes with racing against deadlines. They waste time because they're addicted to the sensation-seeking aspect of last-minute work completion. The book explains that these procrastinators are fooling themselves about their effectiveness under pressure and need to find alternative sources of excitement.

> "The race against the clock comes with the thrill and adrenaline rush, which is what they seek. The reward comes when the task has reached the due date for completion."

**Quick Take**: If you need adrenaline to complete tasks, rewire your reward system to celebrate early completion rather than last-minute heroics.

### Type 2: The Task Avoider
Task avoiders procrastinate to avoid judgment—both positive and negative. They fear being evaluated as either a success or failure, so they avoid making decisions that could lead to either outcome. This type often experiences decision fatigue because they don't exercise their decision-making muscles regularly. Their avoidance stems from deep-seated fears about how others perceive their abilities and outcomes.

> "Task avoiders prefer to not learn more about their strengths and weaknesses in various skills by not making a decision that impacts their own lives."

**Quick Take**: Start making small decisions consistently to build your decision-making confidence and reduce fear of judgment.

### Type 3: The Perfectionist Procrastinator
Perfectionists delay tasks because they fear evaluation of their abilities rather than their effort. They become paralyzed by the need to produce perfect work and would rather avoid judgment entirely than risk imperfect performance. The book emphasizes that perfectionism doesn't earn sympathy from others and actually prevents task completion. Perfectionists need to maintain perspective on the big picture rather than getting lost in details.

> "Perfectionism leads to procrastination. One of the reasons people don't finish tasks is their fear of being evaluated. People don't want to have the ability judged, they'd rather have the effort judged."

**Quick Take**: Done is better than perfect. Focus on completing tasks adequately rather than pursuing unattainable perfection.

### The Four Pain Points Behind Procrastination
The book identifies four primary sources of the psychological pain that drives procrastination: conflict-averse (fear of confrontation), people-averse (fear of social interactions), process-averse (fear of following systems), and detail-averse (fear of being overwhelmed by specifics). Most people fall predominantly into one category, and understanding this pattern helps target the right solutions.

> "The key to overcoming procrastination, is the understanding that we tend to put off things that are emotionally or psychologically painful."

**Quick Take**: Identify your specific pain point and develop targeted strategies to work around or through it rather than letting it paralyze you.

### Solutions for Each Pain Point
For conflict-avoiders, the book suggests reframing issues to focus on common goals rather than disagreements. People-avoiders can involve colleagues who enjoy social interactions or redefine tasks to minimize people contact. Process-avoiders should look for opportunities to improve and streamline processes. Detail-avoiders can focus on the big picture that details are telling and prove their capability through handling detailed work effectively.

**Quick Take**: Work with your natural tendencies rather than fighting them—find creative ways to accomplish tasks while minimizing your specific pain points.

- "Keep the big picture in your mind at all times, it's easy to get caught up in the finer details but if you know the main or large object and have that clear, then you are more likely to prioritize what you should do."

## Action Items
- Take the 10-question procrastination assessment honestly and identify your primary procrastination type
- If you're a Rush Seeker, create immediate rewards for early task completion to rewire your thrill-seeking behavior
- If you're a Task Avoider, start a daily journal to challenge irrational thoughts and practice making small decisions consistently
- If you're a Perfectionist, focus on "good enough" completion and prioritize the big picture over perfect details
- Identify your primary pain point (conflict, people, process, or detail-averse) and develop targeted solutions
- Find alternative approaches to tasks that minimize your specific psychological pain points

## Questions to Consider
- What specific emotions do you feel when you're about to procrastinate on important tasks?
- Which of the three procrastinator types resonates most strongly with your behavior patterns?
- What past experiences have contributed to your fear of evaluation or judgment?
- How can you restructure your reward system to value early completion over last-minute heroics?
- Which pain point drives most of your procrastination, and how can you work around it?
- What small decision-making practice can you implement today to build your confidence?

## Conclusion
This concise toolkit delivers substantial value for anyone struggling with procrastination. Its strength lies in the diagnostic approach that helps readers understand their specific procrastination patterns rather than offering one-size-fits-all solutions. The categorization into three types and four pain points provides clarity and targeted strategies that are more likely to be effective than generic productivity advice.

The book is worth your time if you're tired of vague productivity tips and want to understand the psychological drivers behind your procrastination. The biggest reason to read it is the self-assessment framework that provides immediate insights into your patterns, followed by practical solutions tailored to your specific type. While it's not a comprehensive productivity system, it offers the essential first step: self-awareness combined with targeted strategies for breaking free from procrastination cycles.
