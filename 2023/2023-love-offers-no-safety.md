# Love Offers No Safety
**Editors**: Olumide Femi Makanjuola, Jude Dibia | **Year**: 2023
**Category**: Romance & Relationships

## Summary
"Love Offers No Safety" is a groundbreaking collection of personal narratives from queer men living in Nigeria, offering an unfiltered look at their daily struggles, triumphs, and the complex realities of navigating love and identity in a hostile society. The book emerges from a project that collected intimate stories through interviews, capturing the diverse experiences of men across different ages, backgrounds, and regions in Nigeria. Through these testimonies, readers witness the profound impact of discriminatory laws, religious condemnation, family rejection, and societal pressures on the mental health and wellbeing of Nigeria's queer community.

This powerful anthology serves as both a documentation of resistance and a testament to human resilience. The stories reveal how queer men in Nigeria carve out spaces of joy, community, and love despite facing constant threats to their safety and existence. The book is essential reading for anyone seeking to understand the intersection of LGBTQ rights, African cultural contexts, religious influence, and the universal human need for acceptance and love.

## Core Insights

### The Reality of Legal and Social Persecution
Nine years after Nigeria's Same Sex Marriage Prohibition Act (SSMPA) was signed into law, queer Nigerians continue to face systematic discrimination that permeates every aspect of their lives. The 2014 law strengthened and increased infringements on the rights of a community already under siege, denying them basic human rights that heterosexual society takes for granted, especially the right to simply exist and be recognized. The Nigerian government has actively participated in erasure, with officials declaring to the UN Human Rights Council that "sexual minorities are not visible in Nigeria" – a denial that reinforces hatred and discrimination.

This legal persecution combines with cultural rejection through media portrayals. Early Nollywood movies like "Emotional Crack" and "Men in Love" depicted queerness as inherently evil, predatory, and anti-social – narratives that continue to shape public perception and justify violence against LGBTQ individuals. The result is a society where queer people must constantly navigate threats to their physical safety, employment, housing, and family relationships.

### Faith and Sexuality: The Internal Battle
Religion presents one of the most profound challenges for queer men in Nigeria, where Christianity and Islam influence virtually all aspects of social and political life. Many contributors struggled intensely with reconciling their faith with their sexuality, having grown up with religious doctrines that condemn same-sex attraction as inherently evil and un-African (ironically, from religions that are themselves foreign imports).

The book captures both sides of this spiritual conflict. Some contributors, like E. Chukwori in "A Divine Life in Darkness, A Liberated Life in Light," speak about contemplating suicide while suppressing their sexuality to serve God. Others, like Tahir in "My Sexuality Does Not Affect My Faith," find no conflict between their identity and religious beliefs, noting that the Quran forbids stigmatatization on any grounds. This religious burden creates immense psychological pressure, often leading to self-hatred and internalized homophobia that can be more damaging than external rejection.

### Family as Both Sanctuary and Battlefield
The quest for familial acceptance emerges as a central theme throughout the narratives. While some contributors have found support from their families, most remain doubtful they will ever be fully accepted. This creates a constant state of emotional vulnerability, where family members can withdraw love and support at any moment. The economic dimension adds another layer of complexity – younger queer men who depend on families for financial support face the threat of homelessness and educational disruption, while older queer men often find themselves in emotionally blackmail situations where they must provide financial support in exchange for conditional acceptance.

Statistics reveal the grim reality: only 30% of Nigerians polled in 2019 said they would accept a family member who is lesbian, gay, or bisexual – a significant increase from 11% in 2015, but still indicating that rejection remains the norm. Many contributors express the exhaustion of having to work "extra hard for acceptance" despite their economic and social contributions to their families and society.

### Digital Resistance and Community Building
Despite these challenges, the book documents how queer Nigerians have leveraged social media and digital platforms to create alternative narratives and spaces of resistance. From the early 2000s to present day, visibility has shifted from underground networks to online platforms where queer men can control their own image and storytelling. Platforms like Kito Diaries, Nostrings, and Rustin Times provide spaces for sharing experiences, warnings about blackmailers and predators, and building community connections.

This digital visibility has accelerated social acceptance and enabled a new generation of activists who advocate for LGBTQ rights through online spaces. The 2020 #EndSARS protests marked a watershed moment, where queer Nigerians boldly participated in mainstream protests, waving rainbow flags and asserting their place in Nigerian society. Despite attempts at erasure by fellow protesters who insisted "it was not the right time" to raise LGBTQ issues, queer presence at these protests represented a significant milestone in Nigeria's human rights movement.

### Toxic Masculinity and Relationship Dynamics
The collection reveals how patriarchal expectations and toxic concepts of masculinity affect both heterosexual and queer relationships. Many contributors internalize these harmful gender roles, projecting them onto their queer partners. Some men, like Abubakar, express dominant expectations, stating: "I am the dominant one in a relationship and my partners can attest to that. If I meet someone and he does not show that he is willing to be submissive to me, I do not bother pursuing him."

The pressure to conform to traditional masculine expectations extends to parenthood. Many queer men express the desire to have children, viewing it as essential to performing their "manly duties." However, the path to parenthood often involves marrying women without disclosing their sexuality – a decision that creates emotional distress for everyone involved and perpetuates cycles of deception and heartbreak. This pressure to conform demonstrates how deeply internalized heteronormativity affects even those who reject heterosexual relationships.

## Best Quotes
> "This erasure of queer existence through denial, laws and popular culture reinforces hatred, false representation, and discrimination. The right to exist has become for many queer persons in Nigeria the existential crisis of a lifetime."

> "Being queer in Nigeria has damaged many lives; people have died in silence because of disease and stigmatisation. I just want to be free to go about my business, to speak freely when I want to. The most important thing to me is freedom. My freedom."

> "I do not think there is anything that can be done in Nigeria to make a gay person feel free or accepted in the future. If same-sex relationships cannot lead to marriage in Nigeria, normal association itself cannot be accepted. My hope for the future is to be able to cater fully for my family, myself and to be empowered enough to assist other people."

> "I have a good job and I contribute to society. Why can't people mind their business? Why can't I be accepted for who I am? What kind of threat does a person like me pose to society?"

> "You are a man; you must behave like a man. These words are familiar to all men irrespective of their sexual identity or orientation once they have attained a certain age. Marriage, settling down and having children are pressure points for many men from their mid-twenties onwards."

## Conclusion
"Love Offers No Safety" is an essential, challenging, and ultimately hopeful read that humanizes a community too often reduced to statistics and political debates. The collection succeeds in its mission to present queer Nigerians not as victims or monsters, but as complex human beings navigating love, faith, family, and identity under extraordinary pressure.

The biggest reason to read this book is its radical authenticity – these are stories told in the contributors' own words, without external editing or sanitization. The raw honesty creates an emotional impact that academic analyses or news reports cannot achieve. Readers come away understanding that the fight for LGBTQ rights in Nigeria is not about imposing Western values, but about basic human dignity and the right to exist without fear.

While the stories reveal heartbreaking realities of discrimination and rejection, they also showcase incredible resilience, community building, and the enduring human capacity for finding joy and connection despite systematic oppression. This book is both a testimony to suffering and a celebration of survival – a dual legacy that makes it essential reading for anyone interested in human rights, African studies, or the universal quest for love and acceptance.
