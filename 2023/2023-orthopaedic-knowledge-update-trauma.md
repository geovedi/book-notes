# Orthopaedic Knowledge Update: Trauma 6
**Authors**: William M. Ricci, MD, FAAOS, Samir Mehta, MD, FAAOS | **Year**: 2023
**Category**: Health & Medicine

## Summary
This comprehensive sixth edition of the American Academy of Orthopaedic Surgeons' flagship trauma reference represents the current state-of-the-art in orthopaedic trauma care. The book integrates evidence-based medicine principles with emerging technologies and surgical innovations across the full spectrum of musculoskeletal trauma, from basic biomechanics to complex polytrauma management. It emphasizes a multidisciplinary approach, highlighting how physiologic status, timing of interventions, and individualized treatment decisions dramatically impact patient outcomes.

Orthopaedic surgeons, trauma fellows, and emergency medicine physicians will gain immediate access to current clinical guidelines, evidence-based protocols, and surgical techniques that can be applied directly to their practice. The text provides clear decision-making frameworks for complex scenarios like compartment syndrome, damage control orthopaedics, and geriatric fracture management, supported by the latest research and expert consensus.

## Core Insights

### Evidence-Based Practice Framework
The book establishes the PICO framework as the foundation for clinical decision-making in orthopaedic trauma: Population characteristics (using AO/OTA classification), Intervention options (new vs. standard treatments), Comparison methods (randomized vs. observational studies), and Outcome measures (radiographic healing to patient-reported outcomes). This structured approach ensures that treatment decisions are based on the highest available evidence while accounting for individual patient factors and injury patterns.

> "The treatment of patients with fractures and trauma is an always-evolving field with innovations, tricks, and techniques improving constantly. The surgeon must stay current with the latest management options to understand the risks and benefits associated with these procedures."

**Quick Take**: Always evaluate your treatment decisions using the PICO framework - it ensures you're applying evidence-based medicine to every trauma patient you encounter.

### Biomechanics-Informed Fracture Fixation
Modern orthopaedic trauma care requires understanding the balance between relative and absolute stability based on fracture biology and healing requirements. The book emphasizes that optimal stiffness—not maximum rigidity—is the goal of contemporary fixation techniques. This principle has led to innovations like far cortical locking (reducing construct stiffness by 80-88%) and active locking plates that enable controlled axial motion while maintaining stability.

> "Melding basic mechanical principles with the biology of fracture healing is critical to the understanding of how mechanical conditions support bony consolidation."

**Quick Take**: Think about fracture biology first, then choose the optimal stiffness construct—don't automatically assume that stiffer is always better.

### Damage Control Orthopaedics (DCO) Protocol
For critically injured patients, the book establishes clear physiologic thresholds that determine when temporary external fixation should replace definitive fixation. Recent evidence challenges traditional lactate thresholds, showing that even patients with preoperative lactate levels of 2.8 mmol/L can safely undergo early femoral fixation. The key is identifying the proinflammatory "second hit" period (usually days 2-4) when definitive procedures should be avoided.

**Clinical Protocol**:
- **Assess**: Use lactate, base deficit, pH, and hemodynamic status
- **Decide**: Temporary fixation for unstable patients, definitive fixation for stable patients
- **Timing**: Convert external fixation to intramedullary nailing within 2 weeks to avoid infection risk

### Acute Compartment Syndrome Management Revolution
The text challenges the traditional ΔP <30 mm Hg threshold for fasciotomy, revealing that this single measurement has a 35% false-positive rate. Clinical examination remains the gold standard when possible, with pressure measurements serving as adjuncts in unreliable or impossible examination scenarios. The book introduces selective fasciotomy protocols (anterior and lateral compartments only) with q2-hour postoperative monitoring for selected patients.

**Critical Risk Factors**: Tibial diaphyseal fractures (10.4% ACS incidence), patients aged 12-29 years, Schatzker VI tibial plateau fractures (27% ACS rate)

> "Clinical examination remains the gold standard for diagnosis of acute compartment syndrome. Pressure measurements should be used as an adjunct when clinical examination is unreliable or impossible."

**Quick Take**: Don't rely solely on pressure measurements—your clinical examination remains paramount, and remember that ΔP <30 mm Hg has significant false-positive limitations.

### Geriatric Fracture Specialization
The book dedicates an entire section to the unique challenges of geriatric trauma care, emphasizing that elderly fracture patients have fundamentally different biology, healing potential, and outcome expectations. This includes specialized protocols for periprosthetic fractures, atypical femur fractures, and fragility fractures of the proximal humerus, distal radius, and pelvis.

**Key Principle**: Geriatric fractures require different decision trees—biologic age often matters more than chronological age, and functional outcomes take precedence over radiographic perfection.

### Minimally Invasive Technology Integration
CT-guided stereotactic navigation, 3D printing for preoperative planning, and percutaneous plating techniques are now standard rather than exceptional. The book demonstrates how these technologies improve implant placement accuracy, reduce surgical approach morbidity, and enhance clinical outcomes while emphasizing that technology serves clinical judgment rather than replacing it.

**Emerging Technologies**: Near-infrared spectroscopy (limited current utility), ultrasound elastography for compartment pressure measurement, serum biomarkers for early compartment syndrome detection

## Clinical Action Items

- **Implement PICO framework**: Use this evidence-based structure for every major treatment decision in your trauma practice
- **Re-evaluate compartment syndrome protocols**: Consider selective fasciotomy with close monitoring rather than routine four-compartment releases
- **Challenge traditional lactate thresholds**: Recent data supports earlier definitive fixation even in patients with moderate lactate elevations
- **Adopt biomechanically-informed fixation**: Consider optimal stiffness rather than maximum rigidity in your construct choices
- **Develop geriatric-specific protocols**: Create separate pathways for elderly fracture patients emphasizing functional outcomes
- **Master timing strategies**: Use early definitive fixation (<24 hours) when physiologically possible to improve outcomes
- **Integrate emerging technologies strategically**: Use navigation and 3D planning for complex cases where they offer clear advantages
