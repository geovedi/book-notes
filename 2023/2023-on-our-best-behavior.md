# On Our Best Behavior
**Author**: Elise Loehnen | **Year**: 2023
**Category**: Philosophy & Critical Thinking

## Summary
Elise Loehnen explores how the Seven Deadly Sins have been weaponized against women for centuries, creating a system where women are conditioned to deny their fundamental needs and desires to maintain societal approval. By examining each "sin" through a feminist lens, Loehnen reveals how these religious concepts have evolved into cultural expectations that force women to suppress their rest, ambition, pride, hunger, security, pleasure, anger, and emotions—all in the name of being "good."

This book is essential for women who feel exhausted by the constant pressure to be perfect, who struggle with guilt around self-care, or who sense they're living smaller lives than they were meant for. Readers will discover a framework for understanding why they feel compelled to suppress their natural instincts and how to reclaim their full humanity.

## Core Insights

### The Patriarchal Programming of Women's Behavior
The book reveals how ancient religious concepts have become embedded in our cultural DNA, conditioning women to equate self-denial with virtue. Loehnen explains how women learn to monitor their own behavior through internalized surveillance, constantly asking "Is this too much?" before pursuing their desires.

> "I think you are trying to live up to some sort of saintly ideal, yes. But I think it's deeper, that if you feel like you're good enough, you'll be safe from judgment, loved."

**Quick Take**: Recognize that your impulse to self-limit isn't personal failure—it's inherited programming that can be unlearned.

### Sloth: The Criminalization of Rest
Women are conditioned to believe they must be constantly productive and tireless. The book examines how the "sin" of sloth has been twisted to make women feel guilty for resting, leading to burnout and exhaustion. Good women are expected to work constantly without acknowledgment of their fatigue or need for recovery.

**Quick Take**: Rest is not lazy—it's a fundamental human need that you're entitled to without justification.

### Envy: The Suppression of Ambition
When women feel envy toward others' accomplishments, they're taught to suppress it rather than examine what it reveals about their own desires. This chapter explores how women are conditioned to minimize their wanting and celebrate others' success while ignoring their own hunger for achievement.

**Quick Take**: Your envy is valuable information—it's pointing directly toward what you truly want but believe you can't have.

### Pride: The Rejection of Self-Worth
Women learn to hide their talents and appear modest to avoid intimidating others. The book reveals how the "sin" of pride has been used to keep women small, forcing them to work hard to appear minimizing and focused on finding others to champion their ideas rather than claiming their own brilliance.

**Quick Take**: Your accomplishments don't make you too much—they make you enough.

### Gluttony: The Fear of Taking Up Space
Women are conditioned to make themselves as small as possible, both physically and metaphorically. Loehnen explores how the fear of being "too much" leads to self-policing around food, body size, and the right to occupy space in the world.

**Quick Take**: You have the right to exist in your body without apology or constant self-monitoring.

### Greed: The Denial of Financial Security
Women are taught not to negotiate, ask for more, or prioritize their own financial security. The book reveals how good women are expected to appear grateful for what they're given and avoid talking about money, often spending faster than they save.

**Quick Take**: Your financial security is not selfish—it's necessary for your autonomy and well-being.

### Lust: The Disconnection from Pleasure
Women learn to see themselves as vessels for male desire rather than operators of their own bodies and pleasure. This chapter examines how women are conditioned to want to be seen as sensual but not overtly interested in their own sexual agency.

**Quick Take**: Your pleasure is yours to claim and define, separate from anyone else's expectations or desires.

### Anger: The Silencing of Boundaries
Women are taught that anger is unacceptable, forcing them to swallow violations and abandon their needs. The book explores how suppressing anger prevents women from setting boundaries and advocating for themselves effectively.

**Quick Take**: Your anger is a protective instinct—it tells you when your boundaries have been crossed.

### Sadness: The Rejection of Full Emotional Experience
Women learn to suppress sadness and other "negative" emotions to maintain their role as emotional caretakers for others. This creates a system where women must process their pain privately while supporting everyone else's emotional needs.

**Quick Take**: Your full emotional range is your strength, not your weakness.

- "Whatever tells you that you're not [good enough]."

- "When we overlimit ourselves, we become complicit in denying ourselves a full existence."
- "We fear crossing a line we can't see. We don't want to be perceived as wanting too much, or being too much."

## Action Items
- **Audit your self-talk**: Notice when you're telling yourself you're "too much" or asking for "too much"
- **Reclaim rest**: Schedule unapologetic rest time without needing to justify it
- **Follow your envy**: Use envy as a compass pointing toward your true desires
- **Practice self-advocacy**: Start small by asking for what you need in low-stakes situations
- **Embrace your emotions**: Allow yourself to feel the full range of human emotions without judgment
- **Prioritize your security**: Make financial decisions that serve your long-term well-being
- **Own your accomplishments**: Practice accepting compliments without deflecting
- **Set boundaries**: Use your anger as information about where you need to draw lines

## Questions to Consider
- What would you do if you weren't worried about being "too much"?
- Where do you suppress your natural instincts to appear more acceptable?
- What desires have you labeled as selfish or unreasonable?
- How would your life change if you gave yourself permission to want?
- What parts of yourself have you hidden to maintain relationships or social approval?
- What would it look like to live from your "first nature" rather than your "second nature"?

## Conclusion
This book is a revolutionary examination of how women have been conditioned to live half-lives through the internalization of ancient moral codes. Loehnen provides both the diagnosis and the prescription for women who feel exhausted by the constant pressure to be perfect.

The biggest reason to read this book is that it will give you language for the invisible forces that have been shaping your behavior, freeing you from the guilt and self-blame that keep you small. If you've ever felt like you're suffocating under the weight of unreasonable expectations, this book offers both validation and a path forward to reclaim your full humanity. It's not just empowering—it's essential reading for any woman ready to stop apologizing for taking up space.
