# I'm Just Saying: a Guide to Maintaining Civil Discourse in an Increasingly Divided World
**Author**: Milan Kordestani | **Year**: 2023
**Category**: Self-Help & Personal Development

## Summary
In a world where meaningful dialogue has devolved into shouting matches and social media echo chambers, Milan Kordestani offers a practical framework for rebuilding civil discourse. Drawing from his experiences building The Doe (an anonymous publishing platform that reached nearly 1 million readers) and his personal journey from isolation to connection, Kordestani demonstrates how every micro-interaction shapes our collective ability to communicate across differences.

This book is essential for anyone feeling exhausted by toxic political discourse, struggling to connect across ideological divides, or wanting to transform difficult conversations into opportunities for growth rather than conflict. Readers will gain concrete tools for listening actively, communicating with intention, and finding common ground even when perspectives seem irreconcilable.

## The Civil Discourse Framework

### Part I: The Four Foundations

### üîç Self-Reflection
Before engaging in difficult conversations, examine your own biases, triggers, and communication patterns. Kordestani learned this through his experience as a non-native English speaker who had to be deliberate with every word to avoid misunderstandings.

> "Those turtles taught me so much about words."

**Quick Take**: Start every conversation by checking your own emotional state and assumptions. Ask yourself: "Am I ready to listen as much as I'm ready to speak?"

### üéØ Clear Intention
Define your purpose before speaking. Are you seeking to understand, persuade, or simply vent? The author discovered that focusing on having a "profound and positive impact" transformed his interactions from transactional to transformational.

> "Every micro-interaction I had formed the macro of my entire lifetime."

**Quick Take**: Before any conversation, decide your goal. If it's to "win" or "prove someone wrong," you're already headed for poor discourse.

### üó£Ô∏è Mindful Tone
Your delivery matters as much as your message. Kordestani learned that positivity creates positivity, while negativity breeds defensiveness and isolation. This became evident when he changed his communication style and noticed others responding more positively.

> "How you treat the person who can do nothing for you is how people will perceive you."

**Quick Take**: Match your tone to your intention. If you want understanding, speak to be understood, not to be "right."

### ü§ù Building Trust
Trust is the foundation of civil discourse. The Doe succeeded because of its rigorous verification process that ensured anonymous writers were truthful, proving that without trust, communication breaks down completely.

**Quick Take**: Be consistent in your words and actions. Small promises kept build the trust needed for difficult conversations.

### Part II: Practical Skills

### üëÇ Active Listening
Listening isn't just waiting to speak‚Äîit's seeking to understand. The author emphasizes that language barriers forced him to listen more carefully, which improved his ability to connect across differences.

**Quick Win**: In your next conversation, try to summarize the other person's point before sharing your own. Use phrases like "So what I hear you saying is..."

### üß† Being Attentive
Presence matters more than perfect words. Kordestani learned that being fully present in conversations, rather than planning your response while others speak, creates the space for genuine understanding.

**Quick Win**: Put away your phone during conversations. Make eye contact. Notice body language. Show you're engaged, not just hearing words.

### üéØ Maintaining Focus
Stay on topic rather than drifting into related grievances or past conflicts. The author found that civil discourse breaks down when conversations become platforms for unrelated frustrations.

**Quick Win**: If a conversation starts to drift, gently guide it back: "That's an important point, but can we return to [original topic] first?"

### Part III: Overcoming Challenges

### üåâ Seeking Common Ground
Even opposing views often share underlying values. The Doe published controversial content successfully by helping readers find shared humanity beneath political differences.

> "It is only by hearing opposing perspectives that we can truly strengthen our own opinions."

**Quick Win**: When disagreeing, try to find something to agree with first: "I can see why you'd feel that way, especially when you consider [their valid point]."

### ‚öîÔ∏è Expecting Conflict in Perspectives
Difference of opinion isn't failure‚Äîit's opportunity. Kordestani learned that discomfort is necessary for growth, just as resistance is necessary for building muscle.

**Quick Win**: Reframe disagreements as "clarification opportunities" rather than conflicts. Ask "Help me understand your perspective better" instead of "You're wrong."

### üö´ Avoiding Poor Discourse
Recognize and escape destructive patterns. The author saw that comment sections needed moderation because anger generates engagement but destroys understanding.

**Quick Win**: If you find yourself becoming defensive or wanting to "give someone a piece of your mind," pause and ask: "Will this help us understand each other better?"

## Action Items

- **Daily Practice**: Start one conversation each day with genuine curiosity about someone else's perspective
- **Language Audit**: Notice how often you use "but" versus "and" when responding to others
- **Trust Building**: Keep small promises consistently to build foundation for difficult conversations
- **Presence Challenge**: Have one phone-free, fully-present conversation daily
- **Common Ground Hunt**: In every disagreement, identify one shared value or concern
