# Unscripted
**Authors**: James B. Stewart, Rachel Abrams | **Year**: 2023
**Category**: History & Biography

## Summary
"Unscripted" chronicles the explosive collision of corporate power, sexual misconduct, and family legacy within one of America's most influential media empires. At its heart are three central figures: aging media mogul Sumner Redstone, who built Viacom and CBS into entertainment powerhouses; his daughter Shari Redstone, struggling to assert control as her father's health deteriorates; and CBS CEO Leslie Moonves, whose predatory sexual past becomes the catalyst for a corporate civil war.

The narrative unfolds as the #MeToo movement reaches the upper echelons of corporate America, transforming workplace power dynamics and exposing decades of misconduct. Moonves becomes the first chief executive of a major publicly traded company forced to resign for predatory sexual conduct, setting off a cascade of legal battles, boardroom conspiracies, and family drama that threatens to unravel everything Sumner Redstone built.

This book offers an unprecedented behind-the-scenes look at how generational change, technological disruption, and evolving social norms are fundamentally reshaping American business leadership. Readers interested in corporate governance, media industry dynamics, power structures, and the cultural impact of #MeToo will find this to be a revealing examination of how the old guard of American business is being forced to adapt or die.

## Core Insights

### The Corporate Civil War
The battle for control of CBS and Viacom represents a larger struggle between traditional autocratic leadership and emerging democratic governance models. Moonves declared war on the Redstone family just months before his own misconduct would be exposed, seeking to strip them of their 80% voting control. The resulting corporate civil war saw "bodies all over the road" as alliances shifted, betrayals mounted, and the fundamental question of who really controls major American corporations came into focus.

> "You are seeing a great company in a civil war and there are bodies all over the road." — Leslie Moonves

**Quick Take**: Corporate governance battles are rarely just about business strategy—they're personal, ideological, and often prophetic about deeper cultural changes.

### The #MeToo Corporate Tipping Point
CBS became the testing ground for how major public companies handle sexual misconduct allegations at the highest levels. Unlike Harvey Weinstein's privately held company, Moonves's forced resignation from publicly traded CBS created unprecedented legal and governance challenges. The book reveals how boards of directors, often seen as rubber stamps for powerful CEOs, were suddenly forced to confront allegations they had long ignored or enabled.

> "I can say every report about CBS' toxic work environment is true. This case enrages me so much, and it breaks my heart to look behind the curtain and see the ugliness and moral bankruptcy of institutions and people I admired since childhood."

**Quick Take**: The #MeToo movement didn't just expose individual predators—it fundamentally altered the balance of power between CEOs, boards, and employees.

### Family Dynasty vs. Modern Corporate Governance
Shari Redstone's struggle to control her father's media empire highlights the clash between family dynasties and modern corporate governance. As Sumner's health declined and his behavior became increasingly erratic, Shari faced resistance not just from corporate executives but from her own father's inability to cede control. Her journey from reluctant participant to decisive leader reveals how generational wealth transfers are complicated by personal dynamics, gender bias, and institutional inertia.

**Quick Take**: Even billion-dollar family empires ultimately face the universal challenges of aging parents, succession planning, and letting go of power.

## Conclusion
"Unscripted" is essential reading for anyone who wants to understand how power really works in American business. The book goes beyond tabloid headlines to reveal the intricate connections between personal behavior, corporate governance, and cultural change. While the specific drama of CBS and Viacom might seem like another Hollywood story, it actually illustrates broader questions about accountability, succession, and the evolution of workplace power dynamics that affect every major American company.

The biggest reason to read this book is its unprecedented access to the internal workings of corporate boards and family dynasties during a moment of profound cultural transformation. Stewart and Abrams provide a detailed, documented account of how the #MeToo movement forced one of America's most powerful industries to confront decades of misconduct, and how that confrontation reshaped power structures that had seemed immutable for generations. This isn't just a story about bad behavior—it's a blueprint for how power actually changes hands in modern America.
