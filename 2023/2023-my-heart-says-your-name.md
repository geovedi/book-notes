# My Heart Says Your Name
**Original Title**: Meu coração diz teu nome
**Authors**: Cris Lisbôa | **Year**: 2023

## Summary
A powerful Brazilian novel that tells the story of identical twin sisters, one born with a prominent vascular birthmark covering part of her face. Through lyrical, poetic prose, the narrator explores themes of identity, beauty, shame, love, and self-acceptance. Born during a traumatic home birth where she and her twin Octávia emerged without medical assistance, the protagonist carries not just a physical mark but the weight of others' perceptions throughout her life. The narrative follows her journey from childhood bullying through first love and heartbreak, ultimately finding her way to self-love and acceptance.

This book will resonate with anyone who has ever felt different, struggled with self-image, or sought to find beauty in imperfection. It's particularly powerful for those interested in Brazilian literature, coming-of-age stories, and explorations of how physical appearance shapes identity.

## Core Insights

### The Burden and Beauty of Being Seen
From birth, the protagonist is impossible to ignore - her vascular birthmark makes people stare, comment, and judge. She describes how "people always linger looking at me" and how this constant visibility became both curse and teacher. The birthmark, medically called a "vascular phenomenon," changes color with temperature - pink when kissed, purple when cold, dark red when warm - making her literally wear her emotions on her face.

> "I'm not invisible. Sometimes, that's exactly the problem."
> _Original: "Não sou invisível. De vez em quando, esse é exatamente o problema."_
**Quick Take**: Our differences, while often sources of pain, can become sources of unique identity and strength.

### The Language of Shame
The book masterfully explores how shame becomes a household language, shaping family dynamics. Her mother's inability to accept her daughter's "imperfection" leads to her abandonment, while her father and sister create a world of love within their bookstore home. The protagonist learns that shame isn't hers to carry - it belongs to those who cannot see beyond surface differences.

> "In my house's dictionary, the word shame escaped from the last pages and walked without fear until it planted its flag next to words beginning with I. More precisely, beside imperfection."
> _Original: "No dicionário da minha casa, a palavra vergonha escapou das últimas páginas e caminhou sem medo até fincar bandeira na definição das palavras iniciadas com I. Mais precisamente, ao lado da imperfeição."_
**Quick Take**: Shame is often a reflection of others' limitations, not our own defects.

### Sisterhood as Sanctuary
The relationship between the twins forms the emotional core of the novel. Octávia becomes both protector and mirror - literally, since they share identical features except for the birthmark. She defends her sister fiercely, pulling hair and confronting bullies, while also struggling with her own role as the "perfect" twin. Their bond shows how true love sees beyond physical differences to the soul beneath.

> "Octávia and I didn't need mirrors to know how we were. We just had to look at each other."
> _Original: "Octávia e eu não precisávamos dos espelhos pra saber como éramos, bastava olhar uma pra outra."_
**Quick Take**: True sibling love creates a sanctuary where appearance doesn't matter, only recognition and acceptance.

### First Love and First Heartbreak
The arrival of a boy who sees her beauty - including the birthmark - opens the protagonist to the possibility of romantic love. He touches her face gently, calls her beautiful, and makes her feel desired. But his betrayal reveals that even those who seem to accept us may harbor hidden prejudices, teaching her that external validation cannot replace self-acceptance.

> "My heart says your name."
> _Original: "Meu coração diz teu nome."_
**Quick Take**: Real love requires seeing the whole person, not just accepting or overlooking perceived flaws.

### Reclaiming the Mirror
After devastating heartbreak, the protagonist must learn to see herself again, not through others' eyes but through her own. She realizes that beauty exists in her uniqueness - in her long hair, her freckles, her body's various colors and textures. The mirror becomes friend rather than enemy when she learns that only she gets to define her worth.

> "Putting it all together, it's beauty. Look closely."
> _Original: "Juntando tudo dá uma beleza. Olha bem."_
**Quick Take**: Self-acceptance comes not from changing ourselves, but from changing how we see ourselves.

## Action Items
- Practice seeing people for their whole selves, not just their appearance or perceived flaws
- Challenge your own definitions of beauty and perfection
- Speak up when you witness judgment based on physical differences
- Celebrate what makes you or others unique rather than seeing it as something to hide
- Learn to look at yourself with compassion rather than criticism

## Questions to Consider
How much of our self-worth comes from others' perceptions versus our own inner knowing? What if our greatest "flaws" are actually our most unique features? Can true acceptance come only from within, or do we need external validation to fully love ourselves? How would you live differently if you truly believed that your appearance doesn't determine your worth?

## Conclusion
"My Heart Says Your Name" is a devastatingly beautiful novel that transforms pain into poetry and shame into strength. Cris Lisbôa has crafted a narrative that's both deeply personal and universally resonant, speaking to anyone who has ever felt defined by how others see them. The book's power lies in its refusal to offer easy answers - instead, it presents the messy, complicated reality of learning to love oneself in a world obsessed with perfection.

This is absolutely worth your time for its exquisite prose, raw emotional honesty, and profound insights into human nature. The biggest reason to read it is to experience how literature can turn pain into beauty and help us see the extraordinary in what others might call ordinary or flawed. You'll finish with a deeper understanding of how to love yourself and others more authentically.
