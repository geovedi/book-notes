# Pre-Suasion: A Revolutionary Way to Influence and Persuade
**Author**: Robert B. Cialdini | **Year**: 2016 | **Category**: Psychology & Mental Health

## Summary
"Pre-Suasion" revolutionizes our understanding of influence by demonstrating that the key to persuasion isn't just *what* you say, but *what you make people think about* right before you say it. Cialdini reveals that master persuaders spend significant time preparing their audience's mental environment before making their actual request, creating "privileged moments" when receptivity peaks. Through extensive research and compelling real-world examples, he shows how to arrange for people to be receptive to your message before they encounter it—making them more likely to say yes to everything from sales pitches to personal requests.

This book is perfect for leaders, sales professionals, marketers, or anyone who wants to become more persuasive ethically. Readers will learn specific techniques to optimize timing, focus attention, and create the right mental environment for influence.

## Core Insights

### The Power of Pre-Suasion: Setting the Stage Before the Ask
Pre-suasion is the process of arranging for recipients to be receptive to a message before they encounter it. The best persuaders don't focus solely on crafting perfect arguments; they spend considerable time preparing their audience's mindset first. What we present first changes how people experience what comes next. For example, a consultant who mentioned he wouldn't charge "a million dollars" for a project eliminated price resistance entirely—the high initial number made his actual fee seem reasonable.

> "The best persuaders become the best through pre-suasion—the process of arranging for recipients to be receptive to a message before they encounter it."

**Quick Take**: Your success depends less on the merits of your proposal and more on the psychological frame in which you place it.

### Privileged Moments: When Yes Becomes Easy
A privileged moment is a brief window of opportunity when someone is particularly receptive to your message. These moments occur when you've successfully shifted someone's attention to a concept that's associatively linked to what you'll propose next. The factor most likely to determine a person's choice often isn't the most accurate counsel—it's what has been elevated in attention at the moment of decision.

> "The factor most likely to determine a person's choice in a situation is often not the one that offers the most accurate or useful counsel; instead, it is the one that has been elevated in attention at the moment of decision."

**Quick Take**: Timing isn't everything—it's the only thing. Learn to recognize and create moments when your audience's mind is perfectly prepared for your message.

### Attention Creates Importance: What We Focus On Matters Most
Humans have a fundamental tendency to assign excessive importance to whatever captures our attention. When we focus on something, we automatically assume it's important and meaningful. This means skilled persuaders can direct our decisions simply by controlling what we notice first. Retailers use this by displaying premium products prominently—even if we don't buy them, the high prices make other items seem more affordable.

> "The human tendency to assign undue levels of importance to an idea as soon as one's attention is turned to it."

**Quick Take**: Control attention and you control perceived importance. Make your audience focus on factors that favor your position before making your request.

### What's Focal Is Causal: The Attention-Causality Connection
People naturally assume that whatever they're paying attention to is the cause of events or outcomes. If we see ourselves focusing on a particular factor, we're more likely to think it caused the result. This principle explains why lottery players feel more confident about numbers they've chosen themselves, or why false confessions occur during intense police interrogations—suspects become convinced their attention implies guilt.

> "If people see themselves giving special attention to some factor, they become more likely to think of it as a cause."

**Quick Take**: Direct attention to causes that support your argument, and your audience will naturally connect those causes to your desired outcome.

### The Seven Universal Principles of Influence: New Addition of Unity
Cialdini expands his original six principles (reciprocation, liking, social proof, authority, scarcity, consistency) by adding a seventh: Unity. This principle represents the most powerful form of social influence—when people feel "at one" with others, they treat them like family. Unity can be created through shared genetic ties, geographical connections, or synchronized actions.

> "There is a certain type of unity—of identity—that best characterizes a We relationship and that, if pre-suasively raised to consciousness, leads to more acceptance, cooperation, liking, help, trust, and, consequently, assent."

**Quick Take**: Create a sense of shared identity with your audience, and they'll be far more receptive to your requests and recommendations.

### Unity Through Being Together: The Power of Shared Identity
Family bonds, geographical connections, and place-based identities create powerful unity. People are remarkably willing to help those they perceive as sharing their "place"—whether family members, neighbors, or regional compatriots. During WWII, Japanese officials protected Jewish refugees partly because one rabbi astutely claimed, "We are Asian, like you," creating regional unity that trumped political alliances.

> "From the standpoint of influence, what matters is not the actual reality of a shared place but the perceived reality of it."

**Quick Take**: Emphasize shared connections—family, hometown, region, or any common ground—to create powerful bonds that increase influence.

### Unity Through Acting Together: Synchrony Creates Solidarity
When people act in unison—singing together, marching in step, or even tapping fingers to the same rhythm—they develop remarkable feelings of unity and liking for each other. This synchrony leads to increased cooperation and self-sacrifice. Studies show that 49% of people who tapped tables synchronously with a partner chose to stay and help them with extra work, compared to only 18% of those who didn't synchronize.

> "When people act in unitary ways, they become unitized. The resultant feeling of group solidarity serves societies' interests well, producing degrees of loyalty and self-sacrifice associated usually with much smaller family units."

**Quick Take**: Create shared experiences, especially synchronized activities, to build powerful bonds and cooperation.

## Action Items
- **Pre-suasion Planning**: Before making any request, ask yourself: "What should I make my audience think about first to create receptivity?"
- **Attention Management**: Guide attention to concepts that support your goal before making your actual request.
- **Unity Building**: Identify and emphasize shared identities, connections, or experiences before asking for cooperation.
- **Synchronized Activities**: Use shared activities or experiences to create bonds and increase cooperation.
- **Strategic Self-Disclosure**: Share personal information reciprocally to build unity and trust (like the 36 questions that can create feelings of love between strangers).

## Conclusion
"Pre-Suasion" is worth your time if you want to understand the hidden dynamics that make persuasion successful. Cialdini's greatest insight is that persuasion isn't just about having the best argument—it's about creating the right mental environment *before* you present that argument. The book provides evidence-based techniques that anyone can use ethically to become more influential.

The biggest reason to read this book is that it will change how you approach every communication challenge. Instead of focusing solely on crafting perfect messages, you'll learn to prepare your audience's mind first—creating privileged moments that make "yes" the natural response. Master these principles, and you'll find that influence becomes less about what you say and more about what happens before you say it.
