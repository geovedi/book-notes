# Evicted
**Author**: Matthew Desmond | **Year**: 2016 | **Category**: History & Biography

## Summary
"Evicted" transforms our understanding of poverty in America through groundbreaking research and intimate storytelling. Desmond demonstrates that eviction is not just a consequence of poverty but a primary cause, creating a vicious cycle that traps families in destitution. The book follows eight families in Milwaukee as they struggle to keep their homes, revealing how the affordable housing crisis systematically dismantles lives, communities, and opportunities for upward mobility.

This eye-opening investigation is essential for anyone concerned about social justice, economic inequality, or effective solutions to poverty. It will fundamentally change how you view housing and its role in American society.

## Core Insights

### The Eviction Epidemic
America faces a massive eviction crisis that affects millions annually. In Milwaukee alone, 16,000 adults and children are evicted each year—roughly 16 families daily through court orders alone. When including informal evictions, 1 in 8 Milwaukee renters experienced forced displacement between 2009-2011. This isn't just a Milwaukee problem; similar numbers appear in cities across America. The majority of poor renting families now spend over half their income on housing, with at least 25% dedicating 70% or more to rent and utilities.

> "There is nothing special about Milwaukee when it comes to eviction. The numbers are similar in Kansas City, Cleveland, Chicago, and other cities. In 2013, 1 in 8 poor renting families nationwide were unable to pay all of their rent, and a similar number thought it was likely they would be evicted soon."

**Quick Take**: Eviction has become routine in poor neighborhoods, creating instability that prevents families from building better lives.

### Housing as Poverty's Foundation
Desmond reveals how housing is deeply implicated in creating poverty, not just reflecting it. When families spend 70-80% of income on rent, they can't afford food, clothing, or education for their children. The "rent eats first" principle means children go hungry, parents can't afford transportation to work, and families can't save money or build assets. This housing cost burden systematically prevents upward mobility.

Eviction also causes job loss—workers who experience eviction are 15% more likely to be laid off. The stress and consuming nature of housing insecurity wreak havoc on work performance, creating another barrier to economic stability.

> "We have failed to fully appreciate how deeply housing is implicated in the creation of poverty... But nearly all of them have a landlord."

**Quick Take**: Stable, affordable housing is the foundation upon which economic mobility is built—without it, other anti-poverty efforts struggle to succeed.

### The Devastating Fallout
Eviction's consequences extend far beyond losing shelter. Families lose possessions, jobs, and access to future housing assistance. They're forced into substandard housing in even poorer, more dangerous neighborhoods. Children experience school instability, health problems, and psychological trauma. The year following eviction, families experience 20% higher levels of material hardship, with effects lasting for years.

The psychological toll is severe—50% of recently evicted mothers report multiple symptoms of clinical depression, double the rate of similar mothers who weren't evicted. Eviction even increases suicide rates, with psychiatrists identifying it as a "significant precursor of suicide" and "an exquisitely shameful experience."

> "Eviction does not simply drop poor families into a dark valley, a trying yet relatively brief detour on life's journey. It fundamentally redirects their way, casting them onto a different, and much more difficult, path. Eviction is a cause, not just a condition, of poverty."

**Quick Take**: The cascading consequences of eviction create multiple barriers to escaping poverty, affecting every aspect of family life.

### Exploitation of the Poor
Desmond exposes how poverty persists partly because extractive markets profit from it. Landlords can make substantial money from distressed properties in poor neighborhoods. The book follows landlords like Sherrena Tarver, who views the ghetto's value differently—seeing opportunity where others see blight. This exploitation isn't limited to housing; it extends to payday loans, higher food prices, and other services that drain resources from poor communities.

The housing market relies on government support to enable this exploitation. Courts enforce evictions, police remove families, and taxpayers often subsidize landlords when families can't pay. This system creates what Martin Luther King Jr. called "economic exploitation crystallized in the slum."

> "There is a lot of money to be made off the poor. The 'hood is good."

**Quick Take**: Poverty persists partly because profitable business models have been built around extracting wealth from vulnerable communities.

## Conclusion
"Evicted" is a masterful work that combines rigorous research with powerful human stories to expose one of America's most urgent crises. Desmond's groundbreaking investigation reveals how the eviction epidemic systematically destroys lives and perpetuates poverty across generations.

This book is essential reading because it fundamentally reframes our understanding of poverty in America. By showing how housing instability creates rather than merely reflects poverty, Desmond opens new possibilities for addressing inequality. The book's combination of compelling storytelling and rigorous research makes it both accessible and authoritative.

The biggest reason to read "Evicted" is that it offers not just a diagnosis but hope—showing that housing solutions are some of our most effective anti-poverty tools. As Desmond demonstrates, when families have stable homes, everything else becomes possible: better jobs, improved health, educational success, and stronger communities. In a time of increasing inequality, this book provides both a wake-up call and a roadmap for creating a more just society.
