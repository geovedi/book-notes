# Snatched: From Drug Queen to Informer to Hostage—A Harrowing True Story
**Authors**: Bruce Porter | **Year**: 2016

## Summary
This gripping true crime narrative follows Pilar, a beautiful Colombian socialite who rose from privilege to become a major player in international drug trafficking, then transformed into a DEA confidential informant, and ultimately endured a brutal kidnapping by guerrillas. Born into Cali's elite, Pilar's journey from Olympic-caliber swimmer and Braniff Airlines flight attendant to the wife of major cocaine traffickers puts her at the center of the multi-billion dollar drug empire. Her unique access to the Cali cartel's inner circle makes her invaluable to law enforcement as "Operation Princess," but this dangerous double life leads to her capture by Colombian guerrillas who hold her hostage for four months in horrifying conditions.

This harrowing true story will captivate readers interested in crime narratives, undercover operations, and stories of survival against impossible odds. True crime enthusiasts and those fascinated by the drug war will gain unprecedented insight into how cartels operated and the human cost of the war on drugs. Readers who appreciate personal transformation stories will find Pilar's journey from privileged socialite to desperate informant compelling.

## Core Insights

### The Setting and Main Character
Pilar is a stunning Colombian woman from Cali's upper class, raised among diplomats and cardinals, who became a national swimming champion ("Miss Colombia") and worked as a glamorous airline hostess. Her beauty, charm, and social connections make her irresistible to drug traffickers. She marries Ernesto, a major cocaine smuggler connected to both the Medellín and Cali cartels, and later Steven Cinnante, who runs massive drug transportation operations. By the 1990s, she's living a life of luxury in Florida with unlimited money, properties in Majorca, and connections to the highest levels of Colombian society.

### Key Turning Points
- **1991**: Police knock on Pilar's door in Boca Raton, leading to her recruitment as a DEA confidential informant
- **Operation Ernesto**: She helps set up her ex-husband for a 400-kilo cocaine bust worth $8 million
- **Operation Princess**: The DEA inserts her into the Cali cartel's money-laundering operations
- **Money laundering success**: She repatriates over $4.8 million in drug profits in just months, earning the trust of cartel kingpins
- **Fatal trip**: Her final mission to Colombia leads to her capture by guerrillas

### The Harrowing Experience
Pilar is kidnapped by Colombian guerrillas who mistake her for someone else or believe she owes money. She's held for four months in a windowless dirt-floor room, sleeping on a straw mattress, transported in car trunks between locations, and fed only starchy root vegetables. The psychological torment includes not knowing if she'll live or die, being completely cut off from her children, and enduring isolation. Though treated "better" than typical hostages—she even helps deliver a baby and cares for the child during captivity—the experience leaves her with severe PTSD and triggers multiple sclerosis.

### Resolution and Aftermath
Pilar's rescue comes through "Nixon," another DEA informant who risks his life to find and free her. She returns to America broken physically and financially, diagnosed with multiple sclerosis triggered by the captivity stress. Her DEA compensation of $966,000 is far less than expected, and she loses most of it in a bad business deal. After a 17-year legal battle, she finally wins $1.8 million from the U.S. government for failing to protect her. She adopts her granddaughter Camila and finds modest peace caring for the child and her elderly mother, living on disability payments in Boca Raton.

## Key Takeaways
- The drug war's human cost extends far beyond dealers and users—families, children, and even informants pay devastating prices
- Government agencies often exploit confidential informants without adequate protection or fair compensation
- Multiple sclerosis can be triggered by extreme trauma, adding lifelong physical suffering to psychological wounds
- True transformation is possible—from pampered drug queen to cooperative witness, but redemption comes at tremendous cost
- The Cali cartel operated like a multinational corporation, using sophisticated business practices and political corruption
- Intelligence gathering is often more valuable than arrests in dismantling criminal organizations
- Legal battles against the U.S. government can span decades but sometimes achieve justice

## Questions to Consider
- Was Pilar a victim of circumstances or a willing participant in criminal enterprises?
- Should government agencies have greater responsibility for protecting confidential informants?
- How does the glamorization of drug culture in society contribute to recruitment and participation?
- What drives someone from privilege into criminal enterprises—is it thrill-seeking, financial need, or something deeper?
- Are the tactics used in the war on drugs proportional to the threat they address?
- How do we balance justice for criminals with compassion for those caught in the crossfire?

## Conclusion
**This is absolutely worth reading** for anyone interested in true crime, undercover operations, or the drug war's human cost. The book's biggest value lies in its intimate portrayal of one woman's journey through the highest levels of international drug trafficking and the devastating personal consequences of that world. Porter's meticulous research and unprecedented access to Pilar and the DEA task force create a narrative that's both thrilling and deeply humanizing. The story works on multiple levels—as a suspenseful crime thriller, a cautionary tale about the drug trade, and a powerful story of survival and redemption. What makes it exceptional is its unflinching look at the moral complexities and the very real price paid by those caught between criminals and law enforcement.
