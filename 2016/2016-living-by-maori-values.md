# Living by Māori Values: Understanding Tikanga Māori
**Original Title**: Tikanga Māori: Living by Māori Values
**Author**: Sir Hirini Moko Mead | **Year**: 2016

## Summary
Tikanga Māori represents the complete system of Māori customary values and practices—the ethical framework, social protocols, and spiritual principles that guide proper conduct in Māori society. This comprehensive work explains how these ancestral customs, though suppressed for generations, are experiencing a cultural renaissance and gaining recognition in New Zealand law and education. The book reveals tikanga as a living, adaptive system that maintains ancient wisdom while addressing contemporary challenges.

For anyone seeking to understand Māori culture or develop cross-cultural competence in New Zealand, this book provides essential insights into the fundamental values that shape Māori identity, relationships, and interactions with the natural world. Readers will gain a deeper appreciation for how tikanga functions as both a personal ethical guide and a collective social law that maintains community harmony and cultural continuity.

## Core Insights

### The Foundation: Understanding Tikanga Māori
Tikanga Māori encompasses "the Māori way"—the customs, protocols, and practices that constitute proper behavior according to ancestral law. It's not just etiquette, but a complete ethical system and common law that guides all aspects of life, from daily interactions to major ceremonies. The term derives from "tika" (right, correct, just) and represents behavior that maintains balance and harmony within the community and with the spiritual world.

> "Tikanga refers to the ethical and common law issues that underpin the behaviour of members of whānau, hapū and iwi as they go about their lives and especially when they engage in the cultural, social, ritual and economic ceremonies of their society."

**Quick Take**: Tikanga is both personal ethical guidance and collective social law—knowing the right way to do things in any situation.

### Core Spiritual Values: Tapu, Noa, and Mana
The spiritual foundation of tikanga rests on maintaining balance between tapu (sacred, restricted) and noa (ordinary, accessible) states. Mana—spiritual authority and prestige—flows from proper observance of these principles. Tapu isn't just restriction; it's about recognizing sacredness in people, places, objects, and knowledge, while noa represents the safe, everyday state where normal interactions can occur.

> "All tikanga are underpinned by the high value placed upon manaakitanga – nurturing relationships, looking after people, and being very careful about how others are treated."

**Quick Take**: Understanding sacred-ordinary balance helps maintain respect for spiritual authority while enabling practical daily life.

### Social Architecture: Whanaungatanga and Utu
Māori society operates on interconnected relationships (whanaungatanga) and reciprocal obligations (utu). Whanaungatanga creates the social fabric through kinship connections, while utu ensures balance through appropriate responses—whether positive (reciprocity for kindness) or restorative (compensation for harm). This creates a self-regulating community where everyone has responsibilities and rights.

> "Whanaungatanga, mana, manaakitanga, aroha, mana tupuna, wairua and utu, may be described as conceptual regulators of tikanga, or as providing fundamental principles or values of Māori law."

**Quick Take**: Strong relationships and balanced reciprocity create community resilience and individual security.

### The Cultural Heart: Marae as Living Institution
The marae serves as the physical and spiritual center of Māori cultural identity—a place where tikanga is most visibly practiced and where major ceremonies occur. More than just buildings, marae are ancestors made manifest, where protocols (kawa) maintain tribal knowledge, validate community decisions, and connect living generations with those who have passed on.

**Quick Take**: The marae is where abstract cultural values become concrete through ceremony, language, and shared practice.

### Contemporary Applications: Living Tikanga
Tikanga Māori adapts to modern contexts while maintaining core principles. From environmental management (kaitiakitanga) to business practices, from urban marae to intercultural relationships, traditional values provide guidance for contemporary challenges. The legal system increasingly recognizes tikanga, as seen in resource management and treaty settlements.

> "Tikanga Māori is no longer bound geographically, culturally or ethnically. Wherever Māori go we take our tikanga with us."

**Quick Take**: Ancient wisdom provides practical solutions for modern problems when properly understood and applied.

## Practical Actions
- **Observe protocols respectfully**: When visiting marae or participating in Māori ceremonies, follow local customs and listen carefully to guidance from elders
- **Build relationships through reciprocity**: Practice utu by acknowledging kindness and maintaining balanced exchanges in your relationships
- **Recognize sacred spaces**: Identify and respect places, knowledge, or relationships that hold tapu status in your community
- **Practice manaakitanga**: Focus on nurturing relationships and caring for others in your personal and professional interactions
- **Learn local variations**: Understand that tikanga varies by iwi (tribe) and region—learn the specific protocols of your local area
