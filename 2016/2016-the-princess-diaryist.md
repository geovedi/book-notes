# The Princess Diarist
**Author**: Carrie Fisher | **Year**: 2016

## Summary
Carrie Fisher's intimate memoir reveals what happened behind the scenes of the original Star Wars film, combining her adult reflections with recently discovered diary entries from 1976. At nineteen, Fisher was cast as Princess Leia while navigating her first major film role, her famous Hollywood heritage, and a secret affair with her married co-star Harrison Ford. The book captures the collision between her private insecurities and her public transformation into a cultural icon, offering both the raw immediacy of her teenage diaries and the wisdom of forty years of perspective.

This is essential reading for Star Wars fans seeking authentic behind-the-scenes stories, anyone interested in the reality behind celebrity culture, and those who appreciate brutally honest memoirs about coming of age in the public eye. Readers will gain insight into the human experience behind one of cinema's most beloved characters and understand how fame can both create and destroy identity.

## Core Insights

### The Double Life of Princess Leia
Fisher reveals the profound disconnect between her confident on-screen character and her insecure nineteen-year-old self. While Leia commanded starships and defied Darth Vader, Fisher herself struggled with body image, insecurity about her experience, and the weight of her famous parents' legacy. She was asked to lose ten pounds for the role and spent hours in makeup and hair each day transforming into the princess who would eventually define her identity.

> "Today, while going through some boxes containing some old writing of mine, I found the diaries I kept while filming the first Star Wars movie forty years ago. Stay tuned."

**Quick Take**: Our public personas often hide private battles with self-worth, especially for young women in the entertainment industry.

### The Carrison Affair
The book's centerpiece is Fisher's revelation of her three-month affair with Harrison Ford during filming. At thirty-three, Ford was married with two children, while Fisher was having her first serious relationship. Their weekends together contrasted sharply with silent weekdays on set, filled with marijuana-fueled encounters and Fisher's desperate attempts to impress the taciturn actor. She describes it as "a very long one-night stand" that left her feeling both exhilarated and ashamed.

> "I was about to tell him when Harrison pulled me back into the seat, moving us closer and closer together, face-to-face, until we were two faces, four eyes, one kiss."

**Quick Take**: Affairs rarely fulfill the emotional needs we seek from them; they often amplify our existing insecurities.

### The Burden of Early Fame
Being cast as Leia at nineteen meant Fisher would never have a normal life or career. She recognized immediately that the role would define her forever: "I'm Leia—I can live in a tree, but you can't take that away from me. I never dreamt there actually might be a day when I maybe hoped that you could." The memoir explores how early identification with an iconic character affects personal growth and creative freedom.

> "Bottom line, I was going to be able to pay some if not all of my overhead! Maybe not now, but soon. Sure, if it wasn't very soon I'd be paying bills from an apartment, but at least I'd be able to buy stuff I didn't need again."

**Quick Take**: Early success in a defining role can be both a blessing and a creative prison.

### Hollywood Legacy and Personal Freedom
As the daughter of Debbie Reynolds and Eddie Fisher, Fisher understood both the privileges and limitations of show business inheritance. She initially wanted to avoid acting but found herself drawn in despite her misgivings. Her memoir explores the complicated relationship between talent, opportunity, and the desire to forge one's own identity apart from family fame.

**Quick Take**: Sometimes our greatest resistance leads us exactly where we're meant to be.

### The Diary as Time Capsule
The book's structure—alternating between Fisher's current perspective and her teenage diary entries—creates a powerful dialogue between past and present selves. Her nineteen-year-old self writes with raw intensity about Harrison: "He is like a fantasy. The inevitability of his escape is most likely his most attractive feature." Her older self provides context and compassion for that younger version's pain and confusion.

> "I charm the birds out of everybody else's trees but his."

**Quick Take**: Our younger selves often contain wisdom we can only appreciate decades later.

## Best Quotes

- "You have the eyes of a doe and the balls of a samurai."
- "I can charm the birds out of everybody else's trees but his. Vultures are difficult to charm unless you're off somewhere rotting in the noonday sun."
- "Silence speaks louder than words—it screams, 'BORING!'"
- "My affair with Harrison was a very long one-night stand. I was relieved when it ended. I didn't approve of myself."

## Key Takeaways
- **Embrace your contradictions**: Fisher shows how we can be both strong and vulnerable, confident and insecure, often simultaneously.
- **Document your journey**: Her teenage diaries became invaluable artifacts for understanding her younger self.
- **Find humor in pain**: Fisher uses wit to process difficult experiences, making them more bearable and relatable.
- **Separate character from self**: She learned that being Leia didn't have to consume her entire identity, though it took decades to establish that boundary.
- **Authenticity over perfection**: Her willingness to be honest about her mistakes and insecurities makes her story compelling.

## Questions to Consider
- How do we reconcile our private insecurities with our public responsibilities?
- What role do secrets play in shaping our identities and relationships?
- How can young people in the public eye maintain authentic self-development?
- What happens when our most famous work becomes both our greatest asset and limitation?
- How do we make peace with decisions made by younger, less experienced versions of ourselves?

## Conclusion
This memoir is absolutely worth your time, whether you're a Star Wars fan or simply appreciate brutally honest, beautifully written memoirs. Fisher's unique voice—equal parts witty, vulnerable, and wise—makes even the most painful stories entertaining and insightful. Her ability to view her younger self with both compassion and clear-eyed honesty creates a rare reading experience that's both emotionally raw and intellectually satisfying.

The biggest reason to read this book is Fisher's fearless honesty about the gap between her public persona and private reality. She doesn't romanticize her youth or her affair, instead presenting them with the complexity they deserve. The diary entries are remarkable for their literary quality and emotional intensity, while her contemporary reflections provide the wisdom and distance that make the memoir truly exceptional. In an era of carefully curated celebrity images, Fisher's willingness to reveal her authentic self—flaws and all—is both revolutionary and refreshing.
