# Thinking in Chinese: The Top 10 Chinese Values & How China Became a Superpower
**Authors**: Samuel River | **Year**: 2015

## Summary
Based on nearly a decade of experience working with China's wealthiest and most powerful companies, this book reveals the 10 core Chinese business values that explain their remarkable global success. The author contrasts Chinese business practices with Western approaches, showing how cultural differences drive vastly different outcomes in entrepreneurship and wealth creation.

The book offers practical insights for entrepreneurs who want to understand and potentially adopt Chinese business strategies that prioritize profit maximization through efficiency, cooperation, and strategic thinking. It's particularly valuable for business owners who struggle with competition, want to improve their profitability, or need to understand Chinese competitors in their markets.

Readers will gain a roadmap for implementing these time-tested Chinese principles that have helped transform China from a developing nation into a global economic superpower.

## Core Insights

### 1. Save Money Aggressively
The Chinese approach to wealth focuses equally on earning and saving. They minimize expenses by living frugally, combining home and office spaces, and avoiding status symbols that Western entrepreneurs often prioritize. This mindset allows them to start smaller and grow organically rather than taking on debt.

> "Money isn't only about what you earn, but also, and equally importantly, about how much you save."

**Quick Take**: Calculate what you can save, not just what you can earn. Starting small and debt-free beats overextending for appearance.

### 2. Work Harder and Faster
Chinese entrepreneurs treat time differently—working intensely for long periods (6-7 days weekly, 12-16 hours daily) to achieve goals faster. They integrate life activities into work rather than separating them, focusing on long-term rewards over daily pleasures.

> "The Chinese may work between six to seven days a week, but enjoy at least a full month of vacations with the family once a year."

**Quick Take**: Eliminate artificial work-life boundaries. Integrate activities and focus on achieving goals faster rather than working "smarter" but slower.

### 3. Quality is Relative to Profit
Chinese business owners define quality by minimum acceptable standards that maximize profit. They excel at reducing costs while maintaining just enough quality to keep customers returning, focusing on efficiency over perfection.

> "The concept of quality for a Chinese is very different from the one that westerners usually have."

**Quick Take**: Identify the minimum quality level your market accepts and optimize for maximum profit within those constraints.

### 4. Think Like a Farmer, Not a Hunter
Unlike Western "hunter" mentality focused on competition and conquest, Chinese think like farmers—patiently planting multiple seeds (products) and nurturing them to harvest fruits (profits). They focus on what customers actually want, not what they think customers should want.

> "Like a farmer, the Chinese will patiently place their seeds, their different products, in place, and then watch how costumers behave."

**Quick Take**: Test multiple small offerings, see what customers actually buy, then scale those—don't try to force your vision on the market.

### 5. Think in Dynamics, Not Quantities
Chinese prioritize business flow and customer movement over single high-margin transactions. They'd rather have many small sales than few large ones, even if that means reducing prices or portion sizes to increase volume.

> "For them, if a business is not moving, it's dead. So, instead of focusing in unexpected movements, they focus in a constant flow of people, as if the clients were the blood of their business."

**Quick Take**: Focus on increasing customer traffic and transaction volume rather than maximizing profit per sale.

### 6. Be Patient with Others, Impatient with Yourself
Chinese entrepreneurs accept people's limitations without complaint while pushing themselves relentlessly toward goals. They'll work around supplier delays, employee shortcomings, or market constraints with patient adaptation while maintaining their own ambitious timelines.

> "The Chinese don't waste their time complaining. Instead, they'll focus on how to adapt to the situations presented."

**Quick Take**: Accept external constraints as givens and work around them efficiently while pushing your own productivity relentlessly.

### 7. Control Your Emotions
Chinese business owners separate feelings from business decisions, maintaining professional behavior regardless of personal mood. They understand that customer comfort and satisfaction matter more than their own emotional state.

> "Emotions aren't important for Chinese Business owners. For them, emotions must be arranged and controlled according to the business in hands."

**Quick Take**: Maintain consistent professional behavior regardless of your feelings—customer experience drives profits, not your emotional authenticity.

### 8. Copy Without Shame
Chinese see copying successful models as intelligent strategy, not moral failure. They analyze what works, adapt it, and improve upon it without Western concerns about originality or intellectual property.

> "While westerners are ashamed to copy others, Chinese can only survive by copying the best and they see it as something normal to improve themselves."

**Quick Take**: Study successful competitors and adapt their proven strategies rather than trying to be original for originality's sake.

### 9. Prioritize Cooperation Over Competition
Chinese businesses thrive through family and friend networks, investing together and sharing risks. They build support systems that allow bigger investments and shared resources, creating resilience that individual entrepreneurs lack.

> "The Chinese never do things alone. They invite friends and relatives to work with them, and they cooperate together in everything they do."

**Quick Take**: Build partnerships with complementary businesses rather than viewing everyone as competition—cooperation can create larger markets than competition alone.

### 10. Work for Something Greater Than Yourself
Chinese entrepreneurs are motivated by family legacy and future generations' success, not just personal wealth. They make sacrifices knowing that their efforts will benefit their children and extended family, creating a powerful long-term motivation.

> "They know that the more money they can make the more they help their family. Their head is always focused in moving forward."

**Quick Take**: Connect your business success to a larger purpose—family legacy, community impact, or future generations—for sustained motivation beyond personal gain.

## Action Items

- **Audit your expenses**: Identify at least three monthly costs you can eliminate or reduce by 50% without impacting core business functions
- **Analyze customer traffic**: Measure how many customers visit daily/weekly and focus on increasing this number rather than average sale value
- **Copy successful competitors**: Identify your top three competitors' best practices and implement your versions within 30 days
- **Build partnerships**: Reach out to complementary businesses this month to explore cooperation opportunities
- **Define your bigger purpose**: Clarify how your business serves something beyond personal profit—family, community, or legacy

## Questions to Consider

- What minimum quality level do your customers actually require versus what you currently provide?
- Are you optimizing for profit per transaction or total business flow—and which strategy better serves your market?
- Who could you partner with rather than compete against to create a larger market opportunity?
- What would change if you focused on saving 50% more rather than earning 50% more?
- Are you letting pride about originality prevent you from adapting proven successful strategies?

## Conclusion

This book is worth your time if you're an entrepreneur struggling with profitability or facing competition from Chinese businesses. The raw, unfiltered insights into Chinese business psychology provide a stark contrast to Western entrepreneurial wisdom that often fails to deliver results in competitive markets.

The biggest reason to read this book is its practical, no-nonsense approach to wealth creation that prioritizes results over ideology. While some strategies may feel culturally foreign or even uncomfortable to Western sensibilities, they undeniably work—as evidenced by China's economic transformation from poverty to global superpower in just a few decades.

At minimum, this book will challenge your assumptions about business ethics, competition, and the role of creativity in entrepreneurship. Even if you don't adopt all ten values, understanding them will help you compete more effectively in a globalized economy where Chinese business practices are increasingly dominant.
