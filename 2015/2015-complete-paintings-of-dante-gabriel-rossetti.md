# Complete Paintings of Dante Gabriel Rossetti
**Authors**: Delphi Classics | **Year**: 2015

## Summary
This comprehensive art book presents the complete painted works of Dante Gabriel Rossetti (1828-1882), the renowned Victorian painter, poet, and founding member of the Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood. The collection spans Rossetti's entire artistic career, from his early Pre-Raphaelite works through his later period of sensuous single-figure portraits, providing detailed commentary, historical context, and biographical insights along with reproductions of his paintings, watercolors, and drawings.

Art enthusiasts, students of Victorian art, and Pre-Raphaelite scholars will discover an invaluable reference that captures not only Rossetti's technical evolution but also the profound intersection between his visual and poetic sensibilities that made him one of the most distinctive artistic voices of the 19th century.

## Core Insights

### The Pre-Raphaelite Foundation
Rossetti co-founded the Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood in 1848 with William Holman Hunt and John Everett Millais, seeking to reject the academic conventions of British art and revive the detailed, colorful approach of early Italian Renaissance painting. His early works like "The Girlhood of Mary Virgin" (1849) exemplified this philosophy with their meticulous attention to natural detail and symbolic intensity. The Brotherhood's manifesto emphasized "truth to nature" and direct observation, principles that would inform Rossetti's entire career even as his style evolved.

> "The atmosphere of enthusiasm thus engendered raised all to greater heights than any could have attained by himself."
> _Original: English_

**Quick Take**: Rossetti's collaborative spirit and willingness to challenge established artistic norms created a revolutionary movement that transformed Victorian art.

### The Poetry-Painting Synthesis
What distinguishes Rossetti from his contemporaries is the seamless integration of literary and visual sensibilities. Many of his greatest works illustrate themes from Dante's "Vita Nuova" or his own poetry, creating a unique fusion where painting becomes visual poetry. His masterpiece "Beata Beatrix" (1863) embodies this synthesis, portraying Dante's beloved in a trance-like state that transcends mere portraiture to become a meditation on love, loss, and spiritual transcendence.

> "The picture illustrates the Vita Nuova, embodying symbolically the death of Beatrice as treated in that work."
> _Original: English_

**Quick Take**: Rossetti's dual identity as poet and painter created works that operate on multiple levels of meaning, appealing to both emotional and intellectual sensibilities.

### The Evolution of Style: Narrative to Sensuous
Rossetti's artistic journey shows a clear progression from early narrative compositions with multiple figures to his signature single-figure portraits of the 1860s-70s. While early works like "Paolo and Francesca" tell dramatic stories through complex compositions, later masterpieces like "Venus Verticordia" and "Monna Vanna" focus on the psychological presence of individual subjects, particularly women whose beauty becomes a vehicle for exploring themes of love, desire, and spiritual longing.

> "He gave up painting those quaint little romantic subjects so intense in literary feeling and dramatic expression, and devoted himself to large single figures upon a background of rich accessories."
> _Original: English_

**Quick Take**: Rossetti's stylistic evolution reflects a deeper exploration of human psychology and emotion, moving from external drama to internal states.

### Technical Mastery and Limitations
Despite his poetic genius, Rossetti struggled with the technical aspects of painting, particularly perspective and formal composition. He often "neglected the slow and tiresome but necessary subservient processes" of academic training, leading critics to note that "he never to the end of his life could be brought to care much whether his pictures were in perspective or not." However, his extraordinary use of color, particularly his rich reds, deep purples, and golden tones, created emotional impact that transcended technical imperfections.

> "His mastery of the poetic art was consummate, while he failed to perfectly acquire even the grammar of painting, there should seem no reasonable doubt that his higher rank is as a poet."
> _Original: English_

**Quick Take**: Rossetti's artistic strength lay in emotional expression rather than technical precision, making his work accessible despite formal limitations.

### The Female Archetype and Muse
Throughout his career, Rossetti developed a distinctive female archetype featuring strong jaws, full lips, abundant hair, and intense gazes. These women—modeled variously by his wife Elizabeth Siddal, Fanny Cornforth, and Jane Morris—became vehicles for exploring themes of love, beauty, and spiritual longing. Works like "Proserpine" and "Astarte Syriaca" transform specific models into mythological figures, while portraits like "Aurelia (Fazio's Mistress)" psychological presence through direct engagement with the viewer.

> "The Lady Lilith is seated against a background covered with roses. Dressed in white, she holds a mirror in her hand, and combs her long fair hair."
> _Original: English_

**Quick Take**: Rossetti's women are both specific individuals and universal symbols, embodying his complex vision of feminine power and mystery.

## Major Works and Periods

### Early Pre-Raphaelite Period (1848-1855)
- **"The Girlhood of Mary Virgin"** (1849): Rossetti's first exhibited oil painting, establishing his Pre-Raphaelite credentials
- **"Ecce Ancilla Domini"** (1850): Angelic Annunciation scene featuring his sister Christina as the Virgin
- **"Paolo and Francesca"** (1855): Dramatic watercolor illustrating Dante's doomed lovers

### Transitional Period (1856-1862)
- **"Found"** (unfinished): Social commentary painting depicting a country girl who has come to the city
- **"Beata Beatrix"** (completed 1863): Masterwork commemorating his wife Elizabeth Siddal

### Mature Period (1863-1871)
- **"Venus Verticordia"** (1868): Bold nude portrait with symbolic elements
- **"Monna Vanna"** (1866): Exemplary portrait of Jane Morris in luxurious dress
- **"Dante's Dream"** (1869-71): Large-scale masterpiece combining multiple figures with rich symbolism

### Late Period (1872-1882)
- **"The Blessed Damozel"** (multiple versions): Visual interpretation of his famous poem
- **"Astarte Syriaca"** (1877): Final major work exploring themes of love and divinity

## Key Themes and Symbolism

### The Divine and the Sensual
Rossetti's work consistently explores the tension between spiritual longing and physical desire, often using classical and biblical subjects to investigate this duality. His "Venus Verticordia" literally means "Venus the Turner of Hearts," while religious subjects like "Beata Beatrix" use Christian imagery to explore themes of love and transcendence.

### Memory and Loss
The tragic death of his wife Elizabeth Siddal in 1862 profoundly influenced Rossetti's later work, creating a consistent undercurrent of melancholy and nostalgia. Paintings like "Beata Beatrix" and "Proserpine" explore themes of separation, waiting, and the persistence of love beyond death.

### The Pre-Raphaelite Woman
Rossetti helped establish the iconic Pre-Raphaelite female type with strong features, abundant hair, and intense emotional presence. These women simultaneously embody Victorian ideals of beauty and challenge conventional gender roles through their psychological complexity and assertive gaze.

## Art Appreciation Techniques

### Understanding Symbolic Elements
Look for recurring symbols: roses (love and beauty), poppies (death and sleep), musical instruments (harmony), and mirrors (self-knowledge). Rossetti's symbolic language creates layered meanings that reward close viewing.

### Color and Emotional Impact
Pay attention to Rossetti's distinctive color palette—rich reds, deep purples, golden tones, and lush greens. His colors aren't merely decorative but serve to establish emotional atmosphere and psychological intensity.

### The Gaze and Psychological Presence
Notice how Rossetti's subjects often engage directly with the viewer through intense, sometimes challenging gazes. This direct connection creates an immediate emotional response and psychological involvement.

### Literary Connections
Many paintings relate to specific poems or literary works. Understanding these connections can deepen appreciation of the imagery and symbolism, particularly works inspired by Dante or Rossetti's own poetry.
