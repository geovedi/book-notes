# Made to Stick
**Authors**: Chip Heath, Dan Heath | **Year**: 2007
**Category**: Business & Finance

## Summary
"Made to Stick" reveals why some ideas thrive while others die, introducing the SUCCES framework—six principles that make ideas memorable and impactful. The Heath brothers demonstrate that stickiness isn't about luck or inherent brilliance; it's about understanding how the human brain processes information and crafting messages accordingly.

The book's central insight is that the biggest obstacle to creating sticky ideas is the "Curse of Knowledge"—our tendency to forget what it's like not to know something. This comprehensive guide is essential for marketers, educators, leaders, and anyone who wants their ideas to make a lasting impact. You'll learn practical techniques to transform abstract concepts into memorable messages that inspire action.

## Core Insights

### Simplicity: Find Your Core
Effective ideas strip away the non-essential and focus on the profound core. Simplicity isn't about dumbing down—it's about prioritizing what truly matters. Like Southwest Airlines' guiding principle "We are THE low-fare airline," the best ideas are both compact and profound.

The key is ruthless exclusion. As defense attorneys know, "If you argue ten points, even if each is a good point, when they get back to the jury room they won't remember any." Simplicity means finding your core message and expressing it with the clarity of a proverb.

> "Simple = core + compact"

**Quick Take**: Before communicating anything, identify your single most important message. If you could only say one thing, what would it be?

### Unexpectedness: Break Patterns
To grab attention and maintain it, you must violate expectations. The brain naturally tunes out predictable information, but surprises create an "alertness spike" that demands attention. Movie popcorn containing more fat than an entire day's worth of unhealthy meals—this unexpected comparison made the idea stick.

The trick is using surprise strategically, then maintaining interest through knowledge gaps. Great teachers create mystery by opening gaps in students' understanding, then systematically filling them. This gap theory of curiosity keeps audiences engaged longer than initial surprise alone.

**Quick Take**: What aspect of your idea would most surprise your audience? Start there, then create curiosity gaps they'll want to resolve.

### Concreteness: Make It Tangible
Abstract ideas are forgettable; concrete images stick. Natural sticky ideas are filled with sensory details—ice-filled bathtubs, apples with razor blades, greasy food displays. Our brains are wired to remember concrete data because it connects to existing schemas.

The "Velcro theory of memory" explains this: the more hooks your idea has, the more places it can latch onto in memory. Engineers might understand "maximizing shareholder value," but everyone understands "put a man on the moon and return him safely by the end of the decade."

**Quick Take**: Translate abstract concepts into concrete, sensory language. What would your idea look like, feel like, or sound like in real life?

### Credibility: Help People Believe
Sticky ideas must carry their own credentials. While authority figures can lend credibility, most situations require building belief through other means. The most effective approaches include using vivid details, human-scale statistics, and testable credentials.

Reagan's famous debate question—"Are you better off today than you were four years ago?"—worked because it let voters test the claim themselves. The "Sinatra Test" (if you can make it there, you can make it anywhere) shows how one dramatic example can establish credibility more effectively than mountains of data.

**Quick Take**: How can your audience test your ideas for themselves? Provide proof they can experience directly rather than just taking your word for it.

### Emotions: Make People Care
People care about people, not abstractions. The "Mother Teresa principle" states: "If I look at the one, I will act." Statistics about millions of starving people don't motivate like the story of one specific child.

The key is finding the right emotion to tap. Sometimes it's not what you'd expect. Teenagers quit smoking not from fear of lung cancer, but from resentment at tobacco industry manipulation.WIIFY (What's In It For You) thinking connects your ideas to your audience's self-interest.

> "People are more likely to make a charitable gift to a single needy individual than to an entire impoverished region."

**Quick Take**: Who are the real people affected by your idea? Tell their stories and appeal to identity rather than abstract benefits.

### Stories: Prepare for Action
Stories act as mental flight simulators, allowing us to experience situations without actual risk. Firefighters swap stories after every fire, building mental catalogs of critical situations and appropriate responses. Stories engage, inspire, and provide practical simulation.

Three basic story types consistently work: the Challenge Plot (underdog succeeds), the Connection Plot (people connect across divides), and the Creativity Plot (someone solves a problem in a breakthrough way). Jared's Subway story—losing 245 pounds eating sandwiches—combined the Challenge and Connection plots to create one of advertising's most memorable campaigns.

**Quick Take**: What stories illustrate your idea in action? People remember stories long after they forget statistics and facts.

## Best Quotes

- "The Curse of Knowledge. Once we know something, we find it hard to imagine what it was like not to know it."

- "Sticky = understandable, memorable, and effective in changing thought or behavior."

- "It's like Tolstoy's quote: 'All happy families resemble each other, but each unhappy family is unhappy in its own way.' All creative ads resemble one another, but each loser is uncreative in its own way."

- "Speaking concretely is the only way to ensure that our idea will mean the same thing to everyone in your audience."

- "Stories are like flight simulators for the brain."

## Action Items

- **Before communicating**: Identify your single core message and strip away everything else
- **To grab attention**: Find the most surprising aspect of your idea and lead with it
- **To make it stick**: Translate abstract concepts into concrete, sensory details everyone can understand
- **To build belief**: Provide testable evidence rather than just assertions
- **To inspire action**: Connect your idea to individual people and compelling stories
- **To check your work**: Review your message against the SUCCES framework before sharing

## Questions to Consider

- What abstract language in your communication could become concrete and tangible?
- Where are you assuming your audience knows things they don't (the Curse of Knowledge)?
- What's the most unexpected thing about your idea that breaks expectations?
- Who are the real people behind your statistics and abstractions?
- What stories demonstrate your idea in action rather than just explaining it?
- How can you make your idea simple without making it simplistic?

## Conclusion

"Made to Stick" is worth your time because it transforms communication from guesswork into a systematic process. The SUCCES framework provides practical, memorable principles you can apply immediately to any message, from boardroom presentations to classroom lessons to family conversations.

The biggest reason to read this book is its universal applicability. Whether you're a teacher trying to make lessons memorable, a leader rallying your team, a marketer crafting campaigns, or anyone who needs to influence others, these principles work. The authors combine rigorous research with engaging examples, making complex ideas accessible and actionable.

Unlike many business books that offer abstract theories, "Made to Stick" provides concrete techniques you can use today to make your ideas more impactful. The framework isn't just clever—it's backed by extensive research across psychology, education, marketing, and organizational behavior. This is one of those rare books that can fundamentally change how you communicate for the better, permanently.
