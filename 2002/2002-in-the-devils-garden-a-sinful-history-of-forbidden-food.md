# In the Devil's Garden: A Sinful History of Forbidden Food
**Author**: Stewart Lee Allen | **Year**: 2002 | **Category**: History & Biography

## Summary
Stewart Lee Allen takes readers on a globe-trotting culinary adventure through history's most forbidden foods, revealing how religious, political, and cultural forces have shaped what we consider taboo. From the apple that became Christianity's symbol of temptation to chocolate's reputation as an aristocratic aphrodisiac, Allen uncovers the fascinating stories behind foods deemed too dangerous, immoral, or sinful to eat. The book combines travel writing, food history, and cultural anthropology to explore how taboos around food reflect deeper social anxieties about power, sexuality, and morality.

This culinary detective story will appeal to food lovers, history buffs, and anyone interested in how cultural attitudes shape our relationship with what we eat. Readers will gain a new appreciation for the complex histories behind everyday ingredients and understand how fear and desire have continually battled at our dinner tables.

## Core Insights

### The Apple: Catholic Propaganda Against Celtic Pagans
The Bible never identifies the forbidden fruit as an apple—this was a brilliant propaganda move by the Roman Catholic Church in the 4th century. The Celts revered the apple as sacred, using it in religious ceremonies and calling their paradise "Avalon" (Isle of Apples). When Christian missionaries tried converting northern Europe, they deliberately chose the Celts' sacred fruit to symbolize evil knowledge, transforming the apple from a symbol of divine wisdom into a representation of heresy and sin. Every red apple with its pentagram core became a warning about the dangers of non-Catholic beliefs.

> "The Bible never identifies the evil fruit, he said; it was the Roman Catholics who put the apple there. The Greek Church sees the forbidden fruit only as a symbol of pride and carnal desire."

**Quick Take**: Food taboos often serve as political weapons, with powerful groups demonizing the sacred foods of conquered peoples to undermine their cultural identity.

### The Tomato: From Love Apple to Ketchup
When tomatoes arrived from the Americas, Europeans initially called them "love apples" and associated them with the mandrake plant, believing they were both aphrodisiacs and poisonous. The tomato's sensual appearance—red flesh dripping with juices and seeds—made it seem immoral compared to the chaste potato, which reproduced asexually. For 150 years, tomatoes were considered evil fruits that drove people insane. The famous story of Robert Johnson eating a tomato in public to prove it wasn't poisonous is actually a myth that emerged long after the fact.

> "The Love Apple, dripping unctuous juices and seeds, soft and delicious, inviting the unwary to bite deep into its harlot-red flesh and let the juices flow, was an entirely different class of being: immoral, lascivious, and decidedly un-Christian."

**Quick Take**: Our food fears often reveal more about our cultural attitudes toward sexuality and pleasure than about any real danger from the food itself.

### Chocolate: The Aristocratic Aphrodisiac
Chocolate began as a sacred Aztec drink reserved for royalty and sacrificial victims. When it reached Europe, it became the signature beverage of the aristocracy, symbolizing their decadent lifestyle. The Marquis de Sade demanded "chocolate... black like the Devil's ass" in his prison cell, using it to fuel his notorious sexual exploits. Chocolate houses became centers of political intrigue and sexual liberation, prompting religious authorities to repeatedly ban it. The drink's association with pleasure, power, and forbidden desire made it the ultimate status symbol for those who could afford to indulge in "sin."

> "Chocolate appears as the status beverage of the ancien regime... It's a connection recorded in numerous paintings that depict marquises and marchionesses lounging in bed over a cup of cocoa."

**Quick Take**: Foods that enhance pleasure often become status symbols precisely because they're forbidden to ordinary people, creating a powerful link between indulgence and social hierarchy.

### The Politics of Food and Sexual Identity
Different cultures have long associated specific foods with sexual identity and preference. Ancient Spartans believed eating bull and boy together enhanced masculinity, while some New Guinea cultures practice ritualized semen ingestion as a form of "men's food." European taboos against rabbit, weasel, and fish stemmed from beliefs these animals would cause unnatural sexual behaviors. Even today, associations between meat-eating and masculinity versus salads and femininity persist in Western culture, showing how food taboos reinforce gender norms.

> "Real Men, the moral goes, eat only meat. That's Man with a capital M: Man the Magnificent, Man the Macho, the Meat-Killer and Lord of the Jungle who thrives on 'hero sandwiches' and Manhandler beef stews."

**Quick Take**: Food choices have always been tied to ideas about masculinity and femininity, with societies using dietary restrictions to police sexual behavior and reinforce gender roles.

### Gluttony: Christianity's Original Sin
While most people think lust caused humanity's expulsion from Eden, theologians actually identify gluttony as the primary sin. The fundamental evil wasn't overeating, but simply enjoying food too much, as this indicated focus on earthly pleasures rather than divine will. This theological interpretation led to centuries of anti-eating sentiment, laws restricting culinary enjoyment, and the elevation of starvation as a spiritual virtue. This attitude helped create psychological conditions like anorexia and bulimia, showing how religious food taboos can have lasting harmful effects.

> "The seeming innocuousness of the sin makes it one of Lucifer's favorites for luring the naive deeper into hell."

**Quick Take**: When any natural pleasure is treated as sinful, it creates psychological damage that can persist for generations.

## Takeaways
- Question your food instincts—many of our likes and dislikes come from centuries-old cultural programming, not actual taste or safety concerns
- Recognize how food choices often serve as markers of social status, religious affiliation, or moral alignment
- Understand that today's exotic delicacy may have been yesterday's forbidden sin, and vice versa
- Be aware of how gender stereotypes influence both your food choices and how you judge others' eating habits
- Appreciate that the act of sharing food has always been fundamentally about connection, whether for good or ill

## Questions to Consider
- What foods do you consider "sinful" or indulgent, and where did those attitudes come from?
- How do your food choices reflect your social status, cultural background, or moral values?
- Why do we still associate certain foods with sexual behavior or gender identity?
- How have modern nutritional science and food globalization changed our relationship with historical food taboos?
- What current food trends might future historians view as strange or irrational as we now view medieval food taboos?

## Conclusion
"In the Devil's Garden" is a witty, illuminating journey that will forever change how you look at your dinner plate. Allen's globetrotting research and engaging storytelling reveal that our food taboos say more about our cultural anxieties than about the foods themselves. By understanding the complex histories behind what we consider forbidden, we gain insight into how power, religion, and sexuality shape our most basic daily choices.

The book is worth your time for its fascinating revelations about everyday ingredients and its reminder that culinary pleasure has always been a site of cultural conflict. Allen shows that the battle between indulgence and restriction, between sin and salvation, continues to play out every time we sit down to eat—making our daily meals much more meaningful than we might think.
