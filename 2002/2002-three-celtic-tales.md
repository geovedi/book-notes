# Three Celtic Tales
**Authors**: Moyra Caldecott | **Year**: 2002 | **Category**: Fantasy & Science Fiction

## Summary
"Three Celtic Tales" is a captivating collection of retold Welsh myths from the Mabinogion tradition, each story exploring profound themes of transformation, identity, and the complex relationships between mortals and magical beings. Caldecott breathes new life into ancient Celtic lore while adding her own imaginative expansions to fill narrative gaps in the original texts.

These three interconnected stories—Bran, Branwen and Evnissyen; Taliesin and Avagddu; and The Twins of the Tylwyth Teg—transport readers to a world where giants walk the earth, magic cauldrons grant divine wisdom, and fairy princesses dwell beneath mountain lakes. The collection is perfect for mythology enthusiasts, fantasy lovers, and anyone seeking to understand the deep well of Celtic storytelling tradition.

## Core Insights

### The Power of Sacrifice and Redemption
**Bran, Branwen and Evnissyen** tells the story of the giant king Bran who must become a living bridge to save his people. When his half-brother Evnissyen's jealousy sparks war between Britain and Ireland, Bran ultimately sacrifices his own life, commanding that his severed head be buried to protect Britain as an oracle. This story explores how destructive impulses can be transformed into redemptive acts, and how true leadership often requires personal sacrifice for the greater good.

> "He who would be a chief must also be a bridge."
**Quick Take**: Real leadership means becoming the connection that helps others cross their challenges.

### The Accidental Gift and True Wisdom
In **Taliesin and Avagddu**, the humble boy Gwion accidentally receives three drops of divine wisdom meant for the goddess Caridwen's son. This sparks a legendary shapeshifting chase across the Welsh countryside, culminating in Gwion's rebirth as Taliesin, the greatest bard of Welsh tradition. The story suggests that true wisdom often comes to those who don't seek it, and that divine gifts carry both burden and responsibility.

> "For a moment he crouched, stunned... his mind expanded beyond the Universe to See what has never been seen before."
**Quick Take**: Wisdom finds the humble heart more readily than the ambitious seeker.

### Identity and Recognition
**The Twins of the Tylwyth Teg** presents identical fairy princesses Haelwyn and Olwen, where mistaken identity leads to complicated romantic entanglements between mortals and fairy folk. When a prince chooses one sister thinking she's the other, it explores themes of true identity versus surface appearance, and the painful dynamics of being the "unrecognized" twin. The story beautifully captures the ache of being overlooked while highlighting the transformative power of being truly seen for who you are.

> "What? A father not knowing the name of his own daughter!"
**Quick Take**: True love recognizes essence, not just appearance or mistaken identity.

## Key Takeaways

- Ancient Celtic myths carry timeless wisdom about human nature and spiritual growth
- The Celtic tradition grants special importance to the head as a source of wisdom and prophecy
- Transformation often comes through unexpected channels and mistakes
- Jealousy and insecurity can destroy relationships, but redemption is always possible
- The boundary between mortal and fairy worlds is thin, and love can bridge them
- True identity transcends physical appearance and mistaken assumptions

## Conclusion

"Three Celtic Tales" is absolutely worth your time if you love mythology, fantasy, or stories with deep psychological and spiritual resonance. Caldecott's retellings make these ancient Welsh myths accessible while preserving their mysterious power and emotional depth. The collection shines in its ability to weave together entertainment with profound insights into human nature, transformation, and the thin veil between ordinary reality and magical realms.

What makes this book exceptional is how Caldecott respects the original material while adding her own imaginative extensions that complete narrative gaps. She fills in what happens to overlooked characters like Avagddu and the second twin Haelwyn, giving voice to those traditionally left in the shadows. The writing is lyrical and evocative, transporting readers to mist-shrouded Welsh landscapes where giants tread and fairy princesses dance by moonlit lakes.

Whether you're a mythology enthusiast, a fantasy reader, or someone who appreciates stories with psychological depth, this collection offers a rich journey into the heart of Celtic storytelling tradition. These tales remind us that ancient myths still have the power to illuminate our understanding of ourselves and our place in the mysterious tapestry of life.
