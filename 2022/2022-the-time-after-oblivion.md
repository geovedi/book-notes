# The Time After Oblivion
**Authors**: Jonny Capps | **Year**: 2022 | **Category**: Fiction & Literature

## Summary
"The Time After Oblivion" is a mythological fantasy that reimagines Greek gods and heroes in the modern world. The story begins with the Fates weaving their eternal work of measuring and cutting the threads of life, then transitions to ancient Greek heroes like Jason, Castor, Pollux, Hercules, and Orpheus battling mythical creatures. The narrative quickly shifts to reveal that the Olympian gods, led by Zeus, have been living in a parallel dimension for over two thousand years after losing a war against other pantheons. When ancient heroes start appearing in the modern world, including Hercules causing chaos in Cleveland, Zeus must confront the consequences of his long-lost son's actions while dealing with the mysterious death of Aphrodite and an unseen enemy threatening Olympus.

This book is perfect for mythology enthusiasts who enjoy contemporary retellings of ancient legends, fantasy readers who love seeing mythological characters navigate modern challenges, and fans of action-packed stories that blend humor with epic stakes. Readers will gain a fresh perspective on well-known mythological figures while enjoying a fast-paced adventure that explores themes of family, duty, and the clash between ancient power and modern reality.

## Core Insights

### The Gods' Hidden Dimension
The Olympian pantheon didn't disappear—they retreated to a parallel dimension of Hephaestus's creation after losing a catastrophic war against other pantheons. This separate reality operates under the same physical laws as Earth, meaning gods must perform mundane human tasks like showering, shaving, and maintaining personal hygiene. Zeus's daily routine becomes a metaphor for the gods' fallen glory and their struggle to maintain dignity in diminished circumstances.

> "Each day, Zeus had to get up, shower, shave, put on deodorant, trim his fingernails, blow his nose, and all those other human things. It was one of the parameters of this new dimension that Hephaestus had found for them, and it was obnoxious."

**Quick Take**: Even gods must adapt to changing circumstances, showing that power and status are temporary states that require constant adjustment to new realities.

### Heroes Lost in Time
Ancient Greek heroes like Hercules and Jason have been trapped between dimensions for millennia, their stories continuing beyond what history recorded. When they suddenly reappear in the modern world, they bring their ancient mindsets and combat skills into contemporary settings, creating both comedic and dangerous situations. Their time-displacement highlights the clash between mythological thinking and modern societal norms.

> "There stood his son, dirty and filthy, as if he had not bathed in centuries, wielding a car door as if it were a trophy."

**Quick Take**: The collision of ancient heroism with modern civilization reveals both the timelessness of courage and the absurdity of applying outdated solutions to contemporary problems.

### The Mysterious Enemy
A powerful, unidentified force has systematically weakened the Olympian pantheon, culminating in the murder of Aphrodite. This enemy operates with enough knowledge of divine vulnerabilities to target specific weaknesses, suggesting either an inside threat or a pantheon that has studied the Greeks extensively. Their attacks are precise enough to leave survivors while demonstrating overwhelming superiority.

> "The slaughter of an Olympian seemed to have been their goal all along, and now that they had succeeded, there was nothing to stop them from doing more damage."

**Quick Take**: True power often works from the shadows, and the greatest threats are those we can't immediately identify or understand.

### The Eternal Nature of Fate
The three Fates continue their work across all dimensions and time periods, pulling, measuring, and cutting the threads of life without preference or emotion. They represent the one constant in a universe of change—mortality affects even the gods, and destiny operates beyond divine intervention. Their restoration of a "newly restored string" suggests that even what has been cut can potentially be mended.

> "The Fates have always pulled, measured, and cut the strings."

**Quick Take**: Some forces operate beyond even the highest powers, suggesting that true wisdom lies in understanding and adapting to inevitability rather than fighting against it.

- "Humans no longer seemed to worship gods. They seemed to worship other things, such as money, technology, entertainment, and youth."

- "Sanity, of course, has no place in heroism."

- "The world had changed a lot in two thousand years."

- "Their identities were still a secret, but it did not matter any longer. It made no difference who they were. The fact they existed, and they had so much power over the gods, was destructive enough."

## Key Action Items

- **Embrace adaptation**: Like Zeus learning to shave daily, recognize when resistance to change is futile and find ways to thrive in new circumstances

- **Value unexpected connections**: The heroes' reemergence shows that alliances and relationships can resurface when least expected but most needed

- **Face mortality with dignity**: Even gods must confront their limitations, suggesting that accepting vulnerability is a sign of wisdom, not weakness

- **Question traditional power structures**: The gods' exile demonstrates that no rule lasts forever and that adaptability matters more than authority

## Questions to Consider

1. How do our modern "gods" (money, technology, entertainment) serve the same psychological needs as ancient deities?

2. If you had to maintain your power but lost all your privileges, what aspects of your identity would you preserve?

3. How might ancient wisdom and modern innovation complement each other rather than conflict?

4. What eternal forces in your life operate beyond your control, and how do you respond to them?

5. If mythology teaches us about human nature, what do our contemporary myths say about us?

## Conclusion

"The Time After Oblivion" is worth your time if you enjoy mythological retellings that balance humor with genuine stakes. The book succeeds in making ancient gods feel both powerful and vulnerable, creating a compelling narrative that explores what happens when immortals face mortality and heroes become anachronisms. The biggest reason to read this book is its clever premise of gods grappling with diminished relevance while facing an existential threat that even they may not overcome.

The novel delivers on its promise of action, humor, and mythological intrigue while raising thoughtful questions about power, adaptation, and the eternal nature of storytelling. While some readers might find the pacing uneven as it jumps between ancient battles and modern predicaments, the overall effect is an entertaining exploration of what happens when the divine becomes mortal and myths meet reality.
