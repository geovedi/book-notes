# India's Experiment With Democracy
**Authors**: S.Y. Quraishi | **Year**: 2022 | **Category**: History & Biography

## Summary
"India's Experiment With Democracy" is a comprehensive analysis of the world's largest electoral democracy by former Chief Election Commissioner Dr. S.Y. Quraishi. Written from his unique vantage point as someone who has overseen multiple Indian elections, the book combines personal experience with institutional knowledge to examine India's democratic journey over 75 years. Quraishi, born just two months before India's independence in 1947, presents himself as a "midnight child" who has grown alongside the nation, offering both celebration of democratic achievements and critical assessment of current challenges.

This book is essential for anyone interested in understanding how Indian democracy functions in practice—its triumphs in conducting massive elections, its institutional innovations, and the serious threats it faces from money power, majoritarianism, and the erosion of secular values. Readers will gain unprecedented insights into electoral management, the challenges of maintaining election integrity, and potential reforms needed to strengthen Indian democracy for the future.

## Core Insights

### India's Democratic Paradox: Grand Achievements, Grave Concerns
India has conducted the world's largest electoral exercises successfully since 1952, earning praise as the "gold standard" from international observers like Hillary Clinton. The country gave equal voting rights to women and men from its first election, while it took the US 144 years and UK 100 years to achieve the same. India had a powerful woman prime minister within 19 years of independence, while the US has never had a woman president in 250 years. However, despite these remarkable achievements, the Economist's Global Democracy Index now classifies India as a "flawed democracy," highlighting the growing gap between electoral success and democratic substance.

> "India is secular because the Hindus are secular. After the Partition of India on religious grounds, when Pakistan became an Islamic republic, India proudly chose to be a secular country with a unanimous vote in the Constituent Assembly, 83 per cent of which consisted of Hindus!"

**Quick Take**: India's electoral achievements are genuinely impressive, but democracy is more than just voting—true democratic health requires protecting minority rights, institutional independence, and inclusive representation.

### The Three Curses: Casteism, Communalism, and Corruption
Quraishi identifies three interconnected threats systematically undermining Indian democracy. Caste-based vote banking continues to influence electoral strategies, while communal polarization has intensified in recent years, threatening India's unique selling proposition of unity in diversity. Most dangerously, money power has transformed elections—with the 2019 general election becoming the most expensive in world history at $8 billion, surpassing the $6.5 billion spent on the 2016 US presidential election. The seizure of ₹3,500 crore (about $42 million) in illegal cash, drugs, liquor, and freebies during the 2019 election demonstrates the scale of electoral corruption.

> "For short-terms gains, we are doing long-term damage to the idea of India. This worries me. While the rest of the world can take care of itself, my idea of my India is under serious threat."

**Quick Take**: These three curses reinforce each other—caste and communal identities are exploited through money power, creating a vicious cycle that undermines democratic equality and good governance.

### Representation Deficits: Women and Muslims in Indian Politics
Despite India's democratic credentials, political representation faces serious deficits. Women constitute only 14.4% of MPs in the Lok Sabha, placing India 143rd globally in women's parliamentary presence—behind Pakistan, Bangladesh, and even Sri Lanka. Astonishingly, women's "winnability" as candidates has consistently been higher than men's since 1957, yet political parties remain reluctant to give them tickets. Muslim representation has reached historic lows, with only 4.2% in the 2014 parliament despite Muslims comprising over 14% of India's population. The BJP had only one Muslim MP among its 303 members after 2019.

> "Between 1952 and 1977, when Muslims accounted for about 10 per cent of the population, only 5 per cent of all candidates nominated by the mainstream regional or national parties were Muslim."

**Quick Take**: The lack of representation is not due to candidate viability but political unwillingness—parties refuse tickets despite women and Muslims demonstrating higher win rates when given opportunities.

### Electoral Bonds: Trojan Horse for Democratic Backsliding
Quraishi presents a scathing critique of the electoral bonds scheme, which he argues legitimizes anonymous corporate donations and effectively legalizes crony capitalism. The scheme, introduced through the Finance Act 2017, amended multiple laws including the RBI Act, Companies Act, and Foreign Contribution Regulations Act to enable unlimited anonymous political funding. The Election Commission initially protested strongly against the scheme, warning it would "encourage large sums in illegal donations" and lead to "mushrooming of shell companies," but later reversed its position under government pressure.

> "The introduction of electoral bonds through the budget was not an isolated act. The Finance Act 2017 introduced amendments in the Reserve Bank of India Act, Companies Act, Income Tax Act, Representation of the People Act and Foreign Contribution Regulations Act to make way for electoral bonds."

**Quick Take**: Electoral bonds represent a fundamental assault on transparency in political funding, making corruption institutional rather than incidental and giving corporations unprecedented influence over governance.

### Democratic Resilience and Path Forward
Despite serious challenges, Quraishi remains optimistic about India's democratic future, citing the country's natural resilience and ability to accommodate diverse ideologies. He proposes specific reforms including state funding of elections, complete transparency in political funding, mandatory auditing of party accounts by independent agencies, and increased representation for women and minorities. He also emphasizes the need for electoral reforms to address criminalization of politics, with 184 legislators with criminal backgrounds in the 2014 Lok Sabha—a dramatic increase from 128 in 2004.

> "Being the largest democracy is not good enough; my dream is for India to become the greatest democracy in the world too. I am optimistic. We are an intelligent and hardworking nation, and can achieve this."

**Quick Take**: India's democratic future depends on institutional reforms that address representation deficits, criminalization, and corruption while preserving the electoral management system's independence and integrity.

- "India is still going through the pangs of adolescence—the same identity crisis, the same anxiety. 'Am I still a child or an adult? Are we a developing or a developed country? Are we a socialist country of the poor or a developed country of the rich?'"

## Action Items

- **Electoral Reform Advocacy**: Support campaigns for transparency in political funding, including opposition to electoral bonds and advocacy for state funding of elections
- **Representation Monitoring**: Track and publicize data on women and minority representation in legislatures and political party candidate lists
- **Institutional Independence**: Defend the autonomy of the Election Commission and other democratic institutions from political interference
- **Voter Education**: Participate in voter awareness programs that emphasize informed voting beyond identity-based considerations
- **Criminal Accountability**: Support initiatives to prevent candidates with serious criminal charges from contesting elections

## Questions to Consider

1. How can India balance its electoral achievements with addressing substantive democratic deficits in representation and institutional integrity?

3. How might India's democratic experience offer lessons for other diverse, developing democracies globally?
4. What role can civil society play in countering the "three curses" of casteism, communalism, and corruption?
5. How can technology be leveraged to increase electoral transparency while preventing new forms of manipulation?

## Conclusion
"India's Experiment With Democracy" is an essential read for anyone seeking to understand the complexities of the world's largest electoral democracy. Quraishi provides unprecedented insider insights into both the remarkable achievements and serious challenges facing Indian democracy. The book's greatest strength lies in its balanced perspective—celebrating India's electoral successes while providing fearless critique of democratic backsliding.

This is worth your time if you care about democracy's future globally, as India represents both the promise and perils of democratic governance in diverse, developing societies. The biggest reason to read it is Quraishi's unique authority as someone who has actually managed Indian elections at the highest level, combined with his willingness to speak truth to power about democratic erosion. His warning that being the "largest" democracy is not enough—India must strive to become the "greatest" democracy—serves as both a challenge and inspiration for democratic renewal in India and beyond.
