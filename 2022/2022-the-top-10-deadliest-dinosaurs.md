# The Top 10 Deadliest Dinosaurs
**Authors**: Larry Slawson | **Year**: 2022

## Summary
"The Top 10 Deadliest Dinosaurs" ranks history's most dangerous prehistoric predators based on their physical characteristics, hunting abilities, and potential lethality. Author Larry Slawson examines ten carnivorous dinosaurs from different eras, providing detailed analysis of their size, bite force, hunting strategies, and physical weapons that made them such formidable killers.

The book excludes herbivores and focuses exclusively on meat-eating species across all time periods, evaluating each dinosaur's deadliness through their defense mechanisms, bite strength, and overall size. Perfect for dinosaur enthusiasts, students of paleontology, or anyone curious about prehistoric predators who want to understand which dinosaurs truly dominated their ancient ecosystems.

## Core Insights

### The Deadliness Formula
**Size + Bite Force + Hunting Strategy = Lethality**

The book establishes clear criteria for ranking dinosaur deadliness, emphasizing that it wasn't just about being big. A dinosaur's ability to kill depended on multiple factors working together: raw size and strength, bite pressure (measured in PSI), hunting intelligence, specialized weapons like claws or teeth, and behavioral patterns like pack hunting. This comprehensive approach reveals why some smaller dinosaurs were more deadly than larger ones.

> "All of the following dinosaurs were selected across the entire expanse of time... the final criteria revolved around each dinosaur's potential to seriously harm or kill other species."

**Quick Take**: Deadliness was multi-dimensional - the perfect predator combined size, intelligence, and specialized killing tools.

### 10. Velociraptor: Small but Savage
Despite being turkey-sized, the Velociraptor was equipped with devastating killing tools. Its signature weapon was a large sickle-shaped claw on each hindfoot, combined with 26-30 sharp teeth and three-clawed hands. This pack hunter delivered 1,000 PSI bite force (exceeding a grizzly bear) and hunted everything from small lizards to other dinosaurs.

> "The animal was capable of unleashing 1,000 PSI (pounds per square inch) of bite force on their victims; thus, allowing them to quickly subdue (and kill)."

**Quick Take**: Don't underestimate small predators - specialized weapons and pack hunting made Velociraptors disproportionately deadly.

### 9. Mapusaurus: The Earth Lizard
This massive Argentine predator weighed 6,600 pounds and hunted in coordinated packs. With a bite force of 7,000 PSI (tripling an American alligator), Mapusaurus was capable of taking down enormous prey like Argentinosaurus. Their combination of size (38 feet long), speed (30 mph), and pack intelligence made them unstoppable hunters.

> "Possessing a bite force of approximately 7,000 PSI (greatly exceeding the American Alligator's bite force of 2,980 PSI), the animal was quite capable of injuring (and killing) nearly any opponent in the wild."

**Quick Take**: Teamwork multiplied killing power - even the largest prey couldn't stand against coordinated pack attacks.

### 8. Allosaurus: The Hatchet Hunter
The Allosaurus used its skull like a hatchet, swinging its head with open mouth to slash victims. Its double-hinged jaw allowed incredible mouth opening, while 32 serrated teeth delivered 1,500 PSI bites. Standing 16.5 feet tall and weighing up to 4,400 pounds, it hunted in packs and practiced cannibalism.

> "Through a swinging motion of their head, this open-mouth strategy allowed the Allosaurus to slash its victims, inflicting massive amounts of damage and blood-loss."

**Quick Take**: Innovation in killing methods - the skull-as-hatchet technique shows how predators evolved unique attack strategies.

### 7. Saurophaganax: Lord of Lizard-Eaters
Weighing nearly 10,000 pounds with over 6,000 PSI bite force, Saurophaganax lived up to its name as "Lord of Lizard-Eaters." This massive predator hunted the largest dinosaurs including Amphicoelias and Diplodocus. Its combination of size (43 feet long), strength, and powerful claws made it one of the Jurassic era's most feared predators.

> "Combined with their large and serrated teeth, and a bite force of over 6,000 pounds in pressure, the Saurophaganax was a clear choice for our list."

**Quick Take**: Apex predators specialized in hunting the largest available prey - size was both weapon and deterrent.

### 6. Giganotosaurus: Giant Southern Lizard
One of Earth's largest terrestrial carnivores reached 30,000 pounds and 43 feet long. Its 8-inch serrated teeth delivered 8,000 PSI bite force, while 31 mph speed made it an unstoppable hunter. Despite its enormous size, Giganotosaurus was well-muscled and agile, capable of taking down massive prey like Argentinosaurus.

> "Their teeth were large and serrated, with some of them reaching upwards of eight inches in length. This, in turn, provided them with the ability to bite and kill an animal within seconds."

**Quick Take**: Extreme size didn't mean sluggishness - the largest predators remained fast and agile hunters.

### 5. Majungasaurus: The Bone-Crusher
Despite standing only 6 feet tall, Majungasaurus possessed an exceptional 6,700 PSI bite force, nearly doubling a saltwater crocodile's bite. This Madagascar predator would bite and hold victims, gnawing until delivering a kill-stroke. Fossil evidence suggests cannibalistic behavior, making it one of few dinosaurs known to eat its own kind.

> "The animal would routinely bite and hold on to its victims, gnawing their bodies until a kill-stroke could be performed."

**Quick Take**: Compact predators could deliver disproportionate damage through specialized killing techniques and extraordinary bite strength.

### 4. Carcharodontosaurus: The Shark-Toothed Horror
This African giant weighed up to 33,000 pounds with a massive 5.2-foot skull. Its 8-inch serrated teeth delivered 5,300 PSI bite force, while incredibly strong jaws could lift 935-pound animals. Carcharodontosaurus used its size, strength, and sharp claws to dominate North African ecosystems.

> "Scholars estimate that the Carcharodontosaurus was able to lift animals weighing nearly 935 pounds with its jaws and neck muscles alone."

**Quick Take**: Jaw strength evolved incredible lifting capacity - these predators could physically carry prey as heavy as humans.

### 3. Spinosaurus: Largest Land Carnivore Ever
The largest terrestrial carnivore in history reached 46-59 feet and 46,000 pounds. This semi-aquatic predator hunted both land and water, using 64 conical teeth and 4,200 PSI bite force. Its distinctive 5.4-foot neural spine made it recognizable, while crocodile-like skull design made it effective in both environments.

> "As the largest terrestrial carnivore to have ever existed, the Spinosaurus was an incredibly deadly animal renowned for its immense power, speed, and strength (on both land and in the water)."

**Quick Take**: Environmental versatility created hunting advantages - the ability to dominate multiple ecosystems made Spinosaurus uniquely successful.

### 2. Utahraptor: The Intelligent Pack Hunter
The Utahraptor combined deadly weapons with remarkable intelligence. Weighing 1,500 pounds with 9-inch sickle claws and 600-950 PSI bite force, these pack hunters strategized their attacks. They actively herded prey and set traps, demonstrating cognitive abilities that made them even more dangerous than their physical weapons alone.

> "Operating within large packs, Utahraptors possessed a strategic mindset, allowing them to actively herd and set traps for their victims."

**Quick Take**: Intelligence multiplied physical threat - strategic hunting made Utahraptors disproportionately dangerous despite smaller size.

### 1. Tyrannosaurus Rex: The Undisputed King
T-Rex earned the top spot with the most powerful bite force in land animal history: over 12,800 PSI. Weighing 19,000 pounds with 40-foot length and 12-foot height, this "King of Dinosaurs" combined size, strength, and ferocity into the perfect predatory package. Its massive skull, long teeth, and powerful hindlimbs made it nearly unstoppable.

> "The T-Rex is regularly cited as having the most powerful bite force of any land-based animal in history. On average, scholars believe that they were able to exert a bite force in excess of 12,800 pounds of pressure."

**Quick Take**: The perfect predator combined maximum size with unprecedented bite force - T-Rex represents evolutionary optimization for killing.

## Key Takeaways

- **Size wasn't everything**: Smaller dinosaurs like Velociraptor and Majungasaurus made the list through specialized weapons and hunting techniques
- **Bite force mattered enormously**: The top predators all delivered thousands of PSI, with T-Rex's 12,800 PSI being the ultimate killing weapon
- **Pack hunting multiplied threat**: Many of the deadliest dinosaurs hunted in coordinated groups, making them capable of taking down much larger prey
- **Environmental versatility helped**: Spinosaurus dominated both land and water, giving it access to diverse food sources
- **Intelligence was a force multiplier**: Utahraptor's strategic pack hunting made it more dangerous than its physical characteristics alone would suggest

## Conclusion
"The Top 10 Deadliest Dinosaurs" delivers exactly what it promises - a well-researched, engaging ranking of prehistory's most formidable predators. Larry Slawson's systematic approach to evaluating deadliness through bite force, size, and hunting strategy provides credible insights into dinosaur behavior and evolution.

The book excels at making paleontological information accessible without oversimplifying the science. Each dinosaur profile balances physical measurements with behavioral analysis, creating a comprehensive picture of why these particular species dominated their ecosystems. The comparison of bite forces to modern animals (grizzly bears, alligators, crocodiles) makes these abstract numbers relatable.

This is definitely worth your time if you're fascinated by dinosaurs, natural history, or evolutionary biology. The biggest reason to read it is the fascinating perspective on how evolution optimized these animals for killing - showing that deadliness was a sophisticated combination of physical power, intelligence, and specialized adaptations. While the format is straightforward, the content is rich enough to satisfy both casual dinosaur enthusiasts and more serious students of paleontology.
