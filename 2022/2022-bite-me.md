# Bite Me
**Original Title**: Bite me
**Authors**: Femke Buwalda | **Year**: 2022 | **Category**: Health & Medicine 

## Summary

"Bite Me" is a revolutionary guide that challenges the pervasive diet culture and offers a path to food freedom through intuitive eating and self-acceptance. Dr. Femke Buwalda, a psychologist and dietitian with 35 years of personal dieting experience, combines scientific evidence with psychological methods to help readers break free from the cycle of dieting, weight obsession, and body shame. The book presents a compelling case against traditional weight-loss approaches and offers practical tools for developing a healthy relationship with food and your body.

This book is perfect for anyone tired of the endless diet cycle, those struggling with emotional eating, body image issues, or anyone seeking to understand why conventional dieting fails and what actually works for long-term wellbeing. Readers will gain practical strategies for intuitive eating, self-acceptance, and developing a more compassionate relationship with their bodies and food.

## Core Insights

### Breaking Free from Diet Culture

The book begins by exposing diet culture as a harmful system that equates thinness with moral virtue and health, while stigmatizing larger bodies. Buwalda reveals how this culture affects everyone, not just those in larger bodies, by creating unrealistic standards and promoting weight stigma. The diet industry thrives on failure—95-98% of people regain lost weight, and many gain back more than they lost.

The Minnesota starvation experiment from the 1940s demonstrated that semi-starvation leads to food obsession, depression, and eventual binge eating—responses identical to what modern dieters experience. This biological response is explained through setpoint theory: your body has a natural weight range it defends through hunger hormones and metabolism adjustments.

**Quick Take**: Dieting doesn't fail because you lack willpower; it fails because it works against your body's natural survival mechanisms.

### Debunking Weight and Health Myths

Buwalda systematically dismantles common beliefs about weight and health. BMI, despite widespread use, was developed by an astronomer (not health researcher) studying white European men and doesn't account for age, gender, ethnicity, or body composition. Large studies show that 30% of "normal weight" people are metabolically unhealthy, while nearly half of people with "overweight" are metabolically healthy.

The real health risks aren't from weight itself but from yo-yo dieting, which is linked to higher mortality risk, bone density loss, gallstones, muscle loss, and chronic inflammation. Research shows that healthy behaviors (not smoking, moderate alcohol, eating fruits/vegetables, regular movement) reduce mortality risk equally across all weight categories.

> "The real health risks aren't from weight itself but from yo-yo dieting, which is linked to higher mortality risk, bone density loss, gallstones, muscle loss, and chronic inflammation."
> _Original: "De echte gezondheidsrisico's zijn niet afkomstig van gewicht, maar van jojoën, dat geassocieerd wordt met een hoger sterftekans, botontkalking, galstenen, spierverlies en chronische ontstekingen."_

**Quick Take**: Health behaviors matter more than the number on your scale, and yo-yo dieting is likely more harmful than maintaining a stable higher weight.

### The Three Pillars of Intuitive Eating

Intuitive eating represents a radical shift from external food rules to internal body wisdom. It consists of three essential pillars:

1. **Trust internal hunger and fullness signals** to determine when and how much to eat
2. **Unconditional permission** to eat what you want when you're hungry
3. **Eating for physical rather than emotional reasons**

Research shows that intuitive eaters have more varied diets, enjoy food more, experience fewer eating binges, and have better psychological wellbeing. They also demonstrate improved health markers including lower blood pressure, better cholesterol levels, and improved blood sugar regulation.

**Quick Take**: Intuitive eating works by restoring your natural ability to regulate food intake, a skill most people lose through years of dieting.

### Understanding the Mechanisms Behind Intuitive Eating

Intuitive eating works through three key biological and psychological mechanisms:

**Interoception** is the ability to notice internal body signals like hunger, fullness, and emotions. Diet culture teaches us to ignore these signals in favor of external rules and timing. Learning to recognize and trust these internal cues is fundamental to intuitive eating.

**Habituation** (neural adaptation) means that when you allow yourself to eat anything, the novelty and urgency around "forbidden" foods naturally decreases. The author shares an example of someone who initially ate only cookies for three months when starting intuitive eating, but eventually lost interest and now only eats cookies she genuinely enjoys.

**Restriction release** addresses both physical restriction (undereating or eliminating food groups) and mental restriction (thinking you "shouldn't" eat certain foods). Both forms trigger biological and psychological responses that lead to binge eating and food obsession.

> "Intuitive eating works by restoring your natural ability to regulate food intake, a skill most people lose through years of dieting."
> _Original: "Intuïtief eten werkt door je natuurlijke vermogen om voedselinname te reguleren te herstellen, een vaardigheid die de meeste mensen door jarenlang diëten zijn kwijtgeraakt."_

**Quick Take**: The same biological mechanisms that cause binge eating after diets can be reversed by giving yourself unconditional permission to eat.

### Practical Steps to Start Intuitive Eating

Buwalda provides concrete starting points for those new to intuitive eating:

Begin by eating three complete meals per day, each containing protein, carbohydrates, and fats, regardless of whether you feel hungry. This helps stabilize your eating patterns and reduces the anxiety around food. Eat every 2-5 hours, ideally breakfast within 1-2 hours of waking.

Before starting, temporarily set aside concerns about "healthy eating" and weight loss. These goals can trigger restriction and undermine the intuitive eating process. Approach the journey with curiosity and self-compassion rather than perfectionism.

**Quick Take**: Start with structured meals to rebuild trust in your body's signals before moving to fully intuitive eating.

## Action Items

- **Question diet culture messages**: Notice when you're thinking in diet terms (good/bad foods, guilt around eating, should/shouldn't) and gently challenge these thoughts
- **Practice structured eating**: Start with three regular meals daily, including all macronutrients, to rebuild body trust
- **Listen to hunger and fullness**: Pay attention to your body's signals without judgment—notice what different levels of hunger and fullness feel like
- **Allow all foods**: Give yourself unconditional permission to eat anything, recognizing that this will naturally lead to balanced choices over time
- **Move for joy, not punishment**: Find forms of movement you enjoy rather than exercising to burn calories or "earn" food
- **Ditch the scale**: Remove measuring tools that reinforce external validation rather than internal wisdom
- **Practice body neutrality**: Focus on what your body can do rather than how it looks
- **Seek support**: Connect with others who are also breaking free from diet culture through social media or support groups

## Conclusion

"Bite Me" offers a compelling, research-backed alternative to the endless cycle of dieting and body dissatisfaction. Buwalda combines her personal experience with professional expertise to provide a roadmap for reclaiming your relationship with food and your body. The book is particularly valuable for anyone who's tried multiple diets, struggles with emotional eating, or feels trapped by food rules and weight obsession.

The biggest reason to read this book is that it offers genuine freedom—not another set of rules disguised as flexibility, but a fundamental reorientation toward trusting your body and rejecting harmful cultural messages. While the journey requires patience and self-compassion, the payoff is profound: peace with food, acceptance of your body, and energy for living rather than constantly managing your appearance.

For those exhausted by the diet industry's false promises and ready to try something evidence-based and sustainable, "Bite Me" provides both the scientific foundation and practical tools for genuine transformation. It's not a quick fix, but it may be the last book you'll ever need about food and weight.
