# You Can Make You Rich
**Authors**: Sean Dillon | **Year**: 2010 | **Category**: Business & Finance

## Summary
This book reveals that the biggest threat to your financial success isn't market crashes or bad investments—it's yourself. Sean Dillon argues that emotional decision-making, particularly fear and greed, destroys more wealth than any market downturn. The book provides a framework for long-term wealth creation through disciplined index investing combined with behavioral self-awareness, showing how to protect yourself from your own worst investment instincts.

Perfect for new investors who want market exposure without the stress of active trading, and experienced investors who struggle with emotional decision-making. You'll learn a simple, effective system that builds wealth gradually while protecting you from self-sabotage.

## Core Insights

### Your Greatest Enemy Is Yourself
The book's opening revelation—that the author found the person who lost his money by looking in a mirror—captures the central thesis. We are our own worst financial enemies, making decisions based on subconscious influences, fear, and greed rather than rational analysis. The solution isn't more market knowledge, but better self-control and a systematic approach that removes emotion from investing.

> "Someone lost my money; now he's here in front of me. I am looking straight at him, straight into his eyes. I am looking in a mirror. I am looking at me."
**Quick Take**: Before worrying about market analysis, focus on psychological self-awareness. Your investment success depends more on controlling your emotions than on picking the right stocks.

### Index Investing Wins Long-Term
The book advocates for low-cost index trackers as the core of any investment strategy. Over 20-year periods, index funds consistently outperform most professional fund managers and individual investors. They offer automatic diversification, eliminate single-company risk through competitive destruction, and keep costs extremely low. The S&P500 averaged 8.4% annually over the 20 years ending December 2008, despite recent market crashes.

> "The typical investor would be better off if stocks had no quotation at all."
**Quick Take**: Put 80-90% of your investment capital in low-cost index trackers and let compound interest work its magic over decades rather than months.

### Master Your Emotions or They'll Master You
Fear causes investors to sell at market bottoms, while greed drives them to buy at peaks. Both emotions lead to wealth destruction. The book shows how media sensationalism and 24-hour news cycles amplify these emotions, making rational decision-making nearly impossible during market volatility. Studies prove that investors who receive frequent stock updates earn half the returns of those who ignore news completely.

> "Fear makes us act irrationally. Fear is in the markets. It is a big part of it. Well, get used to it because it is not going anywhere."
**Quick Take**: Develop an investing suit of armor against media noise and market panic. Remember your long-term goals during short-term turbulence.

### Cost Averaging Is Your Secret Weapon
Investing fixed amounts regularly through dollar-cost averaging turns market volatility into an advantage. When markets decline, you buy more shares; when they rise, you buy fewer. Over long periods, this strategy dramatically outperforms lump-sum investing, especially during market crashes. Starting with $100 and adding $100 monthly during the Great Depression would have turned into $15,571 over ten years, versus just $7,223 from a single lump sum investment.

> "If you had invested $12,000 in the S&P500 index at the beginning of September 1929, just before the market crash and the ensuing Great Depression, ten years later you would have had only $7,223 left. But if you had started with $100 and then invested another $100 each month, by August 1939 your money would have grown to $15,571."
**Quick Take**: Set up automatic monthly investments in index funds regardless of market conditions. This forces discipline and takes emotion out of timing decisions.

### Avoid Leverage Like the Plague
Borrowing to invest (margin/leverage) only speeds up your journey—either to wealth or ruin. The book reveals that leverage forces short-term thinking, encourages excessive trading to repay loans, and gives lenders control over your portfolio through margin calls. Even if markets recover, you might be forced to sell at the worst possible time when lenders demand repayment.

> "There are only two things I know of that bring a man down: debt and drink."
**Quick Take**: Never invest with borrowed money. The potential acceleration of gains isn't worth the guaranteed increase in risk and loss of control.

## Action Items
- Invest 80-90% of your portfolio in low-cost index funds tracking major markets (S&P500, FTSE100)
- Set up automatic monthly investments to implement dollar-cost averaging
- Never borrow money to invest—only use surplus funds you can afford to lose
- Create written investment rules and follow them regardless of market conditions
- Limit checking your portfolio to quarterly reviews to avoid emotional decision-making
- If picking individual stocks (10-20% of portfolio), use stop-losses at 15% declines
- Let winning investments run indefinitely; don't sell small gains for quick gratification

## Questions to Consider
- What fears or desires might influence my investment decisions during the next market crash?
- Am I investing for long-term wealth creation or short-term excitement?
- Who or what external influences might be affecting my investment strategy?
- How would I react if my portfolio dropped 50% tomorrow?
- What psychological triggers cause me to want to buy or sell investments?

## Conclusion
This book is worth your time if you're looking for a simple, proven path to long-term wealth without the stress of active stock picking. The biggest value isn't in discovering secret investment techniques—it's in understanding that the person you need to conquer is yourself. By focusing on index investing, dollar-cost averaging, and emotional discipline, you can build substantial wealth while avoiding the self-destructive behaviors that derail most investors.

The book's greatest strength is its psychological focus. While many investment books focus on what to buy, this one explains how to behave. That's actually more important since behavior determines results more than stock selection. If you want a boring but effective system that works while you sleep, this is your guide. If you seek excitement and stock-picking glory, you're exactly who this book was written to warn about.
