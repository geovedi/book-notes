# The Notorious Benedict Arnold
**Author**: Steve Sheinkin | **Year**: 2010

## Summary

This narrative non-fiction book tells the extraordinary true story of Benedict Arnold, America's most infamous traitor. Once a brilliant military hero who risked everything for the Revolutionary cause, Arnold's transformation from patriot to betrayer forms one of history's most compelling dramas. The book follows his journey from a daring Connecticut youth through his remarkable military achievements, including the capture of Fort Ticonderoga and the crucial Battle of Saratoga, to his ultimate decision to betray his country by attempting to sell West Point to the British.

Perfect for history enthusiasts, military buffs, and anyone fascinated by complex human psychology, this book reveals how a combination of personal pride, financial desperation, and perceived slights drove one of America's most promising soldiers to commit the ultimate act of treason.

## Core Insights

### The Making of a Daredevil
Benedict Arnold's character was forged in childhood tragedy and rebellion. Born into a wealthy Connecticut family that fell into ruin, young Arnold was described as a "daredevil" who constantly sought attention through dangerous stunts. After yellow fever killed his sisters and his father's alcoholism destroyed the family business, Arnold responded with increasingly reckless behavior—climbing on burning barn roofs, jumping on spinning waterwheels, and challenging authorities to fistfights. This craving for glory and recognition would drive his entire military career.

> "He was a thrill seeker, a natural athlete, a born show-off."

**Quick Take**: Arnold's lifelong need for validation and respect, born from family shame, made him vulnerable to praise from the enemy and bitter about perceived disrespect from his own countrymen.

### Revolutionary Hero to Strategic Genius
Arnold's military brilliance was undeniable. He organized the capture of Fort Ticonderoga with Ethan Allen, led an epic 300-mile expedition through the Maine wilderness to attack Quebec, and constructed a makeshift fleet that delayed the British invasion from Canada. At Saratoga, he literally saved the Revolution by leading a desperate charge against British lines despite being wounded, turning the battle into America's first major victory that secured French support.

> "To the storming we must come."

**Quick Take**: Arnold's tactical genius and personal courage were crucial to American victory, making his later betrayal even more tragic—without him, the Revolution likely would have failed.

### The Road to Treason
Arnold's betrayal wasn't sudden but gradual, fed by accumulated grievances. After being severely wounded at Saratoga, he spent months recovering while Congress repeatedly passed him over for promotion, elevating less deserving officers. Appointed military governor of Philadelphia, he faced corruption charges for using military resources for personal profit. These humiliations, combined with his wife Peggy Shippen's Loyalist sympathies and connections to British Major John André, created the perfect storm for treason.

> "Delay worse than death."

**Quick Take**: Arnold's treason stemmed from a toxic mix of wounded pride, financial troubles, and feeling unappreciated by the very nation he'd nearly died to create.

### The West Point Conspiracy
The treason plot centered on West Point, the strategic fortress Washington called "the key to America." Arnold negotiated with British General Henry Clinton through Major John André to surrender the fort and its 3,000-man garrison for £20,000 (about $3 million today). The conspiracy unraveled when André was captured with incriminating documents hidden in his boot, leading to his execution as a spy and Arnold's escape to British lines.

> "Should we through your means possess ourselves of three thousand men and [West Point's] artillery and stores with magazine of provisions...the sum even of £20,000 should be paid you."

**Quick Take**: The West Point plot represents perhaps history's most infamous act of treason, where Arnold attempted to trade the military key to American independence for personal wealth.

### Legacy of Shame
Arnold spent his remaining years in England and Canada, largely shunned even by the British who had used him. He died in 1801 at age 60, "without notice" as one British paper reported, forever remembered not as the brilliant general who saved the Revolution, but as its most notorious traitor. Today, at the Saratoga battlefield where he achieved his greatest triumph, there stands only a monument to his wounded leg—no name, no likeness, just a boot.

> "Poor General Arnold has departed this world without notice."

**Quick Take**: Arnold's legacy shows how a single betrayal can erase a lifetime of achievement, serving as America's enduring symbol of treason.

## Takeaways

- **Character matters**: Even brilliant talent can be undone by poor character and unchecked ego
- **Recognition drives loyalty**: People need to feel valued; consistent neglect can breed resentment
- **Complex human nature**: Heroes and villains are often the same people viewed from different angles
- **Historical context matters**: Personal decisions always intersect with larger historical forces
- **Power of forgiveness**: Some betrayals are so profound they transcend forgiveness

## Questions to Consider

- Could America have won the Revolution without Arnold's early military contributions?
- How much responsibility did Congress and other leaders bear for pushing Arnold toward treason?
- Why do we remember Arnold only as a traitor rather than as both hero and traitor?
- What lessons does Arnold's story offer about leadership and recognition in modern organizations?
- Are there circumstances where treason could be justified, or is it always unforgivable?

## Conclusion

**Is this worth your time? Absolutely.** Sheinkin transforms what could have been dry historical biography into a gripping, thriller-like narrative that reads like fiction. This isn't just a story about one man's betrayal—it's a profound exploration of human psychology, the messy reality of revolution, and how greatness and infamy can coexist in the same person.

The biggest reason to read this book is its reminder that history's villains are rarely one-dimensional. Arnold was both the brilliant general who saved the Revolution and the traitor who tried to destroy it. Understanding this complexity makes us wiser about human nature, leadership, and the fine line between heroism and treachery. In an age of political polarization and easy judgment, Arnold's story challenges us to see the humanity behind our historical villains while still holding them accountable for their choices.
