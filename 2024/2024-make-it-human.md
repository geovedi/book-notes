# Make It Human
**Authors**: Sarah McLellan | **Year**: 2024
**Category**: Business & Finance

## Summary
"Make It Human" presents a compelling case for revolutionizing our approach to work by placing human needs at the center of organizational design. Sarah McLellan, a business psychologist, argues that despite technological advancement and productivity gains, global workplace unhappiness has reached unprecedented levels. The book provides a practical framework for building humane workplaces where people can thrive while delivering exceptional business results.

The book is essential reading for leaders, managers, and HR professionals who recognize that traditional management approaches are failing. Readers will gain practical insights into identifying and fixing "culture cracks," building trust-based relationships, and creating environments where both people and businesses flourish. McLellan combines research-based frameworks with real-world examples to show how human-centered organizations outperform their competitors through employee engagement, innovation, and resilience.

## Core Insights

### The Crisis of Workplace Humanity
Modern workplaces have become increasingly dehumanized despite advances in technology and management theory. McLellan reveals that 75% of people report experiencing toxic cultures, while global unhappiness has reached historic levels. The problem stems from an outdated leadership model where ego-driven executives rule, managers remain crushed in the middle, and employees are treated as afterthoughts rather than the organization's greatest asset.

> "The world of work is ripe for revolution. Make It Human provides the path forwards, illustrating how we have been overlooking our greatest gift and opportunity – to make it human."

**Quick Take**: The most valuable competitive advantage in today's economy isn't technology or strategy—it's creating a genuinely human experience of work that attracts and retains talent.

### The Ten Culture Cracks
McLellan identifies ten warning signs that indicate organizational culture is under strain. These include the obsession with busyness (where meeting time equals perceived success), double standards where leaders don't follow rules they create, and the "frozen core" where middle managers become paralyzed by bureaucracy. Other cracks include damaging silence, lack of psychological safety, and environments where asking questions or showing vulnerability feels risky.

> "The standard you walk past is the standard you accept."

**Quick Take**: Culture cracks start small and expand silently until they create catastrophic failure. Every employee plays a role in either preventing or perpetuating these cracks.

### The Body-Mind-Soul Framework
The book presents a comprehensive model for building human organizations with three interconnected components: solid organizational foundations (the body), objective tools and systems for decision-making (the mind), and meaningful manager-employee relationships (the heart and soul). When these elements work together, organizations develop a natural rhythm where trust, communication, and opportunities pulse throughout the company.

> "It starts with the body, with solid organisational foundations. The second step is to fuel the organisational mind with objective tools, data and systems to guide better decisions and connections. This next step focuses on the heart and soul – the relationship between managers and individuals."

**Quick Take**: Get the manager-employee relationship right, and the blood pumps effortlessly around the company, creating networks of trust that fuel engagement and innovation.

### The Evolution of Management
Management has transformed dramatically over three decades. In the 1990s, managers controlled information and career development. The 2000s brought digitization and loss of control. Today, successful managers focus on optimizing human experiences through empathy, coaching, and empowerment. The shift from "human resources" to "human experiences" represents a fundamental reimagining of management's purpose and value.

> "Today, managers are in the business of optimising human experiences to enable growth, and this requires an entirely different profile for success."

**Quick Take**: Traditional management expertise is becoming obsolete; the most valuable managers now excel at understanding people and facilitating growth rather than controlling processes and information.

### Why AI Cannot Replace Human Managers
While technology can enhance management capabilities, McLellan argues against fully replacing managers with AI systems. Human managers provide essential elements that algorithms cannot replicate: genuine empathy during difficult situations, nuanced understanding of individual motivations, personalized career guidance, and the kind of supportive relationships that build lasting trust and loyalty.

> "You can see the algorithm strain. Blake searches through preloaded scenarios, shares standard advice for dealing with difficult people or managing stress, but undoubtedly it falls short. There are no reassuringly supportive words, change in tone, eye contact or advice offered from her own experiences."

**Quick Take**: Technology should augment human management, not replace it. The most valuable managerial skills are fundamentally human and cannot be effectively automated.

## Action Items
- **Conduct a Culture Audit**: Regularly assess your organization for the ten culture cracks McLellan identifies, including busyness obsession, double standards, and communication breakdowns
- **Redesign Manager Selection**: Prioritize emotional intelligence and people skills over technical expertise when promoting to management roles
- **Invest in Management Development**: Provide training focused on empathy, coaching, and building trust rather than traditional command-and-control approaches
- **Create Feedback Loops**: Establish safe channels for employees to raise concerns and ask questions without fear of retribution
- **Model from the Top**: Ensure leaders consistently demonstrate the behaviors they expect from others, particularly regarding work-life balance and inclusive practices

## Questions to Consider
- Which of the ten culture cracks exist in your organization, and what small actions could you take to start addressing them?
- How would your workplace change if managers were primarily evaluated on their ability to build trust and develop people rather than hit performance targets?
- What would it look like to optimize for human experiences rather than efficiency or productivity metrics alone?
- How can you better balance technological tools with human judgment in your management approach?
- What would need to change for your organization to measure success in terms of employee thriving rather than just business results?

## Conclusion
"Make It Human" is worth your time if you're a leader who suspects there must be a better way to work than the current state of burnout, disengagement, and cultural toxicity. McLellan provides both the diagnostic tools to identify organizational problems and practical frameworks for building more humane workplaces. The book's biggest strength lies in its recognition that putting humans first isn't just morally right—it's also the smartest business strategy in an economy where talent, innovation, and adaptability determine competitive advantage.

The most compelling reason to read this book is its actionable roadmap for creating workplaces where people actually want to work, rather than places they endure. In an era of unprecedented employee turnover and widespread workplace dissatisfaction, "Make It Human" offers both a diagnosis of our current crisis and a prescription for building organizations that bring out the best in people while delivering exceptional results.
