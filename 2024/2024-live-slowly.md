# Live Slowly
**Authors**: Jodi H. Grubbs | **Year**: 2024
**Category**: Spirituality & Religion

## Summary
Live Slowly is a gentle invitation to step off the treadmill of hustle culture and embrace a more sustainable, intentional pace of life. Drawing from her experience growing up on island time and navigating profound personal tragedy—including her husband's accident and subsequent death—Grubbs offers eighteen foundational "slow living shifts" that help readers recover their precious lives from the tyranny of busyness. The book weaves together personal stories, spiritual wisdom, and practical rhythms to help exhausted souls find rest, purpose, and deeper connection with God and others.

This guide is perfect for burned-out Christians, people-pleasers, and anyone feeling trapped by the cult of speed who longs to reclaim their time and energy. Readers will discover practical tools for setting boundaries, embracing their unique pace, and finding sacred moments in ordinary life.

## Core Insights

### The Problem with Hustle Culture
Modern life has conditioned us to believe that faster is better and that our worth is measured by productivity. Grubbs reveals how this pace is not only unsustainable but spiritually damaging, preventing us from experiencing God's presence and genuine community. The constant need to be busy is often "a trauma response and fear-based distraction from what you'd be forced to acknowledge and feel if you slowed down."

> "Feeling the need to be busy all the time is a trauma response and fear-based distraction from what you'd be forced to acknowledge and feel if you slowed down."
> _Original: "Feeling the need to be busy all the time is a trauma response and fear-based distraction from what you'd be forced to acknowledge and feel if you slowed down."_
**Quick Take**: Your constant busyness might be protecting you from deeper truths that need your attention.

### The Cost of Speed
Thoreau's wisdom opens the book: "The cost of a thing is the amount of life which is required to be exchanged for it." Every commitment, every rushed moment, costs us precious life energy that we cannot reclaim. Grubbs challenges readers to consider what they're exchanging for their current pace and whether the trade is worth it.

> "The cost of a thing is the amount of life which is required to be exchanged for it."
> _Original: "The cost of a thing is the amount of life which is required to be exchanged for it."_
**Quick Take**: Before saying yes to anything new, ask what portion of your life you're truly trading away.

### The Gift of Acceptance
Sometimes seasons of forced slowing—through illness, loss, or unexpected circumstances—become unexpected gifts. Grubbs shares how her husband's devastating accident and later death forced her to slow down and ultimately discover deeper trust in God. These unwelcome pauses often reveal what we've been missing in our rush.

> "Sometimes life's biggest waves knock us down and sometimes they clean us up."
> _Original: "Sometimes life's biggest waves knock us down and sometimes they clean us up."_
**Quick Take**: Your most difficult seasons may be preparing you for your most profound growth.

### Finding Your Unique Pace
Twenty percent of people are highly sensitive persons who function best when they embrace slower living. Rather than fighting against your natural rhythm, Grubbs encourages embracing your unique design and capacity. This includes recognizing your limits, honoring your energy cycles, and accepting that you weren't meant to keep up with everyone else's pace.

> "Our bodies and our minds were not meant to keep up this wild pace. What we desperately need is a shift, a collective exhale as we find our way again."
> _Original: "Our bodies and our minds were not meant to keep up this wild pace. What we desperately need is a shift, a collective exhale as we find our way again."_
**Quick Take**: Your natural pace isn't a weakness to overcome but a gift to embrace.

### The Practice of Savoring
Slow living isn't just about doing less—it's about being more present to what matters. Grubbs teaches the art of savoring: nibbling chocolate slowly, smelling honeysuckle blooms, lingering over coffee, and truly tasting life's moments rather than rushing through them.

> "So often I spin through life caught in the web of my self-made narrative. And I wonder. How often do I walk the streets of my own life, eyes shut to the sweetness?"
> _Original: "So often I spin through life caught in the web of my self-made narrative. And I wonder. How often do I walk the streets of my own life, eyes shut to the sweetness?"_
**Quick Take**: The sweetest moments of life are often found in the smallest, slowest experiences.

### Boundaries as Sacred Self-Care
Setting boundaries isn't selfish—it's essential for sustainable ministry and life. Grubbs provides practical guidance for saying no graciously, protecting your energy, and creating space for what truly matters. This includes boundary-setting even in faith contexts where expectations can be especially high.

> "This is gracious boundary setting. This is ordinary empowerment and a part of communicating in life so that others don't sabotage your serious attempts to live slowly."
> _Original: "This is gracious boundary setting. This is ordinary empowerment and a part of communicating in life so that others don't sabotage your serious attempts to live slowly."_
**Quick Take**: Your boundaries protect not just your energy but your calling and relationships.

### The Ancient Paths
Jeremiah 6:16 serves as a guiding scripture: "Stand at the crossroads, and look; ask for the ancient paths, ask where the good way is, and walk in it, and you will find rest for your souls." Grubbs frames slow living as returning to ancient, sustainable rhythms rather than adopting a modern trend.

> "Stand at the crossroads, and look; ask for the ancient paths, ask where the good way is, and walk in it, and you will find rest for your souls."
> _Original: "Stand at the crossroads, and look; ask for the ancient paths, ask where the good way is, and walk in it, and you will find rest for your souls."_
**Quick Take**: The wisdom you need for slower living may be found in returning to ancient practices rather than new techniques.

## The 18 Slow Living Shifts

1. **Exhaling** - Intentionally releasing the breath you've been holding
2. **Acceptance** - Embracing seasons rather than fighting against them
3. **Restoration and Reflection** - Creating space for healing and insight
4. **Embracing Your Uniqueness** - Honoring your God-given design and pace
5. **Understanding Capacity** - Recognizing your true limits and boundaries
6. **Awakening to Beauty** - Noticing God's presence in ordinary moments
7. **Sustainable Rhythms** - Establishing life-giving patterns and practices
8. **Soul Care Practices** - Spiritual disciplines that nourish your inner life
9. **Finding Sanctuary** - Creating spaces and times for true rest
10. **Savoring** - Fully experiencing present moments rather than rushing through
11. **Seeking Solitude** - Finding renewal in quiet companionship with God
12. **Lingering with Intention** - Choosing presence over productivity
13. **Being Present to Others** - Offering focused attention in relationships
14. **Being Empowered** - Taking ownership of your pace and choices
15. **Boundaries in Faith Settings** - Protecting your spiritual health
16. **Expectancy** - Watching for God's movement in slow seasons
17. **Releasing** - Letting go of what weighs you down
18. **Resting in Being Held** - Trusting God's sustaining presence

## Action Items
- **Practice Strategic Pausing**: Build three 5-minute pauses into your daily schedule for intentional breathing and presence
- **Conduct a Pace Audit**: Identify where you're living faster than your natural rhythm and make one small adjustment this week
- **Set One Boundary**: Identify one commitment or expectation that's draining you and create a plan to address it
- **Start Savoring Practice**: Choose one daily activity (like drinking coffee or tea) and do it with full presence and attention
- **Create Sanctuary Space**: Designate one physical space or time block as sacred, untouchable time for rest and renewal

## Questions to Consider
- What would your life look like if you embraced your natural pace rather than fighting against it?
- What are you afraid might happen if you slowed down?
- Where have you experienced unexpected gifts in seasons of forced slowing?
- What one small shift could create more space for what truly matters?
- How might your relationships change if you were more present rather than productive?

## Conclusion
Live Slowly is more than a productivity guide—it's a spiritual invitation to reclaim your humanity from the machine of modern busyness. Grubbs offers a gentle, compassionate roadmap for exhausted souls who sense they were made for more than perpetual motion. Her island wisdom, biblical insights, and practical rhythms create a compelling case for slower living as both spiritual formation and sustainable self-care.

**Is this worth your time?** Absolutely if you're exhausted from trying to keep up, burned out from religious performance, or sensing there must be a better way to live. The book's greatest strength is its gentle, non-judgmental approach that meets readers right where they are and offers hope rather than shame.

**The biggest reason to read it**: You'll walk away believing that your natural pace isn't a problem to fix but a gift to embrace, and you'll have practical tools for creating a more sustainable, soul-nourishing rhythm of life.
