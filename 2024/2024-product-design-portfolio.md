# Product Design Portfolio Final Final
**Authors**: Fedor Shkliarau | **Year**: 2024

## Summary
This book is a comprehensive guide for creating product design portfolios that land dream jobs. Written by an experienced designer who has navigated various career stages from internships to freelance work, it provides a practical, actionable framework for building portfolios that effectively showcase skills and attract hiring managers.

The book addresses one of the most challenging yet rewarding projects in a designer's career: creating a compelling portfolio. It focuses on the strategic thinking behind portfolio creation rather than just visual design, helping designers understand what hiring managers and recruiters actually look for when evaluating candidates. The approach is designed to work for product designers, UX designers, interaction designers, and experience designers at all career levels.

## Core Insights

### The RRR Framework: Relevant, Remarkable, Recent
The book introduces the core "RRR" framework for selecting case studies that make the biggest impact. Your portfolio shouldn't just show good work—it should feature work that is RRR. **Relevant** means the work you've done connects to what you want to do next. **Remarkable** means the work stands out and tells a compelling story. **Recent** means showcasing work from the last 3-5 years that demonstrates your current capabilities.

> "Any case study in your portfolio shouldn't be just good, it should be RRR."
**Quick Take**: Quality over quantity—three exceptional RRR case studies are better than many mediocre ones.

### Start with Your Destination
Before building your portfolio, define what kind of work you want to do and who your target audience is. Your portfolio isn't just a showcase of everything you've ever done—it's a strategic tool designed to attract specific opportunities. Whether you're targeting startups, big tech companies, or freelance clients, your portfolio should speak directly to their needs and demonstrate the exact skills they're looking for.

**Quick Take**: Know your target before you build your weapon.

### Case Study Structure that Converts
The book provides a systematic approach to crafting case studies that actually convince hiring managers. Each case study should follow a clear structure that demonstrates your process, decision-making, and impact. The key is to "place the bottom line up front"—start with the results and impact, then explain how you got there. Show enough process to demonstrate your thinking but not so much that it becomes overwhelming.

**Quick Take**: Start with results, then reveal your process.

### Portfolio Design Principles
Your portfolio design should prioritize first impressions and easy navigation. Keep the architecture simple, make it easily navigable, and tease case studies effectively. Don't just link project cards to company websites—lead visitors to contact you. The design should serve the content, not overshadow it, with a focus on making it easy for busy hiring managers to quickly understand your value.

**Quick Take**: Design for scanners, not readers.

### Personality and Authenticity
Your portfolio should reflect who you are as a designer and person. Write an authentic introduction, express yourself naturally in case studies, and create an "About" page that makes you relatable. Recruiters and hiring managers aren't just looking for skills—they're looking for someone they want to work with. Letting your personality shine through helps you stand out in a sea of generic portfolios.

**Quick Take**: Your portfolio is your story, not just your work.

### Time Management and Consistency
Creating a portfolio is challenging precisely because it's personal work with no external deadlines. The book emphasizes setting aside dedicated time, staying focused, striking while the iron is hot (working when motivation strikes), and getting feedback and support. This approach helps overcome the procrastination and perfectionism that often derail portfolio projects.

**Quick Take**: Treat your portfolio like any other project—with deadlines and accountability.

## Action Items
- **Define your target**: Clearly identify the type of work and companies you want to attract before building your portfolio
- **Select 3 RRR case studies**: Choose projects that are Relevant, Remarkable, and Recent to your career goals
- **Create a case study template**: Develop a repeatable structure for presenting your work consistently
- **Design for first impressions**: Ensure your portfolio makes an immediate positive impact on busy hiring managers
- **Schedule portfolio time**: Block dedicated time in your calendar and treat portfolio work like any other professional project
- **Get feedback early**: Share your work with trusted peers and mentors to avoid working in isolation

## Questions to Consider
- What specific type of product design work do you want to be doing in 1-3 years?
- Which of your past projects best demonstrates the skills required for your target roles?
- If a hiring manager only looked at one case study, which one would make them want to hire you?
- How can you structure your portfolio to make the strongest impression in the first 30 seconds?
- What aspects of your personality and working style should come through in your portfolio?

## Conclusion
This book is worth your time if you're struggling with creating a portfolio that actually gets you interviews. The biggest strength is its focus on strategy over aesthetics—teaching you what hiring managers actually care about rather than just how to make things look good. The RRR framework alone is worth the price of admission, providing a clear method for selecting which projects to feature.

The book avoids one-size-fits-all prescriptions and instead provides a flexible framework that adapts to different career stages and goals. Whether you're a junior designer just starting out or a senior designer looking to level up, the principles here will help you create a portfolio that works as a career advancement tool rather than just a gallery of past work.

The combination of practical frameworks, real-world examples, and psychological insights (like dealing with perfectionism and procrastination) makes this one of the most actionable portfolio guides available. If you implement even half of what's in this book, you'll have a portfolio that stands out and, more importantly, lands you the opportunities you're seeking.
