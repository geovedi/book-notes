# Italian Blood
**Original Title**: Sangue italiano. Delitti, criminalità e violenza pubblica dal 1860 a oggi
**Author**: Roberto Casalini | **Year**: 2024
**Category**: History & Biography

## Summary
"Italian Blood" is a sweeping chronicle of Italy's criminal history from unification in 1860 to the present day, collecting over 150 true crime stories that reveal the nation's dark underbelly. The book examines everything from the brutal brigandage wars that followed unification, through the rise of organized crime (Mafia, Camorra, 'Ndrangheta), to political assassinations, terrorism, and modern-day violence. Casalini presents these stories not as isolated incidents, but as interconnected threads that form an alternative history of Italy—one marked by violence, corruption, and a persistent tension between state power and criminal enterprise.

This book offers readers a unique perspective on Italian history by examining the nation through its most violent moments, making it ideal for history enthusiasts, true crime readers, and anyone interested in understanding the complex relationship between Italian society, politics, and criminality.

## Core Insights

### The Violence of Italian Unification
The birth of Italy was not peaceful but bloody. The "war for the South" (1861-1870) was Italy's first civil war, with 120,000 soldiers deployed against briganti and peasant revolts. The military response was brutal and illegal—villages burned, mass arrests, summary executions. General Cialdini received orders to "exterminate them all." This established a pattern of the state using extralegal violence that would recur throughout Italian history.

> "Spesso tra 'l palazzo e la piazza è una nebbia sì folta o uno muro sì grosso che, non vi penetrando l'occhio degli uomini, tanto sa el popolo di quello che fa chi governa o della ragione perché lo fa, quanto delle cose che fanno in India."
> _Original: "Spesso tra 'l palazzo e la piazza è una nebbia sì folta o uno muro sì grosso che, non vi penetrando l'occhio degli uomini, tanto sa el popolo di quello che fa chi governa o della ragione perché lo fa, quanto delle cose che fanno in India."_
**Quick Take**: The gap between Italian rulers and citizens has always been vast, with violence serving as the preferred method of bridging it.

### Crime as Politics, Politics as Crime
From the earliest days of the kingdom, crime and politics intertwined. The Mafia emerged as "instrumentum regni"—instrument of power—providing vote procurement, protection, and order that the state couldn't or wouldn't deliver. By the 1860s, the police chief of Naples was the most renowned camorrista. This symbiotic relationship between criminal organizations and political power became a defining feature of Italian democracy.

> "La mafia è tante cose: imprenditoria della violenza e fattore di mobilità sociale, con la ferocia come capitale da investire."
> _Original: "La mafia è tante cose: imprenditoria della violenza e fattore di mobilità sociale, con la ferocia come capitale da investire."_
**Quick Take**: In Italy, violence has always been a viable business model and career path.

### The Fragility of Italian Democracy
Italian democracy has been cyclically fragile since its birth. The book documents four major periods when democracy was suspended or challenged: the brigandage wars (1860s), Fascism (1920s-1943), the "Years of Lead" terrorism (1969-1980s), and the rise of separatist movements. Each crisis revealed how quickly democratic norms could be abandoned for "states of exception" and emergency powers.

> "La nostra è, fin dagli albori, una democrazia fragile—forse tutte lo sono, l'assalto del 2021 al Campidoglio degli Stati Uniti ce lo ricorda—che a intervalli ciclici dubita di sé stessa, esposta a strappi e torsioni che dall'alto e dal basso la mettono in discussione con la violenza."
> _Original: "La nostra è, fin dagli albori, una democrazia fragile—forse tutte lo sono, l'assalto del 2021 al Campidoglio degli Stati Uniti ce lo ricorda—che a intervalli ciclici dubita di sé stessa, esposta a strappi e torsioni che dall'alto e dal basso la mettono in discussione con la violenza."_
**Quick Take**: Italian democracy has always been just one crisis away from suspension.

### Family as Both Haven and Hell
The book reveals how Italian families incubate violence. The "inferno delle famiglie" where affection coexists with tenacious grudges and private omertà mirrors the national pattern. The infamous Murri and Nigrisoli cases show how rarely families denounced their own—only in two documented cases did family members turn in murderers among them.

> "Il variamente assortito inferno delle famiglie, sede di affetti ma anche di rancori tenaci, dove spesso si giustificano i fatti di sangue con un'omertà privata amorale quanto quella collettiva."
> _Original: "Il variamente assortito inferno delle famiglie, sede di affetti ma anche di rancori tenaci, dove spesso si giustificano i fatti di sangue con un'omertà privata amorale quanto quella collettiva."_
**Quick Take**: The family structure that protects its members also protects their crimes.

### The Persistence of "Brutalità Primigenia"
Despite modernization, Italy's "primordial brutality" continuously resurfaces. The book traces how medieval violence patterns persist in contemporary Italy, from honor killings to modern terrorism. Casalini describes civilized coexistence as "just a thin veneer that quickly peels away, a thin skin enclosing a poisonous pulp."

> "L'individualismo quasi autistico che privilegia il particulare senza mai vedere il mondo. L'avidità come movente di molti delitti che dicono passionali."
> _Original: "L'individualismo quasi autistico che privilegia il particulare senza mai vedere il mondo. L'avidità come movente di molti delitti che dicono passionali."_
**Quick Take**: Modern Italy is just a thin layer of civilization over ancient violence.

## Key Takeaways

- **Violence as Political Tool**: Italian democracy has consistently used emergency powers, military justice, and extralegal violence to maintain control
- **Criminal-Political Symbiosis**: Organized crime has always been integrated into Italian power structures
- **The North-South Divide**: The brutal suppression of Southern resistance established patterns that continue to shape Italian politics
- **Family Protection**: The Italian family system both incubates violence and protects criminals from justice
- **Cyclical Crises**: Italian democracy faces existential threats every few decades

## Questions to Consider

1. How has the legacy of brigandage and its brutal suppression shaped Southern Italian identity and its relationship with the state?
2. What makes Italian democracy particularly vulnerable to periodic crises that justify suspending democratic norms?
3. Why has the relationship between criminal organizations and political power been so durable throughout Italian history?
4. How does the pattern of family protection of criminals reflect broader Italian cultural attitudes toward authority and justice?
5. What does the persistence of violence throughout Italian history reveal about the nation's unresolved tensions between modernity and tradition?

## Conclusion

"Italian Blood" is essential reading for understanding Italy's history from beneath—the view from the gutters, back alleys, and blood-stained streets rather than palaces and parliament chambers. Casalini's comprehensive collection reveals how violence has been not an aberration but a constant thread in Italian national life.

The book's power lies in its cumulative effect—story after story, murder after murder, building into a devastating portrait of a nation where the line between legitimate power and criminal enterprise has always been blurry. This isn't just true crime; it's an alternative history of Italy that explains much about the country's persistent political corruption, organized crime problems, and periodic democratic crises.

**Worth your time?** Absolutely, for anyone seeking to understand Italy beyond the Renaissance art and romantic landscapes. This is the hidden history that makes contemporary Italy comprehensible—the violence that birthed the nation never really ended, it just changed forms. The biggest reason to read it is to understand why Italy remains trapped in cycles of corruption and violence that seem inexplicable from conventional histories.
