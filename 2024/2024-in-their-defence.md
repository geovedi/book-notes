# In Their Defence: A Guide to Protecting Children in the Justice System
**Authors**: Aika Stephenson | **Year**: 2024
**Category**: Philosophy & Critical Thinking

## Summary
"In Their Defence" is an eye-opening insider's account of the youth justice system written by Aika Stephenson, a solicitor and co-founder of Just for Kids Law. Drawing from her personal experience growing up with a father who was frequently incarcerated and her professional work representing thousands of young people, Stephenson reveals how the legal system often fails children and provides essential guidance for parents and caregivers. The book exposes systemic issues while offering practical advice for navigating police stations, courts, and the complex world of youth justice.

Parents, educators, and anyone concerned with children's rights will gain invaluable insights into how quickly ordinary children can become entangled in the criminal justice system, and what they can do to protect them. This is essential reading for understanding why no child is truly immune from legal trouble and how to prepare for that possibility.

## Core Insights

### The Vulnerability of Every Child
Contrary to popular belief, no child is immune from contact with the criminal justice system. Stephenson demonstrates through numerous case studies that children from all backgrounds—middle-class, stable homes included—can find themselves facing police questioning and criminal charges. The factors that increase risk include mental health issues, speech difficulties, living in care, school exclusion, ethnic minority background, and poverty. However, even children without these risk factors regularly encounter the system.

> "No child is ever immune to being caught up in the law, whether as a defendant, witness, or victim, so being armed with knowledge ahead of time is a clear advantage."

**Quick Take**: Assume your child could potentially face legal situations and educate yourself and them accordingly, regardless of their background or upbringing.

### The Reality of Police Interviews
The book exposes how police interviews are designed to secure convictions, not necessarily to discover truth. Stephenson reveals the tactics used, including incomplete disclosure of information to lawyers, pressure tactics, and the use of complex legal language that children cannot understand. She explains why "no comment" interviews are sometimes advisable and how prepared statements can protect vulnerable young people.

> "At times the officer can be obstructive to such a degree that I literally have to write 'Refused' next to every question I ask. This clash of conduct and purpose often surprises those not involved in law, as they assume we are all working together to find out the truth."

**Quick Take**: Always ensure proper legal representation before any police interview and understand that the police are not your child's friend in this situation.

### Joint Enterprise: The Danger of Guilt by Association
One of the most alarming revelations is the widespread use of "joint enterprise" laws, where children can be convicted of crimes committed by others simply by being present. Stephenson explains how teenage brain development makes it nearly impossible for adolescents to foresee consequences or resist peer pressure, yet the law holds them to adult standards of responsibility.

> "The doctrine of joint enterprise implies that we are all responsible for what people around us do... Add these two things together for young people, and in many ways it really is a recipe for disaster."

**Quick Take**: Teach children to remove themselves immediately from any situation where trouble seems likely, as mere presence can lead to criminal liability.

### The School-to-Prison Pipeline
The book reveals how increasingly schools are deferring to police for matters that were previously handled internally, leading to unnecessary criminalization of children. Cases include students arrested for forgetting craft knives in pencil cases or borrowing scissors to cut hair. Stephenson argues this creates a generation of children with criminal records for normal childish mistakes.

> "The arrival of police officers actually based in schools, the Safer Schools Officers (SSOs), has taken police involvement with children to a whole new level. SSOs deal with anything from being the point of contact for teachers, to handling the aftermath of a playground fight."

**Quick Take**: Question school policies that involve police for minor disciplinary issues and advocate for handling such matters internally whenever appropriate.

### Understanding Adolescent Brain Development
Stephenson incorporates scientific research showing that adolescent brains, particularly the frontal lobes responsible for impulse control and consequential thinking, don't fully develop until around age 25. This biological reality means teenagers are genuinely incapable of the same level of foresight and judgment as adults, yet the legal system treats them as fully responsible.

> "The areas of the brain known as the frontal lobes and prefrontal cortex are the parts that control impulses, regulate aggression, calm emotions, encourage consequential and abstract thinking, and allow reasoned, logical and rational decision-making processes. However, they are the slowest areas of the brain to develop during the adolescent years."

**Quick Take**: Adjust your expectations of teenage judgment and provide additional guidance and supervision based on their developmental stage, not just their chronological age.

## Action Items
- Keep a lawyer's contact information readily available and teach your children when and how to use it
- Review your child's school policies regarding police involvement and disciplinary procedures
- Educate children about joint enterprise laws and the importance of removing themselves from potentially problematic situations
- Understand that appropriate adults in police interviews can be compelled to testify—privilege only applies to lawyers
- Prepare children for the possibility of police encounters while maintaining trust in the justice system
- Advocate for school policies that handle minor infractions internally rather than involving law enforcement

## Questions to Consider
- How would you react if your child was arrested for something you consider normal childhood behavior?
- Does your child's school have police officers on site, and how are they used in disciplinary matters?
- What conversations have you had with your children about their rights if stopped by police?
- Are you aware of the specific laws that could affect your child based on their age and activities?
- How can we balance keeping children safe with avoiding unnecessary criminalization?

## Conclusion
"In Their Defence" is a crucial wake-up call for parents, educators, and anyone who cares about children's welfare. Stephenson's unique perspective as both someone who experienced the justice system as a child and now works within it provides invaluable insights that could save families from devastating legal consequences. The book succeeds in demystifying the youth justice system while providing practical guidance that every parent needs.

The biggest reason to read this book is its urgent message that preparing your child for potential legal encounters is now as essential as teaching them to look both ways before crossing the street. In an era where schools increasingly defer to police and social media can create evidence of normal teenage misbehavior, understanding the justice system is no longer optional—it's a fundamental part of responsible parenting.

While the stories can be alarming and the systemic failures frustrating, Stephenson also offers hope through practical advice and her work at Just for Kids Law. This isn't just a critique of a broken system; it's a survival guide for navigating it. Any parent who reads this book will be better equipped to protect their children and advocate for necessary reforms in the youth justice system.
