# Living the Asian Century: An Undiplomatic Memoir
**Authors**: Kishore Mahbubani | **Year**: 2024
**Category**: History & Biography 

## Summary
Kishore Mahbubani's memoir chronicles his extraordinary journey from a childhood of extreme poverty in Singapore to becoming one of Asia's most influential diplomats and thinkers. Born to a dysfunctional family with a gambling-addicted father who spent time in prison, Mahbubani rose through the power of education to serve as Singapore's Ambassador to the UN and President of the UN Security Council. The book offers a unique perspective on the shift from Western to Asian dominance in global affairs, drawing from his 33 years in Singapore's diplomatic service and his front-row seat to international power politics.

This memoir is essential reading for anyone interested in understanding the Asian century from someone who has lived through poverty, witnessed the rise of Asian economies, and participated in the highest levels of global governance. Mahbubani combines personal storytelling with sharp geopolitical analysis, revealing how power truly operates in international relations.

## Core Insights

### From Poverty to Plenty
The book opens with a vivid account of growing up in extreme poverty in 1950s Singapore. Mahbubani's family was so poor that he was placed on a special feeding program in school, where underweight children shared milk from a single ladle. His father's gambling and drinking led to imprisonment, forcing his mother to raise four children alone. Yet Singapore's emerging good governance provided escape routes—bursaries, scholarships, and quality education—that enabled him to break the poverty cycle.

> "Therefore, Singapore played a key role in my ability to escape from the clutches of poverty and enjoy a rich life of diplomacy and learning."

**Quick Take**: Good governance and education are the most powerful tools for breaking intergenerational poverty.

### The Power of Education
Mahbubani's transformation began when he discovered the Joo Chiat Public Library as a child. Despite being ridiculed by peers for reading, he became a voracious reader. At the University of Singapore, he fell in love with philosophy, which "lit fires in his brain" rather than merely filling a bucket. This philosophical training equipped him to understand Western civilization's dominance while developing the confidence to challenge it.

> "Plutarch was right—education isn't about filling a bucket; it's about lighting fires."

**Quick Take**: Education that teaches critical thinking and intellectual independence is more valuable than rote learning.

### How Global Power Really Works
Serving on the UN Security Council from 2001-2002 taught Mahbubani the most important lesson of his career: in international relations, power always trumps principles. He witnessed firsthand how the United States could pressure the UN Secretariat to remove paragraphs from reports, how Britain abandoned its commitment to the International Criminal Court to please America, and how the five permanent members operated as "members" while the ten elected members were merely "visitors."

> "After twenty-six months on the council, I came to the conclusion that power always trumps ethical principles."

**Quick Take**: International institutions serve the interests of powerful nations, not universal principles.

### The Asian Century is Real
Mahbubani documents China's strategic focus on long-term development (like joining the WTO in 2001) while America was distracted by short-term conflicts like the Iraq War. When China joined the WTO, its economy was eight times smaller than America's; by 2022, it was only 1.4 times smaller. This represents the biggest geopolitical shift of our lifetime, yet Western leaders failed to recognize its significance.

> "When future historians reflect on the first two decades of the twenty-first century, they will surely note that the strategic minds of the American establishment were focused on short-term issues, like the Iraq war, while the strategic minds of the Chinese establishment were focused on long-term trends, like China's admission into the WTO."

**Quick Take**: China's patient, strategic approach to development is outpacing America's short-term focus on military conflicts.

### Cultural Self-Confidence is Transformative
The book contrasts the India of 1969, where few believed in their country's future, with the India of 2023, where young entrepreneurs brim with confidence and optimism. This explosion of cultural self-confidence across Asian societies represents the most profound shift in global psychology in centuries.

> "The explosion of cultural self-confidence among many Asian societies has been a transformative and joyous event to see and participate in."

**Quick Take**: Economic success combined with cultural pride creates unstoppable momentum.

## Conclusion

This memoir is worth your time because it offers a rare combination of personal inspiration and geopolitical insight. Mahbubani's life story demonstrates how education and good governance can transform lives, while his diplomatic experience reveals the uncomfortable truths about how global power operates. The biggest reason to read this book is to understand the Asian century from someone who has experienced it from all angles—as a poor child, a rising diplomat, and a respected international thinker.

The book's greatest strength is its authenticity. Mahbubani doesn't hide his biases or his mistakes, making his insights more credible than those of armchair commentators. His journey from colonial subject to confident Asian intellectual mirrors the broader transformation of our world. For anyone trying to understand where global power is heading in the 21st century, this book is essential reading.
