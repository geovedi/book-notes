# The Manager's Guide to Coaching for Change
**Authors**: John L. Bennett | **Year**: 2024 

## Summary
"The Manager's Guide to Coaching for Change" is a comprehensive resource that equips managers with the essential mindset and skills to coach team members effectively through organizational change. Bennett presents coaching as both a skillset and a role that transcends traditional management approaches, emphasizing discovery, partnership, and empowerment over directive leadership. The book bridges the gap between professional coaching techniques and practical managerial application, offering a structured approach to developing employee capabilities, improving performance, and supporting transformational change.

This guide is invaluable for managers, leaders, HR professionals, and anyone seeking to move beyond command-and-control leadership toward a more collaborative, development-focused approach. Readers will gain a complete framework for coaching conversations, six essential coaching skills, and practical strategies for handling the complexities of manager-employee coaching relationships.

## Core Insights

### The Foundation of Managerial Coaching
Coaching is a discovery process that focuses on the goals and agenda of the team member being coached, supporting learning, development, and performance within the organizational context. Unlike traditional management, coaching involves strengthening employees by encouraging them to find their solutions, engaging in open conversations, giving nonjudgmental feedback, and serving as a trusted sounding board. The roles of manager and coach are complementary—truly effective managers embed coaching into the heart of their management practice.

> "Coaching is a partnership with clients in a thought-provoking and creative process that inspires them to maximize their personal and professional potential."
> _Original: "Coaching is a partnership with clients in a thought-provoking and creative process that inspires them to maximize their personal and professional potential."_

**Quick Take**: Coaching differs from managing in that performance management is the manager's responsibility, while coaching is offered at the request and agreement of the team member. Coaching is optional for the employee—management is not.

### The Six-Step Coaching for Change Process
Effective coaching follows a structured six-step process that guides conversations and increases the likelihood of productive outcomes. The process begins with establishing the current situation and context, then moves to identifying needs and desired goals, gathering information, exploring possible actions, creating action plans, and concluding with summary and agreement. This process is dynamic—conversations flow back and forth through intermediate steps as the coach seeks to define and advance understanding of the team member's issues.

1. **Current Situation and Context**: Establish relationship and assess progress on previous action items
2. **Needs and Desired Goals**: Understand current reality and agree upon session goals
3. **Information Gathering**: Collect information that serves the team member's agenda
4. **Possible Actions**: Explore possibilities and establish evaluation criteria
5. **Action Planning**: Develop specific action steps with accountability and support needs
6. **Summary and Agreement**: Review insights and commitments, reinforce team member ownership

> "Effective coaching conversations begin with Step 1 and end with Step 6. However, they are dynamic, and conversations are likely to flow back and forth through the intermediate steps."

**Quick Take**: Having a process or model for coaching guides the conversation, offers credibility, provides predictability for the person being coached, and increases positive outcomes.

### The Six Essential Coaching Skills
Bennett identifies six critical coaching skills that are split between foundational and applied skills. Foundational skills (listening for understanding, asking powerful questions, and reframing) are used more frequently and serve as the basis for applied skills (providing feedback, providing insights, and developing support). These skills align with professional coaching standards and can be systematically developed and applied.

**Foundational Skills:**
- **Listening for Understanding**: Goes beyond passive hearing to comprehend the full message including emotions, context, and underlying concerns
- **Asking Powerful Questions**: Open-ended questions that stimulate reflection, challenge assumptions, and generate new possibilities
- **Reframing**: Helping team members see situations from different perspectives to create new awareness and options

**Applied Skills:**
- **Providing Feedback**: Specific, behavioral, and nonjudgmental information about performance or behavior
- **Providing Insights**: Sharing observations, perspectives, or patterns that the team member may not recognize
- **Developing Support**: Building partnerships and networks to sustain behavioral change

> "The roles of manager and coach are complementary, not contradictory. Truly effective managers and managerial leaders are those who embed effective coaching into the heart of their management practice."

**Quick Take**: These skills can be mapped to the six-step coaching process, with listening and questioning applied in every step, while other skills are used situationally based on team member needs.

### Building Trust and Psychological Safety
Trust is the foundation of effective coaching relationships. Bennett identifies three key components that build trust: ability (skills and competencies), benevolence (genuine care for the team member's interests), and integrity (adherence to acceptable principles and commitments). Psychological safety is equally crucial—team members must feel they can express themselves without fear of negative consequences to their self-image, status, or career.

To build trust in coaching relationships, managers should show selective vulnerability, acknowledge challenges, apologize when making errors, demonstrate consistency and dependability, and model honesty and integrity. However, coaches must be careful with self-disclosure—coaching is about the person being coached, not the coach.

> "Trust is the foundation for relationships. Managers and team members work together to optimize performance by developing the team member's professional, personal, and social capacities."

**Quick Take**: About 50% of people are less trusting by nature until there's reason to trust, while 50% are more trusting until given reason not to. Effective manager-coaches understand and work with both dispositions.

### Coaching Readiness and Success Factors
Not all team members are equally receptive to coaching. Success hinges on three critical factors: openness (accepting that change is needed and being receptive to the process), partnership (trust that allows honest communication), and engagement (commitment to invest time and energy in change). Additionally, agenda setting is vital—the team member's goals must remain the primary focus.

To assess coaching readiness, managers can ask about understanding of coaching, past experiences with coaches, motivation for seeking coaching, and willingness to try new behaviors. Highly coachable team members proactively seek feedback instead of waiting for it, implement feedback to drive development, and demonstrate openness to learning from mistakes.

> "Coaching in any setting can only be truly effective when the team member is willing and open to change."

**Quick Take**: Coaching should not be assumed—managers should ask team members directly if they want coaching, using questions like "Based on the feedback you received and your self-awareness, would you like me to coach you?"

## Action Items
- **Assess coaching readiness**: Before initiating coaching, evaluate team member openness, motivation, and willingness to engage in the process
- **Master the six-step process**: Use the structured coaching process to guide conversations while maintaining flexibility to move between steps as needed
- **Develop foundational skills**: Prioritize listening for understanding, asking powerful questions, and reframing as the core skills that enable effective coaching
- **Build trust intentionally**: Focus on demonstrating ability, benevolence, and integrity to create the psychological safety necessary for productive coaching relationships
- **Practice applied skills**: Learn to provide specific, behavioral feedback and relevant insights while helping team members build support networks
- **Separate managing from coaching**: Be clear about when you're acting as manager (directing, evaluating) versus coach (discovering, partnering)

## Questions to Consider

- How can you balance your responsibility for business results with the team member's agenda in coaching conversations?
