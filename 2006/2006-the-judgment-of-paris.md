# The Judgment of Paris
**Author**: Ross King | **Year**: 2006 | **Category**: Art & Creativity

## Summary
"The Judgment of Paris" tells the dramatic story of the revolutionary decade (1863-1874) that gave birth to Impressionism and transformed Paris from the center of academic art tradition into the birthplace of modern art. Through the compelling rivalry between two artists—the celebrated Ernest Meissonier and the revolutionary Édouard Manet—Ross King reveals how artistic rebellion, social upheaval, and creative vision collided to create one of art history's most significant transformations.

This captivating narrative brings to life the passionate struggle between tradition and innovation, showing how a group of rejected artists challenged the establishment and forever changed how we see the world. Perfect for art lovers, history enthusiasts, and anyone fascinated by how revolutions—both artistic and social—actually happen.

## The Story in a Nutshell

**The Setting**: Mid-19th century Paris, where the art establishment ruled with absolute authority. The Salon exhibition controlled careers, and academic precision was the only path to success.

**The Heroes**:
- **Ernest Meissonier**: The most famous artist of his time, a master of microscopic detail who painted Napoleon's conquests and became incredibly wealthy
- **Édouard Manet**: The rebellious visionary who dared to paint modern life with bold brushstrokes and controversial subjects

**The Conflict**: When Manet and his avant-garde friends challenged centuries of artistic tradition, they faced rejection, scandal, and ridicule—but ultimately changed art forever.

## Core Insights

### 1. The Two Competing Visions of Art

Meissonier represented academic perfection—painstakingly detailed historical scenes that took years to complete and sold for fortunes. His tiny "bonshommes" (little figures) of Napoleon-era cavaliers were so precise that critics joked he could "paint the Prophets of the Sistine Chapel on the setting of a ring." He was the establishment: wealthy, honored, and beloved by the public.

Manet represented artistic revolution—contemporary Parisian scenes with visible brushstrokes and provocative subjects. His "Le déjeuner sur l'herbe" (1863), featuring a nude woman picnicking with clothed men, caused a scandal that would make art history. Manet's work was personal, immediate, and unapologetically modern.

> "Manet painted his way out of a stagnant academic style and won the war for art's future."

**Quick Take**: Art's greatest revolutions come when artists choose to paint their own time rather than someone else's history.

### 2. The Salon des Refusés: The Tipping Point

In 1863, thousands of artists rejected by the official Salon protested so vigorously that Emperor Napoléon III created the Salon des Refusés (Salon of the Refused)—an alternative exhibition for rejected works. This unprecedented event gave Manet's controversial works public exposure and marked the first major crack in the academic monopoly.

The Salon des Refusés wasn't just an exhibition—it was a declaration that artistic authority could be challenged. It showed that when enough artists unite against an oppressive system, even emperors must listen.

**Quick Take**: Sometimes the most powerful revolutions begin with a single, well-publicized rejection.

### 3. War as Art's Catalyst

The Franco-Prussian War (1870-71) and the fall of Napoléon III created social chaos that mirrored the artistic upheaval already underway. As Paris was besieged and the old political order collapsed, so too did the old artistic order. The destruction made space for new beginnings—both politically and artistically.

Manet served in the National Guard, while Meissonier documented the conflict. Both witnessed firsthand how quickly established orders can fall, reinforcing the book's central theme: artistic and social revolutions are inseparable.

**Quick Take**: Major artistic breakthroughs often emerge from periods of social disruption and uncertainty.

### 4. The Birth of Impressionism

The decade-long struggle culminated in 1874 with the first Impressionist exhibition—an independent show organized by Monet, Renoir, Degas, Pissarro, and others who had followed Manet's lead. They chose the name "Impressionism" from Monet's "Impression, Sunrise" (1872), though it was originally meant as an insult by critics.

This exhibition represented the ultimate victory of artistic independence. The artists had created their own system, their own audience, and their own definition of what constituted good art.

> "Eventually art would imitate life; all the old orders would come crashing down; and Manet, Monet and Cézanne would emerge from the wreckage."

**Quick Take**: When the establishment won't recognize you, create your own establishment.

### 5. The Irony of Artistic Fame

Perhaps the book's most powerful lesson comes from Meissonier's fate. Once the most famous artist in the world, he's now largely forgotten, while Manet—who died in obscurity and rejection—is celebrated as the father of modern art. The Academy that rejected Manet now displays his works in their most honored positions, while Meissonier's meticulous masterpieces gather dust in storage rooms.

This reversal demonstrates that artistic success isn't determined by contemporary popularity or critical acclaim, but by who influences future generations.

**Quick Take**: Real artistic legacy belongs to those who change the direction of art, not those who perfect its existing forms.

## Key Takeaways
- **Artistic revolutions require both rebels and martyrs**—Manet provided the vision, but needed fellow artists to create the movement
- **Establishments always resist innovation**, but eventually fall when enough artists challenge their authority
- **Contemporary relevance matters more than technical perfection** in creating lasting artistic impact
- **Social and artistic change are interconnected**—the fall of academic art paralleled the fall of the political establishment
- **True artistic legacy belongs to those who influence future generations**, not those who succeed in their own time

- How does rejection from mainstream success sometimes fuel greater innovation?
- In your field, who represents the Meissoniers (perfecting existing forms) versus the Manets (creating entirely new approaches)?
- What current social changes might be creating space for new artistic or creative movements?
- How much should creators prioritize technical skill versus personal vision and contemporary relevance?

## Conclusion
"The Judgment of Paris" is more than just an art history book—it's a timeless story about how revolutions actually happen. Through meticulous research and compelling storytelling, King reveals that artistic change comes from courage, persistence, and the willingness to be rejected by the establishment in service of a larger vision.

The book's greatest value lies in showing that the birth of Impressionism wasn't inevitable—it was earned through years of struggle, rejection, and unwavering belief in a new way of seeing. Whether you're an artist, entrepreneur, or creative professional, this story demonstrates that changing how the world sees things requires both extraordinary vision and extraordinary courage.

For anyone who has ever faced rejection for their innovative ideas, or who wonders how traditions transform, this book offers both inspiration and a strategic blueprint: find your fellow rebels, create your own platforms, and paint (or work) your way into history.

**Is this worth your time?** Absolutely—especially if you're interested in how creative revolutions happen, the courage to challenge establishments, and the relationship between art and social change. It's five years of art history distilled into one compelling decade that changed how we see the world forever.
