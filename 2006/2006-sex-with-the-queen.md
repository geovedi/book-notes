# Sex with the Queen
**Author**: Eleanor Herman | **Year**: 2006 | **Category**: History & Biography

## Summary
"Sex with the Queen" reveals the shocking truth about royal women's secret lives across 900 years of European history. This eye-opening history exposes how queens, trapped in loveless political marriages and isolated in gilded cages, risked everything for passion, love, and sometimes power itself.

From medieval times to Princess Diana, the book uncovers the double standard that allowed kings unlimited mistresses while queens faced execution for the same behavior. Through meticulous research and vivid storytelling, Herman shows that royal adultery was rarely about lust—it was about politics, power, and the desperate search for human connection in lives of magnificent misery.

History buffs, feminist readers, and anyone fascinated by royal scandals will devour this juicy exploration of what really happened behind palace walls. You'll never look at royal families the same way again.

## Core Insights

### The Royal Double Standard
European kings enjoyed official mistresses who often had more power, grander rooms, and gorgeous gowns than their queens. Charles II of England kept multiple mistresses while married to the "tiny bucktoothed Princess Catherine of Portugal," explaining that God wouldn't make a man miserable "for taking a little pleasure out of the way." Meanwhile, queens were expected to emulate the Virgin Mary—chaste, patient, and obedient.

> "Upon that all the property in the world depends. We hang a thief for stealing a sheep, but the unchastity of a woman transfers sheep, and farm, and all from the right owner." — Dr. Samuel Johnson (1757)

**Quick Take**: This double standard wasn't rooted in misogyny but biology—before 1927's blood tests, only a queen's absolute fidelity could prove the royal bloodline remained pure.

### Politics, Not Passion, Sealed Their Fates
Queens weren't destroyed because they had affairs—they were destroyed because their adultery threatened political power structures. Adultery charges cast doubt on offspring legitimacy, opening doors for rival factions to seize power. Anne Boleyn and Catherine Howard lost their heads not because they were unfaithful, but because they stood in the way of ambitious courtiers jockeying for position.

**Quick Take**: At court, it was never about the sex—it was about who gained or lost power. The guilty Caroline of Brunswick was cleared by powerful friends, while innocent Anne Boleyn was executed by powerful enemies.

### The Gilded Cage Was a Prison
Royal luxury was largely illusion. Palaces were drafty, rat-infested, and lacked basic comforts like window screens. Queens were lonely, isolated from family and friends, with children often removed to be raised by state officials as "property of the state." One queen watched thousands of rats evacuate during a palace fire and wasn't sorry to see it go.

> "All of this is supposed to be fun, and yet one does not see anyone having fun and senses that there is more spite than pleasure." — Elizabeth Charlotte (1705)

**Quick Take**: Understanding this misery helps explain why devout, God-fearing women might jump into adulterous affairs seeking love and understanding.

### Success Through Scandal
Some queens actually thrived despite their affairs. Catherine the Great made Russia a world power while enjoying young lovers. Queen Marie of Romania guided her country through WWI and Communist revolution—with three of her five children fathered by lovers. Their people didn't care about their morals as long as they provided good governance.

**Quick Take**: When queens delivered real benefits to their nations, their personal lives became irrelevant to their subjects' support.

### The Ultimate Price
For others, the cost was devastating. Anne Boleyn declared "Good Christian people, I am come here not to preach a sermon but to die" before her execution. Marie Antoinette proclaimed "It is to live that requires courage, not to die" on her way to the guillotine. Even successful queens often lives of "magnificence and misery."

> "Believe me, ladies, do not envy a splendor that does not constitute happiness." — Josephine Bonaparte

**Quick Take**: Even at the height of power, royal women often discovered that crowns were "bitter pills gilded over" — glittering on the outside but miserable to bear.

## Questions to Consider
- How much of royal women's "scandalous" behavior was really about seeking emotional connection versus political power?
- Would modern DNA testing have changed the course of European history by revealing paternity truths centuries earlier?
- Why do we remain fascinated by royal scandals while ignoring the same behavior in ordinary people?
- How have the double standards around sexuality and power persisted into modern times?
- What does the contrast between royal splendor and personal misery reveal about the nature of power and privilege?

## Conclusion
"Sex with the Queen" is worth your time because it humanizes historical figures we've been taught to see as either virtuous saints or wicked villains. Eleanor Herman reveals that royal women were simply complex humans trapped in extraordinary circumstances, making choices about love, power, and survival that still resonate today.

The book's biggest strength is showing how politics, not passion, determined these women's fates—and how that political calculus remains strikingly relevant in our own age of power, scandal, and public judgment of women's private lives.

Read it to understand that behind every crown was a woman who often wished she could trade her throne for a simple life of authentic connection and happiness.
