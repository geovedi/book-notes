# Edible
**Authors**: Daniella Martin | **Year**: 2014 | **Category**: Science & Nature

## Summary
"Edible" presents a compelling case for entomophagy (eating insects) as humanity's most logical solution to global food sustainability challenges. Martin combines environmental science, nutritional research, cultural anthropology, and culinary exploration to transform readers from disgust to curiosity about insect consumption. Through personal adventures, historical context, and practical guidance, she reveals how 80% of world cultures already embrace insects as protein sources while Western societies remain culturally blocked from this efficient, nutritious, and environmentally friendly food source.

This book serves as both wake-up call and practical guide for anyone concerned about food sustainability, environmental impact, or expanding their culinary horizons. Readers will gain the scientific understanding, cultural perspective, and practical confidence to consider insects as a viable protein source for themselves and the planet's future.

## Core Insights

### The Environmental Imperative: The "McImpacts" of Conventional Meat
Martin's devastating comparison reveals true environmental costs: producing one pound of beef requires 10 pounds of feed, 1,000 gallons water, and 200 square feet of land, while one pound of insects needs just 2 pounds of feed, 1 gallon water, and 2 cubic feet of space. Beyond resource efficiency, insects produce no methane (unlike cattle), can be farmed vertically in cities, and have feed conversion ratios of 1.5:1 compared to beef's 10:1. Insects aren't just alternative protein—they're 1,000 times more efficient biological systems for converting resources into nutrition.

> "Cows and pigs are the SUVs; insects are the bicycles." - David Gracer

**Quick Take**: If environmental impact were listed on menus like calories, insects would be the clear choice for planetary health.

### Cultural Prejudice vs. Biological Reality
The Western aversion to insects is culturally constructed, not biologically based. Martin reveals that 1,900+ insect species are edible, with Mexico alone documenting 500 varieties. Children naturally explore insects with curiosity before cultural conditioning teaches disgust. The author argues that Western culture made an arbitrary historical decision to embrace certain arthropods (shrimp, lobster) while rejecting others, despite their biological similarities. Our food preferences reflect cultural bias, not nutritional wisdom.

> "In a hundred years, it will probably be so common that no one can imagine a world without it."

**Quick Take**: Your "ew" reaction reflects cultural programming, not biological reality—insects are nutritionally superior to many Western delicacies.

### The Nutritional Powerhouse in Small Packages
Insects provide complete protein with all essential amino acids, plus crucial nutrients often missing from Western diets: iron, calcium, B vitamins, and healthy omega-3 and omega-6 fatty acids. Unlike plants, insects offer highly bioavailable nutrients in concentrated forms. Martin explains that insect nutrition isn't just adequate—it's often superior to conventional meats, with crickets providing more calcium than milk per gram and more iron than beef. Their nutritional profile combines the best of plant and animal foods without the inflammatory saturated fats.

**Quick Take**: Insects deliver nutrient density that rivals superfoods while offering the protein completeness of conventional meats.

### The Real Paleo Diet: Our Evolutionary Inheritance
Contrary to modern fad interpretations of paleo eating, humans evolved as insect-eaters, not mammoth hunters. Martin presents evidence that our primate ancestors consumed insects as their primary protein source, and early humans continued this practice throughout evolution. Insects provided the concentrated protein and fat necessary for brain development, with their natural abundance and easy catchability making them evolutionary protein sources. The book argues that returning to insects isn't radical innovation but restoration of our ancestral diet.

**Quick Take**: Humans are biologically adapted to insect consumption—our aversion is cultural, not evolutionary.

### Culinary Discovery: The Surprising World of Insect Cuisine
Far from being primitive survival food, insects offer sophisticated culinary possibilities. Martin describes taste profiles ranging from crickets' "nutty shrimp" flavor to wax worms' "enoki-pine nut" taste and giant water bugs' unique "anchovies in banana-rose brine" complexity. High-end restaurants like Noma and research institutions like the Nordic Food Lab are exploring insect gastronomy, proving they belong in fine dining contexts. Cooking methods include roasting (for nutty flavors), sautéing, and traditional preparation techniques spanning millennia of cultural wisdom.

**Quick Take**: Insect cuisine offers flavor diversity that expands, rather than limits, culinary possibilities.

## Action Items

- **Start with cricket flour**: Add to smoothies, baked goods, or protein shakes for easy nutritional boost without psychological barriers

- **Source from reputable suppliers**: Ensure insects are raised specifically for human consumption with appropriate feed and safety standards

- **Try familiar preparations first**: Begin with roasted crickets seasoned like nuts or insects ground into familiar sauces

- **Host a tasting party**: Make insect consumption a social experience to normalize it and reduce psychological barriers

- **Research local regulations**: Support insect food businesses and advocate for policy changes that legalize and normalize alternative proteins

## Questions to Consider

- If shrimp and lobster are essentially underwater insects, what determines our acceptance of one but disgust toward the other?

- How would your food choices change if environmental impact were displayed on restaurant menus alongside calories and prices?

- If 80% of cultures already eat insects, is it Western culture that's abnormal, not the practice of entomophagy?

- What other culturally-determined food prejudices might be limiting our nutritional and environmental sustainability?

- How might incorporating insects change your emergency preparedness or self-sufficiency planning?

## Conclusion

"Edible" is far more than a bizarre food adventure—it's a compelling argument for humanity's dietary future. Martin transforms a potentially icky subject into fascinating exploration of biology, culture, environmental science, and culinary possibility. Her personal journey from curious skeptic to passionate advocate makes the subject accessible and surprisingly persuasive. While readers may not finish with immediate plans to start farming mealworms, they'll emerge with fundamentally changed perspectives on sustainable food choices and cultural food prejudices.

The book's greatest strength is its ability to make the radical seem reasonable and the impossible seem inevitable. By the final pages, insect consumption doesn't appear as weird alternative but as logical necessity for planetary health. Martin's combination of scientific evidence, cultural perspective, and practical guidance creates an irresistible case that readers will find difficult to dismiss. For anyone concerned about environmental sustainability, food security, or expanding their understanding of global food cultures, this book offers essential reading that might just change how you view your dinner plate forever.
