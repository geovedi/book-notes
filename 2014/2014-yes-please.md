# Yes Please
**Author**: Amy Poehler | **Year**: 2014

## Summary
Amy Poehler's "Yes Please" is a refreshingly honest and hilarious memoir that doubles as a guide to navigating life, career, and creativity without taking yourself too seriously. Written during a chaotic period of divorce, single parenthood, and career demands, Poehler offers wisdom gained from decades in comedy, improvisation, and television. The book is structured in three parts—"Say Whatever You Want," "Do Whatever You Like," and "Be Whoever You Are"—reflecting her philosophy of embracing opportunity and authenticity.

This book is perfect for anyone feeling stuck in their creative or personal life, especially women balancing career ambitions with family responsibilities. Readers will gain practical advice on overcoming self-doubt, embracing vulnerability as strength, and finding humor in life's messiest moments.

## Core Insights

### The Power of "Yes Please"
Poehler's title captures her core philosophy: saying "yes" to opportunity while maintaining boundaries ("please"). The "yes" comes from improvisational training and youthful openness, while "please" represents the wisdom of collaboration and knowing you don't do things alone. This mindset enables taking risks without losing yourself.

> "Can we figure out what we want, ask for it, and stop talking? Yes please. Is being vulnerable a power position? Yes please. Am I allowed to take up space? Yes please."

**Quick Take**: Approach life's opportunities with enthusiasm while maintaining your boundaries and understanding that success requires collaboration.

### Treat Your Career Like a Bad Boyfriend
One of Poehler's most memorable metaphors: your career won't take care of you, won't call you back, and will openly flirt with other people. The key is practicing healthy detachment—caring about your work but not being desperate for results. Your career rewards you when you don't act needy.

> "Your career will never marry you. Ambivalence can help tame the beast. Remember, your career is a bad boyfriend. It likes it when you don't depend on it."

**Quick Take**: Focus on doing good work without becoming emotionally dependent on outcomes or validation. Your career will chase you when you act like other things matter more.

### Do Things Before You're Ready
Poehler challenges the notion that you need to be fully prepared before taking action. Great people succeed precisely because they act before feeling completely ready, using youthful energy and improvisational courage to figure things out along the way.

> "I believe great people do things before they are ready. This is America and I am allowed to have healthy self-esteem."

**Quick Take**: Stop waiting for perfect conditions or complete readiness. Take action first and figure out the details as you go—confidence comes from doing, not preparing.

### Writing and Creativity Are Hard (And That's OK)
Poehler demystifies the creative process by being brutally honest about how difficult it is. Writing isn't romantic or magical—it's "hard and boring and occasionally great but usually not." The key is pushing through resistance by taking action rather than waiting for inspiration.

> "Writing is this: hard and boring and occasionally great but usually not... Writing the book is about writing the book. The doing is the thing."

**Quick Take**: Accept that creative work is difficult and mundane much of the time. Success comes from consistent action, not waiting for muse or motivation.

### Improvisation as Life Philosophy
Drawing from her extensive improv background, Poehler shows how saying "yes, and" to life's opportunities creates growth and possibility. Improv teaches listening, supporting others, being specific, and finding games within scenes—all applicable to navigating relationships and career.

> "The 'yes' comes from my improvisational days and the opportunity that comes with youth, and the 'please' comes from the wisdom of knowing that agreeing to do something usually means you aren't doing it alone."

**Quick Take**: Apply improv principles to life: listen actively, support others' ideas, build on what exists, and stay present in the moment.

## Conclusion
"Yes Please" is absolutely worth your time if you're looking for honest, practical wisdom delivered with humor and humility. Poehler's greatest strength is her refusal to romanticize the creative process or life's struggles—she tells the truth about how hard everything is while still showing up and doing the work anyway.

The book's biggest value lies in its permission to be imperfect, to struggle, and to keep going anyway. Poehler normalizes the chaos of balancing career, parenthood, relationships, and personal growth while maintaining your sense of humor. Her "bad boyfriend" career advice alone is worth the price of admission, offering a revolutionary approach to professional ambition that prioritizes wellbeing over desperate achievement.

Read this when you need permission to be human, when you're tired of self-help gurus promising effortless success, or when you need a friend telling you the truth while making you laugh at life's absurdity. It's the anti-guru guide to actually living a creative, ambitious life without losing yourself in the process.
