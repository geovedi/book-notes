# As You Wish: Inconceivable Tales from the Making of The Princess Bride
**Authors**: Cary Elwes, Joe Layden | **Year**: 2014 | **Category**: Art & Creativity

## Summary
This charming memoir takes readers behind the scenes of one of cinema's most beloved cult classics, The Princess Bride. Written by Westley himself (Cary Elwes), the book chronicles the incredible journey of how an "unfilmable" book became a timeless movie that spans generations. From Elwes's unexpected casting as the farm boy-turned-pirate to the intensive sword training, injuries, and the magical camaraderie among cast members, this is a love letter to filmmaking and the serendipitous creation of something truly special.

**Perfect for**: Film lovers, Princess Bride fans, aspiring actors, and anyone who believes in the magic of movies made with passion rather than budget.

## Core Insights

### The "Unfilmable" Classic That Almost Never Happened
For fifteen years, Hollywood declared William Goldman's The Princess Bride impossible to adapt. Studios couldn't figure out how to market it—was it a fairy tale, adventure, romance, comedy, or satire? Even legendary director François Truffaut couldn't get it made. It took Rob Reiner, coming off successes with This Is Spinal Tap and Stand by Me, to convince a studio head to let him make his "favorite book."

> "Even François Truffaut couldn't make this movie." — William Goldman

**Quick Take**: Sometimes the most passionate projects come from those who love the source material most, not from market research.

### The Accidental Star
Cary Elwes was a 23-year-old unknown actor shooting an indie film in Berlin when Rob Reiner flew to meet him (during the Chernobyl fallout scare) to offer him Westley. Elwes had loved the book since age thirteen, given to him by his stepfather who had worked with Goldman. His lack of sword fighting experience (he'd been told he'd never master it) didn't deter Reiner, who promised training with "the best."

**Quick Take**: Right place, right time, plus genuine passion for the project beats polished experience every time.

### The Greatest Sword Fight in Modern Times
Elwes and Mandy Patinkin (Inigo Montoya) trained eight hours a day, five days a week with legendary stunt coordinators Bob Anderson and Peter Diamond. Patinkin, driven by the personal connection of wanting to "get his father back" through the character, trained for two months before Elwes even started. They studied classic swashbuckling films to create what they called "the swordfight to end all swordfights."

> "I'll become the greatest sword fighter, and my reward will not be to be in this movie... my reward will be that my father will come back." — Mandy Patinkin

**Quick Take**: Extraordinary physical performances come from extraordinary emotional commitment.

### The Gentle Giant
André the Giant (Fezzik) couldn't fit into hotel bathtubs, needed custom chairs, and could drink astonishing quantities of wine (a reported 100+ bottles in one sitting). Yet despite his intimidating size, he was incredibly gentle and kind. During filming, when André accidentally passed gas loudly during a serious scene, making everyone break character, he comforted the embarrassed Elwes by saying, "my farts always make people laugh... That was a big one, wasn't it?"

**Quick Take**: True gentleness has nothing to do with physical size.

### The Comedy Magic of Billy Crystal
Billy Crystal filmed his role as Miracle Max in just three days but created some of the movie's most memorable moments through improvisation. The famous MLT sandwich rant ("mutton, lettuce, and tomato sandwich, where the mutton is nice and lean") was entirely ad-libbed. Crystal stayed in character even between takes, transforming into the crotchety old man the moment makeup was applied.

> "Sonny, true love is the greatest thing in the world... except for a nice MLT—mutton, lettuce, and tomato sandwich."

**Quick Take**: When you cast brilliant comedians and let them riff, you get movie gold.

### The Movie That Wouldn't Die
The Princess Bride opened to only moderate success ($30.8 million) and confused critics who couldn't categorize it. It found life through VHS rentals and purchases, becoming one of the first films to be passed down through generations like The Wizard of Oz. Parents who loved it as kids showed it to their children, creating a cross-generational phenomenon.

**Quick Take**: Sometimes a movie finds its audience decades later, proving great work doesn't expire.

### Lines That Became Cultural Touchstones
The movie generated an extraordinary number of quoted lines that permeate culture: "Hello. My name is Inigo Montoya. You killed my father. Prepare to die," "Inconceivable!," "As you wish," "Have fun storming the castle," and "Never get involved in a land war in Asia." Patinkin still gets asked daily to recite his character's signature line.

**Quick Take**: When dialogue captures universal truths with humor, it becomes eternal.

## Action Items
- **For filmmakers**: Make what you love, not what you think will sell
- **For actors**: Immerse yourself completely in your character, even in comedy
- **For creatives**: Don't let "experts" tell you something's impossible
- **For everyone**: Revisit movies you loved as a child with your own kids

## Questions to Consider
- What makes certain movies become family traditions while others fade away?
- How does genuine passion for a project translate to audience connection?
- Why does humor combined with heart create such enduring stories?
- What role does timing play in creative success versus failure?

## Conclusion
This isn't just a behind-the-scenes book—it's a masterclass in how movie magic happens when the right people come together at the right time with the right material. The Princess Bride succeeded precisely because it defied categorization: it's funny, romantic, adventurous, and heartfelt all at once. Elwes's affectionate memoir captures that rare lightning in a bottle when everyone involved knew they were creating something special, even if Hollywood didn't realize it yet.

Is it worth your time? Absolutely. It's a joyous celebration of creativity, collaboration, and the enduring power of storytelling. The biggest reason to read it is to understand how the impossible becomes possible through passion, persistence, and the magic of "as you wish" — which, as we learn, really means "I love you."
