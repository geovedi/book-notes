# Don't Mention The Wars
**Authors**: Tony Connelly | **Year**: 2014 | **Category**: History & Biography

## Summary
Tony Connelly's "Don't Mention The Wars" is a witty and insightful exploration of European stereotypes, tracing their historical origins and examining how they persist in modern times. As an Irish journalist who grew up in Northern Ireland during The Troubles and later served as RTÉ's Europe Reporter in Brussels, Connelly offers a unique perspective on how we view our European neighbors. The book investigates the psychology behind stereotyping, from ancient historical prejudices to contemporary European tensions, ultimately questioning whether these stereotypes are harmless fun or dangerous oversimplifications.

This book is perfect for anyone interested in European culture, history, or international relations. Readers will gain a deeper understanding of how stereotypes shape our perceptions, why they persist despite globalization, and how they can both reveal hidden truths and fuel dangerous prejudices.

## Core Insights

### The Psychology of Stereotypes: Pictures in Our Heads
Walter Lippmann, the journalist who coined "Cold War," argued that stereotypes are "pictures in our heads" that help us manage the complexity of the modern world. These mental shortcuts simplify the "great blooming, buzzing confusion of the outer world" but also encourage swift, often unfair judgments of others. Research shows we categorize people within milliseconds of first encounter, creating neural pathways that light up "like a pinball machine" when we encounter familiar stereotypes.

> "For the most part we do not first see, and then define; we define and then see." - Walter Lippmann

**Quick Take**: Stereotyping isn't just learned behavior—it's a cognitive reflex that becomes more pronounced when we're stressed, tired, or mentally stretched.

### Historical Roots: From Roman Empire to Modern Europe
European stereotypes date back centuries, forged through historical conflicts, religious wars, and imperial rivalries. The Romans first divided Europe between civilization and barbarians, while the Holy Roman Empire created competing ethnic groups that developed negative images of each other. By the 17th century, English aristocrats had codified these prejudices: "French courteous. Spanish lordly. Italian amorous. German clownish."

> "The treacherous Frenchman, the pious Spaniard, the drunken Dutchman were all born."

**Quick Take**: Many stereotypes we consider modern are actually ancient prejudices passed down through generations, shaped by everything from wars to royal marriages.

### Media Influence: From Pug's Tour to Modern Sitcoms
Media has played a crucial role in perpetuating stereotypes. In 1824, "Pug's Tour through Europe" taught children to see foreigners as caricatures—shoulder-shrugging Frenchmen, beer-guzzling Germans, and cash-counting Dutch. British sitcoms of the 1970s like "Mind Your Language" and "Fawlty Towers" institutionalized these images for millions, portraying foreigners as either comical or threatening.

> "The loudest laughs were when foreigners were made to look silly, even if their linguistic pratfalls were kind of good-natured."

**Quick Take**: What we consume as entertainment shapes our perceptions more than we realize, and these media stereotypes can outlive their historical context by generations.

### The European Crisis: Stereotypes as Weapons
The 2008 financial crisis and subsequent eurozone crisis revived old stereotypes with dangerous consequences. Greeks were portrayed as lazy and corrupt, Germans as ruthless dominators, while the "Polish plumber" became France's symbol of economic threat. These stereotypes weren't just casual prejudices—they became tools in political battles, justifying austerity measures and fueling nationalist movements.

> "Stereotyping is useful in the blame game. In the first stage politicians and national elites find it's convenient to blame outsiders. Then they try to explain their positions using stereotypes." - Aristos Doxiadis

**Quick Take**: Economic crises revive historical stereotypes as nations seek scapegoats, turning cultural differences into weapons of political rhetoric.

### Eastern Europe: The Blank Canvas
For decades, Western Europe viewed Eastern Europe through a Cold War lens of drab uniformity. With the fall of communism and EU expansion, Westerners had to quickly learn about countries they knew little about. Rather than developing nuanced understandings, many subsumed diverse cultures under generic labels like "hard workers" or continued viewing the region through outdated prejudices.

> "In 1938, when Neville Chamberlain gave Hitler the green light to annex the Sudetenland, he described Czechoslovakia as 'a faraway place,' a people 'of which we know nothing.'"

**Quick Take**: Lack of knowledge can be more dangerous than inaccurate stereotypes, creating vacuums that politicians can fill with convenient fictions.

### The Irish Paradox: Both Colonizer and Colonized
Ireland's unique position as both colonized by Britain and participant in British imperial projects created complex stereotyping patterns. The British viewed the Irish as apes and idiots, while the Irish absorbed British attitudes toward other Europeans. This created a strange paradox where Irish people could simultaneously be victims of stereotyping while perpetuating stereotypes against others.

> "We were supposed to laugh at Basil's toe-curling snobbery, but were meant to take Cybil seriously – just as Basil did."

**Quick Take**: Our experience of being stereotyped doesn't necessarily make us immune to stereotyping others—in fact, it can sometimes reinforce the cycle.

## Questions to Consider

1. Are stereotypes ever useful, or are they always harmful oversimplifications that prevent genuine understanding?

2. How do our own experiences of being stereotyped affect how we view others—does it make us more empathetic or more likely to perpetuate the cycle?

3. In an age of globalization and European integration, why do stereotypes persist and even strengthen during times of crisis?

4. Can we distinguish between harmless cultural observations and dangerous prejudices, and where is that line drawn?

5. How has social media and the internet changed how stereotypes are created and spread compared to previous generations?

6. What responsibility do politicians and media have in challenging stereotypes versus reflecting popular attitudes?

## Key Takeaways

- **Question Your Assumptions**: Recognize that your first impressions of other cultures are often shaped by centuries-old prejudices rather than personal experience.

- **Media Literacy**: Be aware of how entertainment and news media reinforce stereotypes, even unintentionally through casual casting or lazy writing.

- **Crisis Awareness**: During economic or political crises, be especially vigilant about stereotypes being used as political weapons or scapegoating mechanisms.

- **Personal Experience Matters**: Direct interaction with other cultures is the most effective antidote to stereotypical thinking, but even well-traveled people can fall into stereotypical patterns when stressed.

- **Historical Context**: Understanding the historical origins of stereotypes can help defuse their emotional power and reveal their often arbitrary nature.

- **Self-Reflection**: Consider how you might appear through others' stereotypical lenses—this empathy can reduce the sting of stereotypes you encounter.

## Conclusion
"Don't Mention The Wars" is both entertaining and profoundly relevant in our increasingly polarized world. Connelly masterfully balances humor with serious analysis, showing how stereotypes reveal as much about ourselves as about those we stereotype. The book's greatest strength is its historical sweep—from Roman times through Brexit—demonstrating that these mental patterns have remarkable persistence.

This is absolutely worth your time if you want to understand the hidden forces shaping European politics, culture, and international relations. The biggest reason to read it is that it will make you question your own assumptions about other cultures while providing fascinating historical context for today's political tensions. In an era of rising nationalism and anti-immigrant sentiment, understanding how stereotypes work has never been more crucial.

Connelly's personal journey from Derry to Brussels provides a perfect framework for exploring how we all carry these "pictures in our heads," and how we might begin to replace them with more nuanced, accurate understandings of our European neighbors.
