# Cliffs End Farm, Isle of Thanet, Kent
**Authors**: Jacqueline I. McKinley, Matt Leivers, Jörn Schuster, Peter Marshall, Alistair J. Barclay, Nick Stoodley | **Year**: 2014 | **Category**: History & Biography

## Summary
Cliffs End Farm reveals an extraordinary archaeological site that served as a mortuary and ritual center for over 2,000 years, with the most remarkable discovery being scientific proof of long-distance maritime mobility between Britain and continental Europe during the Bronze Age and Iron Age. The site, located on the southern edge of the Isle of Thanet overlooking Pegwell Bay, contained human remains of individuals who had traveled from Scandinavia, the Mediterranean, and other distant regions to this important coastal gateway.

The excavations uncovered a complex sequence of ritual and mortuary activity, including Bronze Age barrows, elaborate mortuary features with articulated and disarticulated human remains, and an Anglo-Saxon cemetery focused around the earlier monuments. What makes this site exceptional is the scientific evidence showing that people from across Europe were not just trading with Britain but actually traveling to and being buried there, challenging our understanding of prehistoric mobility.

This groundbreaking archaeological work demonstrates how Thanet's strategic location at the entrance to the Thames estuary made it a cosmopolitan gateway where people, ideas, and cultures converged over millennia.

## Core Insights

### A Gateway for Ancient Global Travelers
The most revolutionary finding from Cliffs End Farm is the scientific proof that people from across Europe were traveling to Britain as early as the Bronze Age. Strontium and oxygen isotope analysis of 26 human individuals revealed three distinct population groups buried at the site: local people, those from "cold climate" regions (interpreted as Scandinavian), and individuals from "southern" areas (likely western Mediterranean or Iberian Peninsula).

> "Of the 13 Late Bronze Age individuals studied, seven showed evidence for immigration, making this a mortuary site dominated by migrants."

This finding fundamentally changes our understanding of prehistoric mobility, showing that maritime connections between Britain and continental Europe were not just about trade goods but involved the actual movement of people over long distances.

**Quick Take**: Prehistoric Britain was more connected to Europe than previously imagined, with Thanet serving as an international gateway where diverse peoples gathered for ritual and burial.

### Ritual Complexity Across Millennia
The Mortuary Feature at Cliffs End represents one of the most complex prehistoric ritual sites ever discovered in Britain. Used continuously from the 10th to 3rd/4th centuries BC, it contained 13 articulated bodies alongside redeposited partial remains, dispersed semi-articulated body parts, and disarticulated bones. These depositions occurred in three distinct phases (11th-9th, 5th, and 4th-3rd centuries BC), suggesting evolving ritual practices over centuries.

> "The activity within the Mortuary Feature spans the period between the 10th and 4th/3rd centuries cal BC and is characterised by the deposition of 13 articulated bodies but includes redeposited partial articulated remains, dispersed semi-articulated remains, and disarticulated bones and bone fragments."

The site revealed intricate relationships between burial rites and geographic origins, with "southern" individuals tending to be among the redeposited remains while "Scandinavian" individuals were more commonly found in in situ burials.

**Quick Take**: Ancient rituals at Cliffs End were complex and evolving, with different treatment of the dead based on their geographic origins, suggesting sophisticated cultural protocols.

### Strategic Maritime Location
The site's location overlooking Pegwell Bay and the Wantsum Channel was no accident. Thanet was an island during prehistoric times, separated from mainland Kent by the Wantsum Channel, which served as the main shipping route into the Thames estuary. This strategic position made Cliffs End an ideal gateway for maritime traffic and cultural exchange.

> "The distribution and types of site reflects the geographical importance of Thanet as a gateway to the Thames Estuary and inland waterways of southern England."

This coastal advantage continued to be recognized in later periods, with the Anglo-Saxons establishing their cemetery around the earlier Bronze Age monuments, deliberately choosing this highly visible location that could be seen by seaborne travelers approaching the Wantsum Channel.

**Quick Take**: Geography shaped destiny at Cliffs End, where the site's visibility from the sea made it a natural gathering place and symbol of authority for over 2,000 years.

### Anglo-Saxon Continuity and Innovation
The Early Anglo-Saxon cemetery (6th-7th centuries AD) demonstrates how later peoples recognized and built upon the significance of earlier sacred spaces. The cemetery was deliberately focused around the Bronze Age barrows, particularly the higher ones that would have afforded greater visibility from the coast.

The Anglo-Saxons also established 74 pits in the southern part of the site, many containing large quantities of marine shellfish, suggesting communal gatherings. The presence of imported Ipswich ware pottery and German lava quern stones indicates continued international connections.

> "Cliffs End was used during both the 6th and 7th centuries AD and in many ways is a typical East Kent cemetery; the principles on which it was organised, its intimate relationship to earlier monuments and its highly visible location in the landscape, are features found throughout the burial grounds of Early Anglo-Saxon East Kent."

**Quick Take**: The Anglo-Saxons respected and incorporated ancient sacred spaces into their own mortuary practices, showing cultural continuity across millennia.

### Scientific Revolution in Archaeology
The Cliffs End project demonstrates how modern scientific techniques can revolutionize our understanding of the past. The extensive radiocarbon dating program (104 samples) combined with isotopic analysis provided unprecedented chronological resolution and proof of human mobility that would have been impossible to determine through traditional archaeology alone.

The scientific evidence revealed that people buried at Cliffs End had predominantly terrestrial diets but came from distinctly different geographic regions, with variations in these patterns across the three chronological periods of burial activity.

> "Analyses of C/N and Sr/O isotopes were carried out on teeth from 26 human individuals, three sheep/goat teeth and one brickearth sample. Apart from the predominance of terrestrial diets, the results show that individuals from three broadly distinguishable origins were buried at Cliffs End Farm."

**Quick Take**: Modern scientific techniques can reveal aspects of ancient life—in this case, human mobility and origins—that are invisible to traditional archaeological methods.

## Conclusion

Cliffs End Farm is essential reading for anyone interested in understanding how scientific archaeology is revolutionizing our knowledge of the ancient past. This remarkable site provides concrete evidence that prehistoric Britain was far more connected to continental Europe than previously imagined, with people traveling vast distances across treacherous seas for reasons that likely involved family, trade, alliance, and ritual.

The site's 2,000-year history of continuous use as a sacred space demonstrates how certain places acquire enduring significance that transcends individual cultures and time periods. The Anglo-Saxon reuse of Bronze Age monuments shows how later peoples recognized and incorporated the sacred landscapes of their predecessors.

What makes this volume particularly valuable is its demonstration of how traditional archaeology, when combined with cutting-edge scientific techniques, can reveal aspects of ancient human behavior that were previously completely invisible to us. The isotope evidence proving that Scandinavians and Mediterranean peoples were traveling to Britain and being there in the Bronze Age fundamentally transforms our understanding of prehistoric mobility and cultural exchange.

This is a landmark publication that sets new standards for archaeological reporting and demonstrates how scientific archaeology can answer fundamental questions about human mobility, identity, and cultural connection. It shows that ancient Britain was not an isolated backwater but an active participant in European networks of exchange and movement.
