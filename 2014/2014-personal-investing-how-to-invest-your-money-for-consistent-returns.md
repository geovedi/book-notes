# Personal Investing: How to Invest Your Money for Consistent Returns
**Authors**: Edwin Lim, Dr. Kaiwen Leong, Edward H. Choi | **Year**: 2014

## Summary
This practical investment guide demystifies the complex world of personal investing by providing a straightforward framework for building wealth consistently. The authors argue that the biggest mistake people make is wasting time earning money without learning how to make that money work for them. Through seven comprehensive chapters, they guide readers from understanding why cash isn't king to implementing defensive strategies and selecting the right investment vehicles.

Perfect for beginner to intermediate investors who want practical strategies without the jargon, this book emphasizes risk management, emotional control, and disciplined approaches over get-rich-quick schemes. The authors draw on decades of banking experience to create actionable frameworks that work in real-world markets.

## Core Insights

### Stop Wasting Time Making Money
The book opens with a powerful wake-up call: most of us focus intensely on earning money but neglect learning how to invest it effectively. The authors emphasize that cash sitting in bank accounts is actually losing value over time due to inflation. During the post-2008 financial crisis period, banks offered meager 0.25% interest rates while inflation averaged 1-4%, meaning your purchasing power was steadily eroding.

> "The worst investment you can have is cash… cash is going to become less over time"
> _Warren Buffett_

**Quick Take**: Your savings account isn't a safe harbor—it's a slow leak in your wealth bucket. Even low-risk investments typically outperform cash over the long term.

### Understand the Investment Psychology Landscape
The book identifies seven emotional stages every investor experiences: optimism, excitement, thrill, anxiety, denial, fear, and finally acceptance or desperation. Most investors fail because they don't anticipate these emotional rollercoasters and make impulsive decisions during critical moments.

> "Being impulsive increases the chance of you misjudging the entry and/or exit point of the investment. Do not be hasty—there will always be opportunities for investment as market trends tend to be cyclical."

**Quick Take**: Your biggest investing opponent isn't the market—it's your own emotions. Knowing your psychological triggers is more valuable than any hot stock tip.

### Build a Defensive Strategy Foundation
The authors' core philosophy is that "the best offense is a good defense" in investing. Before seeking high returns, you must build strategies to protect your capital. This includes diversification, understanding correlation between assets, and having clear exit strategies before entering any investment.

A key defensive principle is never mixing short-term savings with long-term investments. The authors warn that using money intended for mortgage payments or education to chase investment losses is how people dig themselves into financial holes they can't escape.

**Quick Take**: Defensive investing isn't about avoiding returns—it's about ensuring you stay in the game long enough to benefit from compound growth.

### Master the Four Pillars of Investment Success
The book outlines four essential pillars every investor must master:

1. **Understanding Yourself**: Knowing your risk tolerance, emotional triggers, and time horizon
2. **Understanding the Market**: Recognizing that different investment classes respond to different triggers (macroeconomic events for currencies, company performance for stocks)
3. **Having a Clear Strategy**: Not chasing trends or following herd mentality
4. **Consistent Monitoring**: Regular portfolio rebalancing and performance assessment

> "You are the 'driver' of your portfolio and without you at the helm to make the key decisions, you will never reach your destination."

**Quick Take**: Even professional money managers need engaged clients. You can't outsource thinking when it comes to your financial future.

### Choose the Right Investment Vehicles for Your Stage
The authors provide a clear progression of investment options based on experience level and capital:

**Starting Point**: High-yield savings accounts and fixed deposits for emergency funds
**Intermediate**: Structured deposits, index funds, and blue-chip stocks
**Advanced**: Individual stock picking, real estate, and alternative investments

They emphasize that funds and ETFs are excellent starting points because they provide instant diversification and professional management without requiring deep market knowledge. The key is matching investment complexity to your knowledge level and available time for monitoring.

**Quick Take**: Don't run before you can walk. Start with simple, diversified investments and add complexity as your knowledge grows.

### Implement Smart Exit Strategies
Perhaps the most valuable section focuses on when and how to exit investments. The authors observe that most investors know when to buy but have no clear strategy for when to sell. They recommend:

- Setting predetermined exit points before investing
- Using partial profit-taking and loss-cutting techniques
- Avoiding emotional attachment to investments
- Learning to "reduce positions" gradually when markets turn against you

> "Simple strategies can work just as well, so long as they can trigger the appropriate actions. So don't complicate matters if you are not ready."

**Quick Take**: A good exit strategy is like a seatbelt—it may feel restrictive, but it's what keeps you alive when things go wrong.

### Learn from Professional Traders' Techniques
The book provides insights into how professional traders operate, including:

- Using cost averaging (like Warren Buffett's Coca-Cola accumulation)
- Maintaining market event calendars for macroeconomic announcements
- Understanding that volatility creates opportunities for prepared investors
- Keeping strategies simple and executable

The authors stress that retail investors can adopt many professional techniques without the complexity, focusing on the core principles rather than the sophisticated algorithms.

**Quick Take**: You don't need to be a quant trader to think like one. Focus on systematic approaches rather than gut feelings.

## Best Quotes

- "Cash is fantastic to have on a rainy day or for short-term needs, but it is not an asset that will appreciate in value on its own over the long term."

- "Everyone can start investing for their future by simply taking the first steps in low-risk investments. It is a conscious effort and the earlier you start, the sooner you will understand how much you can benefit from it."

- "Always identify several defensive strategies so that you do not get stuck in a corner when the going gets tough. Getting into an investment is always easier then getting out of it, so be prepared to take a hit but don't ever get knocked out."

## Action Items

- **Calculate your inflation-adjusted returns**: Check if your savings accounts are actually losing purchasing power after inflation
- **Create separate accounts**: Keep emergency savings completely separate from investment capital
- **Define your exit strategy**: Before your next investment, write down exactly when and why you'll sell
- **Start a market calendar**: Track major economic announcements that could affect your portfolio
- **Practice position reduction**: If you have losing positions, practice cutting losses gradually rather than holding and hoping
- **Schedule quarterly reviews**: Set reminders to rebalance your portfolio every three months

## Questions to Consider

- What percentage of your wealth is sitting in cash and losing value to inflation?
- When was the last time you reviewed your portfolio allocation?
- Do you know your exact exit strategy for each current investment?
- How do you typically react when investments go against you—panic or stick to your plan?
- Are you investing money you'll need for short-term goals?
- What's your system for avoiding emotional investment decisions?

## Conclusion

This book is absolutely worth your time if you're someone who earns well but invests poorly or not at all. The authors' combined decades in international banking provide practical wisdom that's refreshingly free of jargon and get-rich-quick promises. The greatest strength of this book is its emphasis on psychology and defensive strategies—areas that actually determine most investors' success more than stock selection or market timing.

The framework of the Four Pillars provides a solid foundation for thinking about investing systematically rather than emotionally. While some specific market examples are dated (published 2014), the principles are timeless and applicable across market cycles. The book's biggest insight is that successful investing is less about finding the perfect stock and more about having the perfect process and mindset.

Read this book if you want to transform from a passive saver to an active investor without taking unnecessary risks. It's particularly valuable for professionals who have built successful careers but haven't applied the same systematic thinking to their investment approach.
