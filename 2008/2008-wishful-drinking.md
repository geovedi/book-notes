# Wishful Drinking
**Author**: Carrie Fisher | **Year**: 2008

## Summary
Carrie Fisher's memoir is a razor-sharp, hilarious, and heartbreaking account of growing up in Hollywood royalty as the daughter of Debbie Reynolds and Eddie Fisher. Written after electroconvulsive therapy (ECT) wiped out much of her memory, Fisher reconstructs her extraordinary life through the lens of someone rediscovering who she is—complete withStar Wars fame, addiction, mental illness, and enough family dysfunction to fuel several seasons of a soap opera.

This book delivers a masterclass in finding humor in tragedy, showing readers how laughter can be both a survival mechanism and a form of resistance against life's absurdities.

## Core Insights

### **1. The Geography of Tragedy**
Fisher's central revelation: "If my life wasn't funny it would just be true, and that is unacceptable." She demonstrates how the difference between tragedy and comedy often comes down to perspective—what she calls "location, location, location." The same events can either destroy you or become material you can work with, depending on where you choose to stand emotionally.

> "What that really means is, let's say something happens and from a certain slant maybe it's tragic, even a little bit shocking. Then time passes and you go to the funny slant, and now that very same thing can no longer do you any harm."

**Quick Take**: Your most painful stories become less powerful when you learn to tell them as jokes.

### **2. The ECT Epiphany**
After undergoing electroconvulsive therapy for severe depression, Fisher literally loses memories but gains freedom from the "crippling feeling of defeat and hopelessness." She presents a compelling case that sometimes losing yourself—your history, your traumas, your grudges—might be worth gaining your future.

> "Some of my memories will never return. They are lost—along with the crippling feeling of defeat and hopelessness. Not a tremendous price to pay when you think about it. Totally worth it!"

**Quick Take**: What you remember isn't as important as what you can still imagine.

### **3. Hollywood Inbreeding as American Royalty**
Fisher brilliantly exposes celebrity culture as America's version of royal families, complete with the same problems of inbreeding, entitlement, and inevitable decline. Her family's convoluted romantic history reads like a Greek tragedy performed at the Oscars.

> "Celebrity is sort of like American royalty. So my brother and I are like those sad, sad cases like King Charles the Second of Spain. The last of the Habsburgs."

**Quick Take**: Growing up famous isn't privilege—it's just a more photographed version of dysfunction.

### **4. The Princess Leia Prison**
Fisher's ambivalent relationship with her Star Wars icon reveals how fame can become a golden cage. She discovers that being turned into merchandise—from dolls to PEZ dispensers—means George Lucas owns not just her likeness but part of her identity, every mirror glance costing her a mental royalty payment.

> "I emerge from my three-week-long ECT treatment to discover that I am not only this Princess Leia creature but also several-sized dolls, various T-shirts and posters, some cleansing items, and a bunch of other merchandise."

**Quick Take**: Sometimes the things that make you famous can become the bars of your own prison.

### **5. Survival as Performance Art**
Fisher reframes being a "survivor" not as a noble state but as an ongoing performance where you must "keep getting in trouble to show off your gift." Her candid discussion of bipolar disorder and addiction removes the stigma while acknowledging the exhausting work of staying alive.

> "When you are a survivor, in order to be a really good one, you have to keep getting in trouble to show off your gift."

**Quick Take**: Recovery isn't a destination—it's the world's longest running show.

## Best Quotes

- "You know what's funny about death? I mean other than absolutely nothing at all? You'd think we could remember finding out we weren't immortal."
- "Resentment is like drinking poison and waiting for the other person to die."
- "The instant the camera goes on, you transform into the person the camera wants you to be."
- "George Lucas is a sadist. But like any abused child, wearing a metal bikini, chained to a giant slug about to die, I keep coming back for more."

## Questions to Consider

1. What traumatic event from your past could be retold as comedy? What would change if you did?
2. Which memories would you be willing to lose if it meant gaining freedom from their emotional weight?
3. How has your family's "scandals" shaped your identity, and can you reframe them as stories rather than burdens?
4. What version of yourself have you become for others, and is there still a "you" underneath the performance?

## Conclusion

"Wishful Drinking" is Fisher's hilarious rebellion against both the glamour and the trauma of her life. Her ECT-induced amnesia becomes the perfect device for examining how we construct our identities from the fragments of what we remember and what we've survived. This isn't just a Hollywood memoir—it's a masterclass in self-invention through storytelling.

The book's greatest revelation is that Fisher's comedic talent wasn't just her career—it was her salvation. Every joke, every self-deprecating anecdote, every outrageous family story serves as both armor and art. By learning to laugh at everything that should have broken her, Fisher demonstrates the ultimate power of humor: the ability to transform pain into performance.

Read this when you need reminding that your most traumatic chapters might just be your best material, that survival looks different for everyone, and that sometimes the healthiest thing you can do is learn to find the funny angle. Fisher doesn't just survive her story—she rewrites it, performs it, and invites us to laugh along at the beautiful absurdity of it all.
